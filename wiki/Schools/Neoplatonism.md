# Neoplatonism

> The form of the Platonic tradition inaugurated by [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] (AD 204–70), lasting in its pagan form to the sixth century — a movement pursuing three interlocking aims: a complete rational metaphysics of the One, the soul's salvation through return to its source, and the defense and systematization of the Hellenic tradition against Christianity.

## Summary

"Neoplatonism" is a modern coinage distinguishing the tradition Plotinus founded from Plato's immediate disciples (the Old Academy) and from the Platonism of the earlier Roman Empire (Middle Platonism). Wallis periodizes the pagan movement in three post-Plotinian phases: (1) Plotinus's pupils [[Thinkers/Porphyry]] (c. 232–c. 305) and Amelius; (2) the Syrian and Pergamene schools deriving from [[Thinkers/Iamblichus]] (d. c. 326), whose theology the emperor Julian used in his failed pagan restoration (361–63); (3) the fifth–sixth-century schools of Athens and Alexandria. The Athenian School — Plutarch of Athens, Syrianus, [[Thinkers/Proclus]] (412–85), Damascius, Simplicius — was determinedly pagan and closed by Justinian in 529; the Alexandrian school, important mainly for its Aristotle commentaries, passed into Christian hands and its themes into Islamic philosophy.

**Three aims.** Read off Plotinus's *On the Three Hypostases* (Enn. V.1), the movement has (a) a **metaphysical** aim: three successively more unified levels of divine reality — Soul (*psychē*), Intelligence (*nous*), the One; (b) an **exegetic** aim: showing these principles implicit in the classical Greeks and fully articulate in Plato; (c) a **religious/experiential** aim: direct experience of Reality within one's own mind, held superior to all theoretical knowledge. The fundamental metaphysical principles (systematized in Proclus's *Elements of Theology*): unity is the condition of order and existence, and the cause is always more perfect than its effect.

**Character.** Plotinus's decisive innovation was identifying metaphysical realities with states of consciousness — the Hypostases are both objective beings and levels of inner awareness, a duality that generated the school's deepest interpretive tensions. Neoplatonic mysticism (strict sense: experience of union with Ultimate Reality) is integrated with rational philosophy, not irrationalist; its asceticism is mild and moderate, grounded in the material world's status as divine image — sharply opposed to Manichaean and extreme Christian asceticism, and to Gnosticism, whose evil creator, denial of the World-Soul's divinity, and anti-rationalism Plotinus attacked in Enn. II.9 ("to set oneself above Intelligence is to fall away from it").

## Sources of the System

The Neoplatonists presented the system as restoration of Plato's authentic teaching — Augustine called Plotinus "a man in whom Plato lived again" (*C. Acad.* 3.18); Proclus called Plato's philosophy "a divine revelation to men." Key materials:

- **Plato**: canon of *Timaeus*, *Republic*, *Phaedo*, *Phaedrus*, *Symposium*; Plotinus's exegetical innovation was reading the second part of the *Parmenides* as disguised theology — its hypotheses yielding the One (beyond Being), Intelligence, and Soul. The Form of the Good (*Republic*) was identified with the One. The logical function of the Forms was quietly dropped in favor of their aesthetic and cosmological functions. See [[Concepts/Parmenidean Being and the One (Parmenides)]].
- **Aristotle**: self-thinking Intelligence identified with the Demiurge; matter-form analysis adopted; but Plotinus rejected Aristotle's categories for the Intelligible world, his inseparable soul, and his denial of a principle beyond Intelligence. Porphyry and Iamblichus rehabilitated Aristotle as the authority in logic and natural philosophy — Porphyry admitted the Enneads contain "hidden Stoic and Peripatetic doctrines" and "a condensation of Aristotle's *Metaphysics*" (*V.Pl.* 14).
- **Stoicism**: seed-principles (*logoi spermatikoi*) and cosmic sympathy adopted; corporealism and the World-Soul-as-highest-god rejected.
- **Middle Platonism and [[Schools/Neopythagoreanism]]**: Moderatus of Gades (1st c. AD) already taught a three-hypostasis system from the *Parmenides*; Numenius of Apamea ("Plato is Moses talking Attic") supplied three divine principles and the axiom "all is in all, but in each appropriately to its nature" — though his matter-as-absolute-evil dualism was rejected. Alexander of Aphrodisias's identity of Intelligence with its object shaped Plotinus's Intelligible world.
- ***Chaldaean Oracles***: the sacred text behind [[Concepts/Theurgy (Iamblichus)]].

## Development

Plotinus bequeathed a catalogue of unresolved problems (Wallis pp. 90–93) — matter's status if divinely produced, how the One produces what it does not contain, the One's knowability, the internal structure of the Intelligible world (Being–Life–Intelligence), whether the undescended soul abolishes the Soul/Intelligence distinction — that drove all later development. [[Thinkers/Porphyry]] popularized and "telescoped" the Hypostases toward each other; [[Thinkers/Iamblichus]] counter-attacked by multiplying hypostases (an Ineffable beyond the One; Unparticipated/Participated/Participant; the Being–Life–Intelligence triad), insisting the soul fully descends (see [[Concepts/The Undescended Soul (Plotinus vs Iamblichus)]]), and grounding salvation in theurgic ritual rather than contemplation alone (see [[Contradictions/Plotinus vs Iamblichus - Contemplation vs Theurgy]]). The Athenian School systematized this into the deductive hierarchy of [[Concepts/The Henads (Proclus)]] and the universal triad of [[Concepts/Procession, Remaining, and Return (Neoplatonic Triad)]]. Iamblichus also fixed the exegetic rule that each Platonic dialogue has one *skopos* (dominant theme) to which everything in it must be related.

## Influence

Wallis: "the dominant trend of Christian theology, in both its Platonic and Aristotelian forms, has always been Neoplatonic." Channels: the Cappadocians and Pseudo-Dionysius (Athenian Neoplatonism in Christian dress — with the risk that God becomes "merely the supreme term in the metaphysical hierarchy"); [[Thinkers/Augustine]] (reading Plotinus "through Porphyrian eyes"); Boethius; Eriugena ("in creating other things God is equally creating himself"); Byzantine theology (creation as theophany; Psellus; the Hesychast controversy); Islamic falsafa via the *Theology of Aristotle* (extracts of Enneads IV–VI) and the *Liber de Causis* (from Proclus) — al-Farabi, Avicenna's Active-Intelligence prophetology, [[Thinkers/Al-Ghazali]]'s attack and [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]'s reply; Jewish thought (Ibn Gebirol, the Cabala, ultimately [[Thinkers/Spinoza]]); [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]] (who detected the *Liber de Causis*' spuriousness via Moerbeke's Proclus but inherited the imprint); the Rhineland mystics and Cusanus's learned ignorance; the Renaissance (Pletho, Ficino, Pico, Bruno); the Cambridge Platonists; German Idealism (Schelling Plotinian, Hegel Procline); Bergson. Wallis's closing valuation: the movement's enduring worth lies in its analysis of religious experience and consciousness rather than its metaphysics — "paradoxicality is inherent in the nature of conscious experience itself."
