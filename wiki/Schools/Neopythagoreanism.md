# Neopythagoreanism
> Not a continuation of the ancient school but a *reconstruction* of it — "a branch of Platonism that emphasizes the role of number in the cosmos and regards Pythagoreanism as the origin of this emphasis." The image of Pythagoras that the Middle Ages and Renaissance inherited.

## Summary
On the chapters by John Dillon (the Academic tradition) and Jaap-Jan Flinterman (Rome and Asia Minor) in [[Sources/A History of Pythagoreanism - Carl A. Huffman (ed.) (2014)]], "Neopythagoreanism" is best defined doctrinally: its principles — the **One (Monad)** and the **Indefinite Dyad** — "are, in fact, Platonic." It is the long afterlife in which Platonists projected Plato's own *unwritten doctrines* back onto Pythagoras and the "ancients." This is the institutional counterpart to the [[Concepts/Pseudo-Pythagorean Writings]] and the doctrinal background to the [[Concepts/The Three Ancient Lives of Pythagoras|three ancient Lives]].

## The Academic Tradition (Dillon)
- **Speusippus and Xenocrates are the "substantial founders of Neopythagoreanism"**, retrojecting Plato's first principles onto the Pythagoreans. Speusippus placed the **One "above Being"** (a distinction the original Pythagoreans never made) and, in *On Pythagorean Numbers*, Pythagoreanized the *Timaeus*, identifying the Decad with the Paradigm.
- **Xenocrates** made the Monad an intellect and equated **Number = Intellect = God** ("*estque numerus animus et deus*"), adopting Pythagorean *bios* traits (no oaths, vegetarianism). On Aristotle's critique of this form-number doctrine see [[Sources/Aristotle - Metaphysics]].
- **Heraclides of Pontus** shaped the Pythagoras *life-myth* (seven incarnations; the anecdote of the philosopher as the disinterested "spectator at the games") — a literary rather than philosophical interest that fed the biographical tradition.
- The Imperial revival runs through **Eudorus** (a supreme One above a secondary Monad/Dyad), **Philo**, **Plutarch** (dualist One / Indefinite Dyad), and the "tendency" (not a school) of **Moderatus of Gades**, **Nicomachus of Gerasa**, and **[[Thinkers/Numenius of Apamea]]**. Moderatus alone shows resentment, claiming the Platonists had "stolen" the best of Pythagoreanism. Numenius posits [[Concepts/The Three Gods - Father, Creator, and Creation (Numenius)|three gods]] (Father / Creator / Creation), makes [[Concepts/Matter as a Malevolent Principle (Numenius)|matter an evil Indefinite Dyad]], reads "Plato as Moses speaking Attic Greek," and rates Plato "not better than the great Pythagoras but perhaps not worse either." His polemic *On the Divergence of the Academy from Plato* attacks the skeptical Academy (Arcesilaus, Carneades) as a betrayal of the true Pythagorean Plato. See [[Thinkers/Numenius of Apamea]].

## Rome and Asia Minor (Flinterman)
The Roman evidence mostly attests *posthumous reputations*, not organized membership; "Pythagorean" often connoted a **meddler in the supernatural** and was an individual stylistic choice:
- **Nigidius Figulus** — the supposed "reviver" of Pythagoreanism rests on weak evidence (Cicero's ambiguous *qui illam renovaret*); his reputation is as *"Pythagoricus et magus."*
- **Varro** (numerological interest, "Pythagorean style" burial), **Cicero** ("Italian philosophy"), and the Augustan poets (Horace; Ovid, *Met.* 15 on metempsychosis) used Pythagorean material without doctrinal commitment. **Quintus Sextius** and **Sotion** transmitted self-examination and vegetarianism on ethical grounds.
- **Apollonius of Tyana** — known chiefly through Philostratus' fictionalizing *Life* (commissioned by Julia Domna) — and **Alexander of Abonouteichos** (Lucian's hostile target, claiming to be "like Pythagoras" with a golden thigh) exemplify the Early Imperial *travelling Pythagorean wonder-worker*. (Carcopino's reading of the Porta Maggiore basilica as evidence of an organized Roman cult is rejected.)

## Significance
Neopythagoreanism is the source of nearly everything "everyone knows" about Pythagoras — the *theios anēr*, the inventor of mathematics, the high priest of divine wisdom — the very image that [[Sources/Lore and Science in Ancient Pythagoreanism - Walter Burkert (1972)|Burkert]] dismantles. Its transmission into Boethius, the Middle Ages, and Ficino is traced in [[Concepts/Pythagoras in Medieval and Renaissance Thought]].
