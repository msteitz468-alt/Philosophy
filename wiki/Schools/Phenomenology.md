# Phenomenology
> The philosophical movement that investigates the structures of experience and consciousness as they present themselves — "to the things themselves."

## Summary

Phenomenology is the philosophical movement and method founded by Edmund Husserl in the early twentieth century, whose aim is the systematic investigation of conscious experience without presupposing the causal-scientific framework that natural science brings to its objects. Its motto is "to the things themselves" (*zu den Sachen selbst*) — direct, unprejudiced description of phenomena as they show themselves.

### Husserlian Phenomenology (Updated 2026-06-29)

[[Thinkers/Edmund Husserl]] (1859–1938) founded phenomenology as a rigorous science. Its genealogy: [[Thinkers/Franz Brentano]]'s descriptive psychology (classification of mental acts by intentional structure) → Husserl's integration of Bolzano's ideal meanings → phenomenology as the "science of the essence of consciousness" (*Ideas I*, §34).

**Phenomenology is not the whole of Husserl's system.** It stands in relations of mutual dependence with pure logic, formal and material ontology, epistemology, and ethics. The "dependence" concept (Fundierung) is the spine of the whole system.

Key features:

- **Intentionality**: Every act of consciousness is directed toward an object via an ideal noematic sense. Structure: act (noesis) — noematic sense ("object as intended") → object ) horizon. See [[Concepts/Intentionality and Noema (Husserl)]].
- **Epoché / phenomenological reduction**: Bracketing the natural attitude's assumption of a mind-independent world, to redirect attention to the structure of consciousness. The positive move: "noematic quotation" — attending to the sense ("tree as perceived") rather than the object (the tree). Not skepticism; not Berkeleyan idealism. See [[Concepts/Phenomenological Reduction and Epoché (Husserl)]].
- **Eidetic analysis**: The search for essential structures (*Wesen*) of consciousness through free variation in imagination (eidetic variation). See [[Concepts/Essences and Eidetic Variation (Husserl)]].
- **Transcendental ego**: The "pure I" from which acts radiate — not the empirical psychological self but the subject for whom the world appears. Husserl posited a constituting subject as the ultimate ground of meaning.
- **Constitution**: The structured intentional correlation through which objects acquire meaning (not existence) in consciousness. See [[Concepts/Transcendental Ego and Constitution (Husserl)]].
- **Life-world (Lebenswelt)**: The pre-scientific world of everyday experience that science presupposes but does not thematise. The "Crisis" diagnosis: modern mathematical physics has become alienated from the life-world. See [[Concepts/Intersubjectivity and the Life-World (Husserl)]].
- **Intersubjectivity**: Objects are constituted as "there for everyone" through empathy (Einfühlung) — the intuitive transfer of the sense "I" to another experienced as a fellow conscious subject.

**Husserl's anti-naturalism**: Phenomenology is explicitly anti-naturalist about the essence of consciousness — the essence of consciousness is irreducible to the essence of neural activity. See [[Contradictions/Husserl vs Naturalism — Phenomenal Consciousness and the Hard Problem]].

**The noema debate**: The major interpretive dispute in Husserl scholarship concerns the noema — whether it is a Fregean abstract sense mediating reference (West Coast: Føllesdal, Dreyfus, Smith), the object-as-encountered-within-the-epoché (East Coast: Sokolowski, Drummond), or something else entirely.

### Heidegger's Transformation

[[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]] radicalised phenomenology in several directions:

1. **From consciousness to Dasein**: Husserl starts from consciousness directed at objects; Heidegger starts from [[Concepts/Dasein (Heidegger)|Dasein]]'s practical involvement in a world. Heidegger objected that Husserl "said too little about the being of the subject" and took the directedness-at-objects model as basic when it is derivative.

2. **From epistemology to ontology**: Husserl's phenomenology remained epistemological — how do we know objects? Heidegger turned it toward ontology — what *is* the being of the entities we encounter? Phenomenology and ontology "coincide."

3. **From the transcendental ego to thrownness**: Husserl's ego is transparent to itself and constitutes its world. Heidegger's Dasein is *thrown* into a world it did not choose, absorbed in equipment and the "they," and barely aware of itself in everyday functioning.

4. **From timeless essences to historicality**: Husserl posited a "third realm" of timeless meanings (*Sinn*). Heidegger rejected all supratemporal entities and insisted that meaning is historically situated.

5. **Destruktion**: Heidegger added a destructive dimension — a critical dismantling of the tradition's inherited concepts to recover the original experiences they conceal. Derrida's "deconstruction" is a close relative.

### The Phenomenological Movement

The movement extends well beyond Husserl and Heidegger:

- **[[Thinkers/Jean-Paul Sartre]]**: Existential phenomenology — consciousness as nothingness, radical freedom, bad faith (see below).
- **Maurice Merleau-Ponty**: Phenomenology of embodied perception.
- **Emmanuel Levinas**: Ethics as first philosophy — the face of the Other.
- **Hans-Georg Gadamer**: Philosophical hermeneutics — understanding as historically-effected consciousness.
- **Jacques Derrida**: Deconstruction — the radicalisation of Heidegger's *Destruktion*.
- **Karl Jaspers**: Existential philosophy (*Existenzphilosophie*) — boundary situations, transcendence.

### Sartre's "Negative Phenomenology"

[[Thinkers/Jean-Paul Sartre]] takes phenomenology in a third direction, distinct from both Husserl and Heidegger. He keeps Husserl's **intentionality** but radicalises it: if consciousness is *purely* directedness, there is no prior ego doing the directing, so the "I" is only a transcendent *object* of reflection ("the I is a thing"). Against Husserl's *transcendental ego* and 1930 "monad" — which Sartre reads as a relapse into psychologism — and against Husserl's avowed "positivism" of original givenness, Sartre makes intentionality a **negation** (*néantisation*): consciousness *is not*, it is a *néant* through which the world's appearing is possible. Welten therefore calls it a **negative phenomenology**. From Heidegger he borrows the ontological vocabulary (*pour-soi*/*en-soi*) but inverts the starting point — beginning from the *lack* of being rather than from Being. Over his career Sartre drifts from phenomenology toward **dialectic** (via Hegel/Kojève), culminating in the *Critique of Dialectical Reason*. See [[Concepts/Consciousness as Intentionality and Néantisation (Sartre)]] and [[Concepts/Being-for-itself and Being-in-itself (Sartre)]].

### Phenomenology and Other Traditions

Phenomenology stands in productive tension with:
- **Analytic philosophy**: [[Thinkers/Ludwig Wittgenstein]] and [[Thinkers/A.J. Ayer]] represent the "other" twentieth-century tradition. Ayer dismissed Heidegger's ontological language as paradigmatic metaphysical nonsense. The continental/analytic divide, while oversimplified, reflects genuinely different conceptions of philosophy's task.
- **Pragmatism**: [[Thinkers/William James]]'s emphasis on lived experience and the primacy of practice has affinities with Heidegger's anti-Cartesianism.
- **Cognitive science**: Hubert Dreyfus applied Heidegger's ready-to-hand/present-at-hand distinction to critique AI; the embodied cognition movement draws on Merleau-Ponty.

## Key Claims / Positions

### How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Husserl**: Phenomenology as rigorous science — descriptive analysis of intentional consciousness leading to apodictic certainty.
- **[[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]]**: Phenomenology as hermeneutic ontology — "letting that which shows itself be seen from itself." The method is retained but its object shifts from consciousness to Being.
- **[[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]**: [[Concepts/Heterophenomenology (Dennett)]] is a deliberate response to phenomenology — a third-person, naturalist method that takes subjects' reports as data but does not privilege first-person access.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Whether Heidegger after the Turn can still be called a phenomenologist is contested. If Being discloses *itself* through art and language (rather than being disclosed *by* Dasein), the constituting subject that phenomenology requires seems to vanish.
- The relationship between Husserlian and Heideggerian phenomenology is itself a contested philosophical question: radicalisation, betrayal, or parallel project?
