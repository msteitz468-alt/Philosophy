# Pythagoreanism
> The religious-philosophical movement founded by [[Thinkers/Pythagoras]] at Croton (c. 530 BC) — a brotherhood bound by ritual taboo and the doctrine of the soul's transmigration, from which a distinct mathematical-cosmological strand later branched off.

## Summary
Pythagoreanism is uniquely hard to characterize because, as Walter Burkert argues in [[Sources/Lore and Science in Ancient Pythagoreanism - Walter Burkert (1972)]], the tradition grows *richer the further it stands from the events* (Zeller's law: *recentiores deteriores*). The movement was, at its origin, a **religious society** organized around (1) belief in the immortality and transmigration of the soul ([[Concepts/Metempsychosis (Transmigration of Souls)]]) and (2) a body of cryptic ritual precepts, the **acusmata** ([[Concepts/Acusmata and the Two Sects (Acusmatici and Mathematici)]]). The mathematical, astronomical, and harmonic doctrines that later defined "Pythagoreanism" in the popular and Neoplatonic imagination are, on Burkert's reading, a later development — documented first in [[Thinkers/Philolaus]] and then heavily reshaped by Plato's Academy.

## Structure and the Two Sects
The early school split into two recognized strands:
- **Acusmatici** — keepers of the *acusmata* ("things heard"): ritual sayings and taboos transmitted as revealed authority ("*autos epha*" — "the Master said it"). The acusmatici are, on the primary version of Iamblichus' source, *incontestably* Pythagoreans.
- **Mathematici** — those who pursued *mathēmata* ("learnings": number, harmonics, cosmology). The acusmatici regarded the mathematici as followers not of Pythagoras but of the innovator **Hippasus**; the mathematici claimed to be the *truer* Pythagoreans.

Burkert reads this division as the historical seed later flattened into the single image of "Pythagoras the mathematician." (Iamblichus, unable to imagine that *mathēmata* were ever the junior partner, reverses the two labels — a tell that Burkert uses to recover the original.)

## Zhmud's Reframing: a Political Hetairia, Not a Sect (Zhmud 2012)
[[Sources/Pythagoras and the Early Pythagoreans - Leonid Zhmud (2012)]] rejects much of the framing above:
- **Not a sect or cult.** Zhmud directly attacks Burkert's use of Bryan Wilson's sociology of "sects": the concept presupposes dissent from an *orthodox* religion — which polytheistic Greece (no church, dogma, scripture) did not have. The sources never call the group a *thiasos*; there was no special cult, no priests, no written *nomoi*. What existed was a **political *hetairia*** — an aristocratic sociopolitical association (*hetairoi*, *philoi*, the burned *synedria*, Milo's house as meeting-place), bound by *philia* and a shared **way of life** (*Πυθαγόρειος τρόπος τοῦ βίου*), not a religious fraternity.
- **The acusmatici/mathematici split is a late fiction.** On Zhmud's analysis the whole story traces to **Nicomachus of Gerasa** (1st–2nd c. AD); "acusmata" is **Iamblichus' coinage** (the genuine word is *symbola*); and no actual Pythagorean is ever shown observing the taboos. The split therefore cannot be the key to the school's nature. See [[Concepts/Acusmata and the Two Sects (Acusmatici and Mathematici)]].
- **No common doctrine.** There never was an "all-Pythagorean" teaching; soul-theories and *archai* differ from one Pythagorean to the next (Alcmaeon, Hippasus, Hippon, Philolaus, Ecphantus). Who counts as a Pythagorean is fixed by *recognition* by contemporaries, not by doctrine — a **"family resemblance"** (Zhmud's Wittgensteinian phrase), not a creed.
- **No secrecy machinery.** Zhmud denies communal property (an inference from the proverb *koina ta philōn*), the five-year silence (first in Seneca), and "oral-only teaching until Philolaus" (a fiction grown from the Plato-plagiarism anecdotes) — early Pythagoreans (Alcmaeon, Menestor, Hippon, Hippasus, Iccus) wrote books.

## Political Arc and Continuity (Aristoxenus' catalogue)
Zhmud builds the school's history on **Aristoxenus' catalogue** of 218 Pythagoreans (preserved in Iamblichus *VP* 267, organized by ~27 cities), the most reliable prosopographical source:
- The Crotonian *hetairia* survived the Cylonian conspiracy and dominated Croton's politics through the first half of the 5th c.; there was **no unified "Pythagorean empire,"** only individual Pythagoreans linked across cities by *philia*.
- Anti-Pythagorean revolts (~450 BC) burned the *synedria*; survivors fled to mainland Greece — **[[Thinkers/Philolaus]]** and others to **Thebes** and **Phlius**. Political activity persisted in Italy down to **Archytas** (*stratēgos* of Tarentum, 367–361).
- The "last Pythagoreans" (the circle of Xenophilus, Aristoxenus' teacher) were alive c. 350; ancient Pythagoreanism then died out, after which the comic "Pythagorists" of Middle Comedy appeared.

## Doctrinal Threads
- **Number and harmony**: "all things are number"; cosmos as a *harmonia* of limiters and unlimiteds — best attested for [[Thinkers/Philolaus]]. See [[Concepts/Number as the Essence of Reality (Pythagoras)]].
- **Cosmology**: the central fire, counter-earth, and music of the spheres — see [[Concepts/Harmony of the Spheres]].
- **Way of life**: purification (*katharsis*), dietary and ritual abstinence, secrecy (*siōpē*), communal property.

## Huffman Volume (2014): Disaggregating "Pythagoreanism"
The Cambridge [[Sources/A History of Pythagoreanism - Carl A. Huffman (ed.) (2014)]] treats "Pythagoreanism" not as one thing but as many historical layers and strands, each given its own page in this vault:
- **Community and life**: [[Concepts/Pythagorean Society and Politics]] (the Croton *hetairia*, the Sybaris war, the Cylonian fall), [[Concepts/Pythagorean Way of Life and Ethics]] (the *bios*, *acusmata* as practice, friendship as "harmonious equality"), and [[Concepts/Pythagoreanism and Orphism]] (overlapping but distinct religious circles).
- **Science**: [[Concepts/Pythagorean Mathematics]] (Netz: a "network," with [[Thinkers/Archytas]] the hinge — "there were no cards") and [[Concepts/Pythagorean Harmonics]] (Barker: the *kanonikoi* and the real, testable ratio-theory).
- **The Peripatetic sources** are our best evidence: Aristoxenus (sympathetic, an insider via Tarentum) vs Dicaearchus (hostile, painting Pythagoras as a *psychagōgos*/sorcerer); both descend from Aristotle's practice of citing "the Pythagoreans" as a group, never Pythagoras himself.

## Reception
- **Plato and the Academy** (Speusippus, Xenocrates, Heraclides Ponticus) absorbed and rewrote Pythagorean number-doctrine; Burkert argues much "ancient Pythagoreanism" is actually projected back *from* Platonism. Russell's quip that "what appears as Platonism is... in essence Pythagoreanism" is, on this view, half-true in reverse: later Pythagoreanism is in essence Platonism. The Huffman volume sharpens this: the One/Indefinite Dyad principles ascribed to the "ancients" are "in fact, Platonic" — see [[Schools/Neopythagoreanism]].
- **The forgeries**: after the school's 4th-c. demise, "Pythagoreanism" survived largely as the [[Concepts/Pseudo-Pythagorean Writings]] (Doric pseudepigrapha by "Archytas," "Timaeus," "Ocellus," etc.) — Pythagoreanizing Platonism in disguise.
- **Neo-Pythagoreanism** (1st c. BC onward, culminating in Iamblichus) made Pythagoras the high priest of divine wisdom and inventor of mathematics — the very image Burkert dismantles. Its biographical deposit is [[Concepts/The Three Ancient Lives of Pythagoras]], and its medieval/Renaissance afterlife is traced in [[Concepts/Pythagoras in Medieval and Renaissance Thought]].
