# W.V.O. Quine

> Willard Van Orman Quine (1908–2000), American philosopher, logician, and Harvard professor. Author of the 1951 essay "Two Dogmas of Empiricism," one of the most influential papers in 20th-century philosophy. The essay dismantled the two central dogmas of logical empiricism (the analytic/synthetic distinction and reductionism), replacing them with confirmation holism ("the web of belief"), a naturalistic and pragmatic conception of epistemology, and the view that ontological questions are continuous with scientific ones.

## Profile

- **Dates / life**: Born Akron, Ohio 1908; studied mathematics and philosophy at Oberlin and Harvard (PhD 1932 under Whitehead, with influence from Russell, Carnap, Tarski, Gödel); traveled in Europe (Vienna Circle, Warsaw school); taught at Harvard from 1936 until retirement (Edgar Pierce Professor); died 2000. Major works include *Word and Object* (1960), *From a Logical Point of View* (1953, containing "Two Dogmas"), *Ontological Relativity and Other Essays* (1969, containing "Epistemology Naturalized"), *The Roots of Reference* (1974), and many technical papers in logic and set theory.
- **In the vault**: Primary source [[Sources/Two Dogmas of Empiricism - W.V.O. Quine (1951)]] (the 1951 *Philosophical Review* text with 1961 revisions). The essay is the single most important primary text for the vault's critique of logical empiricism / positivism and the turn to holism and naturalism. It directly engages the Humean and Kantian background already present and supplies the conceptual tools (holism, underdetermination, posits) that frame later discussions of Popper and Kuhn.

## Core Positions (from "Two Dogmas of Empiricism")

- **The two dogmas of empiricism**:
  1. **Analytic/synthetic distinction**: There is a fundamental cleavage between statements true by virtue of meanings alone (analytic) and statements true (or false) by virtue of matters of fact (synthetic).
  2. **Reductionism**: Each meaningful statement is equivalent (or at least confirmationally tied) to a logical construct upon terms referring to immediate experience. In its strong form, every meaningful statement is translatable into sense-datum language.

- **Critique of meaning and synonymy**: Meaning cannot be identified with naming or reference (Frege's "Evening Star"/"Morning Star"; Russell's "Scott"/"the author of Waverley"; "9" vs. "the number of planets"). Co-referential singular terms can differ in meaning; astronomical fact, not reflection on meanings, establishes identity in some cases. For predicates, extension (what the term is true of) must be distinguished from intension/meaning ("creature with a heart" vs. "creature with a kidney").

- **Analyticity cannot be explained without circularity**:
  - Logical truths (first class: "No unmarried man is married") are true under all reinterpretations of non-logical vocabulary.
  - The philosophically interesting class (second class: "No bachelor is married") requires synonymy to reduce to logical truth.
  - Definition, interchangeability *salva veritate*, and Carnapian state-descriptions or semantical rules all either presuppose synonymy/analyticity or fail to capture the second class. The notion remains "a metaphysical article of faith" for empiricists.

- **Verification theory of meaning and reductionism fail**:
  - If meaning = method of empirical confirmation, then synonymy = sameness of confirmation method.
  - But radical reductionism (Locke/Hume term-by-term or Carnap's *Aufbau* statement-by-statement translation into sense data + logic) does not succeed. Even heroic constructions leave key relations ("is at") undefined and cannot actually render physical-object statements in the base language.
  - The attenuated dogma (each synthetic statement has its own unique confirming/disconfirming sensory range) is equally untenable.

- **Confirmation holism ("the web of belief")**:
  - Our knowledge or beliefs form "a man-made fabric which impinges on experience only along the edges" or "a field of force whose boundary conditions are experience."
  - A recalcitrant experience at the periphery forces readjustment of truth values across the interior because of logical interconnections. There is latitude in which statements to revise.
  - No statement is immune to revision (even logic or mathematics can be revised for pragmatic reasons, as in quantum mechanics); conversely, any statement can be held true "come what may" by suitable adjustments elsewhere.
  - The unit of empirical significance is not the individual statement but the whole of science.

- **Naturalism, posits, and pragmatism**:
  - Physical objects are "irreducible posits" — convenient intermediaries for predicting experience, not definable in experiential terms. Epistemologically they stand on the same footing as the gods of Homer or the irrational numbers (both mythical entities introduced to simplify theory).
  - Forces, classes, abstract mathematical entities are posits of the same kind.
  - Ontological questions (e.g., whether to quantify over classes) are on a par with ordinary scientific hypotheses: choosing a conceptual scheme or framework. There is no analytic/synthetic boundary that would allow Carnap-style double standards (ontology as "mere language choice," science as factual).
  - The considerations that guide theory choice and ontological commitment are pragmatic: conservatism, simplicity, explanatory power, predictive success. "Each man is given a scientific heritage plus a continuing barrage of sensory stimulation; and the considerations which guide him in warping his scientific heritage to fit his continuing sensory promptings are, where rational, pragmatic."

- **Blurred boundaries**: The supposed sharp line between speculative metaphysics and natural science dissolves. Science is continuous with the rest of our web of belief.

## Influence, Reception, and Contrasts in the Vault

- **On the Hume/Kant background**: Quine attacks the exhaustiveness and sharpness of Hume's Fork (relations of ideas vs. matters of fact) that Kant inherited and tried to overcome with synthetic a priori. The fork is the direct ancestor of the analytic/synthetic dogma Quine dismantles. See updates to [[Concepts/Hume's Fork (Relations of Ideas vs Matters of Fact)]] and [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]].
- **Vs. logical empiricism / Carnap (the explicit target)**: The two dogmas were the main instruments by which the Vienna Circle and Carnap tried to demarcate meaningful empirical science from metaphysics and to reduce the former to experience. Quine shows the instruments fail internally. (Carnap is discussed at length in the essay; a dedicated [[Thinkers/Rudolf Carnap]] page remains for future primary-source treatment.)
- **Relation to Popper**: Both are post-positivist critics of verificationism and the "given." Popper supplies a deductivist methodology of bold conjectures and attempted refutations; Quine supplies holism and underdetermination (any theory can be saved by adjustments). The tension between localized falsification and web-wide readjustment is a productive open issue (see [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)]] and the new holism concept). Quine predates (1951) the main Popper English publications but addresses the same intellectual milieu.
- **Relation to Kuhn**: Strong affinities — theory-laden observation, meaning change across frameworks (incommensurability has roots in the failure of synonymy/analyticity), science as non-cumulative across revolutions, the role of pragmatic values in theory choice. Kuhn's "different worlds" and goal-less progress echo Quine's web and posit-myth language. Differences: Kuhn is historical/sociological; Quine is more logical/pragmatist/naturalist. See [[Thinkers/Thomas Kuhn]] and [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]] (Quine adds a third pole).
- **Relation to Russell**: Quine develops and radicalizes certain Russellian themes (critique of meaning as naming; logical analysis) while rejecting others (the hope for a clean acquaintance/description or correspondence foundation that survives holism). Russell's "cosmic impiety" warning against making truth man-dependent remains relevant to Quine's pragmatism.
- **Later development**: This essay is the gateway to Quine's mature naturalism ("Epistemology Naturalized," 1969) and ontological relativity / indeterminacy of translation (*Word and Object*). Future sources will expand the picture.

A single anomalous deal, loss, or market shift does not falsify one node in isolation; it prompts distributed re-evaluation.
