# Arthur O. Lovejoy

> American philosopher and intellectual historian (1873–1962). Founder of the modern "history of ideas" as a distinct discipline. Best known for *The Great Chain of Being* (1936, William James Lectures at Harvard), the definitive study of the Chain of Being (plenitude + continuity + gradation) as the dominant, often implicit presupposition of Western thought from Plato to the late Enlightenment — and of its internal conflicts, temporalizing, and philosophical failure. Also author of *The Revolt Against Dualism* (1930) and *The Thirteen Pragmatisms* (1908). Taught at Johns Hopkins (longtime professor) and elsewhere; student/contemporary of William James at Harvard.

## Key Contributions

**History of Ideas as Method (unit-ideas)**:
- Not a history of systems, schools, or individual thinkers primarily, but of recurrent "unit-ideas" — elemental conceptual components (e.g., the three principles of plenitude, continuity, and gradation) and the specific combinations/compounds they form in different contexts, periods, and domains (philosophy, theology, science, poetry, common sense).
- "Endemic assumptions" and "dialectical motives": vague, general intellectual habits (e.g., nominalistic motive, organismic/holistic motive, craving for continuity or sufficient reason) that operate across an individual's or era's thought, often unconsciously, shaping explicit doctrines.
- Ideas migrate and mutate: the same components recombine in new compounds; implicit presuppositions become explicit only when tensions arise or consequences are drawn out.
- This book exemplifies the method applied to one of the half-dozen most potent complexes in Occidental philosophy/science/poetry.

**The Great Chain of Being (this primary source)**:
- See dedicated [[Sources/The Great Chain of Being - Arthur O. Lovejoy (1936)]] and [[Concepts/Great Chain of Being (Lovejoy)]].
- The Chain = fusion of **plenitude** (all possible kinds must exist; generative "good" overflows without envy or need), **continuity** (no gaps; nature makes no leaps; infinitesimal gradations), and **unilinear gradation** (*scala naturae*: single hierarchy from bare existence to *ens perfectissimum*, each link adding the "least possible" difference and higher powers).
- Dominant presupposition ("the most widely familiar conception of the general scheme of things") that predetermined ideas on God, the world, value, evil, science, and man's place for ~2000 years.
- Internal dualism/tension: otherworldly self-sufficient Absolute (no need of world) vs. this-worldly generative fecundity (must produce maximum diversity; "two Gods" in much of Western religion despite identification).
- 18c high point in popular form (Pope's *Essay on Man* optimism: "whatever is, is right"; plenitude solves problem of evil via "partial evil, universal good").
- Breakdown: temporalizing (18c biology, geology, pre-Darwin) converts static Chain into progressive Becoming; Romanticism reinterprets plenitude as infinite striving/process (Schelling: God "coming to be" through history; "the more perfect has risen from its own less perfect condition").
- Philosophical moral (Ch. XI): the long experiment presupposing complete rational intelligibility of the world (sufficient reason for every detail and the world's exact fullness, no arbitrariness) is "the history of a failure." A temporal, changing world cannot be deduced from eternal necessary truths; plenitude + continuity generate unmanageable infinities and contradictions (qualitative continuum is incoherent); complete rationality excluding arbitrariness "becomes itself a kind of irrationality." World is contingent; "Will is prior to Intellect." Yet the idea had "curiously happy consequences" (stimulated the classificatory and biological sciences that helped dissolve its static version).

**Other Work**:
- *The Thirteen Pragmatisms* (1908): discriminated the many shades in William James's unsystematic thought; distinguished what is "rationally valid" from what is "believed on faith."
- *The Revolt Against Dualism* (1930): critique of the bifurcation of nature (Descartes onward) into monism of spirit (Romantic idealism) or monism of matter (physical sciences); defense of "natural dualism" grounded in common sense, experience, memory, and retrospection (affinity with James and, per Stanlis, Robert Frost).
- Strong influence on and from William James (teacher at Harvard); contemporary with Robert Frost (who read *Chain* and *Revolt* with deep engagement; both shared skepticism of pure Platonic monism/idealism and of mechanistic behaviorism; preference for dualism over Jamesian "pluralism").

## Life and Reception

Born 1873 (nearly exact contemporary of Frost 1874); died 1962. European mother, American father; early independence from institutional religion (Episcopal) while retaining piety. Harvard graduate work (philosophy, "golden age" with James, Royce, Santayana, etc.); studied comparative religion in Paris. Taught at Stanford (early), Washington University, Missouri, and especially Johns Hopkins (long career). Venerated "great scholarship" but was himself a broad, cross-domain historian rather than narrow specialist; resisted the "Ph.D. Octopus" (per James critique) in favor of genuine intellectual interests. The *Chain* (1936) remains the standard reference; method influenced intellectual history broadly (e.g., "unit-ideas" as tool for tracing presuppositions).
