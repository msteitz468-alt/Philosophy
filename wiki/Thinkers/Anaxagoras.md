# Anaxagoras

> Greek philosopher (c. 500 – 428 BCE) from Clazomenae in Asia Minor. Renowned for bringing philosophy/science to Athens, where he was a close friend and advisor of Pericles and teacher of Euripides.

## Key Contributions

**Nous (Cosmic Mind)**:
- Postulated **Nous** (Mind) as the active, ordering cause of the cosmos.
- Described Nous as completely separate from all other things, pure, unmixed, all-knowing, and self-ruling.
- Nous is not a creator-god, but a physical/metaphysical agent that initiated a cosmic vortex (*perichoresis*) that separated the mixed primordial matter into distinct elements and worlds. See [[Concepts/Nous as Cosmic Ordering Force (Anaxagoras)]].

**Infinite Divisibility and Portions**:
- Declared that matter is infinitely divisible: there is no smallest particle of matter, and no vacuum/void.
- Formulated the principle that **"everything is in everything"** (portions of all qualities exist in every piece of matter). A piece of bread contains portions of hair, bone, flesh, and gold; when we digest bread, the flesh and bone in the bread separate out to nourish our bodies.
- A physical object is named after whatever portion is most dominant in it (e.g., gold appears as gold because it contains more portions of "gold-ness" than of anything else).

**Astronomy and Scientific Naturalism**:
- Offered a purely naturalistic explanation of celestial bodies: declared that the sun is a red-hot stone larger than the Peloponnese, and that the moon has valleys and shines by reflected light.
- Prosecuted for impiety in Athens (c. 450 BCE) for these views, Pericles helped him escape to Lampsacus, where he died.

## Relevance to Vault

- **The Introduction of Nous**: Established Mind (*Nous*) as a primary cosmological concept. This was a massive step toward teleology, though Socrates and Plato later complained that Anaxagoras only used Nous to start the universe and then fell back on purely mechanical, material explanations for everything else.
- **The Athens Transition**: Marked the shift of philosophical activity from the colonies (Ionia, southern Italy) to Athens, which became the center of classical Greek philosophy.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] Mechanistic vs. Teleological order: Socrates in the *Phaedo* recounts his disappointment upon reading Anaxagoras: he expected Nous to arrange things for "the best" (teleology), but found Anaxagoras using air, ether, water, and other mechanical causes instead.
