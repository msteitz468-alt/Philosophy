# Thomas Hobbes

> English philosopher (1588–1679) widely regarded as one of the founders of modern political philosophy. Developed a thoroughly materialist metaphysics and an influential theory of the social contract, arguing that peace and social order can only be maintained through the establishment of an absolute sovereign power.

## Key Contributions

**Materialist Human Nature**:
- Rejected substance dualism in favor of a mechanistic, materialist view of the world. Argued that humans are completely physical systems governed by motion, appetite, and aversion.
- Described human life in its natural state—the **State of Nature**—as a state of perpetual war of all against all, where life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." See [[Concepts/State of Nature (Hobbes)]].

**The Social Contract and Sovereign Power**:
- Proposed that to escape the state of nature, individuals rationally choose to enter into a **Social Contract**, collectively transferring their natural right to self-preservation to a centralized political authority (the **Leviathan**).
- Held that the sovereign's power must be absolute, indivisible, and unlimited to prevent the collapse of society back into civil war. See [[Concepts/Social Contract (Hobbes)]].

## Relevance to Vault

- **The Realist political axis**: Hobbes represents the foundational realist view of political power, contrasting sharply with the constitutionalism of John Locke and the checks-and-balances design of [Publius](file:///home/mark/Documents/Philosophy/wiki/Thinkers/Publius%20(Hamilton,%20Madison,%20and%20Jay).md). See [[Contradictions/Hobbesian Absolutism vs. Lockean Constitutionalism]].
- **Materialism vs. Teleology**: Hobbes' mechanical materialism aligns with Democritus and Lucretius, rejecting Aristotelian final causes and Hegelian spiritual teleology.
- **Necessity and Freedom**: Hobbes' view of choice as caused by physical appetites parallels Spinoza's necessitarianism but contrasts with Kant's transcendental freedom.
