# Martin Heidegger
> "He was (with the possible exception of Wittgenstein) the greatest philosopher of the twentieth century. He was (with the possible exception of Hegel) the greatest charlatan ever to claim the title of 'philosopher'."

## Summary

Martin Heidegger (1889–1976) was a German philosopher whose single question — *What is Being?* — restructured continental philosophy in the twentieth century. His masterwork *Being and Time* (*Sein und Zeit*, 1927) argued that the Western philosophical tradition had forgotten the question of Being by burying it under epistemology, subjectivism, and science. To recover it, Heidegger analysed the entity that asks the question — *Dasein* (human existence understood as the being whose own being is an issue for it). He showed that Dasein is essentially *being-in-the-world*, not a Cartesian subject confronting objects; that its being is *care* (*Sorge*); and that care is grounded in *temporality*. The later Heidegger shifted from Dasein-centred analysis to a receptive openness in which Being itself discloses through art, language, and thinking — the "Turn" (*Kehre*).

**Primary-text note (2026-07-01)**: The vault now holds *Being and Time* itself ([[Sources/Being and Time - Martin Heidegger (Stambaugh rev. Schmidt)]], full ingest, §§1–83 plus the later marginalia from Heidegger's own copy). The positions below are therefore grounded in the primary text as well as Inwood's survey. The primary text added dedicated pages: [[Concepts/Phenomenological Method and Destruktion (Heidegger)]], [[Concepts/The They (das Man) and Falling Prey (Heidegger)]], [[Concepts/Disclosedness — Attunement, Understanding, Discourse (Heidegger)]], [[Concepts/Anxiety (Angst) (Heidegger)]], [[Concepts/Reality and the External World (Heidegger)]], [[Concepts/Conscience, Guilt, and Resoluteness (Heidegger)]], [[Concepts/World-Time and the Vulgar Concept of Time (Heidegger)]]. Notable primary-text precisions: the book's stated aim is "the interpretation of *time* as the possible horizon for any understanding whatsoever of being" — never delivered, ending at §83 in open questions; existential guilt ("being the (null) ground of a nullity") precedes and enables morality; and the marginalia reread the whole apparatus in the later vocabulary of *Seyn* and *Ereignis*.

## Life

Heidegger was born on 26 September 1889 to a poor Catholic family in Messkirch, Baden. His philosophical interest was first aroused by Franz Brentano's *On the Various Meanings of Being according to Aristotle* (1862) and Carl Braig's *On Being: An Outline of Ontology* (1896). Trained in Catholic-scholastic philosophy ([[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]], Duns Scotus), he studied at Freiburg under Edmund Husserl, graduating in 1913 with an anti-psychologistic dissertation. His 1915 habilitation on Duns Scotus's theory of categories earned him the right to lecture.

After service in the First World War (postal and meteorological duties), Heidegger broke with "the system of Catholicism" in 1919 and turned fully toward Husserl's phenomenology. He became Husserl's assistant, then moved to Marburg (1923–28) where he lectured on [[Thinkers/Plato]]'s *Sophist*, [[Thinkers/Aristotle]], and the Pre-Socratics, and befriended the theologian Rudolf Bultmann. Hannah Arendt, his student and lover, called him the "hidden king" of philosophy.

*Being and Time* was published in 1927. Heidegger returned to Freiburg in 1928 as Husserl's successor. The 1929 debate with Ernst Cassirer on [[Thinkers/Kant]] and his *Kant and the Problem of Metaphysics* established his international reputation.

### Nazism

On 1 May 1933 Heidegger joined the NSDAP and became rector of Freiburg University. His rectoral address put labour, military service, and knowledge on equal footing as student duties; it was "noticeably free of anti-Semitism" but embraced the "self-assertion of the German university" in National Socialist terms. He resigned the rectorate in April 1934. His Nietzsche lectures (1936–early 1940s) are claimed to contain "covert criticism of Nazism," diagnosing empire-building as stemming "not primarily from 'dictators' and 'authoritarian states', but from the 'metaphysical essence of modernity', the will to mastery over nature." After 1945 he was banned from teaching until 1951. He never publicly repudiated his engagement. Inwood concludes that *Being and Time* "neither commits Heidegger to Nazism nor immunizes him against it"; Heidegger saw Nazism "in terms of its possibilities, not its sheer actuality," and no philosophy provides "an unfailing amulet" against political manipulation.

### Later Years

After the war Heidegger distanced himself from French existentialism in *On Humanism* (1947), addressing Jean Beaufret. He lectured on the Pre-Socratics (Anaximander, [[Thinkers/Parmenides]]), Hölderlin, and technology. He died on 26 May 1976 and was buried in Messkirch.

## Key Philosophical Positions

### The Question of Being

The central question of Heidegger's entire philosophy: *What is Being?* The Western tradition once asked this question ([[Thinkers/Aristotle]]'s *Metaphysics*: "Being is said in many ways") but subsequently forgot it, substituting epistemology ([[Thinkers/Kant]]), subjectivism ([[Thinkers/Descartes]]), and the sciences. Heidegger's project is to recover and radicalise this question. See [[Concepts/Being and the Ontological Difference (Heidegger)]].

### Dasein

The human being is not a "rational animal" or a "thinking thing" but *Dasein* — the entity whose own being is an issue for it. Dasein has no fixed essence; "the essence of Dasein lies in its existence." Its characteristics are not properties but "possible ways for it to be." See [[Concepts/Dasein (Heidegger)]].

### Being-in-the-World

Dasein is always already practically engaged with equipment, others, and meaning — not a subject confronting objects. The world is a web of significance (*Bedeutsamkeit*), not Cartesian extended matter. See [[Concepts/Being-in-the-World (Heidegger)]] and [[Concepts/Readiness-to-Hand and Presence-at-Hand (Heidegger)]].

### Truth as Unconcealment

Truth is not correspondence of assertions to facts but *aletheia* — unconcealment, the process by which beings show themselves. "There is truth only in so far as Dasein is and as long as Dasein is." See [[Concepts/Truth as Unconcealment (Heidegger)]].

### Care and Temporality

Dasein's being is *Sorge* (care): "ahead-of-itself-Being-already-in-(the-world) as Being-alongside (entities encountered within-the-world)." Care is grounded in ecstatic temporality — the three ecstases of future, having-been-ness, and present. See [[Concepts/Care (Sorge) (Heidegger)]] and [[Concepts/Temporality (Heidegger)]].

### Being-toward-Death and Authenticity

Death individualises Dasein and snatches it from the "endless multiplicity of possibilities" into "the simplicity of its fate." Conscience calls Dasein to choose to choose; resoluteness is the authentic mode of disclosedness. See [[Concepts/Being-toward-Death and Authenticity (Heidegger)]].

### The Turn and Art

The later Heidegger shifts from Dasein-centred questioning to receptive listening: Being discloses itself through art, language, and thinking. In *The Origin of the Work of Art*, art is "the truth of beings setting itself to work." Poetry (*Dichtung*) is prior to all other arts as the projective saying that names things for the first time. See [[Concepts/Historicality and the Turn (Heidegger)]] and [[Concepts/Art and Truth (Heidegger)]].

## Key Works

- *Being and Time* (*Sein und Zeit*, 1927) — the one indisputably great work
- *Kant and the Problem of Metaphysics* (1929)
- *Introduction to Metaphysics* (1935/1953)
- "The Origin of the Work of Art" (1935/1950)
- *Nietzsche* lectures (1936–1944, pub. 1961)

- *On Humanism* (1947)

## Influence

Heidegger's influence spans theology (Bultmann, Karl Rahner, Paul Tillich), psychoanalysis (Ludwig Binswanger), literary criticism (Emil Staiger), and philosophy. [[Thinkers/Nietzsche]] was the subject of his most sustained historical engagement. In France, Sartre, Merleau-Ponty, Emmanuel Levinas, and Jacques Derrida (whose "deconstruction" descends from Heidegger's *Destruktion*) are all deeply indebted. Hans-Georg Gadamer's hermeneutics, José Ortega y Gasset's philosophy of life, and Karl Jaspers's existentialism all bear Heidegger's mark.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Heidegger's resoluteness provides no objective moral criteria: "Hitler was no less resolute than Christ or Socrates." The relationship between authenticity and ethics remains deeply contested.
- > [!warning] *Being and Time* was left incomplete: the projected third section (Being as such and its relation to time) never appeared. Whether the later philosophy constitutes its fulfilment or its abandonment is unresolved.
- The relationship between Heidegger's philosophy and his Nazism remains the most discussed question in Heidegger scholarship.

## Heidegger and Husserl (2026-06-29 Ingest)

Based on the Smith *Husserl* ingest, the intellectual and biographical relationship is as follows:

- **Intellectual debt**: Heidegger's entire phenomenological method derives from Husserl; he also drew extensively on the unpublished *Ideas II* (embodiment, the life-world, intersubjectivity) — material that was "in the air" at Freiburg and fed *Being and Time* directly.
- **Transformation**: Heidegger replaces Husserl's Cartesian-inflected transcendental phenomenology (consciousness bracketed from the world) with "fundamental ontology" centred on Dasein's being-in-the-world. He objects that Husserl's focus on consciousness-directed-at-objects is epistemological and derivative; Dasein is primordially absorbed in practical engagement, not a spectating consciousness.
- **Continuation vs. rupture**: Interpreters disagree. Dreyfus emphasises discontinuity; Crowell emphasises continuity. The dispute mirrors the question of whether Husserl's transcendental turn is compatible with realism.
- **Biography**: *Being and Time* (1927) was dedicated to Husserl "in admiration and friendship"; Heidegger as Rektor (1933) enforced the Nazi ban excluding Husserl from the university; subsequent editions removed the dedication. The relationship ended in estrangement.
- **The Oedipal dynamic**: Smith: "In the continental tradition, the great thinker needs to 'kill off the father' in order to proceed with his own creativity."
