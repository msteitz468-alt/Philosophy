# Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860)

> German philosopher. Best known for his metaphysical voluntarism and his profound **metaphysical pessimism**, characterizing the ultimate reality of the universe as a blind, striving, non-rational force called "Will."

## Life and Context

Born in Danzig (Gdańsk), Poland/Prussia, to a wealthy merchant family. Schopenhauer studied medicine and philosophy in Göttingen and Berlin. He completed his doctoral dissertation, *On the Fourfold Root of the Principle of Sufficient Reason*, in 1813, which laid the epistemological groundwork for his philosophy.

His masterwork, *The World as Will and Representation* (often translated as *The World as Will and Idea*), was published in late 1818 (dated 1819). He attempted to teach at the University of Berlin, scheduling his lectures at the same time as the famous G.W.F. Hegel, whom he detested. Hegelianism dominated, and Schopenhauer's lectures were poorly attended, prompting him to retire from academia and live as a reclusive scholar in Frankfurt.

His work remained largely ignored for decades until the publication of his essays *Parerga and Paralipomena* in 1851, which finally brought him widespread international fame in his final decade.

---

## Major Works

- *The World as Will and Representation* (1819): Schopenhauer's comprehensive philosophical system, dividing reality into the world as representation (phenomena structured by space, time, and causality) and the world as Will (the noumenal thing-in-itself).
- *Parerga and Paralipomena* (1851): A collection of philosophical essays, aphorisms, and reflections that popularized his pessimistic worldview.

---

## Key Philosophical Positions

* **The World as Representation**: Downstream of Immanuel Kant's epistemology, Schopenhauer holds that the world we perceive (phenomena) is structured entirely by our cognitive faculties (space, time, and causality), which he calls the "Principle of Sufficient Reason." We can only know things as they appear to us, not as they are in themselves.
* **The World as Will**: Schopenhauer identifies the Kantian *thing-in-itself* (noumenon) as **Will** (*Wille*). Unlike human willpower, the metaphysical Will is a single, blind, insatiable, non-rational, force that underlies all matter, life, and physical forces. 
* **Metaphysical Pessimism**: Because the Will is infinite and has no ultimate goal, it is in a state of eternal, unsatisfied striving. In living things, this manifests as desire, which leads to inevitable suffering. Once a desire is satisfied, boredom sets in, followed by new desires. Thus, life is an endless oscillation between pain and boredom.
* **Denial of the Will**: The only escapes from the suffering of the Will are:
  1. **Aesthetic Contemplation**: Temporary release through art (especially music), where we become "disinterested" observers free from the demands of desire.
  2. **Asceticism and Compassion**: Permanent quietening of the Will through recognizing that all individuals are expressions of the same unified Will, leading to universal compassion (*Mitleid*) and the systematic denial of personal desires.

## Influence and Legacy

Schopenhauer's voluntaristic metaphysics and emphasis on the non-rational had a profound influence on late 19th- and early 20th-century thought. He was the primary philosophical catalyst for **Friedrich Nietzsche** (who later rejected his pessimism but retained his voluntarism as the "Will to Power") and **Richard Wagner**. 

His concept of the blind Will driving human behavior anticipated **Sigmund Freud**'s theory of the unconscious and the id. In literature and art, his pessimism influenced writers like Leo Tolstoy, Thomas Mann, and Marcel Proust, and his philosophy of science influenced Ludwig Boltzmann and Albert Einstein.
