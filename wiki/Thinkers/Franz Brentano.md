# Franz Brentano
> Austrian philosopher and psychologist who introduced "intentionality" as the mark of the mental and whose "descriptive psychology" became the direct precursor of Husserl's phenomenology.

## Summary

Franz Brentano (1838–1917) was an Austrian philosopher, psychologist, and Catholic priest whose work bridges scholastic philosophy and modern analytic/phenomenological thought. His most influential contribution is the revival of the medieval concept of **intentional inexistence** as the defining characteristic of mental states — now known simply as **intentionality**.

Brentano's *Psychology from an Empirical Standpoint* (1874) distinguished **descriptive psychology** (the classification and analysis of mental acts in terms of their intrinsic structure) from **genetic psychology** (the causal explanation of mental states). Phenomenology is the heir of descriptive psychology.

## Intentional Inexistence

Brentano's key thesis: every mental act contains its object "intentionally within itself" (*intentionale Inexistenz*). In every mental phenomenon, consciousness is directed toward something:
- In perceiving, we perceive *something*.
- In judging, we judge *that something is so*.
- In loving/hating, we love or hate *something*.

This directedness — intentionality — is what distinguishes mental states from physical phenomena. Physical phenomena (colours, shapes, sounds as experienced) do not themselves "contain" objects in this way; they are the *objects* of mental acts.

Brentano classified mental phenomena into three fundamental types:
1. **Presentations (Vorstellungen)**: Simple directedness without judgment or feeling.
2. **Judgments**: Acceptance or rejection of what is presented as existent/non-existent, true/false.
3. **Emotions (love/hate)**: Positive or negative attitudes toward what is presented.

## Relation to Husserl

[[Thinkers/Edmund Husserl]] studied with Brentano in Vienna (1884–86). Brentano gave him:
- The concept of intentionality as the structural core of mental life.
- An admiration for Hume (rigorous empiricism applied to mental phenomena).
- Acquaintance with Bolzano's logic (propositions-in-themselves as ideal objects).
- The methodological commitment to descriptive (not causal) analysis of consciousness.

Husserl dedicated his first book (*Philosophy of Arithmetic*, 1891) to Brentano. But Husserl revised Brentano's account substantially:
- Where Brentano said the object is "immanent" in consciousness, Husserl argues the object need *not* be immanent — the act is directed toward a possibly external, possibly non-existent object *via* an ideal noematic sense.
- Husserl replaced Brentano's psychological taxonomy with a transcendental phenomenology grounded in ideal meanings and formal ontology.

Also influenced by Brentano: [[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]] (via Brentano's *On the Manifold Meaning of Being according to Aristotle*, which first aroused Heidegger's interest in the question of Being), and Alexius Meinong (theory of non-existent objects).

## Ethics

Brentano developed an objective ethics grounded in the analogy between truth and value: just as correct judgments track truth, correct emotional attitudes track objective value. Husserl's ground norm ("Do the best that is attainable") is directly adapted from Brentano's ethical principles. See [[Concepts/Formal and Material Ethics (Husserl)]].
