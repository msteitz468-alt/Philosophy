# Bertrand Russell

> British philosopher, logician, mathematician, essayist, and social/anti-war activist (1872–1970); co-founder (with Frege and Moore) of analytic philosophy and a principal architect of modern mathematical logic. Author (with A. N. Whitehead) of *Principia Mathematica* (1910–13) and of the encyclopedic, opinionated *A History of Western Philosophy* (1945), the book that won him broad fame and contributed to his 1950 Nobel Prize in Literature.

## Profile

- **Dates/place**: Born in Wales, 1872; educated at Trinity College, Cambridge; died of influenza in 1970, aged 97.
- **Career/life** (from the book's bio): *Principia Mathematica* with Whitehead (first vol. 1910); jailed four months as a pacifist in WWI (wrote *Introduction to Mathematical Philosophy* there); ran a progressive school with his wife in Sussex (1927–32); taught at Chicago and UCLA before WWII; lifelong antiwar activist; **Nobel Prize in Literature, 1950**; also wrote *ABC of Relativity* and *Why I Am Not a Christian*; three-volume autobiography (1967–69).
- **In the vault**: author of **two** ingested works — the survey *History of Western Philosophy* (1945) and the primary epistemology *The Problems of Philosophy* (1912). The latter gives the vault Russell's *constructive* theory of knowledge: [[Concepts/Sense-Data vs. Physical Objects (Russell)|sense-data vs. physical objects]], [[Concepts/Knowledge by Acquaintance and Knowledge by Description (Russell)|acquaintance vs. description]], the restatement of [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)|Hume's induction problem]] (the chicken), a qualified realism about universals, and the [[Concepts/Correspondence Theory of Truth (Russell)|correspondence theory of truth]] + [[Concepts/The Value of Philosophy (Russell)|value of philosophy]]. Also the "Russell" who attacked the pragmatic theory of truth, referenced on [[Sources/Pragmatism - William James (1907, Standard Ebooks)]] — see "cosmic impiety" below. Note: *Problems* (1912) already rejects "truth is man-made," so his anti-pragmatist realism was **lifelong**, not a late development.

## Core Positions (from *A History of Western Philosophy*, 1945)

- **Philosophy as the No Man's Land between theology and science** — "speculations on matters as to which definite knowledge has, so far, been unascertainable," but appealing "to reason rather than to authority." Its value: "to teach how to live without certainty, and yet without being paralyzed by hesitation." See [[Concepts/Philosophy as the No Man's Land between Theology and Science (Russell)]].
- **The social-circumstances method** — philosophy is "both an effect and a cause of the character of the various communities in which different systems flourished"; the historian of ideas must give "the times that formed them and the times that they helped to form."
- **Social cohesion vs. individual liberty** — the master dialectic of the whole history (disciplinarians vs. libertarians), with **liberalism** as "an attempt to escape from this endless oscillation." See [[Concepts/Social Cohesion vs. Individual Liberty (Russell)]].
- **The philosophy of logical analysis** (his own school) — piecemeal, inductive, scientific: "successive approximations to the truth, in which each new stage results from an improvement, not a rejection, of what has gone before"; its chief merit "scientific truthfulness… the habit of basing our beliefs upon observations and inferences as impersonal… as possible." "In abandoning a part of its dogmatic pretensions, philosophy does not cease to suggest and inspire a way of life." See [[Concepts/Philosophy of Logical Analysis (Russell)]].
- **"Cosmic impiety"** — his warning that making truth depend on human satisfaction (pragmatism, idealism from Fichte on) removes "the necessary element of humility" and feeds "the intoxication of power… the greatest danger of our time." The realist counter to [[Concepts/Pragmatic Theory of Truth (James)]] / [[Concepts/Humanism - Reality as Malleable (Schiller and James)]].
- **Empiricism + logic**: heir of Locke/Hume's "piecemeal" British tradition, but seeking "something less sceptical than Hume's system." Occam's razor "a most fruitful principle in logical analysis."

## Characteristic verdicts (his, opinionated)

- **[[Thinkers/Spinoza]]**: "the noblest and most lovable of the great philosophers… ethically he is supreme." (His single warmest judgment.)
- **[[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]**: "little of the true philosophic spirit… The finding of arguments for a conclusion given in advance is not philosophy, but special pleading."
- **[[Thinkers/Plato]]**: "hardly ever intellectually honest, because he allows himself to judge doctrines by their social consequences… He introduced this vice into philosophy."
- **[[Thinkers/Aristotle]]**: merits "enormous" before his predecessors, demerits "equally enormous" through his successors' worship — his authority "a serious obstacle to progress."
- **[[Thinkers/Hume]]**: "developed to its logical conclusion the empirical philosophy of Locke and Berkeley, and by making it self-consistent made it incredible… a dead end"; "the bankruptcy of eighteenth-century reasonableness." **Karl Popper** later accepted Hume's negative result on induction but offered a deductivist critical-rationalist bypass (falsifiability + corroboration) rather than Russell's a priori inductive principle in *Problems of Philosophy*. See [[Thinkers/Karl Popper]] and [[Sources/The Logic of Scientific Discovery - Karl Popper (Routledge Classics)]].
- **[[Thinkers/Kant]]**: "generally considered the greatest of modern philosophers," but "a pre-Humian type of rationalism" who never really answered Hume.
- **[[Thinkers/Nietzsche]]**: "I dislike Nietzsche because he likes the contemplation of pain, because he erects conceit into a duty… Nietzsche despises universal love; I feel it the motive power to all that I desire."
- **[[Thinkers/William James]]**: right about radical empiricism ("would, on this ground alone, deserve a high place"), wrong about truth (the "cosmic impiety" charge).

## Relations to other vault thinkers

- **Empiricist lineage** ([[Thinkers/Locke]] → Berkeley → [[Thinkers/Hume]]): Russell is the 20th-century analytic heir — "piecemeal," inductive, anti-system — but tries to rebuild on logic what Hume's scepticism dissolved.
- **Vs. [[Thinkers/William James]] / pragmatism**: the canonical realist critic of the pragmatic theory of truth; both the doctrine and Russell's "cosmic impiety" rebuttal now live in the vault.
- **Vs. the system-builders** ([[Thinkers/Spinoza]], Leibniz, [[Thinkers/Kant]], Hegel): admires Spinoza ethically, but rejects "statuesque" Continental systems ("a table is really a colony of souls") in favor of detail and analysis.
- **Vs. the irrationalists** ([[Thinkers/Nietzsche]], Schopenhauer, Rousseau): the explicit antagonists of his liberal, rationalist, universal-sympathy ethic.
- **Successor-critic of [[Thinkers/Aristotle]]'s logic**: "in logic, this is still true at the present day" — modern logic (Russell's own) as the overdue replacement of the Aristotelian syllogistic ([[Concepts/Modal Copula (Aristotelian Modality)]]).

- **Quine as critic and developer**: Quine (primary-sourced via "Two Dogmas") was deeply influenced by Russell's logic and analysis but radicalized the critique of "meaning" as an obscure intermediary and rejected the hope for clean foundations (acquaintance/description, correspondence as more than a regulative ideal). Quine's holism and naturalism take the "piecemeal" spirit further while undercutting the atomism sometimes associated with early analytic philosophy. See [[Sources/Two Dogmas of Empiricism - W.V.O. Quine (1951)]] and [[Thinkers/W.V.O. Quine]]. Quine also inherits and transforms Russell's anti-pragmatist realism in a more thoroughgoing naturalist direction.
