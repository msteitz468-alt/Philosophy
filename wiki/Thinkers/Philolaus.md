# Philolaus of Croton
> The earliest Pythagorean whose written doctrine survives in (partly) genuine fragments — and, on Walter Burkert's reconstruction, the true historical source of the "number cosmology" usually credited to Pythagoras himself.

## Summary
Philolaus (c. 470–385 BC) was a Pythagorean of the generation after Pythagoras who, around the late fifth century, recast Pythagorean lore in the conceptual idiom of Ionian *physiologia* (natural inquiry). In [[Sources/Lore and Science in Ancient Pythagoreanism - Walter Burkert (1972)]], Burkert makes Philolaus the pivotal figure of the whole "Pythagorean question": because the genuine doctrine of *Pythagoras* cannot be recovered behind the religious legend, the earliest *philosophical* Pythagoreanism we can actually document is that of Philolaus' book. The doxographic formula "down to Philolaus it was not possible to learn any Pythagorean doctrine" (D.L. 8.15) registers exactly this — he is the first to put anything in writing.

## Key Positions

**Limiters and Unlimiteds (πέραντα / ἄπειρα)**:
- The cosmos is built from two classes of things — *limiters* and *unlimiteds* — bound together by **harmony** (ἁρμονία). "Things that are neither alike nor of the same kind have necessarily been locked together by harmony, if they are to be held together in a cosmos." Harmony is the fitting-together of opposites (an idea Philolaus shares with [[Thinkers/Heraclitus|Heraclitus]]).
- Knowledge is possible because the world is a *harmonia* of opposites that can be grasped through number; "all things that are known have number."

**Epistemic modesty, not a closed system**:
- Burkert defends the authenticity of key fragments (esp. fr. 6) precisely because Philolaus *leaves gaps* — he declines to say how harmony "came to be," entrusting the question of Being to "a divine intelligence." A forger copying Aristotle would have filled the gap; a real thinker leaves the awkward remainder. This anti-systematic texture is Burkert's internal criterion of genuineness.

**The cosmos of the central fire** (see [[Concepts/Harmony of the Spheres]]):
- Philolaus' is the famous non-geocentric system: at the centre is not the Earth but a **central fire** ("the hearth of the universe"), around which the Earth, a **counter-earth** (ἀντίχθων), the Moon, Sun, five planets, and the sphere of fixed stars all revolve — ten moving bodies, the perfect Pythagorean number.
- This makes Philolaus, not Pythagoras, the author of the cosmology that early-modern readers (Copernicus among them) hailed as anticipating heliocentrism.

## Zhmud's Qualifications (Zhmud 2012)
[[Sources/Pythagoras and the Early Pythagoreans - Leonid Zhmud (2012)]] agrees Philolaus' fragments are largely genuine but corrects several points of the Burkert-based picture:
- **"First to publish a book" is a fiction.** The famous formula that no Pythagorean doctrine could be learned "down to Philolaus" grew out of the **Plato-plagiarism anecdotes**, not history — earlier Pythagoreans (Alcmaeon, Menestor, Hippon, Hippasus, Iccus) already wrote books. Philolaus' book contained *his own* theories, not a secret "all-Pythagorean" teaching (there never was one).
- **Not the source of "all is number."** Philolaus' genuine principles are *limiters* and *unlimiteds* joined by *harmonia*; things are **not made of numbers**. "To have number" (B4) means to be *knowable/structured* (epistemology), and the One/Hestia is "the first thing fitted together," not a corporeal unit. Aristotle "confuses the cosmogony with the number theory" (Cherniss, Huffman). See [[Concepts/Number as the Essence of Reality (Pythagoras)]].
- **Detach the harmony of the spheres from Philolaus.** Zhmud argues (against Burkert *and* Huffman) that the audible cosmic-music doctrine is *older* than Philolaus and presupposes a geocentric model — so it does **not** belong to his central-fire system, which a moving Earth would silence. See [[Concepts/Harmony of the Spheres]].
- **The decad is not his organizing motive.** The "perfect number ten" / counter-earth is driven by *astronomy* (explaining lunar-eclipse frequency), not arithmology; the decad-as-perfect-number is an **Academic** idea (Plato, Speusippus). Zhmud notes *three*, not ten, was the older Pythagorean "complete" number.
- **The soul.** Philolaus put reason in the brain and life/sensation in the heart; there is no evidence he held the soul immortal. The "soul as attunement/harmony" voiced by his pupils Simmias and Echecrates in the *Phaedo* derives from **medicine** (cf. [[Thinkers/Alcmaeon of Croton]]'s *isonomia*), not from his cosmic *harmonia*.
- **Thebes circle.** After the ~450 BC revolts Philolaus taught at **Thebes** (his pupils Simmias and Cebes appear in the *Phaedo*); his group is better called a philosophical school than a political [[Schools/Pythagoreanism|hetairia]].

## Graham's Reading (Huffman volume, 2014)
Daniel Graham's chapter in [[Sources/A History of Pythagoreanism - Carl A. Huffman (ed.) (2014)]] makes Philolaus "the starting-point of Pythagorean philosophy, not Pythagoras," and reinforces the corrective on the number-doctrine:
- His core is **limiters and unlimiteds fitted together by *harmonia*** (fr. 1: "Nature in the world-order was fitted together both out of things which are unlimited and out of things which are limiting"), *not* "all is number." "Philolaus never makes this strong identity claim, and Aristotle's statements to this effect seem to arise from a misreading of the weaker claim" — things "*have* number" by being ordered, knowable pluralities (the octave; fr. 4: "all things that are known have number").
- His **epistemology is quasi-Kantian**: "There will not be anything that is going to know at all, if everything is unlimited" (fr. 3) — limit is the condition of knowability.
- His cosmology is **"hestiocentric"**: earth is a *planet* revolving (with the counter-earth) around the central fire/hearth (fr. 7: "the first thing fitted together, the one in the center of the sphere, is called the hearth"). Graham defends the **counter-earth** as having a genuine *empirical* (eclipse-explaining) function, not mere numerology — converging with Zhmud against Burkert's arithmological reading.
- A four-fold **psychology/physiology** (brain = intellect, heart = life/sensation, navel = growth, genitals = generation; fr. 13) anticipates Aristotle's levels of soul and sides with [[Thinkers/Alcmaeon of Croton]] (brain as seat of intellect) — the link to [[Concepts/Pythagorean Medicine and the Life Sciences]] (via Meno / the *Anonymus Londinensis*). Aristotle follows Philolaus closely but faults him for lacking an efficient/final cause.
- On Betegh's Orphism chapter, Philolaus' central **Hearth** parallels the Derveni cosmogony's central fire, yet — tellingly — Philolaus "never says *who* fitted the limiters and unlimiteds together": early Pythagorean cosmology is ordered but not theological. See [[Concepts/Pythagoreanism and Orphism]].

## Relevance to the Vault
- **Where the "number metaphysics" really belongs**: The doctrine summarized on [[Concepts/Number as the Essence of Reality (Pythagoras)]] is, on Burkert's analysis, far better attested for Philolaus and the fifth-century Pythagoreans than for Pythagoras himself. See [[Contradictions/Pythagoras the Scientist vs the Religious Charismatic]].
- **The Plato connection**: A spurious "letter of Plato" had Plato buy "three Pythagorean books" via Philolaus for 100 minas — part of the ancient machinery (Burkert argues) by which Academic number-metaphysics got retrojected onto early Pythagoreanism. Plato's *Phaedo* puts Philolaus' pupils (Simmias, Cebes) on stage.
