# Maimonides (Moses ben Maimon, Rambam)

> Moses ben Maimon (1138–1204), known by the Hebrew acronym **Rambam** and in Latin as **Maimonides** — physician, jurist, Talmudist, and the foremost philosopher of medieval Judaism. His *Guide for the Perplexed* (*Moreh Nevukhim*, c. 1190) is the supreme medieval attempt to reconcile the Hebrew Bible with Aristotelian philosophy, and a decisive influence on [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]] and (by inversion) [[Sources/Ethics - Spinoza (Cambridge, Kisner ed. 2018)|Spinoza]].

## Life and Context

Born in Córdoba in Almohad-ruled al-Andalus, the same intellectual world as [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]] (his near-contemporary). Religious persecution under the Almohads drove his family into exile across North Africa; he eventually settled in Fustat (Old Cairo), where he served as physician to the court of Saladin's vizier and as head (*nagid*) of the Egyptian Jewish community. He wrote in Judeo-Arabic. Besides the *Guide* he produced monumental legal works — the *Commentary on the Mishnah*, the *Mishneh Torah* (a comprehensive code of Jewish law), and the Thirteen Principles of Faith. The *Guide* was promptly translated into Hebrew (Ibn Tibbon) and Latin, entering Christian scholasticism within decades.

## The Project of the *Guide*

The *Guide* addresses the **"perplexed"**: the observant Jew also trained in philosophy, thrown into anxiety by the literal conflict between Scripture and demonstrative science. It is deliberately **esoteric** — truths "at one time apparent, and at another time concealed," scattered like the gold glimpsed through silver filigree, written "to the one man" and not the multitude, even employing intentional contradictions to veil metaphysics from the unprepared. (See [[Concepts/Negative Theology and Divine Attributes (Maimonides)]] for the method.)

## Key Positions

- **[[Concepts/Negative Theology and Divine Attributes (Maimonides)|Negative theology]]** (Part I): God is absolutely simple and incorporeal; no positive essential attribute can be predicated of Him. We know Him only through *negations* and *attributes of action*. Scriptural anthropomorphisms are homonyms to be read figuratively. The terminus is reverent silence.
- **[[Concepts/Critique of the Kalam (Maimonides)|Rejection of the Kalam]]** (Part I): he reconstructs and demolishes the atomism and **occasionalism** of the Mutakallimun — the very doctrine [[Thinkers/Al-Ghazali]] defends — charging that they bend physics to fit dogma. Nature, for Maimonides, has real and stable forces.
- **[[Concepts/Creation vs Eternity (Maimonides)|Creation, not demonstrated]]** (Part II): neither Aristotle's eternity nor creation *ex nihilo* is provable; he proves God on premises valid even if the world were eternal, then accepts creation on prophetic authority plus an argument from cosmic design.
- **[[Concepts/Prophecy (Maimonides)|Naturalized prophecy]]** (Part II): an emanation from the Active Intellect through the rational and imaginative faculties, requiring perfected intellect, imagination, and morals; Moses the homonymous exception.
- **[[Concepts/Providence and the Problem of Evil (Maimonides)|Providence and evil]]** (Part III): evil is privation and mostly self-inflicted; providence is graded by intellectual perfection; Job is a parable whose resolution is intellectual.
- **[[Concepts/Reasons for the Commandments (Maimonides)|Rational reasons for the Law]]** (Part III): no precept is arbitrary; much ritual is a pedagogical weaning from Sabean idolatry; the highest human perfection is knowledge of God issuing in *imitatio Dei*.

## Place in the Vault's Medieval Cluster

Maimonides is the **third corner** of the Aristotle-vs-revelation triangle the vault already holds in Arabic ([[Thinkers/Al-Ghazali]] vs. [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]):
- On **causation**, he stands with Averroes and Aristotle *against* Ghazali — real natures, no occasionalism ([[Contradictions/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation]]).
- On **creation**, he breaks with Aristotle and Averroes (no eternal world) but more cautiously than Ghazali — eternity *undemonstrated* rather than *disproved* ([[Contradictions/Temporal Creation vs Eternal World]]).
- He is the chief conduit by which this Arabic debate, and a rigorous negative theology, reached [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]] ("Rabbi Moses"), who adopts his verdict that the world's beginning is known by faith, not reason, while replacing pure negation with the analogy of being.
- [[Sources/Ethics - Spinoza (Cambridge, Kisner ed. 2018)|Spinoza]], educated on the *Guide*, inverts it — negative-theology's transcendent God becomes immanent *Deus sive Natura*, and the rational law becomes mere political expediency.
