# Anselm of Canterbury (1033–1109)

> Benedictine monk, philosopher, and theologian. Recognized as the father of **Scholasticism** and famous for formulating the **ontological argument** for the existence of God in his *Proslogion*.

## Life and Context

Born in Aosta, Italy, Anselm entered the Benedictine Abbey of Bec in Normandy, France, under the tutelage of the great scholar Lanfranc. He eventually succeeded Lanfranc as Prior and Abbot of Bec, turning the monastery into a major center of learning. In 1093, Anselm was appointed the Archbishop of Canterbury in England. He spent much of his archiepiscopate in conflict with the Kings of England (William II and Henry I) over the independence of the church, twice going into exile.

Anselm's writings mark a major transition in medieval intellectual history. He sought to apply rational analysis to Christian doctrine, operating under the methodological maxims:
* **Credo ut intelligam**: "I believe that I may understand."
* **Fides quaerens intellectum**: "Faith seeking understanding" (the original title of his *Proslogion*).

Anselm was canonized as a saint and declared a Doctor of the Church by the Catholic Church.

---

## Major Works

- *Monologion* (1077): A rational meditation on God's existence and attributes, written as a soliloquy using a posteriori proofs from goodness and scale.
- *Proslogion* (1078): A prayerful contemplation containing his famous single, a priori proof of God's existence (the ontological argument) in Chapters 2 and 3.
- *Cur Deus Homo* (Why God Became Man, 1098): A theological treatise developing the "satisfaction theory" of the atonement, explaining the rational necessity of the Incarnation.

---

## Key Philosophical Positions

* **Faith Seeking Understanding**: Anselm held that belief in divine revelation must come first, but that once faith is established, it is the duty of the Christian to seek to understand those truths rationally. Reason does not replace faith, but elucidates it.
* **The Ontological Argument**: Formulated in the *Proslogion* (Chapters 2 & 3). Anselm defines God as *"that than which nothing greater can be conceived"* (*aliquid quo nihil maius cogitari possit*). He argues that if such a being existed only in the mind (*in intellectu*), we could conceive of a greater being who also existed in reality (*in re*). Therefore, to maintain the definition, God must exist in reality. See [[Arguments/Ontological Argument|Ontological Argument]].
* **Exemplary Realism**: Downstream of Platonism, Anselm held that universal concepts (like goodness, justice, and truth) are real, transcendent archetypes. Individual things are good or true only in proportion as they participate in these absolute standards.

---

## Influence and Legacy

Anselm's ontological argument sparked immediate debate, starting with the critique of his contemporary, the monk Gaunilo, who wrote *On Behalf of the Fool*. In the high Middle Ages, the argument was rejected by [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas|Thomas Aquinas]], who favored a posteriori cosmological proofs. 

However, it was revived in the early modern era by [[Thinkers/Descartes|René Descartes]] and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, and defended in the 20th century in modal form by Charles Hartshorne and Alvin Plantinga. Anselm's emphasis on dialectical reasoning paved the way for the development of the Scholastic university system.

## Relevance to Vault

Anselm anchors the medieval rationalist tradition:
- **Foundations of Scholasticism**: Bridges the early medieval Patristic tradition of [[Thinkers/Augustine|Augustine]] and the high medieval Scholasticism of [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas|Thomas Aquinas]].
- **Ontological Argument Anchor**: Formulates the primary text for the [[Arguments/Ontological Argument|Ontological Argument]], serving as the essential background for Descartes' rationalist proof and Kant's/Hume's epistemological critiques.
- **Epistemic Order**: His maxim "faith seeking understanding" represents the classical view of reasoning from established axioms, which can be contrasted with the radical doubt of Descartes or the empirical induction of Hume.
