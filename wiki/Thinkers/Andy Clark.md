# Andy Clark

> Andy Clark (b. 1957), British philosopher of mind and cognitive science; professor of cognitive philosophy at the University of Sussex (previously Edinburgh, Washington University in St. Louis, Indiana). Best known for two linked theses that dominate his work: the **extended mind** (with David Chalmers, 1998) — that cognition literally spills out of the skull into body, tools, and environment — and, in his later work, **predictive processing** — that the brain is a multilevel prediction machine that constructs experience as a "controlled hallucination." *The Experience Machine* (2023) is his popular synthesis uniting the two: predictive brains are the ideal internal organs for building extended minds.

## Summary

Clark describes himself as "a professor of cognitive philosophy (an odd title that reflects a rather eclectic set of interests spanning philosophy, neuroscience, psychology, and artificial intelligence)." His career forms an arc across the anti-Cartesian tradition in cognitive science: from connectionism (*Microcognition*, 1989; *Associative Engines*, 1993), through embodied and extended cognition (*Being There*, 1997; *Natural-Born Cyborgs*, 2003; *Supersizing the Mind*, 2008), to a decade building the predictive-processing framework (*Surfing Uncertainty*, 2016) and finally *The Experience Machine* (2023), which brings the science to a general audience.

The unifying commitment is anti-representationalist and anti-internalist: the mind is not a passive inner picture-maker sealed behind the "magical membrane" of skin and skull (Susan Hurley's phrase, which Clark endorses), but an active, world-engaged, prediction-driven engine. In *The Experience Machine* he argues that perception, action, emotion, and even bodily sensation are all built the same way — by matching top-down predictions against bottom-up prediction-error signals, weighted by estimated reliability (**precision**). Because experience is prediction-shaped, it is also plastic: it can be retrained, reframed, and "hacked."

## Core Positions (from *The Experience Machine*, 2023)

- **Brain as prediction machine**: "reality as we experience it is built from our own predictions." The traditional feedforward "smart camera" model — sensory data processed step by step from the outside in — is inverted: predictions flow top-down, and sensory input mainly serves to *correct* them via prediction-error signals. See [[Concepts/Predictive Processing (Clark)]].

- **Controlled hallucination**: perception is "inner guessing" kept in check by sensory evidence; "the brain is constantly painting a picture, and the role of the sensory information is mostly to nudge the brushstrokes when they fail to match up with the incoming evidence." Ordinary hallucination is the same process running unconstrained. See [[Concepts/Controlled Hallucination (Clark)]].

- **Precision-weighting as attention**: the "single most powerful tool in the predictive processing toolkit." The brain estimates the reliability of its predictions vs. its sensory evidence and weights them accordingly; "what we informally think of as 'attention' is implemented... by mechanisms that alter these precision-weightings." Misfiring precision explains functional disorders, chronic pain, psychosis, and autism. See [[Concepts/Precision-Weighting (Clark)]].

- **Action as active inference**: "ordinary daily actions are caused by predictions of bodily sensation." To move, the brain predicts the proprioceptive signals that *would* occur if the action were already complete, then eliminates the resulting error by moving the body — action as "self-fulfilling prophecy." This gives "the first fully unified treatment of perception and action." See [[Concepts/Active Inference and Action (Clark)]].

- **The predictive body**: the primary job of the prediction machinery "is to help us stay alive." Interoception, emotion, hunger, and mood are anticipatory constructs (homeostasis + allostasis), not readouts of present bodily states. See [[Concepts/Interoception and Constructed Emotion (Clark)]].

- **Extended mind, now explained**: predictive brains "do not care whether key information is stored in their own internal states... or outside, in notebooks, apps, and GPS systems." Predictive processing supplies the account of "what brains do" that the original 1998 extended-mind paper lacked, solving its "recruitment puzzle." We are "natural-born cyborgs." See [[Concepts/Extended Mind (Clark and Chalmers)]].

- **Hacking the prediction machine**: because only *confident* predictions grip experience, interventions that raise or reshape confidence work — placebo (even open-label), VR analgesia, self-affirmation, reframing, meditation, psychedelics (the REBUS "shake the snow-globe" mechanism). See [[Concepts/Hacking the Prediction Machine (Clark)]].

- **Deflating the hard problem**: Clark is a deflationist (not eliminativist — "it's not my view that conscious experience doesn't exist"). Following Dennett's "strange inversion," the web of behaviors comes first and the ineffable quale is "just a handy label for that web"; self-opaque, simplified self-models lead us to a "metaphysical inflation" of that shorthand into mysterious qualia. See [[Concepts/Predictive Processing and the Hard Problem (Clark)]].

## How Clark Relates to Prior Thinkers in the Vault

- **[[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]**: Clark's closest ally. He builds directly on Dennett's "strange inversion of reasoning" and the deflationary, third-person program of *Consciousness Explained*. Where Dennett gives the **Multiple Drafts** architecture, Clark gives the **predictive-processing mechanism** that could implement a similarly non-Cartesian, non-Theater mind. Both reject a single neural "finish line." Clark is more willing than Dennett to say "qualitative consciousness is real. But perhaps... it isn't exactly what we think."
- **[[Thinkers/Ernest Nagel]]** / Thomas Nagel & Joseph Levine: Clark treats the "what is it like" puzzle and the "explanatory gap" as *conceptual* artifacts of self-opacity, aligning against Nagel's irreducible-subjectivity conclusion — the same axis as [[Contradictions/Dennett vs Nagel - Multiple Drafts vs Subjective Experience]].
- **[[Thinkers/Kant]]** and Helmholtz: Clark's Helmholtzian "unconscious inference" ("Given everything I know, how must the world be for me to be receiving this pattern of signals?") is the cognitive-science heir to Kant's insight that experience is structured by the mind's own contribution, not passively received.
- **[[Thinkers/Descartes]]**: Clark's explicit foil. He cites Descartes's 1664 *Treatise on Man* feedforward "wax imprint" model as the ancestor of the rejected passive-window view, and the extended mind directly attacks Cartesian internalism.
- **[[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]]**: strong (unstated by Clark but structural) convergence — the world-engaged, tool-coupled, always-already-acting agent of predictive/extended cognition echoes [[Concepts/Being-in-the-World (Heidegger)]] and [[Concepts/Readiness-to-Hand and Presence-at-Hand (Heidegger)]]. Clark's "natural-born cyborg" is a naturalized, mechanistic cousin of Dasein's absorbed coping.
- **[[Thinkers/John Searle]]** and [[Concepts/Emergence and Consciousness (Hofstadter)]]: Clark sits on the naturalist/functionalist side of the vault's consciousness debates, offering a concrete processing story rather than Searle's biological-intentionality objection.
