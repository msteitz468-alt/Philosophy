# Sun Tzu

> Chinese general, military strategist, and writer (Sun Wu; traditionally fl. c. 544–496 BCE), advisor to King Ho Lu of the state of Wu in the late Spring-and-Autumn period; reputed author of **The Art of War** (*Sūnzǐ Bīngfǎ*), "the oldest military treatise in the world." His thirteen terse chapters founded the East Asian strategic tradition and became, far beyond war, a touchstone for statecraft, business, negotiation, sport, and competitive strategy.

## Profile

- **Identity/dates**: "Sun Tzu" = "Master Sun"; personal name **Sun Wu**. Per Ssu-ma Ch'ien's *Shih Chi*, a native of the **Ch'i State** who served **Ho Lu, King of Wu**. Traditional dates put him in the late 6th c. BCE (a contemporary, roughly, of Confucius); many scholars date the text to the Warring States period (5th–4th c. BCE) and treat authorship as possibly composite. Historicity is debated.
- **The founding anecdote** (Shih Chi): asked to demonstrate his method on 180 palace women, Sun Tzu appoints the King's two favorite concubines as company commanders; when they laugh instead of obeying clear, twice-repeated orders, he executes them — over the King's protest — establishing that once command is delegated and orders are clear, "**the general… is not bound by [the sovereign's] commands.**" Discipline, clarity, and the competent general's autonomy in one stroke.
- **Work in the vault**: *The Art of War* in the scholarly **Lionel Giles (1910)** translation with classical commentary — see [[Sources/The Art of War - Sun Tzu (Giles trans., 1910)]].

## Core Doctrine (from *The Art of War*)

- **War is grave and calculative.** "A matter of life and death… which can on no account be neglected." Victory is decided in advance by **calculation** of the **five factors** (Moral Law/*tao*, Heaven, Earth, Commander, Method) and **seven considerations** — "many calculations lead to victory, and few calculations to defeat." See [[Concepts/Calculation and the Five Factors (Sun Tzu)]].
- **Win without fighting.** "Supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance **without fighting**"; the best is to take things "whole and intact." The highest generalship attacks the enemy's *plans*; sieges are the worst option. See [[Concepts/Winning Without Fighting (Sun Tzu)]].
- **Know the enemy and know yourself.** The signature maxim — self-knowledge and enemy-knowledge together make victory secure. See [[Concepts/Know the Enemy and Know Yourself (Sun Tzu)]].
- **All warfare is based on deception.** Seem unable when able, far when near; mislead, bait, unbalance. See [[Concepts/All Warfare Is Based on Deception (Sun Tzu)]].
- **Momentum, formlessness, and striking weakness (*shih*).** Combine direct (*cheng*) and indirect (*qi*); be "formless" like water — "avoid what is strong and strike at what is weak"; there are "no constant tactics," only adaptation to the adversary and ground. The tempo of "wind, forest, fire, mountain." See [[Concepts/Shih - Momentum, Formlessness, and Striking Weakness (Sun Tzu)]].
- **Foreknowledge.** What makes the wise sovereign succeed is *foreknowledge* — obtained not from spirits or general laws but from **human intelligence** (the five classes of spies). See [[Concepts/Foreknowledge and the Use of Spies (Sun Tzu)]].
- **Restraint and economy.** Protracted war ruins the state (Ch. II); "no ruler should put troops into the field merely to gratify his own spleen" (Ch. XII). War as a last, costly resort — the bloodless win is the ideal.

## Relations to other vault thinkers

- **[[Thinkers/Thucydides]]** (the natural pairing): both clear-eyed realists about war and power, near-contemporary (5th c. BCE) on opposite ends of Eurasia. But Sun Tzu is *prescriptive* (how to win, ideally without battle), Thucydides *descriptive/tragic* (how empire breeds revolt and power corrupts) — see [[Concepts/Power, Empire, and Revolt (Thucydides)]].
- **Machiavelli** (surveyed in [[Sources/A History of Western Philosophy - Bertrand Russell (1945)]]) and **[[Thinkers/Publius (Hamilton, Madison, and Jay)]]**: kindred realism about conflict, interest, and power — Sun Tzu on the battlefield, Machiavelli on the prince, Publius on institutional checks ("ambition must be made to counteract ambition").
- **[[Thinkers/Garry Kasparov]]**: chess as the purest peacetime laboratory of Sun Tzu's strategy — calculation, initiative, positional advantage (*shih*), exploiting the opponent's weaknesses; Kasparov's human-judgment-vs-machine reflections extend the "know yourself / know the enemy" theme to AI.
- **Ethics/just-war counterpoint** ([[Thinkers/Augustine]] [[Concepts/Tranquillitas Ordinis - Peace as Order (Augustine)|peace-as-order]]; [[Thinkers/Kant]] [[Concepts/Formula of Humanity as End in Itself (Kant)|persons as ends]]): Sun Tzu's deception and instrumental use of people stand in tension with the vault's just-war and deontological threads — though his preference for bloodless victory and restraint-from-passion partly converge with proportionality.
- **Taoist resonance**: the "Moral Law" (*tao*) and the water/formlessness imagery echo Lao Tzu (not yet in the vault) — strategy as wu-wei-like adaptation to circumstance rather than force.

## Vault / AI / Personal Relevance

- **Strategy layer for the whole vault**: Sun Tzu supplies a compact doctrine of *competition under uncertainty* that the career and AI threads can draw on directly — calculation before action, self-and-rival knowledge, positioning over brute force, intelligence/reconnaissance, adaptation.
- **AI/agentic**: "win without fighting" ≈ resolve by reframing rather than costly search; *shih*/formlessness ≈ adaptive policies with "no constant tactics"; foreknowledge ≈ reconnaissance/context-gathering before acting; the five dangerous faults of a general ≈ failure modes of an agent (recklessness, over-caution, provocability).
