# Ibn Rushd (Averroes)

> Abu al-Walid Muhammad ibn Ahmad ibn Rushd (1126–1198 CE), known in the Latin West as **Averroes**. Last great philosopher of Islamic al-Andalus (Cordoba). Jurist, physician, and above all “The Commentator” — the most scholarly and literal expositor of Aristotle in the Arabic tradition. Author of *Tahafut al-Tahafut* (The Incoherence of the Incoherence), a systematic defense of demonstrative philosophy against Al-Ghazali’s attack.

## Life and Context

Born in Cordoba to a family of prominent Maliki jurists. Served as judge and court physician under the Almohad dynasty. Exiled late in life (philosophical works fell under suspicion). Died in Marrakesh. His Aristotle commentaries (nearly the entire corpus) became foundational for medieval Latin and Hebrew philosophy, even as his own original works largely disappeared in the Islamic East.

He stands at the end of the classical *falasifa* tradition (Al-Kindi, Farabi, Avicenna, Ibn Tufayl) and the culmination of the engagement with Greek thought in Islam.

## Major Works

- Extensive literal commentaries on Aristotle (short, middle, and long forms on many texts; the long ones especially influential in the West).
- *Tahafut al-Tahafut* (c. 1180) — direct response to Ghazali’s *Tahafut al-Falasifa*. Point-by-point refutation preserving Aristotelian positions on eternity, causation, knowledge, soul, etc.
- Medical work (*Kitab al-Kulliyat* / Colliget), legal writings (*Bidayat al-Mujtahid*), and the *Fasl al-Maqal* (Decisive Treatise) on the harmony of religion and philosophy (philosophy obligatory for those capable; religion for all).

## Key Philosophical Positions (from the Tahafut)

- **Defense of Aristotle**: The world is eternal in the sense of having no temporal beginning (time and motion are eternal; God as eternal simultaneous cause, not a “new will” producing novelty). Real secondary causes and natures exist in things (fire burns by its form/quality); occasionalist denial of necessary connections between cause and effect is sophistical and destroys intellect, logic, science, and essences.
- **God and Creation**: God as First Mover and cause; emanationist elements retained but subordinated to Aristotelian analysis. Critiques both Ghazali’s arbitrary eternal will and some Neoplatonic excesses.
- **Soul and Intellect**: Engages Avicenna on individuation. Defends forms of survival; the rational soul’s connection to the body and its powers. (Note: the strong “unicity of the intellect” position later associated with “Averroism” in the Latin West is more a development of certain interpretations than Averroes’ own unqualified view in this text.)
- **Resurrection and Religion**: Affirms the necessity of some doctrine of afterlife and bodily resurrection (or simulacra) for moral order and the virtue of the masses. Religious laws are essential political arts derived from reason + prophetic inspiration. The philosopher must not publicly undermine the principles of the religion in which he was raised; the learned are heirs of the prophets. Miracles can occur through special divine action on dispositions without abolishing all natural causation.
- **Elites vs. Masses**: Philosophy (demonstration) for the qualified few; religion (with its images, stories, and prescribed acts) for universal formation of virtue and happiness. Both can be true in their domains; the conflict arises from misapplying demonstrative standards to the masses or dogmatic standards to the philosophers.

## Influence and Reception

- **Latin West**: Enormous. Aristotle + Averroes commentaries shaped university curricula. “Averroism” (often the unicity of intellect, double-truth theory, eternity) became a flashpoint; condemned in Paris 1270 and 1277. Thomas Aquinas constantly cites and corrects “the Commentator” while building his own synthesis of Aristotle + Christianity (see especially *Summa Theologica* I, QQ. 75–76 on the soul as form of the body and rejection of one intellect for all men).
- **Islamic World**: Limited long-term influence; philosophy as a living tradition largely curtailed after the 12th c. in many regions (Ghazali’s critique contributed to the climate).
- **Jewish and Renaissance**: Significant via Hebrew translations and Latin Averroism; figures like Maimonides engaged the tradition; later Renaissance debates.
