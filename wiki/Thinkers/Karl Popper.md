# Karl Popper

> Karl Raimund Popper (1902–1994), Austrian-born British philosopher. Author of *Logik der Forschung* / *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* (1934/1959), the foundational text of critical rationalism: science progresses by bold conjectures subjected to rigorous attempted refutation; falsifiability demarcates the empirical; there is no logic of discovery, only of testing; induction is neither possible nor needed.

## Profile

- **Dates / life**: Born Vienna 1902 (Jewish descent, raised Lutheran); studied mathematics, physics, philosophy at University of Vienna; early engagement with (and criticism of) the Vienna Circle; emigrated to New Zealand 1937 (taught at Canterbury University College); moved to London School of Economics 1946, professor of logic and scientific method; knighted 1965; died 1994.
- **Major works**: *Logik der Forschung* (1934, written in reaction to positivism), *The Open Society and Its Enemies* (1945, critique of historicism/Plato/Hegel/Marx), *The Poverty of Historicism*, *Conjectures and Refutations* (1963, essays including the classic demarcation lecture), *Objective Knowledge* (1972), *The Self and Its Brain* (with Eccles), autobiographical *Unended Quest*.
- **In the vault**: Two primary sources — [[Sources/The Logic of Scientific Discovery - Karl Popper (Routledge Classics)]] (the technical 1934/59 text) and [[Sources/Conjectures and Refutations - Karl Popper]] (the 1963 essay collection, *ingested 2026-06-04*), which supplies the popular demarcation lecture, the sources-of-knowledge essay, the three views of knowledge, verisimilitude, the Presocratic critical tradition, and Popper's political philosophy (anti-historicism, open society). Strong anticipatory references already existed in the Hume induction concept and Epicurus thinker pages.

## Core Positions (from *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*)

- **No logic of discovery, only of testing**: The "eureka" moment (conceiving a new theory) is psychological/creative ("irrational element," intuition per Einstein); epistemology/logic of science concerns only the subsequent critical examination and testing of statements once formulated. Distinguishes sharply from psychology of knowledge.

- **Falsifiability as criterion of demarcation**: Empirical science is characterized by the logical possibility of refutation by experience. A theory is scientific if it prohibits certain observable states of affairs (i.e., certain basic statements can contradict it). "The more a theory prohibits, the more it says." Verifiability fails for universal laws and theories; falsifiability succeeds due to the logical asymmetry (universal statements are contradicted, never entailed, by singular statements). This is a *proposal* / convention for drawing the line between science and metaphysics (or pseudo-science), not a discovery of a natural kind.

- **Deductive method of testing (hypothetico-deductive)**: From a conjecture + initial conditions, deduce predictions (especially those that are surprising or forbidden by background knowledge). Test the predictions. Positive result: temporary "corroboration" ("the theory has proved its mettle" by surviving severe tests so far). Negative result: falsification of the system (or the implicated part). No inductive inference from instances to laws ever occurs or is required.

- **Solution to Hume's problem of induction**: Accepts Hume's negative result in full (no rational foundation for the uniformity of nature; inductive inferences not justified by reason). Bypasses the problem by rejecting the positivist demand that scientific statements be "conclusively decidable" (both verifiable and falsifiable). Science can be empirical and rational while being only *unilaterally* decidable (falsifiable). The "fundamental thesis of empiricism" (experience decides truth/falsity) is preserved without induction.

- **The empirical basis and basic statements**: Singular statements usable for falsification ("basic statements") are not incorrigible reports of "the given" or perceptual experience. They are themselves hypothetical, inter-subjectively testable, and accepted by *decision* or convention of investigators. No ultimate foundation; testability in principle goes on ad infinitum. Objectivity resides in inter-subjective testability, not in subjective conviction or private experience.

- **Theory of method as rules**: Methodological rules are conventions (not empirical descriptions of what scientists do, nor purely logical). The supreme rule: all other rules must be designed so as not to protect any statement from falsification. Example: auxiliary hypotheses are acceptable only if they increase (not decrease) the falsifiability of the system. Science is defined by its critical, anti-dogmatic attitude and procedures.

- **Corroboration, not probability or verification**: "Degree of corroboration" measures how severely a theory has been tested and survived (depends on the improbability/risk of the tests). Distinct from logical probability (Popper argues universal laws have zero probability; probability cannot serve as degree of confirmation or "reliability"). Later developed into verisimilitude (truthlikeness) as the regulative ideal.

- **Metaphysics and heuristics**: Metaphysical ideas (e.g., atomism, determinism, field theories) have repeatedly guided science even when not yet falsifiable. They are not "meaningless" (contra early verificationism); they become scientific when formulated so as to risk refutation. "Metaphysical research programmes" can be fertile.

- **Critical rationalism**: The attitude of the rationalist is the critical attitude: willingness to submit one's conjectures to the severest tests and to let the best (most corroborated, most falsifiable) survive. "I may be wrong and you may be right, and by an effort, we may get nearer to the truth."

## Further Positions (from *Conjectures and Refutations*, 1963)

- **No authoritative sources of knowledge**: Neither observation (empiricism) nor intellectual intuition (rationalism) is an authoritative *source* of truth; both classical schools share the false "manifest truth" doctrine, whose shadow is the *conspiracy theory of ignorance*. The right question is not "What are the best sources?" but "How can we detect and eliminate error?" (→ [[Concepts/No Authoritative Sources of Knowledge (Popper)]])

- **Three views of knowledge — scientific realism**: Against *essentialism* (ultimate explanation by essences) and *instrumentalism* (theories as mere tools), Popper defends *conjecturalism*: theories are genuine, fallible conjectures about the structure of reality, true or false. (→ [[Concepts/Three Views Concerning Human Knowledge (Popper)]])

- **Truth and verisimilitude**: Adopts Tarski's objective (correspondence) theory of truth as a regulative idea, and defines **verisimilitude** (truth-content minus falsity-content) as the aim of science — a false theory can be nearer the truth than another; science seeks *content/truthlikeness*, the opposite of probability. (→ [[Concepts/Verisimilitude (Truthlikeness) (Popper)]])

- **The critical tradition and the Presocratics**: The invention of *rational criticism* — the freedom to improve on one's teacher's doctrine — is traced to the Ionian school (Thales → Anaximander → Anaximenes). Science began not with cautious observation (the "Baconian myth") but with bold cosmological conjecture. "All science is cosmology."

- **Sympathetic re-reading of Kant**: Kant's real problem was cosmological (how is Newton's science possible?); his error was believing Newton's theory certainly true (forgivable before Einstein). Critical rationalism is "the finishing touch to Kant's own critical philosophy" and to Kant's principle of autonomy.

- **Political philosophy (anti-historicism, open society)**: Rejects *historicism* (the doctrine that history obeys laws permitting prophecy) by distinguishing conditional scientific prediction from unconditional prophecy; critiques the Hegelian/Marxist *dialectic* for tolerating contradiction; opposes utopian/violent social engineering in favour of *piecemeal* reform and the *open society*. (→ [[Concepts/Critique of Historicism and Dialectic (Popper)]]) The vault's first primary-source entry into Popper's politics (fuller treatment in *The Poverty of Historicism* and *The Open Society and Its Enemies*, not yet ingested).

## Influence, Reception, and Contrasts in the Vault

- **On the problem of induction cluster**: Direct successor and critic of Hume (accepts the negative, rejects custom as sufficient "solution"; offers methodology instead). Engages Russell's restatement (the chicken) and his proposed a priori inductive principle. The vault's [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]] page already flagged Popper's falsificationism as the major 20th-century bypass attempt; this source supplies the primary grounding.

- **Vs. Vienna Circle / logical positivism** (referenced via Hume, Russell, early Wittgenstein citations in the text): Polemical target. Rejects equating meaning with verifiability; falsifiability is demarcation only. Defends the legitimacy and historical role of metaphysics. Prefaces criticize the narrowing of philosophy to linguistic analysis.

- **Vs. / with Quine**: Quine (1951, primary-sourced) supplies the deeper diagnosis of why the verificationist tools Carnap and others used were broken (circular analytic/synthetic + failed reductionism), yielding confirmation holism and the web of belief instead. Both reject the positivists, but Quine emphasizes underdetermination and the latitude of web-wide readjustment; Popper emphasizes severe attempted refutations of specific theories and methodological rules that maximize exposure. The tension (holism vs. localized falsification) is productive and noted on the falsifiability, demarcation, and induction concepts. See [[Sources/Two Dogmas of Empiricism - W.V.O. Quine (1951)]] and [[Thinkers/W.V.O. Quine]].

- **Vs. Kant**: Explicitly names the demarcation problem "Kant's problem." Retains the centrality of distinguishing science from metaphysics but replaces synthetic a priori foundations with fallible, critical, empirical method.

- **With Epicurus / ancient empiricism**: The multiple-explanations + reject-only-what-is-contradicted-by-sensation attitude in the *Letter to Pythocles* is noted as proto-falsificationist / verificationist leanings in ancient atomist empiricism. Popper provides the modern logical articulation and the full critical methodology.

- **With pragmatism (James)**: Shared language of "corroborate and verify," but Popper's corroboration is survival under deliberate attempts at refutation within a rule-governed critical method, not "leading satisfactorily" or "expedient in the way of our thinking." Distinction can be sharpened on the James pages.

- **Broader 20th c. philosophy of science**: Enormously influential on discussions of demarcation. The major rival is **[[Thinkers/Thomas Kuhn]]**, now primary-sourced in the vault ([[Sources/The Structure of Scientific Revolutions - Kuhn (Chicago, 4th ed.)]]): Kuhn replaces Popper's permanent critical revolution with paradigm-bound *normal science* punctuated by rare revolutions, denies bare falsification, and rejects approach-to-truth — see the dedicated [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]]. **[[Thinkers/Paul Feyerabend]]** — Popper's own apostate student — is the radical limit: in [[Sources/Against Method - Paul Feyerabend|*Against Method*]] he declares critical rationalism just one more rule history breaks ("anything goes") and is now primary-sourced. **[[Thinkers/Ian Hacking]]** (*Representing and Intervening*, 1983; now primary-sourced) engages Popper's realism positively (shares conjecturalism and anti-instrumentalism) but grounds entity realism in experimental use and intervention ("if you can spray them...") rather than (or alongside) severe testing and verisimilitude; also treats Lakatos as offering a surrogate for truth without correspondence. See [[Sources/Representing and Intervening - Ian Hacking (1983)]] and the expanded [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]]. Lakatos' research programmes (a Popper–Kuhn mediation) remain a further reaction (not yet in the vault). Popper's emphasis on criticism, error-elimination, and the growth of knowledge (via conjectures and refutations) remains a live framework in philosophy of science, evolutionary epistemology, and methodology.

- **Political / Open Society**: The same critical rationalism underpins his defense of the open society against historicist and totalitarian "certainty." Now partly primary-sourced via *C&R*'s *Refutations* essays (→ [[Concepts/Critique of Historicism and Dialectic (Popper)]]); the full-length *Open Society* and *Poverty of Historicism* remain natural later additions for the political-philosophy / history-of-ideas leg. Resonates with the Federalist design of checked, correctable power ([[Concepts/Checks and Balances (Federalist 51)]]) and contrasts with the comprehensive ideal cities of Plato and Augustine.

- **Wiki as critical-rationalist artifact**: Pages function as conjectures; lint, contradiction pages, and source-driven updates function as attempted refutations and error-elimination; frontmatter + wikilinks + index provide intersubjective testability and provenance; "basic statements" ~ the accepted readings/summaries drawn from raw sources by decision; no summary is immune to future falsification by new sources or better criticism. "Corroboration" as a non-probabilistic quality metric for context. "No ad hoc auxiliaries" as discipline against patching explanations to save face.

Strategy/playbooks (the Alliant one) as conjectures to be tested in the field; "severe tests" = real deals, retention data, edge cases. Deliberate exposure of plans to criticism (networking, interviews) rather than confirmation-seeking.

- **Science communication / Neil deGrasse Tyson source** (in raw): Popular modern counterpart emphasizing scientific thinking as evidence-based, open to correction — congenial but less formally articulated than Popper's demarcation and methodology.
