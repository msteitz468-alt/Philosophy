# John Locke (1632–1704)

> English philosopher and physician, widely regarded as the founder of British empiricism. Author of *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689/1690, revised 1694+), the *Two Treatises of Government*, *A Letter Concerning Toleration*, *Some Thoughts Concerning Education*, and *The Reasonableness of Christianity*. The mind at birth as a blank slate ("white paper," "empty cabinet") furnished entirely by experience; all ideas originate in sensation or reflection; vehement critic of innate principles and ideas.

## Life and Context

Born 29 August 1632 in a small thatched cottage in Wrington, Somerset (near Bristol). Educated at Westminster School and Christ Church, Oxford. Physician and close associate of Anthony Ashley Cooper (later 1st Earl of Shaftesbury); served in various administrative and diplomatic roles. Exiled in Holland during the political troubles of the 1680s; returned after the Glorious Revolution. Lived his later years in Essex and London, continuing to revise and publish major works until his death in 1704.

The *Essay* grew out of conversations (circa 1671) among friends who found themselves at an impasse on a remote moral/religious subject. Locke proposed that before pursuing such questions they should first "examine our own abilities, and see what OBJECTS our understandings were, or were not, fitted to deal with." The work was written "by incoherent parcels" over many years with long interruptions, eventually reaching the bulk of the published version.

**Context in the vault**: The pivotal figure who supplies the constructive empiricist theory of ideas and the systematic refutation of innatism that stands between the classical/rationalist threads (Plato, Aristotle, Descartes, Spinoza) and Hume's radical sceptical development of empiricism. The *Essay* is the direct intellectual predecessor explicitly acknowledged in Hume's own framing.

## Major Works (in the Complete Works volume)

- *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (primary epistemological text; four books: I. No innate principles/ideas; II. Of Ideas — origin, simple vs. complex, primary/secondary qualities, power, identity, association; III. Of Words/Language; IV. Of Knowledge and Probability, faith, reason, enthusiasm).
- *A Letter Concerning Toleration* (1689) and sequels (Second, Third, Fourth Letters).
- *Two Treatises of Government* (published 1689, written earlier): First Treatise refutes Filmer's patriarchal absolutism; Second Treatise develops the social contract, natural rights, state of nature, property, consent, and right of revolution.
- *Some Thoughts Concerning Education* (1693): practical pedagogy emphasizing habit, example, and the formation of character over rote learning.
- *The Reasonableness of Christianity* (1695) and two Vindications: argues that the core of Christianity is simple and accessible (faith that Jesus is the Messiah), not a system of abstruse metaphysics.
- *Some Thoughts on the Conduct of the Understanding* (posthumous): practical advice on improving the faculty the *Essay* had analyzed.
- Miscellaneous letters, poems, and the biographies by Pierre des Maizeaux and Leslie Stephen included in the Delphi volume.

## Key Philosophical Positions (from Book I + front matter of the *Essay*)

**The negative thesis (Book I — fully covered in this ingest chunk)**: There are no innate principles (speculative or practical) and no innate ideas.

- **Against innate speculative principles**: The candidates "Whatsoever is, is" and "It is impossible for the same thing to be and not to be" enjoy no universal assent. Children, "idiots," and large portions of mankind (including "savages") have no apprehension of them. Universal consent, even if it existed, would not prove innateness (other routes to agreement are possible). The common reply that we know them "when we come to the use of reason" actually concedes they are not known from the beginning and fails for other reasons: reason is the faculty of deducing unknowns from knowns; if these maxims required reason to be discovered they are not the original inscriptions claimed; general abstract ideas (the materials of the maxims) are themselves acquired.
- **Against innate practical/moral principles**: No moral rule commands as general and ready assent as the speculative maxims. "Justice and keeping of compacts" is observed within outlaw groups for convenience, not as an innate law recognized toward outsiders. Actions (not professions) best reveal convictions; self-interest leads men to praise rules they violate when it suits them. Conscience is merely one's own judgment of the moral quality of actions and is shaped by education and custom — it can (and does) support contradictory rules in different people or societies. Moral rules require proof and deduction; one can always ask "why?" of the Golden Rule or any other, which would be absurd for a truly self-evident innate principle. Enormous cultural and historical variation in what is practiced without scruple (infanticide, parricide, cannibalism, exposure of the sick, etc.) further refutes innateness.
- **Component ideas must be innate if propositions are**: Many of the ideas presupposed by the supposed innate maxims (identity, impossibility, whole/part, God, worship) are themselves late, obscure, disputed, or absent in children and in various cultures. "Identity" is far from clear (bodily change, resurrection, transmigration examples). The idea of God, while deducible by mature reason from the works of creation, is not universal (documented nations without any notion or name) and is not present in children.
- **Methodological moral**: Principles that are treated as unquestionable on the basis of long familiarity, education, or borrowed authority should be examined. The demand for clear marks distinguishing genuine innate principles from pretenders is legitimate and unmet by the "universal consent" argument alone.

**The positive theory (Book II — this chunk: Ch. I ideas in general/origin; Ch. II simple ideas; relevant Ch. VIII primary/secondary qualities)**: All ideas from experience via two fountains — SENSATION (external sensible objects → ideas of qualities like color, heat, taste) and REFLECTION (mind's own operations → ideas of perceiving, thinking, doubting, willing, etc.). Mind initially "white paper, void of all characters." Simple ideas are uncompounded uniform appearances (e.g., coldness/hardness as distinct as smell/whiteness); mind passive in receiving them, cannot invent or destroy new simple ideas, only compound. Primary qualities (solidity, extension, figure, number, motion/rest): real, inseparable, resemblances in our ideas. Secondary qualities (colors, sounds, tastes, etc.): powers in bodies (depending on primary/insensible particles) to produce sensations in us; no resemblance in the bodies themselves (e.g., fire produces warmth or pain by distance; porphyry has no color in dark; same water hot/cold to different hands). Ideas (perceptions in mind) carefully distinguished from qualities (powers in bodies).

## Relevance to Vault

Locke supplies the missing constructive half of the empiricist story that Hume both inherits and radicalizes. The vault's Early Modern cluster now has its proper foundation:

- **Vs. Descartes / rationalist innatism**: Locke's blank slate and experience-only account of ideas is the explicit counter to the innate "trademark" idea of God, the cogito as substantial self-knowledge, and the clear-and-distinct criterion as an internal non-empirical standard. Hyperbolic doubt clears the ground; Locke argues the ground was never written on in the first place. Now with primary/secondary (primary resemblant like Descartes' clear extension/motion; secondary powers/subjective like his confused sensory qualities) and the two fountains fully detailed.
- **Predecessor to Hume**: Hume's Copy Principle is "derivative from Locke." Hume takes the anti-innatism and the theory of ideas from sensation/reflection and drives them to their sceptical conclusions (no impression of necessary connexion, no impression of an enduring self, induction not rationally grounded). The vault now has the Locke → Hume transmission in primary sources.
- **Vs. classical threads**: Direct engagement with (and rejection of) Platonic recollection/innate knowledge and Aristotelian essentialism/innate principles of demonstration. The developmental and cross-cultural arguments parallel Sextus' use of disagreement and relativity (Modes) but reach a constructive empiricist outcome rather than epoché.
