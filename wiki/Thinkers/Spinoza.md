# Benedict de Spinoza (Baruch Espinoza, 1632–1677)

> Dutch philosopher of Portuguese Sephardic Jewish descent. Author of the *Ethics* (posthumous 1677), the only work published under his own name during his lifetime (*Principles of Descartes’ Philosophy*, 1663), and the anonymously published *Theological-Political Treatise* (1670). Developer of substance monism (*Deus sive Natura*), attribute parallelism, conatus as the actual essence of things, a fully naturalistic and deterministic ethics culminating in the intellectual love of God, and a rigorous geometrical method applied to metaphysics and morals. One of the most radical and systematic thinkers of the early modern period; excommunicated from the Amsterdam Jewish community in 1656.

## Life and Context

Born Baruch de Espinoza (later Latinized Benedict) in Amsterdam to a family of Portuguese Jewish merchants who had fled the Inquisition. Attended the Talmud Torah school; studied Hebrew, Bible, Talmud. After his father’s death, briefly involved in the family business with his brother. Began studying Latin with Franciscus van den Enden (freethinker, political radical); encountered Cartesianism, Collegiant dissenters, and broader European thought.

In 1656, at age 23, he was issued a *herem* (writ of excommunication) by the Amsterdam Portuguese Jewish community. The language was severe and final (no provision for repentance); it forbade members from communicating with him or even coming within four cubits of him. Likely reasons included his emerging heterodox views (denial of personal immortality of the soul, challenges to traditional authority and perhaps aspects of revealed religion). The event severed his ties with family and community. He earned his living grinding lenses (a trade that contributed to his early death from respiratory illness in 1677 at age 44).

He associated with radical Protestant Collegiants and freethinkers. In 1663 he published (under his own name) a geometric exposition of Descartes’ *Principles of Philosophy* with an appendix of his own metaphysical thoughts—his only work published under his name while alive. The *Theological-Political Treatise* (1670, anonymous) defended freedom of thought and speech, offered pioneering biblical criticism (treating Scripture as a historical document), and argued for the separation of philosophy from theology and for a secular state. It caused enormous controversy.

The *Ethics* was completed around 1675. Spinoza traveled to Amsterdam to arrange publication but withdrew it upon learning of opposition (including from authorities who had already acted against other radicals). After his death in 1677, friends published the *Opera Posthuma* (Latin) and a Dutch translation, including the *Ethics*, *Treatise on the Emendation of the Intellect*, correspondence, and other works.

Spinoza lived modestly, declined academic positions (including a chair at Heidelberg), and cultivated a small circle of correspondents and supporters. His lens-grinding work supported him while allowing time for philosophy. He was widely regarded in his lifetime and immediately after as a dangerous atheist or pantheist; his works were banned in various places.

## Major Works

- *Principles of Descartes’ Philosophy* (1663, published under his name): geometric presentation of Descartes with Spinoza’s own *Metaphysical Thoughts* appendix.
- *Theological-Political Treatise* (1670, anonymous): biblical hermeneutics, defense of *libertas philosophandi*, critique of superstition and clerical power, political theory (democracy as most natural and free form of government).
- *Ethics* (completed ~1675, published posthumously 1677): the systematic masterpiece (see dedicated source summary).
- *Treatise on the Emendation of the Intellect* (unfinished, posthumous): programmatic sketch of the project of finding the highest good through knowledge of the mind’s union with nature.
- *Political Treatise* (unfinished, posthumous): constitutional theory and analysis of forms of government.
- Correspondence (posthumous): invaluable for development of ideas, responses to objections, and personal context.

## Key Philosophical Positions (primarily from the Ethics)

- **Substance Monism / Deus sive Natura**: There is only one substance (God or Nature). Finite things are modes (affections) of this substance, not independent substances (contra Descartes and Aristotle). God is the immanent cause of all things; everything is in God and cannot be conceived without God. God has infinite attributes (we know only thought and extension); each attribute expresses the eternal and infinite essence of the one substance.

- **Attributes and Parallelism**: Thought and Extension are parallel attributes of the same substance. “The order and connection of ideas is the same as the order and connection of things” (2p7). A mode under extension (a body) and the corresponding mode under thought (its idea/mind) are one and the same thing expressed in two ways. No causal interaction between mind and body in the Cartesian sense; the same causal order appears under each attribute.

- **Conatus**: Each thing, insofar as it can by its own power, strives to persevere in its being (3p6–7). This striving (*conatus*) is the actual essence of the thing. It grounds the theory of affects (joy = increase in power of acting; sadness = decrease) and the ethics (virtue = power; acting from one’s own nature).

- **Ideas and Knowledge**: Ideas are not passive representations but have power (to affirm their content). Adequate ideas (caused from within the mind’s own ideas) vs. inadequate ideas (partly caused externally). Three kinds of knowledge/cognition: (1) imagination (inadequate, sensory, source of falsity and bondage); (2) reason (adequate, common notions); (3) *scientia intuitiva* (intuitive knowledge of singular things as following from God’s essence—the highest, yielding the greatest joy and freedom).

- **Affects/Emotions**: Bodily affections (increases or decreases in power of acting) together with the ideas of those affections. Active affects arise from adequate ideas (we act); passive affects (passions) arise from inadequate ideas (we are acted on). All emotions can in principle be understood and, to the extent we have adequate ideas, transformed or overcome.

- **Freedom, Virtue, Blessedness**: Freedom is acting from the necessity of one’s own nature (adequate ideas), not from external compulsion or “free will” in the libertarian sense. Virtue and power are the same (4def8). The highest good is the understanding of God/Nature. Blessedness (*beatitudo*) is the joy that accompanies the intellectual love of God (*amor Dei intellectualis*)—the mind’s intuitive knowledge of itself and God as eternal. This love is eternal (insofar as the mind conceives things under a species of eternity) and is our greatest happiness. “All things excellent are as difficult as they are rare” (5p42s).

- **Naturalism and Critique of Teleology**: No purposes, final causes, or supernatural interventions in nature. God/Nature does not act for ends or from a will like a human king. Traditional religious ideas of a personal, providential God are projections of human prejudices (Appendix to Part I). Ethics and politics must be derived from the understanding of nature, not from divine commands or supernatural grace. Secular, this-worldly blessedness through knowledge.

- **Method**: *More geometrico*—definitions (of essences), axioms, propositions with demonstrations, scholia, corollaries. Because conceptual dependence mirrors causal dependence in nature (1a4), the deductive order of the text reveals the actual order of things.

## Influence and Reception

Immediately controversial: accused of atheism, pantheism, fatalism. Works banned in the Netherlands and elsewhere. Major underground influence on the Radical Enlightenment (Bayle, Toland, Diderot, etc.). 19th-century revival (German Idealism, Romanticism—Goethe, Coleridge; “pantheism controversy”). 20th-century: influence on Marxism (via naturalism and critique of ideology), psychoanalysis (affects), contemporary metaphysics (monism, panpsychism debates), environmental ethics, and political theory (freedom, democracy, critique of superstition). The *Theological-Political Treatise* is foundational for modern biblical criticism and liberal political thought on toleration.

## Relevance to Vault

Spinoza is the essential counterweight and synthesis to the vault’s Descartes material and the classical cluster. The *Ethics* offers both a profound philosophical position and a methodological model of exceptional rigor.

- **Direct contrast with Descartes (now in vault)**: Monism + parallelism (one substance; mind and body as parallel expressions of the same thing) vs. substance dualism and interaction. God/Nature as immanent, deterministic, non-purposive cause vs. transcendent creator. Compatibilist freedom through adequate ideas vs. libertarian will. Geometrical deduction from essences vs. meditative hyperbolic doubt + reconstruction. The two together form a powerful paired study of early modern rationalism’s internal tensions and possibilities.

- **Classical philosophy**: 
  - Aristotle: Shares vocabulary of substance, essence, attribute, demonstration, and the idea that understanding essences yields knowledge of properties; but rejects pluralism of substances, hylomorphism (soul as form of *this* body), and final causes/teleology in favor of strict immanent necessity and one substance.
  - Plotinus/Neoplatonism: Everything flows from a single source (“in” God); intellectual ascent and love; but fully immanent and naturalistic rather than transcendent emanation with a hierarchy of hypostases and return to the One beyond being.
  - Aquinas and scholasticism: God proofs and natural law rethought under monism and naturalism (no separate supernatural order; “grace” language replaced by knowledge of nature). “Grace perfects nature” becomes understanding nature as it is (God).
  - Augustine: Intellectual love of God and eternal blessedness have resonances, but stripped of transcendence, personal will, and grace; achieved in this life through adequate ideas rather than confession and rest in a transcendent God.
  - Stoics: Conatus and virtue-as-power/living-according-to-nature echo Stoic themes; determinism accepted but with a different (monist, non-teleological) metaphysics and an ethics of joy in understanding rather than apatheia.
  - Epicureans/Lucretius: Thorough naturalism and this-worldly ethics; but rationalist deduction and monism vs. atomism and pleasure-calculus.
  - Sextus: No epoché; instead, movement from inadequate to adequate ideas yields certainty and freedom.

## Deleuze's Reading of Spinoza (from [[Sources/The Deleuze Reader]])

Deleuze treats Spinoza as the philosopher of immanence par excellence and the primary source for his ethics without morality:
- **Univocal Being:** Being is said in one sense of everything; no vertical axes of participation or emanation. "Being is univocal, equal to itself, and offers itself equally to all beings."
- **Expression (implicatio/explicatio):** The One expresses itself through an infinity of attributes in modes. No hierarchical power — the essence "unfolds" without any loss or descending grace.
- **The body as philosophical model:** "We do not know what the body can do." Consciousness registers only effects, never causes — generating the triple illusion of final causes, free will, transcendence.
- **Ethics vs. morality:** Spinoza's *Ethics* is, for Deleuze, not a moral system but a typology of immanent modes of existence — ethology. See [[Concepts/Ethics Without Morality (Deleuze-Spinoza)]].
- **Sad passions and despotism:** Deleuze reads Spinoza politically: sad passions (fear, guilt, resentment) are the instruments of power over subjects. Joy and active affection are simultaneously the ethical aim and the condition of freedom.
