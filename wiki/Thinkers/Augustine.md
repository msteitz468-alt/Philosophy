# Augustine

> Aurelius Augustinus (354–430 CE), Bishop of Hippo Regius (North Africa). "Doctor Gratiae" (Doctor of Grace) of the Catholic Church. Last of the great Latin Church Fathers and first major medieval theologian. Author of the *Confessions* (his most personal and widely read work, c. 397–400), *City of God*, *De Trinitate*, *Enchiridion*, numerous sermons/letters, and anti-heretical treatises (Manichees, Donatists, Pelagians). Synthesized Scripture, patristic tradition (Tertullian to Ambrose), Nicene orthodoxy, and Greco-Roman philosophy (especially Neo-Platonism) into a powerful, unsystematic but coherent Christian outlook centered on the sovereign God of grace, the divided human will, original sin as privation, and the soul's pilgrimage/rest in God. Enormous influence on Western Christianity (Catholic, Protestant, mystical), psychology, philosophy of history, and the entire subsequent tradition — "even Aquinas is more of an Augustinian at heart than a 'proper' Aristotelian."

## Life and Context (from Confessions + Outler Introduction)

- Born 354 CE in Tagaste (Numidia, Roman North Africa) to pagan father (Patricius) and devout Christian mother (Monica).
- Education in rhetoric; early "stormy fellowship of human life," theater, "cauldron of illicit loves."
- Manichean phase (material dualism, promise of wisdom); later disillusioned.
- Professor of rhetoric in Carthage, Rome, Milan.
- Intellectual turning via "the Platonists" (Neo-Platonism/Plotinus) — liberation from materialism, vision of immaterial reality and the One, but insufficient for continence or stable peace.
- Milan: Ambrose's preaching (authority + beauty of Scripture), stories of conversions (Victorinus, Antony, imperial agents).
- **Garden conversion (Book VIII, Milan ~386 CE)**: Violent inner turmoil of divided will; child's voice "Tolle, lege"; Romans 13:13–14 resolves the crisis toward continence and faith. Baptized by Ambrose Easter 387 with Alypius and son Adeodatus.
- Monica's death at Ostia (ecstatic shared vision of eternal life); return to Africa, monastic life at Tagaste, ordained presbyter (391, Hippo), consecrated bishop ~395/396 (despite misgivings).
- *Confessions* written as bishop, ~397–400: retracing the pilgrimage to celebrate grace and model honest self-examination.
- Later: *City of God* (philosophy of history, two cities), Pelagian controversy (grace, predestination, "give what you command"), vast literary output until death 430 during Vandal siege of Hippo.

Augustine saw himself as summator and defender of the Church's faith rather than innovator. Center: Holy Scriptures + God's abundant, unmerited grace. Tensions: grace vs. freedom (insisted on both, plus double predestination/irresistible grace in later works); celebration of mercy alongside conviction that most are justly damned.

## Key Contributions (focused on the Confessions)

**Grace as Sovereign and Transforming (Doctor Gratiae)**:
- Central theme of all his writings: "the sovereign God of grace and the sovereign grace of God."
- Grace: God's unmerited love/favor, prevenient and occurrent; touches inmost heart/will; transforms the will so it is capable of good; "Give what you command and command what you will."
- Prevenient: God turns wills to faith even when averse/adverse (Monica's tears as instrument).
- "Grace does not destroy nature but perfects it" (later axiomatic in Aquinas).

**Psychology of the Will, Interiority, and Memory**:
- Master analyst of motives, emotions, divided will ("two wills... one old, one new... at war within me").
- Confession as double act: acknowledgment of sins (humility) + praise of God (truth-telling in God's presence).
- **Memory (Book X)**: "Vast fields and spacious palaces... storehouses... plains, and caverns... innumerable and innumerably full." Not only sensible images but abstract knowledge (numbers, rules of reasoning). Path to self-knowledge and to God ("Let me know you, O my Knower"). The inner man as the true arena of the drama.
- Self as mystery: "I have become a question to myself."

**Evil as Privation of Good (not positive substance)**:
- Famous analysis in the pear theft (Book II): stole "simply that I might steal"; "My sole gratification in them was my own sin"; "love of evil for evil's sake." Perverse imitation of divine liberty/power; "friendship all unfriendly" (social dimension of sin).
- "Evil was nothing but a privation of good." Soul fornicates when turned from the higher Good toward lower goods or the turning itself. Pride, ambition, curiosity, etc., as masks for the one true Good.

**Conversion, Divided Will, and the Mediator**:
- Neo-Platonism (Plotinus) provided intellectual ascent and vision of the One but could not loose the "fetters of incontinence."
- Stories of others' conversions (Victorinus' humble submission, garden at Treves) create intolerable tension.
- "Tolle, lege" and the Pauline text effect the "radical shift in mood and will." Two stages: release from incontinence/pride; slower growth in understanding and baptismal faith.
- Necessity of the God-Man Mediator (Christ) for true return; contemplation alone insufficient.

**Time, Creation, and Hermeneutics (Books 11–13)**:
- Time is not in God (eternity) but creaturely: "What then is time? If no one asks me, I know; if I wish to explain it to one that asketh, I know not."
- Distention of the soul: past present in memory, present in attention ("a present of things present"), future in expectation.
- Creation ex nihilo, co-temporal with time; no "before" creation in time.
- Rich allegorical reading of Genesis 1 alongside literal (e.g., firmament = Scripture; "increase and multiply" = spiritual fecundity). "The Creator is the Redeemer!"

**Philosophy of History and the Two Cities** (fully developed in *City of God*, c. 413–426 CE):
- Personal pilgrimage as microcosm of the drama of the [[Concepts/Two Cities (Augustine)|Two Cities]]: earthly city (love of self to contempt of God) vs. City of God (love of God to contempt of self). Grace shapes destinies of societies as well as souls.
- Written in response to Alaric's sack of Rome (410 CE) and pagan charges that Christianity caused Rome's fall. The 22 books range from defending against pagan religion (Books I–V) and Greek philosophy/Neoplatonism (Books VI–X) to a comprehensive philosophy of history and eschatology (Books XI–XXII).
- **Supreme Good = Peace**: Book XIX's argument that all philosophical attempts to locate happiness within this life fail; the supreme good is "peace in life everlasting." Augustine defines peace as [[Concepts/Tranquillitas Ordinis - Peace as Order (Augustine)|pax omnium rerum tranquillitas ordinis]] — the tranquillity of order at every scale of existence, from the body's organs to the heavenly city's fellowship in God. Even wars are waged in pursuit of peace.
- **The Universal Way**: Books VIII–X engage Plotinus and Porphyry (the "fiercest enemy of Christians") directly. Porphyry admitted he had not found a *via universalis* for soul liberation. Augustine: Christ is this universal Way — "As God, he is the goal; as man, he is the way." The Platonists saw the destination from a hilltop but could not find the road; their pride barred them from accepting the humble Mediator. See [[Concepts/Universal Way of Salvation (Augustine)]].
- **Rome and Providence**: God used Rome's genuine civic virtues (love of honor, justice, discipline) instrumentally and rewarded them with temporal empire — but Rome never worshipped the true God and therefore never achieved true justice. "Remove justice, and what are kingdoms but gangs of criminals on a large scale?" Rome's true fulfillment is in becoming part of the City of God.
- **Bodily resurrection and vision of God**: Book XXII. The eternal Sabbath as the seventh age — "There we shall rest and see; we shall see and love; we shall love and we shall praise. Behold what will be, in the end, without end."

**Influence**:
- Shaped medieval piety, doctrine, mysticism for 1000+ years. Appealed to by Reformers (evangelical grace) and by Catholic tradition (authority, sacraments, grace perfecting nature).
- Psychology: depth analyses of will/motives foundational for later Western conceptions of self (existentialism, depth psychology).
- "Christian Socratism": reflective thought must issue in practical consequence and love.

## Relevance to the Vault

Adds the pivotal patristic voice that bridges and elevates the classical cluster into Christian scholasticism (Aquinas) while providing unmatched models of honest self-examination, memory work, and grace-enabled transformation.

- **To [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]] and the Summa sources**: Primary patristic authority. "Even Aquinas is more of an Augustinian at heart." Supplies the experiential ground (divided will healed by grace, memory/intellect/will as *imago Dei*, evil as privation, restless heart → beatific rest) for the systematic *quaestio* synthesis. Trinitarian analogy in the mind directly from Augustinian psychology. "Grace does not destroy nature but perfects it" is the hinge. Bidirectional: Confessions illuminates the *Summa*; *Summa* systematizes Augustinian themes.
- **To [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] and Neo-Platonism**: Explicit "conversion to Neo-Platonism" in Book 7 as necessary intellectual step (immateriality, ecstasy, One beyond) but insufficient without Christ/Mediator and transforming grace. Shared language (privation of evil, ascent/return/likeness, contemplation) is transposed: Plotinian henosis vs. Christian beatific vision + bodily resurrection + ecclesial life. Strengthens Plotinus as "Plato redivivus" while showing its limit and Christian fulfillment. Wallis ([[Sources/Neoplatonism - R. T. Wallis (1972)]]) adds that Augustine read Plotinus "through Porphyrian eyes" — [[Thinkers/Porphyry]], the school's chief anti-Christian polemicist, was ironically its main conduit to the Latin West — and that Augustine's grace-over-ascent argument structurally parallels [[Thinkers/Iamblichus]]'s case against self-sufficient contemplation (see [[Contradictions/Plotinus vs Iamblichus - Contemplation vs Theurgy]]).
- **To [[Concepts/Eudaimonia]]**: "Restless is our heart until it comes to rest in you" as the Christian answer/elevation — activity of the soul finds its true end and rest only in the uncreated Good (God), via grace. Complements and completes Aristotle (natural happiness imperfect; supernatural beatitude perfects it).
- **To [[Concepts/Ataraxia]] and [[Concepts/Return of the Soul (Epistrophe)]]**: Three (or more) paths to tranquility/return contrasted and enriched: Epicurean prudent pleasure/withdrawal, Stoic control/equanimity, Plotinian contemplation/henosis, vs. Augustinian pilgrimage of grace, confession, and rest in God. "Return" now personal, historical, sacramental, and dependent on the Mediator rather than pure dialectic.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- No major contradictions with vault's prior classical sources. Provides personal narrative and psychological depth that complements systematic works: aligns with Aquinas on grace/nature, *imago Dei* (memory/intellect/will), beatific vision as rest, privation of evil; engages Plotinus directly (Book VII conversion and ecstasy as real but incomplete without Mediator); contrasts Epicurean/Lucretian materialism and mortalism, Stoic self-sufficiency, Pyrrhonian suspension (by offering grace-enabled resolution rather than ataraxia via physics or epoché). Evil as privation is shared with Plotinus but developed experientially.
- *City of God*: Augustine's late **predestination** teaching (developed sharply against Pelagius) implies most humans are justly damned; his allowance of coercive force against Donatist heretics sits in tension with his own pilgrim-city theology. These are tensions within Augustine himself, not with the vault's prior sources.
- Open: *Enchiridion* for a concise creed/prayer summary. Deeper dives into the Pelagian controversy (will/grace), the *De Trinitate* (Trinitarian psychology), or anti-Donatist writings. Potential for "Grace and Nature across Traditions" synthesis. Just war as a dedicated concept page would extend the political theology thread.
