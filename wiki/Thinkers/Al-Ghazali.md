# Al-Ghazali (Abu Hamid al-Ghazali)

> Abu Hamid Muhammad ibn Muhammad al-Tusi al-Ghazali (1058–1111 CE), the most influential religious intellectual of medieval Sunni Islam — jurist (*faqih*), Ash'arite theologian (*mutakallim*), and ultimately Sufi mystic. Author of *Tahafut al-Falasifa* (The Incoherence of the Philosophers), the most rigorous theological critique of the Islamic Aristotelians, which provoked [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]'s point-by-point reply, the *Tahafut al-Tahafut*.

## Life and Context

Born in Tus (northeast Persia). Studied at the great madrasa of Nishapur under al-Juwayni, the leading Ash'arite theologian of the day. In 1091, on the patronage of the Seljuk vizier Nizam al-Mulk, he became professor of law at the prestigious Nizamiyya college in Baghdad. During this Baghdad period (1091–1095) he wrote the *Tahafut* and its companion works: *Maqasid al-Falasifa* (an exposition of the philosophers' aims, written as a prelude to refuting them), *Mi'yar al-'Ilm* (an exposition of Avicennan logic, which he regarded as a religiously neutral tool he urged theologians to adopt), and *Al-Iqtisad fi al-I'tiqad* (Moderation in Belief, the constructive Ash'arite sequel).

In 1095 a spiritual crisis — partly a recognition that worldly glory, not faith, had driven his career — led him to abandon his chair, leave Baghdad, and live for some eleven years as a wandering ascetic, composing his magnum opus *Ihya' 'Ulum al-Din* (The Revivification of the Religious Sciences). He returned to teaching at Nishapur and Tus, where he died in 1111. By his own account (in his autobiography) he had also passed through a period of radical **skepticism**, distrusting first the senses (the star that looks the size of a coin) and then reason itself, until "in His mercy God restored to him his faith in reason" — a trajectory often compared to [[Thinkers/Augustine]]'s and, structurally, to [[Concepts/Cogito (Descartes)|Descartes' methodical doubt]].

## Intellectual Formation: Ash'arism

In law al-Ghazali was a Shafi'ite; in *kalam*, an **Ash'arite**. Ash'arite theology supplied the metaphysical engine of the *Tahafut*: a world of transient atoms and accidents continuously and directly created by God, in which what we call natural causation is only the customary, divinely-decreed concomitance of events — there is **no necessary causal connection between created things**. This [[Concepts/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation (Ghazali and Ibn Rushd)|occasionalism]] makes miracles unproblematic: there are no fixed natures for a miracle to violate.

## The *Tahafut*: Method and Targets

His targets are al-Farabi and, above all, **Avicenna** (Ibn Sina), as the most reliable Islamic exponents of Aristotle — though their Aristotle is overlaid with Neoplatonic emanation. The animating charge: the philosophers' God produces the world by the *necessity of His essence*, as the sun emits light; so conceived, God is denuded of will, power, life, and knowledge, and "is not the God of the Qur'an." At issue is never God's existence but the **nature of the godhead** — voluntary agency vs. natural necessity.

Crucially, the *Tahafut* is **purely destructive**: "We have named the book *The Incoherence of the Philosophers*, not *The Introduction to Truth*." He argues "as one who demands and denies, not as one who claims and affirms," marshalling now Mu'tazilite, now other sectarian premises against the philosophers without committing to any. The unifying epistemic weapon is a [[Concepts/Criterion of Demonstrability (Ghazali)|criterion of demonstration]]: scriptural claims stand literally unless the philosophers can *demonstrate* (to the standard of their own logic) that the literal sense is impossible — and on the contested metaphysical points, he argues, they cannot.

## The Three Condemned Doctrines

Of the twenty doctrines refuted, three are condemned not as mere innovation but as outright unbelief (*kufr*), their holders liable to the death penalty:
1. The **eternity of the world** (Disc. 1) — see [[Concepts/Eternal Will and Temporal Creation (Ghazali)]].
2. That **God knows only universals**, not temporal particulars (Disc. 13) — see [[Concepts/God's Knowledge of Particulars (Ghazali)]].
3. The **denial of bodily resurrection** (Disc. 20) — see [[Concepts/Bodily Resurrection and the Immaterial Soul (Ghazali)]].

## Influence and Reception

The *Tahafut* put Islamic philosophy permanently on the defensive, yet — because to refute the *falasifa* al-Ghazali first had to expound them clearly — it also spread their ideas and bound *kalam* thereafter to engagement with philosophy. Its most famous answer is [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]'s *Tahafut al-Tahafut* ([[Sources/Incoherence of the Incoherence - Ibn Rushd]]), which accuses al-Ghazali of sophistry and equivocation (especially on "eternal will") and charges that denying causal necessity destroys intellect and science. Al-Ghazali's occasionalist datum — that we observe only concomitance, never necessity — anticipates, by a very different route, [[Thinkers/Hume]]'s analysis of [[Concepts/Necessary Connexion and the Two Definitions of Cause (Hume)|constant conjunction]].
