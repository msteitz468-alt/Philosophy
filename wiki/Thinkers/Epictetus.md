# Epictetus

> Stoic philosopher (c. AD 50–135), originally a slave, teacher in Rome and Nicopolis. His teachings preserved by student Arrian in the *Discourses* and *Enchiridion* (*Handbook*). Focuses on practical ethics: the dichotomy between what is in our power (*eph' hēmin*, prohairesis – rational choice/judgment) and what is not (externals like body, property, reputation, fate). Philosophy as daily discipline, proper use of impressions, acceptance of role in the divine order, equanimity, and living according to nature.

## Key Ideas from Sources
- **Core Division (Enchiridion)**: Things in our power (thought, impulse, will to get/avoid – our own doing) are free/unhindered. Things not in our power (body, property, reputation, office – not our doing) are weak/servile. Desire only what is in power; aversion only to what is in power.
- **Impressions and Assent**: Not things disturb us, but judgments about them. Examine impressions before assenting.
- **Role and Duty**: Play the role assigned by nature/God without complaint. The philosopher's identity is to live and speak in alignment with reason.
- **Indifference and Equanimity**: Treat externals as indifferent. Face death, loss, fortune with the attitude of a returning traveler or participant in life's festival.
- **Divine Order**: Self as instrument of God; obedience to universal nature/reason. God and providence.
- **Fragments and Golden Sayings**: Short axioms on virtue vs fortune, self-discipline, rational faculty, speech/silence, praise of divine, freedom from externals, duty of the philosopher.

The Enchiridion is a compressed practical manual; Golden Sayings and Fragments are gnomic teachings.

## The Discourses (Diatribai, recorded by Arrian — Four Books)

The full *Discourses* (ingested 2026-07-01 via the Carter/Higginson translation, [[Sources/The Works of Epictetus - Discourses, Enchiridion, and Fragments (Carter-Higginson)]]) supply the argumentative machinery that the *Enchiridion* only summarizes. They are verbatim classroom conversations, in the blunt diatribe register — Epictetus rebukes complacent students and mocks logic-chopping divorced from conduct. The system:

- **Prohairesis is the whole of ethics.** Only the faculty of choice is "our own"; body, property, reputation, even our limbs are "another's" and morally indifferent. "The essence of good and evil is a certain disposition of the will" (I.29). See [[Concepts/Prohairesis (Moral Purpose)]].
- **The right use of impressions** (*phantasiai*) is the one power Zeus left wholly in our hands; philosophy lives in the moment before assent. Logic matters only as the assay of true vs. false appearance, never for its own sake. See [[Concepts/Proper Use of Impressions and the Discipline of Assent (Epictetus)]].
- **Philosophy begins in disagreement**: shared preconceptions (*prolēpseis*) are correct in the abstract but clash in application, driving the search for a criterion (II.11). See [[Concepts/Preconceptions (Prolepseis) (Epictetus)]].
- **Three fields of training** (III.2), in order of urgency — desire, action/duty, assent — worked by *askēsis* (counter-habit). The primary-text root of Hadot's three disciplines. See [[Concepts/The Three Fields of Training - Desire, Action, and Assent (Epictetus)]].
- **Kinship with God** (*sungeneia*): we are portions of the divine reason, watched by an inner *daemon*; dignity, fearlessness, and glad piety all follow (I.3, I.14, II.8). See [[Concepts/Kinship with God (Epictetus)]].
- **The Cynic ideal** (III.22): the true Cynic as *kataskopos*, God's possessionless scout sent to show mankind where good and evil lie. See [[Concepts/The Cynic Ideal - Scout of Zeus (Epictetus)]].
- **Freedom** (IV.1, the longest discourse): not legal status but the will's independence — wanting only what is up to us, so nothing can enslave us; exemplified by Socrates and Diogenes; identical with willing what happens. See [[Concepts/Stoic Freedom (Eleutheria) (Epictetus)]].

## Skeptical Counterpoint (Sextus Empiricus / Pyrrhonism)

Sextus opens the *Outlines* by naming "the followers of Aristotle and Epicurus, the Stoics, and certain others" as the central *Dogmatists* who claim to have found the truth. Pyrrhonian Skepticism (the third philosophy) generates equipollent oppositions via the modes to reach epoché (neither affirm nor deny the non-evident) and ataraxia.

Direct contrasts with Epictetus/Stoicism:
- Both target impressions and equanimity, but Stoic: examine impressions then *assent* only to what is in our power (*prohairesis*), accept role in divine order. Pyrrhonian: *suspend* on non-evident (including the real nature of impressions or providence); equanimity from dropping the layer of belief.
- Dichotomy of control assumes a knowable division between "in power" and externals; the modes (esp. relativity, circumstances, customs) relativize or suspend even that.
- "Play the role assigned" and service to God/providence vs. live by appearances + handed-down customs/laws *without* dogmatic commitment to their non-evident grounding.
- Ataraxia/equanimity via active acceptance/endurance vs. via suspension (ataraxia "follows by chance" on epoché, per Apelles anecdote).

See new [[Sources/Outlines of Pyrrhonism - Sextus Empiricus]], [[Thinkers/Sextus Empiricus]], [[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]], [[Concepts/Equipollence (Isosthenia) and the Modes]]. This completes the Hellenistic ethics map (Stoic duty/control, Epicurean prudent withdrawal/physics, Pyrrhonian search/suspension) and supplies methodological tools for the wiki (equipollence generation before synthesis; suspension on underdetermined claims; "chronicler" reporting of appearances). Enriches daily practice and examined life themes.

## Modern Reception (Stockdale; Ryan Holiday)

Epictetus is the philosopher of the modern practical-Stoicism revival. Admiral **James Stockdale**, shot down over North Vietnam in 1965, said to himself as he parachuted, *"I'm leaving the world of technology and entering the world of Epictetus"* — and credited the *Enchiridion*'s dichotomy of control with sustaining him through seven and a half years of imprisonment and torture (recorded in *Courage Under Fire: Testing Epictetus's Doctrines in a Laboratory of Human Behavior*). Ryan Holiday's *The Obstacle Is the Way* (see [[Sources/The Obstacle Is the Way - Ryan Holiday (2014)]]) organizes its whole program around Epictetus's **three *topoi*** (via Hadot) as Perception, Action, and Will (see [[Concepts/The Three Disciplines - Perception, Action, and Will (Hadot)]]), foregrounds *ta eph'hēmin, ta ouk eph'hēmin* and its Serenity-Prayer descendant (see [[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)]]), and quotes Epictetus's *"persist and resist"* for the discipline of action. Holiday was first pointed to Epictetus in college by Dr. Drew Pinsky. See [[Thinkers/Ryan Holiday]].
