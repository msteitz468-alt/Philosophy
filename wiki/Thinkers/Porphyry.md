# Porphyry

> Plotinus's pupil, biographer, and editor (born Tyre c. 232, d. early 4th c.) — the systematizer and popularizer whose version of Neoplatonism dominated the Latin West, and the school's sharpest anti-Christian polemicist.

## Summary

Educated under the critic Longinus before joining [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] in Rome, Porphyry arranged his master's 54 treatises into the six *Enneads* and wrote the biography that is our only substantial source for Plotinus's life. His own works include the *Sentences* (a digest of Plotinian metaphysics), the *Isagoge* (the introduction to Aristotle's logic that became a medieval standard — part of his broader rehabilitation of Aristotle as the authority in logic), *De Abstinentia*, the *Letter to Anebo*, *De Regressu Animae*, and the lost *Against the Christians*. Wallis treats him and Amelius as the first post-Plotinian phase of [[Schools/Neoplatonism]].

## Key Positions (Wallis)

- **Telescoping the Hypostases.** Where Plotinus keeps the levels distinct, Porphyry systematically blurs them — treating Soul as simply "Intelligible," collapsing lower realities toward illusion: "The soul is present 'in' Intelligence when she exercises intuitive thought and 'in' her own sphere when she reasons discursively" (*Sent.* 3, 27). Iamblichus attacked this vehemently. The anonymous *Parmenides* commentary (plausibly Porphyrian) pushes negative theology to "an ineffable preconception that represents the One in silence."
- **Rationalist critique of theurgy.** In the *Letter to Anebo* he argues ritual "works only through the sympathy governing the sensible cosmos and cannot therefore affect the higher souls of the gods" (II.5d); in *De Regressu Animae*, theurgy at best purifies the pneumatic envelope of the lower soul, cannot raise the soul to the Intelligible order, is unnecessary for the philosopher, and is open to abuse. This is one pole of [[Contradictions/Plotinus vs Iamblichus - Contemplation vs Theurgy]].
- **Ethical emphasis and moderate asceticism** (*De Abstinentia*), more scholastic than Plotinus but still insufficiently systematic to become the later school's authority — that role fell to [[Thinkers/Iamblichus]].
- **Anti-Christian polemic** whose seriousness forced Christian replies for a century; [[Thinkers/Augustine]] read Plotinus largely "through Porphyrian eyes," making Porphyry the main conduit of Neoplatonism to the Latin West.
- **Debt to [[Thinkers/Numenius of Apamea]].** Porphyry "follows the teachings of Numenius" on the soul's pre-incarnational struggle with the material demons of the West (Numenius fr. 62b: "we would be very surprised if Porphyry asserted anything differing from Numenius"), and drew on Numenius's *Cave of the Nymphs* allegory for his own treatise of that name — one channel by which Middle-Platonic doctrine entered the school.
