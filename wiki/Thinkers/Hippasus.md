# Hippasus of Metapontum
> The early Pythagorean who turns the "Pythagoras the scientist" question into a real historical problem: the first figure to whom genuine mathematics, the discovery of incommensurability, and a quantitative acoustic experiment can be securely attached.

## Summary
Hippasus (early-to-mid 5th c. BC) is the pivot of the modern debate over Pythagorean science. Both [[Sources/Lore and Science in Ancient Pythagoreanism - Walter Burkert (1972)|Burkert]] and [[Sources/Pythagoras and the Early Pythagoreans - Leonid Zhmud (2012)|Zhmud]] agree that real numerical/scientific work is documented for Hippasus; they disagree sharply about what this implies for [[Thinkers/Pythagoras]] himself. For **Burkert**, Hippasus is where the genuine science *begins* — the *mathematici* who carried it were, on the earliest evidence, the contested junior strand who "derive not from Pythagoras but from Hippasus." For **Zhmud**, Hippasus is not a starting point ex nihilo but the immediate heir of a mathematical tradition begun by Pythagoras: "Who then was the predecessor of Hippasus...? We have no more suitable candidate than Pythagoras" — so "in acknowledging Hippasus, we therefore ought to acknowledge Pythagoras." See [[Contradictions/Pythagoras the Scientist vs the Religious Charismatic]].

## Genuine Contributions (Zhmud's reconstruction)
**Incommensurability / the irrational** (Zhmud's central attribution):
- Hippasus discovered the incommensurability of the diagonal of the square (or the side and diagonal of the pentagon), early-to-mid 5th c. The premises were already in place: the theory of even and odd (Euclid IX.21–34), indirect proof (*reductio*), and *pythmenes* (least numbers).
- Zhmud rejects the **late dating** (Knorr's 430s) and rejects the "foundation crisis" narrative: there is no evidence the discovery shattered "all things are number," and Aristoxenus (fr. 23) reports the number-loving Pythagoreans untroubled. See the crisis revision on [[Concepts/Number as the Essence of Reality (Pythagoras)]].

**The disc experiment** (Aristoxenus fr. 90):
- Hippasus made four bronze discs of equal diameter but thicknesses in the ratios 4:3, 3:2, 2:1; struck, they sounded the concords. Zhmud calls this "the first experiment to give a true mathematical expression of a physical law" — reproducible, quantitative, with the variable isolated. (It is genuine, sharply unlike the false **smithy/hammer legend** — a fabrication, probably by Nicomachus — which both Zhmud and Burkert reject.)
- "Too complex to be a first attempt," it confirmed concord-ratios Pythagoras had already found by experiment on a string. See [[Concepts/Number as the Essence of Reality (Pythagoras)]].

**Other**: the ranking of concords by "similars/dissimilars" (Eudemus fr. 142, the "number nine"); the construction of the **dodecahedron inscribed in a sphere**; the proportions theory; the recognition that the octave cannot be divided into two equal parts (linked to irrationality). His *archē* was **fire**.

## The Legend and the *arrhētos* Pun
Hippasus is said to have drowned at sea / been expelled for disclosing a secret (variously the incommensurable, or the dodecahedron-in-sphere). Zhmud argues the six surviving versions all attach to Hippasus precisely because of the double meaning of **ἄρρητος** ("irrational" / "unspeakable, secret"): the mathematical term generated the betrayal legend. He is "not simply a peg on which to hang anonymous discoveries" but a real philosopher-scientist known to Aristotle, Theophrastus, Aristoxenus, and Eudemus. His omission from Proclus' catalogue of geometers follows from the later habit of re-crediting his discoveries to Pythagoras himself.

## On the Cylonian Conflict
Zhmud reinterprets the dark portrait of Hippasus politically: it stems "not... because of his betrayal of the school's mathematical 'secrets', but... his very real political rivalry" within the Pythagorean *hetairia* during the [[Schools/Pythagoreanism|Crotonian]] troubles, where Hippasus appears as an insider rival (alongside Cylon).

## The Huffman Volume (2014): Pioneer of Harmonics
In [[Sources/A History of Pythagoreanism - Carl A. Huffman (ed.) (2014)]], Andrew Barker's harmonics chapter makes Hippasus "**the real pioneer**" of [[Concepts/Pythagorean Harmonics|Pythagorean harmonics]] — Pythagoras "contributed nothing demonstrable" and the blacksmith story is "pure fiction." Oliver Primavesi's Aristotle chapter ties the concord ratios to Hippasus' experiment with **four bronze discs of thicknesses 12:9:8:6** (octave 2:1, fifth 3:2, fourth 4:3). The sequence is then: Hippasus (discovery/experiment) → [[Thinkers/Philolaus]] (metaphysical integration) → [[Thinkers/Archytas]] (mathematical perfection). On the mathematics, Reviel Netz's chapter is more cautious than Zhmud, treating even the incommensurability attribution as among the *undecidable* ascriptions (see [[Concepts/Pythagorean Mathematics]]).
