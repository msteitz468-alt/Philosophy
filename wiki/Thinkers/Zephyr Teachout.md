# Zephyr Teachout
> American law professor and political activist whose central thesis is that corporate monopoly is a form of political tyranny, not merely an economic inefficiency.

## Summary

Teachout is a Fordham law professor, former Democratic congressional candidate, and leading intellectual of the modern anti-monopoly movement. Her work revives and updates the Brandeisian tradition — Louis Brandeis's warning that concentrated private power is incompatible with political democracy — and extends it to the platform economy of the 21st century.

Her philosophical contribution is to reframe the antitrust debate from an *economic* question (do consumers get low prices?) to a *political* one (who governs?). Monopolies, on her account, are not merely market distortions — they are a new form of quasi-governmental authority that taxes, regulates, surveils, and punishes without democratic accountability. The appropriate analogy is not to a large firm but to a *private Leviathan*.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Monopoly is tyranny.** When a corporation has the power to set the terms of an interaction — controlling employment, information, food supply, healthcare — it has crossed from "economic actor" to "governing entity." This is a political category mistake that mainstream economics obscures by treating firms as profit-maximizers existing outside politics.
- **The consumer welfare standard is the key error.** The doctrine, dominant since roughly 1981 (the Chicago School ascendancy), holds that antitrust law should only intervene when consumers face higher prices. Teachout argues this ignores wage suppression, political capture, supplier exploitation, and democratic subversion — all harms that occur while prices may remain low.
- **Private government as the diagnosis.** Big Tech, Big Ag, Big Finance, and Big Healthcare each constitute what she calls "private governments": entities that *regulate* — i.e., issue binding rules with no democratic input — over workers, suppliers, and citizens.
- **Anti-monopoly is a broad philosophy**, not just merger law. It encompasses campaign finance reform, forced arbitration bans, public utility regulation, co-op enabling laws, and labor rights. All are tools to disperse concentrated power.
- **Ethical consumerism is a trap.** The injunction to "vote with your wallet" reinforces corporate authority by framing the citizen as consumer. The appropriate response to monopoly is political organization and legislative demand, not boycotts.
- **Strategy:** Stop mergers, break up existing monopolies, pass laws overturning post-1981 Supreme Court precedent, empower state attorneys general, build a mass anti-monopoly movement analogous to the Progressive Era trust-busting movements.

## Intellectual Lineage

- **Louis Brandeis** — Teachout explicitly inherits the "curse of bigness" thesis: Brandeis argued in the early 20th century that concentrated power corrupts character and institution alike; "absolute power leads to excesses and to weakness."
- **William O. Douglas** (Supreme Court) — epigraph: "all power tends to develop into a government in itself."
- **James Madison / Federalist No. 10** — Teachout updates Madison's faction theory: the danger Madison identified in political factions now operates through economic monopolies.
- **[[Aristotle]]** — Teachout invokes Aristotle's *political animal* thesis: humans concentrate and wield power; this is universal, and unchecked, leads to tyranny. The same analysis that Aristotle applied to political tyrants applies to monopolists.
- **Hannah Arendt** — power analysis; the distinction between legitimate authority and domination.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Conflicts with [[Thinkers/James M. Buchanan]] on the source of monopoly: Buchanan's public choice theory argues that monopoly is *created* by government (regulatory capture, rent-seeking). Teachout inverts this: deregulation ideology (Chicago School) *enabled* monopoly by disarming antitrust. See [[Contradictions/Teachout vs Buchanan — Private vs Government Sources of Monopoly]].
- > [!warning] Tension with [[Thinkers/Mancur Olson]]: Teachout calls for a mass anti-monopoly movement, but Olson's collective action theory predicts that diffuse public interests will be systematically outorganized by concentrated corporate interests. Teachout's response (historical examples: trust-busting, labor movement) is empirical but doesn't fully address the logic.
- > [!warning] Question: Is Teachout's framework republican (civic tradition against domination) or socialist (critique of capital)? She denies being anti-market — she wants decentralized competitive markets — but her "moral economy" chapter blurs the line.
