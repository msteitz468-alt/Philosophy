# John McDowell
> A prominent contemporary philosopher known for his synthesis of analytic philosophy and German idealism, particularly his works on the conceptual nature of experience and his reading of Wittgenstein's rule-following remarks.

## Biography and Historical Context
John McDowell (born 1942) is a South African philosopher who taught at University College Oxford before moving to the University of Pittsburgh. His most influential work, *Mind and World* (1994), draws on Immanuel Kant and Wilfrid Sellars to resolve the epistemological tension between the "space of reasons" (rational justification) and the "space of nature" (empirical causes). He argues that our sensory experiences are already conceptualized, allowing nature to exert a rational constraint on our beliefs without falling into Sellars's "Myth of the Given."

## Core Contributions

### Rule-Following and Semantic Realism
McDowell has written extensively on Wittgenstein's rule-following remarks (PI 201). He rejects Saul Kripke's skeptical reading (Kripkean "sceptical paradox"), arguing that grasping a rule is not an interpretation that stands between the rule and our action. Instead, grasping a rule is a practice-embedded ability. In his contribution to *Wittgenstein's Philosophical Investigations: A Critical Guide* (2010), McDowell defends the claim that understanding is a definite state, arguing against Warren Goldfarb's view that Wittgenstein rejects the idea of understanding-states. McDowell clarifies that understanding is not a "mental mechanism" or an occult inner medium, but a genuine state characterized by our shared, primitive propensity to find certain applications of rules natural.
