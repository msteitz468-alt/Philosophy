# Gordon Tullock
> "The organized pressure group arises because differential advantages are expected to be secured through the political process..."

## Summary

Gordon Tullock (1922–2014) was an American economist and legal scholar, recognized as one of the founders of **public choice theory** and a pioneer in **law and economics**. Despite having no formal training in economics (his degree was in law), Tullock made foundational contributions to economics, most notably the concept of **rent-seeking** and the choice-theoretic modeling of voting and bureaucracies.

Alongside James M. Buchanan, Tullock co-authored *The Calculus of Consent* (1962), which established the constitutional economics subfield. Tullock's intellectual style is characterized by a radical, unsentimental application of individual self-interest to areas traditionally treated with normative reverence, such as judicial systems, bureaucracy, and public charity.

## Life and Context

Tullock was born in Rockford, Illinois, in 1922. He studied at the University of Chicago, earning a JD in 1947. He served in the U.S. Foreign Service in China and Korea, where he taught himself Chinese and began to develop his realistic view of bureaucratic institutions. 

After leaving the Foreign Service, Tullock began his academic career, teaching at the University of South Carolina, the University of Virginia, Virginia Tech, George Mason University, and the University of Arizona. He co-founded the Public Choice Society and the journal *Public Choice* (originally called *Papers on Non-Market Decision Making*).

## Key Philosophical and Economic Positions

### Rent-Seeking and the Social Costs of Monopoly

Tullock's most famous paper, "The Welfare Costs of Tariffs, Monopolies, and Theft" (1967), introduced the concept that later came to be known as **rent-seeking** (a term coined by Anne Krueger in 1974). Tullock showed that the social cost of government-granted monopolies or protective tariffs is not just the allocative deadweight loss (Harberger triangle). Rather, the true social cost includes the resources wasted by interest groups lobbying, campaign contributing, and competing to *obtain* and *defend* those monopolies (the "Tullock rectangle"). Rent-seeking represents a massive, negative-sum drain on societal output.

### Implicit Logrolling and Vote-Trading

Tullock formalized the analysis of logrolling (vote-trading) in *The Calculus of Consent*. He showed that vote-trading is the natural mechanism through which intense minority preferences are registered in a democratic system. However, he also showed that when local projects are funded by general taxation, majority logrolling leads to systemic overinvestment in local public works (pork-barrel spending) and overextension of the public budget. See [[Concepts/Logrolling and Vote-Trading (Buchanan-Tullock)]].

### Bureaucratic Self-Interest

In *The Politics of Bureaucracy* (1965), Tullock challenged the traditional view (championed by Max Weber and Woodrow Wilson) that civil servants are selfless public administrators. Tullock modeled bureaucrats as utility-maximizing individuals who seek to maximize their own budget, power, and prestige. This budget-maximizing behavior leads to over-provision of public services and systemic bureaucratic inefficiency.

### The Economics of Legal Systems

Tullock applied economic models to the legal system, arguing that the adversarial system (common law) wastes enormous resources compared to inquisitorial systems (civil law) because lawyers engage in zero-sum expenditures to influence juries, rather than focusing on establishing truth.

### The Social Dilemma

In *The Social Dilemma: Of Autocracy, Revolution, Coup d’Etat, and War* (1974), Tullock analyzed revolutions and dictatorships. He showed that most revolutions are not driven by ideological uprisings of the masses, but are rather coordinated coups led by self-interested elites seeking to capture the state's apparatus for their own private gain.

## Key Works

- *The Calculus of Consent: Logical Foundations of Constitutional Democracy* (1962, with James M. Buchanan)
- *The Politics of Bureaucracy* (1965)
- *The Social Dilemma: Of Autocracy, Revolution, Coup d’Etat, and War* (1974)
- *The Vote Motive* (1976)

## Influence and Legacy

Tullock's work profoundly reshaped political science and economics, introducing the concept of rent-seeking and the choice-theoretic model of interest groups. He is widely considered one of the most innovative and iconoclastic economists of the 20th century, whose work was frequently cited in the awarding of the Nobel Prize to James M. Buchanan.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Tullock's radical skepticism regarding human motivations led him to reject most explanations of political behavior based on ideals, duty, or civic virtue. Critics argue his model fails to explain why citizens vote at all (the paradox of voting) or why bureaucrats frequently act against their budget-maximizing interests to enforce public rules.
- Rent-seeking models assume that groups compete symmetrically for political rents, but critics argue that capital and concentrated business interests have systemic organizational advantages over labor and consumer groups.
