# Thomas Aquinas

> Italian Dominican friar, priest, philosopher, and theologian (1225–1274), Doctor of the Church ("Angelic Doctor"). Author of the *Summa Theologica* (his unfinished *magnum opus*), *Summa contra Gentiles*, commentaries on Aristotle (including the *Nicomachean Ethics*), and numerous other works. Systematically synthesized Aristotelian philosophy (reason, nature, virtue, eudaimonia) with Christian revelation, patristic theology (especially Augustine), and Scripture into a comprehensive scholastic framework. Famous for "grace does not destroy nature but perfects it"; natural law as participation in eternal law; the beatific vision as ultimate human happiness; the *quaestio* method of rigorous disputation and synthesis.

## Key Contributions from Part I (QQ. 75-102) — The Treatise on Man

*Source: `raw/Thomas Aquinas - Summa Theologica.txt` (Dominican Province, 2nd ed., London, 1932). See [[Sources/Summa Theologica, Part I (QQ. 75-102) - Thomas Aquinas]] for full seven-pass treatment.*

**Hylomorphism — Soul as Form of Body (Q. 75-76)**:
- The soul is not a body but the *act* of a body — the first principle of life as actualization, not a substance alongside the body.
- The human soul is *subsistent* (has per se existence) because it has per se operation (understanding without any bodily organ). Hence it can survive the body's death.
- The soul is *incorruptible*: a subsistent form cannot be separated from itself; no contrariety in the intellect; natural desire for perpetual existence cannot be in vain.
- The soul is the *form of the body* (Aristotle, De Anima II.2): refutes Plato (soul = man), Averroes (one intellect for all), and the motor view (intellect merely drives body from outside). Socrates understands — therefore the intellect is Socrates' own form. The primary source for the positions being refuted is now available: [[Sources/Incoherence of the Incoherence - Ibn Rushd]] (Averroes’ *Tahafut al-Tahafut* defends a rigorous Aristotelian line on eternity, real secondary causation, and the intellect against Ghazali’s occasionalist theology; Aquinas corrects the implications for individuation and personal immortality while retaining the underlying hylomorphism and causal realism).
- One soul per person; one soul with multiple powers (not multiple souls with different organs, as Plato held).

**Powers of the Soul — Five Genera and Taxonomy (QQ. 77-79)**:
- Powers: vegetative (nutrition, growth, generation), sensitive (five exterior senses + four interior), appetitive (concupiscible + irascible), locomotive, intellectual.
- Intellect as *passive* (possible intellect = tabula rasa) and *active* (abstracts intelligible species from phantasms) — both powers of the soul, not a separate divine intellect (contra Averroes).
- **Synderesis**: the natural, indemonstrable habit of the first practical principles — "good is to be done, evil avoided." The subjective foundation of natural law.
- **Conscience**: application of moral knowledge to particular acts (not a power but an act).

**Will and Free Choice (QQ. 82-83)**:
- The will necessarily desires happiness (last end) — cannot be coerced, but naturally ordered.
- The will is free in all particular choices: reason can go either way in contingent matters.
- Free choice = *intellectual appetite* — "desire proceeding from counsel." The will's act of choosing, not a separate power.
- God moves the will but does not destroy freedom — operates per each thing's own nature.

**Theory of Abstraction (QQ. 84-86)**:
- The soul knows material things through *intelligible species abstracted from phantasms* by the active intellect. Must always "turn to phantasms" even in act of understanding.
- Three levels of abstraction: natural philosophy (from individual matter) / mathematics (from sensible matter) / metaphysics (from all matter).
- Intelligible species = means of knowing, not the object known. The intellect reaches the real extra-mental thing through the species.

**Self-Knowledge and the Separated Soul (QQ. 87-89)**:
- The soul knows itself *by its act*, not by its essence (only God knows Himself by His essence). Self-knowledge is immediate (I perceive I understand) but universal knowledge of the soul's nature requires inquiry.
- The separated soul knows by divinely participated species (like angels), not by phantasms — a different mode but genuine knowledge. Body-union was for the soul's *good* (gives it appropriately particular knowledge for its weak intellectual level), not a prison.

**Production of Man — Soul, Body, Woman, Imago Dei (QQ. 90-93)**:
- Soul created (from nothing) by God alone — not educed from matter, not made by angels.
- Body formed from earth by God directly (for fittingness and sacramental reasons).
- Woman from man's rib: for dignity of man as principle of the race, love/bond, domestic partnership, sacramental signification. Not from head (ruling) nor foot (servitude) — from side (equality in partnership).
- **Imago Dei (Q. 93)**: Three degrees — image of creation (natural aptitude in all men), image of re-creation/grace (in the just), image of likeness/glory (in the blessed). The image is specifically in the *mind* (memory, intellect, will = Augustinian Trinitarian analogy), not the body.

**State of First Man — Original Justice, Gifts, Paradise (QQ. 94-102)**:
- Adam knew all naturally knowable things by infused species; not the beatific vision (he could have sinned); superior to our knowledge through intelligible effects without sensory noise.
- **Original justice** = supernatural gift: reason subject to God → lower powers to reason → body to soul. Loss of grace broke this entire threefold ordering (explaining the Fall's full effects).
- Man was created in grace; all virtues present in innocence (in act or habit as appropriate).
- Immortality = supernatural gift, not natural property of the body. Tree of Life = real tree with life-preserving power.
- Paradise = real corporeal place in the east; also spiritual; an appropriate abode for man's composite nature.

## Key Contributions from the Source (Summa Theologica I-II)

**Context and Method**:

- After Part I (God as exemplar, creation), Part I-II treats man as the "image of God" — an intelligent being with free will and self-movement, the principle of his own actions.
- Uses the scholastic *quaestio* format: objections (from reason, Aristotle, Scripture, authorities), *sed contra* (counter-authority), *respondeo* (Aquinas' synthesis), replies to objections. Draws on Aristotle ("the Philosopher"), Augustine, Damascene, Boethius, Scripture, and natural reason.
- Overall *Summa* structure reflects *exitus-reditus*: all things come forth from God and return to Him. I-II focuses on the *reditus* of rational creatures via free acts ordered to the last end.

**Last End and Happiness (Treatise on the Last End, QQ. 1-5)**:

- Every human action is for an end (final cause first in intention).
- Proper to rational nature (via intellect and will) to know and direct oneself to an end.
- Human acts specified morally by their end.
- There is one last end (happiness/beatitude): cannot be infinite series or multiple un-ordered ends (contradicts perfection and the nature of appetite/will).
- Not in created goods (wealth, honor, pleasure, power, virtue alone, or any finite thing) — these are incomplete or external.
- In its object/cause: the uncreated Good, God (Who alone perfectly satisfies the will by infinite goodness).
- In its essence (for man): something created in him — the attainment, possession, enjoyment, or operation of union with God.
- Specifically: an operation of the speculative intellect — "the sole contemplation of God seen in His Essence" (the beatific vision). In this life, imperfect participation (active or contemplative life); perfect and continual in heaven (as the angels).
- "We call men happy, but only as men" (echoing Aristotle's imperfect happiness in this life).

**Human Acts, Passions, Habits/Virtues**:

- Voluntary acts from deliberate will; morality from object, end, circumstances.
- Passions (concupiscible/irascible) subject to reason can be good.
- Habits: virtues (habits perfecting powers for good acts) — cardinal (prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance) and theological (faith, hope, charity, infused by grace). Gifts of the Holy Spirit, beatitudes.

**Treatise on Law (QQ. 90-108)**:

- Law: "a rule and measure of acts" (from *ligare*, to bind); pertains to reason as first principle directing to the end/common good; promulgated by competent authority (whole people or viceregent); ordered to the common good.
- Kinds: Eternal law (God's reason governing the universe); Natural law (eternal law participated in by rational creatures via "the light of natural reason whereby we discern what is good and what is evil" — first practical principle: "good is to be done and pursued, and evil avoided"); human (positive) law (derived from natural, for common good, changeable); divine law (Old Law and New Law of the Gospel, for supernatural end).
- Private persons (e.g., father of family) can make ordinances but not law properly so called.

**Treatise on Grace (QQ. 109-114)**:

- Grace necessary for the supernatural end (beatific vision) which exceeds natural powers.
- "Grace does not destroy nature but perfects it" (foundational principle).
- Justification, merit (with free will cooperating), infusion of virtues, etc.

## Augustine and the Confessions (Primary Patristic Source)

Augustine (354–430) is the dominant patristic authority cited throughout the *Summa* ("especially Augustine"). "Even Aquinas is more of an Augustinian at heart than a 'proper' Aristotelian" (Outler). The *Confessions* (c. 397–400) supplies the experiential, psychological, and narrative ground for the systematic *quaestio* synthesis of Aristotle + revelation + tradition.

Key bridges from the *Confessions*:
- Trinitarian analogy in the mind (memory/intellect/will) as *imago Dei* (Q. 93; directly Augustinian psychology from *Confessions* Book X + *De Trinitate*).
- Grace: "grace does not destroy nature but perfects it" — the experiential ground is Augustine's narrative of the divided will ("two wills at war") healed only by transforming, prevenient grace ("Give what you command and command what you will").
- Last end / beatitude: the "restless heart" finds rest only in the uncreated Good (God); beatific vision as the created operation of union — elevation of Aristotelian eudaimonia.
- Evil as privation of good (not a positive substance); interiority ("in interiore homine habitat veritas"); memory as the vast inner space in which the soul seeks and finds (or fails to find) God and self-knowledge ("I have become a question to myself").
- Original justice/sin, free choice as intellectual appetite, necessity of the God-Man Mediator, and time as distention of the soul (co-temporal with creation).

See dedicated [[Sources/Confessions - Augustine]], [[Thinkers/Augustine]], and new concepts [[Concepts/Restless Heart - Rest in God (Augustine)]], [[Concepts/Memory - Interiority and Self-Knowledge (Augustine)]], [[Concepts/Time and Eternity - Distentio Animi (Augustine)]]. The *Confessions* illuminates why grace is necessary and how nature is perfected; the *Summa* systematizes these insights into treatises on the last end, human acts, law, and grace.

**Life/Context**:

- Dominican (Order of Preachers); taught in Paris, Rome, etc.; "Dumb Ox" nickname from early silence (later eloquent). Died 1274 en route to Council of Lyon; canonized 1323. His synthesis became foundational for Catholic theology and Western philosophy (Aristotelianism baptized).
