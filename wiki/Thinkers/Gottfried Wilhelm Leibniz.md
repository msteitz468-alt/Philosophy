# Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz

> German philosopher, mathematician, logician, and diplomat (1646–1716). A key figure in early modern rationalism alongside Descartes and Spinoza. Developed the theory of the monadology, proposed the pre-established harmony of mind and body, formulated the Principle of Sufficient Reason, and argued that our universe is the "best of all possible worlds."

## Key Contributions

**The Monadology**:
- Proposed that the universe is composed of an infinite number of simple, indivisible, non-material substances called **Monads**.
- Described monads as "windowless" (having no causal interaction with each other), each acting as a mirror of the entire universe from its own perspective. See [[Concepts/Monad (Leibniz)]].

**Pre-Established Harmony**:
- Resolved the mind-body problem by asserting that God pre-programmed the actions of all monads so that they run in perfect coordination (**Pre-Established Harmony**), like two perfectly synchronized clocks, without direct interaction. See [[Concepts/Pre-Established Harmony (Leibniz)]].

**The Principle of Sufficient Reason & Optimism**:
- Formulated the **Principle of Sufficient Reason**: nothing happens without a reason why it should be so rather than otherwise.
- Argued that since God is all-powerful, all-knowing, and all-good, He chose to create the **best of all possible worlds**, resolving the problem of evil by claiming that some apparent evils are logically necessary for the greater good of the whole.

## Relevance to Vault

- **The Monist-Pluralist Metaphysical Axis**: Leibniz offers a radical pluralist alternative to Spinoza's substance monism ([Deus sive Natura](file:///home/mark/Documents/Philosophy/wiki/Concepts/Deus%20sive%20Natura%20-%20Substance%20Monism%20(Spinoza).md)) and Descartes' dualism.
- **The Great Chain of Being**: Leibniz's monadology is a major anchor in Arthur Lovejoy's history of ideas, representing the peak of the principles of continuity and gradation. See [[Sources/The Great Chain of Being - Arthur O. Lovejoy (1936)]].
- **Causality and Occasionalism**: Leibniz's pre-established harmony offers a unique resolution to the Descartes causality problem, contrasting with occasionalism and natural secondary causation.
