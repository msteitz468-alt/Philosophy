# Immanuel Kant (1724–1804)

> Prussian philosopher, central figure of the Enlightenment and German Idealism. Author of the *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781/1787), *Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals* (1785), *Critique of Practical Reason* (1788), *Critique of the Power of Judgment* (1790), *Metaphysics of Morals* (1797), and numerous essays on history, politics, religion, and education. Famous for the "Copernican revolution" in epistemology (objects conform to our cognition), the categorical imperative in ethics, and the synthesis of rationalism and empiricism. "Two things fill the mind with ever new and increasing admiration and reverence...: the starry heavens above me and the moral law within me."

## Life and Context

Born in Königsberg (then East Prussia, now Kaliningrad, Russia), Kant never left the region. Studied at the University of Königsberg; worked as a private tutor, then lecturer (1755), finally professor of logic and metaphysics (1770). Lived a highly regular, disciplined life ("the Königsberg clock"). Early works in natural science (nebular hypothesis, earthquakes); "silent decade" (1770s) leading to the *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781), which he revised in 1787. The 1780s–90s saw the critical system completed with the practical and aesthetic/judgment critiques, plus popular essays (*What is Enlightenment?*, *Idea for a Universal History*, *Perpetual Peace*) and late works on religion, anthropology, and the *Metaphysics of Morals*.

Context: Post-Leibniz/Wolff rationalism and post-Locke/Hume empiricism/skepticism; Pietism (influencing his moral rigor); Enlightenment debates on reason, freedom, religion, and progress; the "Copernican" turn responding to Hume's awakening from "dogmatic slumber" on causation (while seeking to secure science, morality, and religion against skepticism). Popper later named the problem of demarcation between science and metaphysics "Kant's problem" and adopted Kant's framing while rejecting the synthetic a priori solution in favor of falsifiability and critical method (see [[Sources/The Logic of Scientific Discovery - Karl Popper (Routledge Classics)]] and [[Concepts/Problem of Demarcation (Kant and Popper)]]).

## Major Works

- *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781/1787): Transcendental idealism; space/time as a priori forms of intuition; categories as a priori concepts; phenomena vs noumena; limits of theoretical reason; antinomies.
- *Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals* (1785): Search for the supreme principle of morality (this source).
- *Critique of Practical Reason* (1788): Full development of practical philosophy; fact of reason; postulates of practical reason (freedom, immortality, God); primacy of practical over theoretical reason.
- *Critique of the Power of Judgment* (1790): Aesthetic and teleological judgment; bridging theoretical and practical; beauty, sublime, purposiveness.
- *Metaphysics of Morals* (1797): Doctrine of Right (politics/law) and Doctrine of Virtue (ethics of ends); application of the moral law.
- Essays: *What is Enlightenment?* (1784), *Idea toward a Universal History with a Cosmopolitan Aim* (1784), *Conjectural Beginning of Human History* (1786), *Toward Perpetual Peace* (1795), *Religion within the Boundaries of Mere Reason* (1793), *Anthropology from a Pragmatic Point of View* (1798).

## Key Philosophical Positions (from *Groundwork* Preface + First + Second Sections)

**Need for a pure metaphysics of morals**: Moral philosophy must be a priori (pure), grounded in reason alone, not empirical anthropology, happiness, or inclination. Moral laws have absolute necessity and bind all rational beings. Popular philosophy mixes rational and empirical, leading to corruption of morals. A metaphysics of morals investigates what pure reason can achieve; it must precede application.

**Good will**: The only thing good without limitation. Talents of mind, temperament, and gifts of fortune are conditionally good (can be used for evil without good will). The good will is good in itself, through its willing/principle, not through effects or success in achieving ends. Even if unable to accomplish anything (due to fate or nature), it "shines like a jewel for itself" with full worth in itself. Nature's purpose for reason is not happiness (which instinct could secure better) but a good will as the highest good and condition for all else.

**Duty**: The concept that reveals the good will under hindrances. An action has moral worth only if done *from duty* (not merely in conformity with duty from inclination or prudence). Examples:
- Merchant not overcharging: from prudence (self-interest), not duty.
- Preserving life when no inclination remains (adversity has extinguished taste for life): from duty.
- Beneficence when no sympathy remains (grief clouds the mind): from duty.
- Promoting one's own happiness from duty (when inclination conflicts or tempts violation of other duties).

**Three propositions** (inferred/developed):
1. An action has moral worth only if done from duty.
2. Moral worth lies not in the aim or expected effect, but in the maxim (subjective principle of volition) according to which the action is done.
3. Duty is the necessity of an action from respect for the law. Respect is the consciousness of the will's subjection to the law (without mediation of inclination or fear); it is for the law itself as objective and necessary.

**The supreme principle / Categorical Imperative (first formulation)**: "I ought never to conduct myself except so that I could also will that my maxim become a universal law." (Or: Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law.)

**Test (false promise example)**: In embarrassment, may I make a promise intending not to keep it? Prudence might sometimes say yes, but duty no. Universalize the maxim: "Make an untruthful promise when in embarrassment I cannot otherwise escape." This destroys itself—promises would become pointless (no trust). One cannot consistently will it as universal law. Hence contrary to duty. The law is recognized by common human reason (even if not formulated abstractly) and serves as its practical compass.

**Hypothetical vs Categorical Imperatives (Second Section)**: Hypothetical imperatives command action as means to some other end the agent wills (or might will) — conditional (skill/technical or prudence for happiness). Categorical imperatives command action as necessary in itself, without further end — unconditional, apodictic law concerning only form/universal lawfulness. Only categorical can be practical law binding all rational wills. All prior "moral" principles yield only hypothetical (heteronomous) imperatives.

**System of CI Formulas (Second Section)**: The single categorical imperative has equivalent expressions: (1) Universal Law / Law of Nature formulation (already in First); (2) Formula of Humanity: Act so that you use humanity (rational nature) in your own person or any other always at the same time as end, never merely as means (persons have absolute worth as objective ends; grounds non-instrumentalization); (3) Autonomy: the will as law to itself via maxims' suitability for universal legislation; (4) Kingdom of Ends: act as if always a legislative member in a realm of rational beings as ends through self-given universal laws ("as if" the moral community were realized; retains categorical force even if others do not comply).

**Autonomy as supreme principle; critique of heteronomy**: Autonomy (will law to itself, independent of objects of volition) is the sole principle of morality and ground of dignity of rational nature. Heteronomy (law from any external "alien" source — inclination, happiness, perfection, feeling, divine will, etc.) can produce only conditioned imperatives and must "necessarily miscarry" for grounding morality. All previous ethical systems diagnosed as heteronomous.

Common rational moral cognition already possesses the principle in practice and can judge correctly without philosophy (Socrates' method of making people aware of their own principle). Philosophy secures and systematizes it. The Second Section is analytic; the synthetic a priori status of the CI requires the critique of practical reason (Third Section transition).

## Heidegger's Critique (2026-06-29 Ingest)

Heidegger's relationship with Kant is characterized by a "destructive retrieval" of the first *Critique*:
- **Kant as Ontologist**: In his 1929 *Kant and the Problem of Metaphysics* (and the planned Part II §1 of *Being and Time*), Heidegger reads Kant not as an epistemologist (which was the dominant Neo-Kantian reading of his day) but as an ontologist trying to lay the groundwork for metaphysics.
- **The Transcendental Imagination**: Heidegger identifies the transcendental power of imagination (*Einbildungskraft*) in the first edition (A) of the *Critique of Pure Reason* as the original root of both sensibility and understanding, grounding the categories in time. Heidegger argues Kant "shrank back" from this insight in the second edition (B) by subordinating imagination to understanding and reason.
- **Time and the Ego**: Kant recognized time as the a priori form of inner sense, linking it to the unity of apperception. However, Heidegger argues Kant still treated the "I think" as an atemporal, static subject (Cartesian residue) rather than recognizing the ecstatic, finite temporality of Dasein.
- **Primacy of Practical Reason**: Heidegger treats Kant's doctrine of the primacy of practical reason and freedom "more seriously, perhaps, than Kant did," grounding freedom not in a moral law but in Dasein's temporal transcendence.

See [[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]] and [[Concepts/Temporality (Heidegger)]].

## Husserl's Engagement with Kant (2026-06-29 Ingest)

[[Thinkers/Edmund Husserl]] engaged deeply with Kant throughout his career, positioning phenomenology as both a continuation and a radical revision of the critical project:

- **Shared structure**: Husserl's "phenomenology of reason" echoes Kant's "critique of reason" — both seek conditions of possibility of knowledge from within experience. Both posit a priori structures that shape what can be known.
- **Transcendental idiom absorbed in *Ideas I***: By 1913, Husserl adopted the neo-Kantian language of "transcendental phenomenology," and his account of constitution echoes Kant's account of the categories as conditions of possible experience. However, Husserl grounds constitution in intentionality rather than in Kantian categories applied to a brute sensory manifold.
- **Rejection of the Ding an sich**: Husserl rejects the unknowable thing-in-itself. When a perceptual intention is intuitively fulfilled, it reaches the existing thing *itself* — not merely a phenomenal appearance. The proper theory of intentionality was unavailable to Kant.
- **Categorical vs. eidetic a priori**: Kant's categories are functions of synthesis applied to sensory intuition; Husserl's material a priori are real structures of domains of objects (essences), grasped through eidetic variation rather than deduced from the conditions of synthesis.
- **Ethics**: Husserl treats Kant's categorical imperative as the *ground norm* (Grundnorm) of Kantian ethics — a formal metanormative measure that "defines" the normativity of all moral propositions within that system. He criticises Kant for excluding love and compassion from moral grounding, placing all moral authority in pure practical reason alone.

See [[Thinkers/Edmund Husserl]], [[Concepts/Transcendental Ego and Constitution (Husserl)]], and [[Concepts/Formal and Material Ethics (Husserl)]].
