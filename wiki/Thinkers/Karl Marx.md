# Karl Marx

> German philosopher, economist, political theorist, and sociologist (1818–1883). Developed the framework of historical materialism and the critique of capitalism, arguing that human societies progress through class struggles and that the economic base of a society determines its social and political superstructure.

## Key Contributions

**Historical Materialism**:
- Formulated the theory that the material conditions of a society's mode of production (its economic base) shape its political, legal, and ideological structures (its superstructure).
- Taught that history is not driven by the self-unfolding of ideas (as Hegel argued), but by the contradictions and conflicts within the material/economic sphere. See [[Concepts/Historical Materialism (Marx)]].

**Class Struggle**:
- Asserted that "the history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles." Under capitalism, this conflict is polarized between the bourgeoisie (owners of the means of production) and the proletariat (wage laborers).
- Envisioned that class contradictions would inevitably lead to a proletarian revolution and the establishment of a classless, communist society. See [[Concepts/Class Struggle (Marx)]].

## Relevance to Vault

- **The Inversion of Hegel**: Marx adopted Hegel's dialectical method but rejected his idealism, swapping *Geist* for material economic relations. This is the classic idealist-materialist division. See [[Contradictions/Dialectical Idealism vs. Historical Materialism]].
- **Alienation and Labor**: Marx's analysis of how capitalism alienates the worker from the product of their labor builds directly on Hegel's [Master-Slave Dialectic](file:///home/mark/Documents/Philosophy/wiki/Concepts/Master-Slave%20Dialectic%20(Hegel).md).
- **Critique of Liberalism**: Marx challenges the individual rights and contract theories of Locke and Mill, viewing them as ideological justifications for bourgeois property relations.

## Versus Mancur Olson (2026-06-29 Ingest)

Mancur Olson's *The Rise and Decline of Nations* (1982) presents a direct challenge to Marx's theory of class action:
- **The Paradox of Class Action**: Marx assumes that the working class (the proletariat) will naturally act collectively to overthrow capitalism once they overcome "false consciousness." Olson argues this is a logical error because classes are large groups, and individuals within them face strong incentives to free-ride. An individual proletarian incurs high personal risks by participating in revolution, while hoping others will fight and secure the classless society — meaning large-scale class revolution requires selective incentives or coercion to overcome free-riding.
- **The "Internal Contradiction" of Stability**: Both Marx and Olson identify an "internal contradiction" in stable societies. For Marx, it is the dialectical contradiction between the forces and relations of production leading to class struggle. For Olson, it is the non-dialectical conflict between the advantages of peace/stability and the gradual accumulation of rent-seeking distributional coalitions that ultimately undermine economic growth.

See [[Thinkers/Mancur Olson]] and [[Concepts/Collective Action and Free Riding (Olson)]].

## Versus Constitutional Economics (2026-06-29 Ingest)

Buchanan and Tullock's public choice framework reinterprets Marx's critique of the capitalist state:
- **The Exploitative State**: Marx viewed the state as the executive committee of the ruling class, structured to exploit the proletariat. Buchanan and Tullock model this exploitation using decision-making rules: if the constitutional rules allow collective choices to be made by less-than-unanimous voting thresholds (like simple majority), winning coalitions will naturally use the state's coercive tax-spending powers to redistribute income to themselves, imposing external costs on the minority. Exploitation is thus a consequence of non-unanimous voting rules, not class dialectics.
- **Economics of the State**: Both Marx and the public choice theorists apply economic models to political institutions, but public choice rejects Marx's historical materialism in favor of methodological individualism, focusing on the utility calculations of individuals rather than classes.

See [[Thinkers/James M. Buchanan]], [[Thinkers/Gordon Tullock]], and [[Concepts/Constitutional Political Economy (Buchanan-Tullock)]].
