# Pythagoras

> Greek philosopher, mathematician, and religious reformer (c. 570 – 495 BCE). Born in Samos, migrated to Croton in southern Italy, where he founded a highly influential, semi-mystical brotherhood.

> [!warning] Contested portrait. The "mathematician-scientist" Pythagoras described below is the *traditional* picture (Burnet, Russell). Walter Burkert's [[Sources/Lore and Science in Ancient Pythagoreanism - Walter Burkert (1972)]] argues on source-critical grounds that this is largely a later retrojection, and that the historical Pythagoras was a *religious* charismatic. Leonid Zhmud's [[Sources/Pythagoras and the Early Pythagoreans - Leonid Zhmud (2012)]] is the major modern rebuttal of Burkert, defending Pythagoras as *both* a religious teacher *and* a genuine pioneer of the sciences. All three readings are set against each other on [[Contradictions/Pythagoras the Scientist vs the Religious Charismatic]]. Read the sections below as the received tradition, qualified by the **Source-Critical Caveat** *and* the **Zhmud Rehabilitation** at the end.

## Key Contributions

**Number as the Essence of Reality**:
- Famously declared that "all things are numbers." 
- Discovered that the intervals of the musical scale can be expressed as mathematical ratios (e.g., 2:1 octave, 3:2 fifth, 4:3 fourth).
- Concluded that since music—which moves the soul—is governed by mathematical ratios, the entire cosmos must be structured according to number and harmony. See [[Concepts/Number as the Essence of Reality (Pythagoras)]].

**Metempsychosis (Transmigration of Souls)**:
- Taught that the soul is immortal and undergoes reincarnation (transmigration) into humans, animals, or plants after death.
- Famously stopped a man from beating a dog because he claimed to recognize the voice of a deceased friend in the dog's whimpering.
- Prescribed strict ascetic rules (e.g., vegetarianism, silence, avoiding fava beans) to purify the soul and escape the cycle of reincarnation.

**Cosmic Harmony and Tetraktys**:
- The **Tetraktys**: The sacred triangular figure of ten points arranged in four rows (1+2+3+4=10). It symbolized the organization of space and the universe.
- **Music of the Spheres**: Postulated that the movements of the celestial bodies emit a beautiful, continuous harmony that humans cannot hear because they have been exposed to it from birth.

## Relevance to Vault

- **The Mathematical Turn**: Pythagoras shifted philosophy from the materialist naturalism of the Milesians (seeking a physical substance like water or air) to a formalist, mathematical idealism (seeking the structural/mathematical relations of things). This is the direct ancestor of modern mathematical physics, which describes the world using equations rather than physical models.
- **Foundations of Platonism**: Bertrand Russell remarks: *"I do not know of any other man who has been as influential as he was in the school of thought... what appears as Platonism is, when analysed, found to be in essence Pythagoreanism."* Plato's Theory of Forms, mathematical education in the *Republic*, and cosmology in the *Timaeus* are deeply Pythagorean.

## Source-Critical Caveat (Burkert 1972)
Walter Burkert's *Lore and Science in Ancient Pythagoreanism* subjects every claim above to the rule that the Pythagorean tradition grows *richer the further it lies from the events* (Zeller). His findings:
- **Only the religious doctrines are early-attested.** [[Concepts/Metempsychosis (Transmigration of Souls)]] and the ritual [[Concepts/Acusmata and the Two Sects (Acusmatici and Mathematici)|acusmata]] are fixed by hostile contemporaries (Xenophanes, Aristotle); the mathematics and astronomy are not.
- **The number-cosmology belongs to [[Thinkers/Philolaus]] and Plato's Academy**, not demonstrably to Pythagoras — the "all things are numbers" metaphysics is best documented a century later. See [[Concepts/Number as the Essence of Reality (Pythagoras)]].
- **The musical "discovery" is legend.** The smithy/hammer experiment crediting Pythagoras is *physically false* (pitch is not proportional to hammer weight or string tension), hence a later rationalization of the older myth that Pythagoras commanded magical music. The real numerical work (e.g. bronze discs) is better attributed to **Hippasus**.
- **"Pythagoras the mathematician" collapses.** Burkert endorses re-dating the discovery of incommensurability to the late fifth century and rejects Proclus' attribution of it (or of demonstrative geometry) to Pythagoras himself.
- The *mathematici* who carried the science were, on the earliest evidence, the contested **junior** strand of the school — see [[Schools/Pythagoreanism]].

## Zhmud Rehabilitation (Zhmud 2012)
Leonid Zhmud's [[Sources/Pythagoras and the Early Pythagoreans - Leonid Zhmud (2012)]] is the major modern counter-statement to Burkert. It does **not** restore the old uncritical portrait; it argues, on stricter source-critical grounds, that Burkert over-corrected.
- **"Both, not either/or."** The earliest evidence shows a *sophos* "famous both for his religious teaching and for *historia* and *philosophia*"; Heraclitus' hostile jibes (B129: *historiē* "beyond all other men"; B40, B81) already attack a *thinker*, not only a wonder-worker. Aristotle fr. 191: Pythagoras "first dedicated himself to the mathematical sciences... but later could not refrain from the wonder-working of Pherecydes."
- **The legend grew, the science didn't.** The *supernatural* element is thinner in 5th-c. testimony and thicker afterwards — so strict criticism deflates the *miracle-worker*, not the mathematician. Isocrates' *Busiris* (independent of the Academy) already credits Pythagoras with philosophy and the mathematical sciences.
- **Genuine contributions to the *beginnings* of the *mathēmata***: the numerical ratios of the musical concords (Xenocrates fr. 87), the first three proportions (arithmetic/geometric/harmonic), the theory of even and odd, and the theorem that bears his name (Aristoxenus fr. 23: he "valued the science of numbers and advanced it, separating it from the merchants' business"). The higher reaches — incommensurability, the disc experiment — belong to his heir [[Thinkers/Hippasus]].
- **Biography Zhmud accepts**: birth on Samos c. 570 to **Mnesarchus** (a wealthy merchant, not the legendary seal-engraver); a real (biographical, not doctrinal) link to **Pherecydes**; flight from **Polycrates'** tyranny c. 532; migration to **Croton**, where his charisma drew an aristocratic *[[Schools/Pythagoreanism|hetairia]]* of ~300 youths that came to dominate the city; the war with **Sybaris** (510); the **Cylonian** conflict (a faction-fight within the oligarchy and within the society itself, not an anti-tyrannical revolt); death by self-starvation at the temple of the Muses in **Metapontum**, early 490s. He even had an attested family (Theano; Telauges, Arimnestus, Myia) — "no ordinary Presocratic."
- **Biography Zhmud rejects**: the Egyptian/Babylonian/Oriental **travels** — "we have no grounds to believe that they ever happened." They are literary conjectures bred by "naive diffusionism" and the *prōtoi heuretai* topos; the Peripatetic biographers ignore them, and Egypt had no metempsychosis for Pythagoras to import (see [[Concepts/Metempsychosis (Transmigration of Souls)]]).
- **On the soul**: Zhmud holds Pythagoras had a religious doctrine of [[Concepts/Metempsychosis (Transmigration of Souls)|transmigration]] (borrowed from Orphism, not invented by him) but *no philosophical theory of the soul* — and he was likely **not** a strict vegetarian (the meat of *sacrificial* animals was permitted).

## Huffman Volume (2014): The Reception Problem
The Cambridge [[Sources/A History of Pythagoreanism - Carl A. Huffman (ed.) (2014)]] is the current authoritative synthesis; G.E.R. Lloyd's opening chapter sharpens the methodological impasse rather than resolving it.
- **"The real Pythagoras is more or less totally inaccessible."** Guthrie (1962) and Burkert (1962/1972) reached opposite conclusions from the *same* evidence; every reconstruction is value-laden. The modern labels — shaman, charismatic, sage, mathematician, scientist — *all* fail ("shamanism" is "something of a red herring," since shamans hold institutionalized roles and are not reborn in animals).
- **The five earliest sources** (Xenophanes, Heraclitus, Empedocles, Herodotus, Ion of Chios) attest a famous "wise man" focused on the soul: "*None of the five earliest sources mentions mathematics or harmonics... and the evidence for a moral philosophy as such is slender.*" Heraclitus' jibe: "Much learning [*polymathiē*] does not teach sense: otherwise it would have taught... Pythagoras."
- **He did not discover the "Pythagorean theorem"** — the Babylonians knew the triplets (Plimpton 322); the attribution is post-Euclidean, an instance of the Greek hunt for a *prōtos heuretēs*. [[Concepts/Metempsychosis (Transmigration of Souls)|Metempsychosis]] remains his one well-attested doctrine.
- **Everything "everyone knows" is largely reception.** The rich portrait comes through [[Concepts/The Three Ancient Lives of Pythagoras|the three ancient Lives]] (Diogenes Laertius, Porphyry, Iamblichus) and [[Schools/Neopythagoreanism]] — each reconstructing Pythagoras for its own ends. See also the Peripatetic split: Aristoxenus (sympathetic) vs Dicaearchus, who covertly paints Pythagoras as a *psychagōgos* — a bewitching "charismatic charlatan."
- Huffman's volume confirms the deflationary consensus that the technical mathematics belongs to [[Thinkers/Philolaus]] and [[Thinkers/Archytas]], while leaving the *persona* question deliberately open — see [[Contradictions/Pythagoras the Scientist vs the Religious Charismatic]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] The Discovery of Irrational Numbers: The Pythagoreans believed all physical relations could be expressed as ratios of whole numbers. The discovery of the incommensurability of the diagonal of a square (e.g., $\sqrt{2}$) created a major crisis, as it proved that mathematics contains relations that cannot be expressed as rational proportions.
