# John Stuart Mill

> English philosopher, political economist, and Member of Parliament (1806–1873). A major figure in classical liberalism, he defended and refined utilitarian ethical theory, formulated the famous "Harm Principle" to protect individual liberty, and warned against the social tyranny of the majority.

## Key Contributions

**Refinement of Utilitarianism**:
- Defended utilitarianism against critics who called it a "doctrine worthy of swine." Argued that pleasures differ in *quality* as well as quantity, famously writing: "It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied."
- Defined the **Greatest Happiness Principle** as holding that actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, and wrong as they tend to produce the reverse. See [[Concepts/Greatest Happiness Principle (Mill)]].

**Defense of Individual Liberty**:
- Formulated the **Harm Principle** in *On Liberty*, stating that the only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others.
- Warned against the **Tyranny of the Majority**—not just political tyranny, but social tyranny and conformity that stifle individuality and progress. See [[Concepts/Harm Principle (Mill)]].

## Relevance to Vault

- **The Deontology-Consequentialism Clash**: Mill's utilitarian ethics stands as the primary counterweight to Kant's deontological moral theory. See [[Contradictions/Utilitarian Consequentialism vs. Kantian Deontology]].
- **Faction and Liberty**: Mill's concern with the tyranny of the majority complements Madison's institutional solutions to factions in [Federalist No. 10](file:///home/mark/Documents/Philosophy/wiki/Sources/The%20Federalist%20Papers%20-%20Publius%20(1787-1788).md).
- **Epistemic Progress**: Mill's defense of free speech in *On Liberty* as a mechanism for error-correction and finding truth parallels Popper's critical rationalism.

## Connection to Mancur Olson (2026-06-29 Ingest)

Mancur Olson's collective-action logic directly builds on and formalizes Mill's economic and political insights:
- **The Paradox of Shorter Workdays**: In his *Principles of Political Economy*, Mill observed that workers might collectively benefit from a general limit on the workday, yet fail to achieve it through voluntary agreement because each individual worker has an incentive to work longer hours for extra income if everyone else complies. Olson formalizes this as the classic free-rider paradox, showing why public goods require state-enforced coercion or selective incentives.
- **Representative Government and Sclerosis**: Mill's defense of representative government was paired with warnings about interest groups ("sinister interests") distorting policy. Olson's *The Rise and Decline of Nations* shows how stable representative democracies accumulate these special interests over time, culminating in institutional sclerosis.

See [[Thinkers/Mancur Olson]] and [[Concepts/Collective Action and Free Riding (Olson)]].

## Connection to Constitutional Economics (2026-06-29 Ingest)

Buchanan and Tullock's *The Calculus of Consent* (1962) formalizes and extends Mill's political theory:
- **Tyranny of the Majority**: Mill warned against the tyranny of the majority as a social and political threat. Buchanan and Tullock provide a mathematical model for this threat: under simple majority rule, a bare majority can easily authorize spending that benefits themselves while shifting the tax costs to the minority (imposing external costs). 
- **Constitutional Checkreins**: Mill supported institutional mechanisms like proportional representation and representation of minorities. Buchanan and Tullock formalize how structural checks (bicameralism, qualified majorities) act as protective constraints that lower external costs and safeguard individuals from majoritarian exploitation.

See [[Thinkers/James M. Buchanan]], [[Thinkers/Gordon Tullock]], and [[Concepts/Constitutional Political Economy (Buchanan-Tullock)]].
