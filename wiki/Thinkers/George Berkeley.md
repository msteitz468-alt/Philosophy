# George Berkeley (1685–1753)

> Anglo-Irish philosopher, bishop, and empiricist. Best known for developing the philosophy of **immaterialism** or **subjective idealism** (summarized by the maxim *esse est percipi*: "to be is to be perceived"). 

## Life and Context

Born in County Kilkenny, Ireland, and educated at Trinity College, Dublin. Berkeley was ordained in the Church of Ireland and later served as the Bishop of Cloyne. In the late 1720s, he traveled to America in a failed attempt to establish a missionary college in Bermuda, living for a time in Rhode Island (where he influenced American education and philosophy).

Berkeley wrote his most famous works in his twenties, including *An Essay Towards a New Theory of Vision* (1709), *A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge* (1710), and the more popular *Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous* (1713). His goal was to combat the rise of skepticism, atheism, and materialism that he saw emerging from the physical sciences and modern rationalism.

---

## Major Works

- *A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge* (1710): Berkeley's systematic exposition of immaterialism, arguing that the belief in mind-independent material substance is incoherent.
- *Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous* (1713): A philosophical dialogue between Hylas (representing material realism) and Philonous (representing Berkeley's idealism) designed to present immaterialism in an accessible, persuasive form.

---

## Key Philosophical Positions

* **Rejection of Material Substance**: Berkeley argues that there is no such thing as mind-independent physical matter. What we call "objects" (tables, chairs, trees) are nothing but collections of sensible ideas (colors, shapes, textures) that exist solely in the minds that perceive them.
* **Esse est Percipi**: "To be is to be perceived" (*esse est percipi aut percipere*: to be is to be perceived or to perceive). For any sensible object, its existence consists in its being perceived by a conscious mind.
* **God as the Ultimate Perceiver**: To explain how the physical world continues to exist when no human is observing it, Berkeley posits God as an omnipresent, infinite spirit who constantly perceives all things. The stability and regularity of the laws of nature are the direct result of God's ideas being printed on our minds in a coherent, orderly fashion.
* **Critique of Abstract Ideas**: Berkeley denies that the mind can form abstract ideas (e.g., a general idea of a triangle that is neither equilateral nor scalene, or "matter" separate from concrete qualities). He argues that general terms only represent specific, concrete ideas used as signs for others.

---

## Influence and Legacy

Berkeley is grouped with John Locke and David Hume as the third of the great British Empiricists. While his immaterialism was often dismissed as absurd or counter-intuitive by contemporaries (e.g., Samuel Johnson's famous "kicking a stone" attempt to refute it), Berkeley's critiques of material substance paved the way for Hume's thoroughgoing skepticism. His views directly influenced the transcendental idealism of Immanuel Kant and later 19th-century German and British idealisms.

In the 20th century, his analysis of sensation and critique of unobservable entities anticipated phenomenalism and instrumentalism in the philosophy of science.
