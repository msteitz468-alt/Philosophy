# William James

> American philosopher and psychologist (1842–1910), the leading popularizer and systematizer of **pragmatism** and a founder of modern psychology. In *Pragmatism* (1907) he turns C. S. Peirce's principle into both a method for dissolving idle metaphysical disputes and a "genetic" theory of truth (truth as a process of verification, "what works"), and champions a pluralistic, "unfinished," meliorist universe.

## Profile

- **Dates/place**: 1842–1910; American (Harvard). Brother of the novelist Henry James. Trained in painting, then medicine; became Harvard's central figure in psychology and philosophy.
- **Key works** (per this source): *Pragmatism: A New Name for Some Old Ways of Thinking* (1907, the text ingested). He references his own earlier *The Will to Believe* (an essay he says he "unluckily" titled) and his religion book — *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902) — "regarded as making for the reality of God." (Not in the vault yet: *The Principles of Psychology* 1890, *A Pluralistic Universe* 1909, *The Meaning of Truth* 1909, *Essays in Radical Empiricism*.)
- **Lineage he claims**: dedicates the book to **J. S. Mill**; credits **C. S. Peirce** with the originating principle ("How to Make Our Ideas Clear," 1878), and **John Dewey** + **F. C. S. Schiller** with the instrumental/humanist theory of truth. Names **Socrates, Aristotle, Locke, Berkeley, Hume** as pragmatism's "forerunners… preluders only."
- **Self-distinction**: pragmatism is "no logical connection" with his separate doctrine of "radical empiricism" — "one may entirely reject it and still be a pragmatist" (Preface).

## Core Positions (from *Pragmatism*, 1907)

- **Pragmatism as mediator**: between the "tender-minded" (rationalist/idealist/religious/monist) and "tough-minded" (empiricist/materialist/sceptical/pluralist) temperaments — keeping "the richest intimacy with facts" while staying open to religious hypotheses. See [[Concepts/Tender-Minded and Tough-Minded (James)]].
- **The pragmatic method**: settle disputes by tracing each alternative's *practical consequences*; "no difference anywhere that doesn't make a difference elsewhere." Theories are "instruments, not answers… in which we can rest"; their meaning is their "cash-value" in experience. See [[Concepts/Pragmatic Method (James)]].
- **The pragmatic theory of truth**: "Truth *happens* to an idea. It *becomes* true, is *made* true by events" — by verification. "The 'true'… is only the expedient in the way of our thinking." Truth lives "on a credit system." Truth is *in rebus* (a name for verification-processes), like health/wealth/strength — not a static essence *ante rem*. See [[Concepts/Pragmatic Theory of Truth (James)]].
- **The one and the many**: the deepest "classification with the maximum number of consequences." Oneness and manyness are "absolutely coordinate"; pragmatism sides with **pluralism** and may replace the "Absolute" with the "Ultimate." See [[Concepts/The One and the Many (James)]].
- **Common sense as inherited hypotheses**: the common-sense categories (thing, kind, cause, one time/space, etc.) are not eternal intuitions but "extraordinarily successful hypotheses" frozen into language; three stages of thought (common sense / science / critical philosophy), none "absolutely more true."
- **Humanism**: "to an unascertainable extent our truths are man-made products"; "the world stands really malleable… Man engenders truths upon it." We "carve the statue" from the marble of sensation. See [[Concepts/Humanism - Reality as Malleable (Schiller and James)]].
- **Meliorism + pluralistic theism**: salvation as a *possibility* whose probability grows with the actual conditions (incl. our acts); God as "*primus inter pares*," one helper in a still-growing universe. See [[Concepts/Meliorism (James)]].

## Relations to other vault thinkers

- **[[Thinkers/Hume]]** (and Locke, Berkeley): James places himself in the empiricist line and praises their pragmatic moves (e.g., Berkeley on matter, Locke on personal identity reduced to consciousness, Hume on the soul as "verifiable cohesions"). He *radicalizes* the naturalist, anti-rationalist temper — but where Hume stops at description of custom and ends in [[Concepts/Mitigated Scepticism (Hume)]], James turns the same materials toward a constructive, world-making, hopeful philosophy. Hume's "Epicurus" critique of the **design argument** is essentially James's ("design is a blank cartridge").
- **[[Thinkers/Nietzsche]]**: striking kinship — truth as perspectival and "man-made," "untruth as a condition of life" ≈ "the true is the expedient," anti-dogmatism, a "yes" to a dangerous/unfinished world. Differences of tone: James is meliorist, democratic, religion-friendly; Nietzsche is aristocratic, anti-Christian, revaluing. See [[Concepts/Perspectivism (Nietzsche)]].
- **[[Thinkers/Kant]]**: James rejects categories "fulminated before nature began" in favor of "categories gradually forming themselves in nature's presence" ("the whole chasm between rationalism and empiricism yawns"); treats the "categorical imperative" and "the Truth" as rationalist idols.
- **[[Thinkers/Spinoza]]**, **[[Thinkers/Plato]]**, **[[Thinkers/Plotinus]]** and the **Absolute** (Royce/Bradley/Hegel, Vedanta): the monistic, "edition de luxe, eternally complete" universe James pragmatically resists — granting it a real "moral-holiday" value but denying its necessity. Leibniz's *Théodicée* is his stock example of rationalism's "feeble grasp of reality."
- **[[Thinkers/Aristotle]]/[[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]**: the adequation/correspondence theory of truth and the omnipotent, simple God are exactly what James's "truth happens" and "primus inter pares" theism depart from.
