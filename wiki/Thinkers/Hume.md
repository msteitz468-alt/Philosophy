# David Hume (1711–76)

> Scottish philosopher, historian, and essayist; the central figure of the Scottish Enlightenment and the culmination of British empiricism. Author of *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40), the *Enquiry concerning Human Understanding* (1748), the *Enquiry concerning the Principles of Morals* (1751), the *Essays*, the *Dialogues concerning Natural Religion* (1779, posthumous), and the best-selling *History of England*. Source of the problem of induction, the regularity/two-definition account of causation, the analysis of belief as a lively feeling produced by custom, compatibilism about liberty and necessity, the argument against miracles, and "mitigated scepticism." "A wise man proportions his belief to the evidence."

## Life and Context

Born in Edinburgh to a minor Berwickshire gentry family; studied at the University of Edinburgh. Abandoned a planned legal career; after an intense period of study and a nervous crisis, withdrew to La Flèche in France (the very Jesuit town where Descartes was schooled) and there, by age 28, wrote *A Treatise of Human Nature*. The *Treatise* "fell dead-born from the press" — a failure Hume attributed to its manner and haste rather than its substance. He recast Book I's epistemology as the *Philosophical Essays / Enquiry concerning Human Understanding* (1748), and Book III's ethics as the second *Enquiry* (1751), which he thought "incomparably the best" of all his writings.

A "colourful life" (Millican): tutor, military secretary (on a diplomatic/military expedition), librarian to the Faculty of Advocates in Edinburgh (where he wrote the *History of England*, his great commercial success), secretary at the British embassy in Paris (lionized in the salons), and Under-Secretary of State. Twice denied a university chair on suspicion of irreligion. Famous friendships (Adam Smith, who recorded his serene death) and a famous quarrel (Rousseau). Wrote the brief, equable autobiography "My Own Life" (Appendix V) months before dying of intestinal cancer in 1776, facing death with a tranquillity that scandalized observers expecting deathbed terror. "The best of the philosophers, both in his life and in his death" was the verdict of his friends.

Context: the Enlightenment; the maturing of British empiricism after **Locke** (the *Essay*'s theory of ideas from sensation/reflection, explicit anti-innatism and tabula rasa in Book I, "empty cabinet" starting point — now grounded in primary source in the vault) and **Berkeley** (immaterialism; critique of abstraction); the Newtonian model of an "experimental" science Hume sought to extend to "human nature"; and a culture in which open irreligion was dangerous, shaping Hume's habitual irony and indirection. See new [[Thinkers/Locke]] and [[Sources/An Essay Concerning Human Understanding - Locke]].

## Major Works

- *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40): Book I (understanding), II (passions), III (morals). The original, fullest statement; later disowned in favour of the *Enquiries*.
- *Essays, Moral, Political, and Literary* (from 1741): influential in politics, aesthetics, and especially economics.
- *An Enquiry concerning Human Understanding* (1748): the mature epistemology (see dedicated source summary).
- *An Enquiry concerning the Principles of Morals* (1751): sentimentalist ethics; utility and the moral sentiments.
- *The Natural History of Religion* (1757) and *Dialogues concerning Natural Religion* (written 1750s, published posthumously 1779): the critique of natural/rational religion, esp. the design argument.
- *The History of England* (1754–62): his contemporary fame and fortune rested here.
- "My Own Life" (1776).

## Key Philosophical Positions (from the *Enquiry*)

- **Empiricism / the Copy Principle**: all the materials of thought derive from experience. Perceptions divide into *impressions* (lively) and *ideas* (faint copies), differing in force and vivacity; every simple idea copies a prior simple impression. Meaning-test: "from what impression is that supposed idea derived?" See [[Concepts/Impressions and Ideas - Copy Principle (Hume)]].
- **Hume's Fork**: every object of reason is either a *relation of ideas* (a priori, demonstrative, contrary implies contradiction) or a *matter of fact* (contingent, contrary always conceivable). See [[Concepts/Hume's Fork (Relations of Ideas vs Matters of Fact)]].
- **The Problem of Induction**: all factual inference rests on cause and effect, known only by experience, and presupposes that the future will resemble the past — a principle provable neither by demonstration nor (without circularity) by experience. So induction is not founded on reason. See [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]].
- **Causation — constant conjunction + the two definitions**: we never perceive a necessary connexion in objects, only the constant conjunction of event-types; the idea of necessity copies an *internal* impression — the mind's customary transition from cause to effect. Hence the [[Concepts/Necessary Connexion and the Two Definitions of Cause (Hume)|two definitions of cause]].
- **Custom and belief**: the inference reason cannot justify is performed by *custom/habit*; *belief* is not a separate idea but a more vivid, forcible *manner of conceiving*, a feeling produced by a present impression and not summonable at will. See [[Concepts/Custom and Belief (Hume)]].
- **Compatibilism (liberty and necessity)**: necessity = constant conjunction + inference, which manifestly hold of human action (the uniformity of human nature); liberty = acting per the determinations of the will (not absence of cause). Both are required for morality. See [[Concepts/Compatibilism - Liberty and Necessity (Hume)]].
- **Animal reason**: animals, infants, and philosophers infer alike, by custom/instinct, not argument — confirming the naturalistic account.
- **Of Miracles**: proportion belief to evidence; no testimony establishes a miracle (a violation of a law of nature) unless its falsehood would be more miraculous than the event — a bar no actual report has met. See [[Concepts/Of Miracles (Hume)]].
- **Against the design argument** (Providence/Future State): the cause may be credited only with what is exactly sufficient to produce the observed effect; from a finite, mixed world no infinite, perfectly just deity or future state can be inferred.
- **Mitigated (Academical) scepticism**: Pyrrhonian doubt is unanswerable in the study but powerless in life ("Nature is always too strong for principle"); its useful residue is durable modesty + the limitation of inquiry to quantity/number and matters of fact — everything else to "the flames." See [[Concepts/Mitigated Scepticism (Hume)]].
- **Naturalism about the self and religion**: no impression of an enduring self as substance; religion explained naturally (from fear, wonder, propensity to the marvellous) rather than rationally grounded.

## Influence and Reception

Immense. Roused **Kant** from his "dogmatic slumber" (on causation), prompting the *Critique of Pure Reason* and the project of synthetic a priori knowledge; Kant's *Groundwork* (1785) then supplies the full a priori deontological response (Second Section: autonomy of the will and critique of all heteronomous principles — sentiment, custom, happiness, moral feeling, perfection, divine command — as yielding only hypothetical imperatives; system of CI formulas; kingdom of ends). Primary-sourced via [[Sources/Metaphysics of Morals - Immanuel Kant]] + Kant concepts (universal law, humanity, autonomy, heteronomy). Provoked **Thomas Reid** and the Scottish "common sense" school. Anticipated **logical positivism** (Hume's Fork ≈ analytic/synthetic + verification; the "flames" passage as a meaning criterion) and **Bayesian/evidentialist** epistemology ("proportion belief to evidence," the testimony calculus). Foundational for contemporary debates on causation (regularity theories, counterfactuals, the "New Hume" sceptical-realism reading), the problem of induction (**Popper** accepted the negative result and offered falsificationism / critical rationalism as methodological bypass; **Goodman**'s new riddle), compatibilism (the dominant modern view of free will), philosophy of religion (the miracle and design arguments still central), and naturalized epistemology/cognitive science (belief as trained, sub-symbolic expectation). His sentimentalist ethics underwrites much modern moral psychology. Primary grounding for Popper now in [[Sources/The Logic of Scientific Discovery - Karl Popper (Routledge Classics)]] + [[Thinkers/Karl Popper]].

## Relevance to Vault

The empiricist capstone of the Early Modern cluster and the vault's deepest source on the *limits* of reason. Completes a triad: **Descartes** (rationalist certainty) → **Spinoza** (necessitarian system) → **Hume** (empiricist scepticism that replaces foundations with custom, feeling, and instinct).

- **Vs. Descartes**: rejects innate ideas, the inference from clear-and-distinct conceivability to existence, and the substantial self; turns Descartes' own conceivability test into a proof only of *possibility*. The vault's certainty-seeking thread ([[Concepts/Clear and Distinct Perception (Descartes)]], [[Concepts/Method of Hyperbolic Doubt (Descartes)]]) meets its empiricist limit.
- **Vs. Spinoza**: denies a priori intelligibility of nature and the Principle of Sufficient Reason; necessity is in the mind, not the things — a direct check on [[Concepts/Geometrical Method - Rationalism (Spinoza)]] and [[Concepts/Deus sive Natura - Substance Monism (Spinoza)]].
- **Vs. Sextus / the skeptics**: a *third* path on scepticism — neither Pyrrhonian suspension→ataraxia ([[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]]) nor Cartesian doubt→certainty, but mitigated scepticism→modesty + naturalism. "Nature is always too strong for principle."
- **Vs. Epicurus/Lucretius**: kindred naturalism, anti-superstition, and evidence-proportioning (Section XI literally argues *as* Epicurus); but sceptical where Epicurus dogmatized about atoms.
- **Vs. Aristotle/Aquinas/Ibn Rushd**: dissolves real powers and final causes; attacks the design argument; reframes the causation debate the vault holds via [[Concepts/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation (Ghazali and Ibn Rushd)]] — siding with neither real powers nor occasional causes but with constant conjunction + mental habit.
- **AI / agentic / vault meta**: the problem of induction = the limit of all learning from finite data (generalization, distribution shift); belief-as-vividness/custom = a precursor to predictive-processing and connectionist cognition ("reasoning is a species of instinct"); "proportion belief to evidence" + the testimony calculus = a near-Bayesian rubric for source reliability and the vault's contradiction-flagging; Hume's Fork + the "flames" test = a meaning/verification lint heuristic; mitigated scepticism = the ideal maintainer's temperament (modesty + limitation of inquiry to what the method can reach).
- **Personal / career**: evidence-proportioning in underwriting and risk judgment (CPCU); the uniformity-of-human-nature thesis as the working premise of negotiation, sales, and character-reading ([[Concepts/Persuasion & Sales Science]]); custom/habit as the true engine of expertise ([[Concepts/Expertise & Deliberate Practice]]).
- **Successor — [[Thinkers/William James]] / pragmatism**: James names Hume (with Locke and Berkeley) a "forerunner" of pragmatism who used its method "in fragments," and praises Hume's reduction of the soul to "verifiable cohesions." [[Concepts/Custom and Belief (Hume)]] (belief as a lively *feeling*, "reasoning a species of instinct") prefigures [[Concepts/Pragmatic Theory of Truth (James)]] (truth as a working leading), and [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]] underwrites James's claim that even our "oldest truths" are revisable — but where Hume ends in [[Concepts/Mitigated Scepticism (Hume)]], James turns the same materials toward a constructive, world-making [[Concepts/Meliorism (James)]]. Hume's "Epicurus" critique of the design argument is essentially James's ("design is a blank cartridge").

- **Popper / critical rationalism**: The most important 20th-century response to Hume's negative result on induction. Popper accepts the critique completely but rejects both custom (as insufficient for a *logic* of science) and a priori principles (Russell). Instead he supplies a deductivist methodology of conjecture and attempted refutation whose demarcation criterion (falsifiability) also solves the problem of demarcation (Kant's problem). Corroboration replaces inductive confirmation or probability. See [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]] (expanded subsection), [[Thinkers/Karl Popper]], and the primary LScD source. This is the hinge from the vault's Early Modern empiricist cluster into philosophy of science.

## Successor — A.J. Ayer / Logical Positivism

Ayer calls Hume "always my master" and takes the *Enquiry*'s "commit it to the flames" passage as "a rhetorical version of our own thesis" — his [[Concepts/Verification Principle (Ayer)|verification principle]] is a logical sharpening of [[Concepts/Hume's Fork (Relations of Ideas vs Matters of Fact)|Hume's Fork]]. Ayer inherits the Copy Principle meaning-test, the causation-as-constant-conjunction account, and the mitigated-scepticism temperament. He also *dissolves* (rather than solves or bypasses) the problem of induction: it is a "fictitious problem" because the only justification for inductive procedure is its practical success, and demanding a logical guarantee is asking for the impossible. This is distinct from both Hume's unresolved scepticism and Popper's methodological bypass. See [[Thinkers/A.J. Ayer]], [[Sources/Language, Truth and Logic - A.J. Ayer (1936)]].
