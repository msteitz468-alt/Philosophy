# Jean-Paul Sartre
> The 20th century's most publicly consequential French intellectual, who argued that human consciousness *is not* anything fixed and is therefore condemned to be free — and who spent his life pushing that freedom from the lone individual toward community, history, and revolution.

## Summary

Jean-Paul Sartre (Paris, 21 June 1905 – 15 April 1980) was a novelist, philosopher, playwright, and political activist whose social influence, on Welten's account, exceeded that of any French intellectual since Victor Hugo (50,000 attended his funeral). Raised in his grandfather Charles Schweitzer's library — "I had found my religion … nothing seemed more important to me than a book" — he never knew his biological father and described in *Les mots* (*The Words*, 1964) a childhood in which he was brought up "by books." His life was a string of *refusals*: he declined a professorship, marriage, Communist Party membership, and, in 1964, the Nobel Prize for Literature — refusals consistent with his own philosophy of the human being as a *negation*. His lifelong partner was Simone de Beauvoir ("Castor").

Welten organises Sartre's thought as a single restless trajectory. It begins in **phenomenology**: hearing Raymond Aron describe how the phenomenologists could philosophise about "that glass, that table," Sartre devoted himself to Husserl and developed a radicalised reading of *intentionality* — consciousness is nothing but directedness at the world, with no prior "I" behind it (*The Transcendence of the Ego*, *Sketch for a Theory of the Emotions*, *The Imaginary*, and the novel *Nausea*). This matured, written largely in a POW camp under the Occupation, into his ontological masterwork *L'être et le néant* (*Being and Nothingness*, 1943): consciousness as *being-for-itself* (pour-soi), a "hole in being," opposed to the inert *being-in-itself* (en-soi); existence precedes essence; the human being is a "useless passion" forever fleeing its own freedom into *bad faith*. The analysis of **the Other** through the Look (shame, the body, conflict, love, sexuality) overcomes solipsism by making the Other's subjectivity the truth of my being-seen.

After the war Sartre became the public face of **existentialism** (the 1945 lecture *Existentialism is a Humanism*) and increasingly of **engagement**: *What is Literature?* (1948) demands committed prose, and "Materialism and Revolution" turns him toward a dialectical, quasi-Hegelian politics. His self-described masterwork, the *Critique de la raison dialectique* (1960), abandons the lone individual for **totalisation**, series, groups, and the *fraternity-terror* of revolutionary community. His late work fused theory with action: support for Algerian independence (and, via Frantz Fanon, for anti-colonial **counter-violence**), the May 1968 students, and ultra-left causes — at the cost of friendships (Camus, Merleau-Ponty) and two bombings of his Paris flat. He went blind in the 1970s, conducted dictated interviews, and died "as he had lived: controversial, elusive, and a cause of scandal."

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Negative phenomenology.** Against Husserl's positive givenness, Sartre makes consciousness a *néantisation* — it appears only as the negation "I am not the tree." See [[Concepts/Consciousness as Intentionality and Néantisation (Sartre)]].
- **The emotions are magic, not feelings that befall us** — a way of transforming a world consciousness cannot otherwise handle. See [[Concepts/Magical Theory of Emotions (Sartre)]].
- **Pour-soi vs en-soi**, and **existence precedes essence**: the human being has no fixed nature; the stone *is* what it is, I *exist*, i.e. perpetually transcend myself. See [[Concepts/Being-for-itself and Being-in-itself (Sartre)]], [[Concepts/Existence Precedes Essence (Sartre)]].
- **Radical freedom and situation**: a situation determines nothing; freedom is not "freedom of choice" over objects but the un-grounded way I take up my situation as mine. See [[Concepts/Radical Freedom and Situation (Sartre)]].
- **Bad faith (mauvaise foi)**: lying to oneself "against better knowledge," fleeing freedom by treating oneself as an en-soi; consciousness is wholly *lucid*, so there is no Freudian unconscious to hide in. See [[Concepts/Bad Faith (Mauvaise Foi) (Sartre)]].
- **The Look**: "Hell is other people" — the Other steals not my consciousness but my freedom, fixing me as an object; love and sexuality are structurally conflict. See [[Concepts/The Look and the Other (Sartre)]].
- **Existential psychoanalysis**: recover the *original choice* underlying a life, studied through writing (Baudelaire, Genet, Flaubert). See [[Concepts/Existential Psychoanalysis and the Original Choice (Sartre)]].
- **Engaged literature**: prose is an act (a "speech act"), poetry is immanent like painting/music; only committed prose can change the world. See [[Concepts/Engaged Literature - Prose vs Poetry (Sartre)]].
- **Totalisation, series, group, fraternity-terror**: the social analysis of the *Critique*, recovering Marxism without Lenin and Stalin. See [[Concepts/Totalization, Series and Group (Sartre)]].
- **Violence and colonialism**: violence is never original but a *response* (secondary to primary violence); colonialism is a *system*, not a moral question of "rotten apples"; decolonisation needs counter-violence. See [[Concepts/Violence, Colonialism and Counter-Violence (Sartre and Fanon)]].

## Influences and Interlocutors

- **Edmund Husserl** — source of intentionality, which Sartre radicalises and turns against Husserl's transcendental ego and "monad." See [[Schools/Phenomenology]].
- **[[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]]** — the ontological vocabulary of *Being and Nothingness*; but Sartre starts from the *lack* of being, not Being.
- **[[Thinkers/Hegel]]** — the pour-soi/"unhappy consciousness," via Kojève's Paris lectures; the late dialectic.
- **[[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]** — scarcity, alienation, class, the eleventh Feuerbach thesis; but a *subjective* dialectic, against "scientific" Marxism. See [[Contradictions/Sartre vs Freud - Lucidity vs the Unconscious]] for the parallel break with Freud.
- **[[Thinkers/Kant]]** — the late "Critique" of dialectical reason as critical (conditions of knowing), not dogmatic.
- **Frantz Fanon, Simone de Beauvoir, Albert Camus, Maurice Merleau-Ponty, Raymond Aron** — collaborators and antagonists.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Conflicts with **Freud** on the unconscious: Sartre insists consciousness is wholly lucid and replaces the unconscious with bad faith — see [[Contradictions/Sartre vs Freud - Lucidity vs the Unconscious]].
- > [!warning] Tension with [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]] and orthodox Marxism: Sartre rejects the *materialist* dialectic ("reality itself is not dialectical"; only our knowing is) and denies natural/human rights as having any foundation, while still seeking revolution.
- **Humanism**: Sartre titles a lecture *Existentialism is a Humanism* yet, Welten argues, is an *anti-humanist* in that he refuses any base set of human values; "the human is the future of the human."
- **Continuity vs rupture**: whether the lone-individual ontology of *Being and Nothingness* and the social dialectic of the *Critique* are one project (Welten) or two.

## Sartre's Husserlian Roots (2026-06-29 Ingest)

The Smith *Husserl* source clarifies Sartre's debt to and transformation of Husserlian phenomenology:

- **Berlin 1933–4**: Sartre studied Husserlian phenomenology with Aron Gurwitsch in Berlin, reading Husserl in the original.
- **Being and Nothingness (1943)**: Subtitled "An Essay in Phenomenological Ontology" — Sartre explicitly develops existentialist ontology *within* the phenomenological framework. He adopts Husserl's intentionality but radicalises it: if consciousness is *purely* directedness, there is no prior transcendental ego doing the directing. The "I" arises only as a transcendent object of reflection ("conscience de soi").
- **Transformation of the epoché**: Sartre does not perform Husserl's phenomenological reduction (he believes it is impossible — the world cannot be genuinely bracketed). Instead, he begins directly from the phenomenological description of consciousness in the natural attitude.
- **Pre-reflective self-consciousness**: Smith notes approvingly that Sartre characterised self-consciousness as pre-reflective — consciousness is "translucent" and pre-reflectively conscious-of-itself, without a separate higher-order act of monitoring. This is close to Husserl's own resolution via inner time-consciousness (retention/protention).
- **Negative phenomenology**: Where Husserl describes consciousness as intentional *towards* objects, Sartre makes consciousness a *néant* (nothingness) — consciousness *is not*, it is the negation through which the world's appearing is possible.

See [[Thinkers/Edmund Husserl]] and [[Concepts/Consciousness as Intentionality and Néantisation (Sartre)]].
