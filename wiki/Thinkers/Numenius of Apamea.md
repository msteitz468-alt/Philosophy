# Numenius of Apamea

> Neopythagorean-Middle Platonist of Apamea in Syria (fl. c. 150 AD), the most important named forerunner of [[Thinkers/Plotinus]]: he built a graded triad of gods, made matter a positive evil coeval with God, and read Plato as "Moses speaking Attic Greek."

## Summary

Numenius survives only in fragments quoted by later writers (Eusebius, Calcidius, Porphyry, Proclus, Nemesius, Origen, Macrobius) — see [[Sources/The Neoplatonic Writings of Numenius - Guthrie (1917)]]. Working a century before Plotinus, he fused Platonism with Pythagoreanism and read both against the "wise nations" (Brahmins, Hebrews, Magi, Egyptians), holding that the same truth is expressed allegorically everywhere. His treatises *On the Good*, *On the Indestructibility of the Soul*, *On the Mystery-Teachings of Plato*, and the polemical *On the Divergence of the Academy from Plato* were studied and expounded in Plotinus's own school — so much so that Plotinus's followers had to defend him against the charge of merely repeating Numenius (Porphyry, *V. Plot.* 17; Amelius's treatise *On the Doctrinal Difference between Plotinus and Numenius*). He is the clearest bridge from Middle Platonism into full [[Schools/Neoplatonism]].

## Key Positions

- **The divine triad.** Numenius "teaches three Gods, calling the First *Father*, the Second *Creator*, and the Third *Creature*; for according to him the world is the Third God" (fr. 36). The First God is simple, unmixed, uncreating pure Intellect — "the Good-in-itself," dwelling in "an inexpressible, indefinable, immediately divine solitude," to be reached only by leaving the sensible world behind, "the alone with the alone" (fr. 10). The Second God is "double": by gazing on and ordering matter he becomes the Demiurge, but "he himself remains intelligible." The First "is free from all labor, inasmuch as he is King; while the Creator rules in that he passes through the heaven" (fr. 27a). See [[Concepts/The Three Gods - Father, Creator, and Creation (Numenius)]].
- **The First God as the standing, undiminished source.** "The First God is the Standing One, while the Second is in motion" (fr. 30); knowledge and being are given "undiminished," like one candle lit from another without loss to the first (fr. 29) — an image and mechanism later central to [[Concepts/Emanation (Proodos)]].
- **Matter as malevolent principle.** Against the Stoics (matter as neutral, "indifferent"), Numenius holds with Pythagoras that matter is *evil* — an unlimited, quality-less "Indefinite Dyad," "a swift-flowing stream of infinite depth" (fr. 11), coeval and uncreated alongside God. The world is a mixture of the Good and the badness of Matter; hence Numenius praises Plato for teaching *two* world-souls, "one very beneficent, the other malevolent, namely matter" (fr. 16–17). See [[Concepts/Matter as a Malevolent Principle (Numenius)]]. This dualism was explicitly **rejected** by [[Thinkers/Plotinus]].
- **True Being is timeless and incorporeal.** Being "never was, nor ever became, but always *is*" in the present-moment aeon; it neither increases nor moves (fr. 19–20). Being cannot be the four elements or matter (which are generated, transitory, unstable); what holds bodies together must itself be incorporeal — so "the Incorporeal" deserves the highest rank (fr. 11–13). Anticipates and echoes [[Concepts/Parmenidean Being and the One (Parmenides)]] and the *Timaeus* being/becoming split.
- **The soul: immaterial, immortal, one with God.** A regress argument: every body needs a further principle to bind it, on to infinity, until we reach the incorporeal — which is the soul (fr. 44). Numenius posits *two souls* (rational and irrational), not two parts of one soul (fr. 53); teaches "the unification and indiscerptibility of the soul with its source" (fr. 51); and sees embodiment as a fall — "all incarnations are of evil" (fr. 50), the soul retained in the body "as in a prison, by impulsive passion." His allegory of the *Cave of the Nymphs* (Homer, *Odyssey*) reads the cosmos and the soul's descent (through Cancer) and ascent (through Capricorn) astronomically (fr. 54).
- **Radical syncretism / allegory.** "What is Plato but Moses speaking Attic Greek?" (fr. 9c). He quotes the Hebrew prophets, allegorizes Genesis ("the Spirit of God hovered over the water" → souls' affinity for moisture in generation, fr. 35a), tells an allegorized story of Jesus, and interprets Homer as concealed theology (evil demons who feast on sacrificial smoke, fr. 64).
- **"All is in all."** Numenius holds "that all is in all, but in each thing in a manner appropriate to its nature" (fr. 48) — the correspondence axiom later rigidified by [[Thinkers/Iamblichus]] and Proclus.

> [!warning] Conflicts with [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] on matter and evil
> Numenius makes **matter a positive, malevolent, uncreated principle** coeval with God (a Gnostic/dualist strain). Plotinus, defending the goodness of the One's overflow, treats **matter as mere privation** — the last, formless residue of emanation, not a rival power — and explicitly criticizes the Gnostic view. The two mark the fork between *dualist* and *monist* Neoplatonism. See [[Concepts/Matter as a Malevolent Principle (Numenius)]] and [[Concepts/The One (To Hen)]].

## The Academy Polemic

In *On the Divergence of the Academy from Plato*, Numenius argues that Plato's successors betrayed him. Speusippus, Xenocrates, and Polemo kept the doctrine; then Arcesilaus and [[Thinkers/Zeno of Elea|Zeno of Citium]] (the Stoic — not the Eleatic) fell out as rival students of Polemo, and Arcesilaus dragged the Academy into skepticism (the "incomprehensibility of presentation," *akatalēpsia*), which Numenius treats as sophistry masking a Pyrrhonism inherited from Diodorus and Pyrrho. He mocks it with the comic tale of **Lakydes**, whose slaves rob his sealed storeroom and then use his own doctrine of *akatalēpsia* to deny he can ever *know* it was robbed — until Lakydes concedes: "in the school we argue this way, but it is different in life" (fr. on Lakydes). A hostile Middle-Platonic verdict on the [[Thinkers/Sextus Empiricus|skeptical]] Academy as a corruption of the true (Pythagorean) Plato.

## Influence and Reception

- **Plotinus and Ammonius Saccas.** A fragment from Nemesius on the soul's immateriality is ascribed *jointly* to Numenius and Ammonius Saccas, Plotinus's teacher (fr. 66) — placing Numenius squarely in the intellectual lineage behind the *Enneads*. [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] took over the graded intelligible hierarchy and the undiminished-giving model while dropping the malevolent matter and the split Demiurge.
- **Porphyry** "follows the teachings of Numenius" on the soul's pre-incarnational struggle with material demons (fr. 62b) and drew on his *Cave of the Nymphs* allegory. See [[Thinkers/Porphyry]].
- Transmitted the "all is in all" correspondence law to [[Thinkers/Iamblichus]] and the later school.
