# Bryan Caplan
> Democracies fail not because politicians betray voters but because voters get precisely what they irrationally demand.

## Summary

Bryan Caplan (b. 1971) is an American economist at George Mason University who works in public choice theory, political economy, and philosophy of mind. His major contribution is the theory of **rational irrationality**: the claim that voters systematically hold false beliefs about economics — not out of ignorance but out of a rational preference for comforting ideology over truth — and that this irrationality, because it is costless to the individual voter, is the primary driver of bad democratic policy.

Caplan's *The Myth of the Rational Voter* (2007) is his central statement. It attacks two orthodoxies simultaneously: (1) the mainstream democratic theory that voters are merely ignorant but error-cancellingly rational in aggregate (the "Miracle of Aggregation"), and (2) the Classical Public Choice theory (Downs, Tullock) that democratic failure stems from rational ignorance plus interest-group capture. Caplan argues both frameworks presuppose that cognitive failures are random, which the empirical evidence (especially from the 1996 Survey of Americans and Economists on the Economy) refutes. Voter errors are *systematic*, biased in four predictable directions: anti-market, anti-foreign, make-work, and pessimistic.

The mechanism: because one vote almost never determines an election, the private cost of holding an irrational political belief is approximately zero. Voters therefore rationally "consume" the psychological pleasure of their preferred beliefs without restraint. Democracy is a commons for cognition, not a market — and like all commons, it generates a tragedy.

## Key Positions

### The Paradox of Democracy
Democracies persistently adopt policies (e.g., protectionism, occupational licensing) that economists across the political spectrum consider harmful. Standard explanations — interest-group capture, voter ignorance — cannot fully explain this because they require random or interest-driven bias. See [[Concepts/Paradox of Democracy (Caplan)]].

### Rational Irrationality
Extending the public choice tradition, Caplan models irrationality as a consumption good subject to demand. When the price of irrationality falls to zero (as it does for voters who cannot individually affect outcomes), demand for false but emotionally satisfying beliefs rises to the satiation point. This unifies the psychological literature on cognitive bias with economic methodology. See [[Concepts/Rational Irrationality (Caplan)]].

### The Four Systematic Biases
Caplan identifies four directional biases in lay economic belief, documented by comparing public opinion to the consensus of PhD economists: antimarket bias, antiforeign bias, make-work bias, and pessimistic bias. These are not randomly distributed — they systematically push policy toward protection, restriction, and redistribution. See [[Concepts/Systematic Economic Biases (Caplan)]].

### Miracle of Aggregation — Critique
The statistical argument that random errors cancel out in large electorates (giving decisive influence to any informed minority) is valid but assumes random errors. Systematic bias defeats it entirely. See [[Concepts/Miracle of Aggregation]].

### Democratic Fundamentalism
Caplan coins this label for the quasi-religious commitment to democracy that places it beyond critique from any external standard — the mirror image of the "market fundamentalism" charge routinely leveled at economists. See [[Concepts/Democratic Fundamentalism (Caplan)]].

### Reform Implications
Because the problem is irrational demand (not special-interest supply), Caplan's reforms target demand: greater reliance on markets and expert-insulated institutions (courts, central banks), competency requirements for voting, expanded economics and statistics education, and economists adopting a "one-handed" Bastiatic pedagogy — stating simplified but accurate conclusions decisively.

## Intellectual Position

Caplan builds on [[Thinkers/James M. Buchanan]] and [[Thinkers/Gordon Tullock]] (public choice tradition) but revises their framework: where Buchanan-Tullock emphasize *supply-side* failures (politicians shirking voters), Caplan emphasizes *demand-side* failures (voters demanding bad policies). He engages seriously with Donald Wittman's defense of democracy, granting much of Wittman's critique of Classical Public Choice but arguing Wittman never closed off the "extreme voter stupidity" path to democratic failure. He draws on [[Thinkers/Frédéric Bastiat]] as a pedagogical model and on [[Thinkers/Mancur Olson]] as a complementary diagnosis of collective action failures.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Caplan's benchmark for "correct" economic belief is professional economists' consensus — a benchmark critics challenge as itself ideologically skewed toward free-market conclusions. See discussion in [[Sources/The Myth of the Rational Voter (Caplan)]].
- > [!warning] If voters are rationally irrational because their individual votes don't matter, why do they form passionate political identities? Caplan's model predicts apathy, but voters display intensity. He addresses this via "preferences over beliefs" but the tension remains.
- > [!warning] Caplan's reform proposals (epistocracy, expert insulation) face a regress problem: who selects the experts, and by what non-democratic procedure? See implicit tension with [[Thinkers/Plato]] on philosopher-kings.
