# Michel Foucault
> Diagnostician of power, knowledge, and the self — Foucault repeatedly dissolved and reconstituted his own methods in response to concrete historical problems, producing three distinct phases: archaeology, genealogy, and ethics of the self.

## Life Overview

Paul-Michel Foucault was born 15 October 1926 in Poitiers to an affluent, conservative medical family. His father was a prominent surgeon; Foucault identified with his scalpel-as-scalpel imagery ("I transformed the scalpel into a penholder") while refusing medicine as a career. His formative years were shaped by Vichy occupation, the criminalization of homosexuality under Amendment 334 (1942), and provincial Catholic conservatism — all of which fed the problematics of his mature work on confinement, normalization, and the body politic.

At ENS (c. 1946–1951), his masters were Louis Althusser (anti-humanist Marxism), Jean Hyppolite (Hegel), and Georges Canguilhem (history and philosophy of science). He suffered severe depression, self-harm, and suicidal crises connected to unresolved homosexuality; he joined and left the PCF. He obtained a diploma in psychopathology and worked briefly at Sainte-Anne psychiatric hospital and Fresnes prison — environments that seeded *Histoire de la folie* a decade later.

His career was peripatetic: Uppsala (1954–58), Warsaw (1958), Hamburg, Clermont-Ferrand, Tunis (1966–68), the experimental Vincennes department (1969–70), then the Collège de France (1970–84), where his annual public lectures became major intellectual spectacles. He died of AIDS-related illness on 25 June 1984. His partner Daniel Defert founded AIDES, France's first AIDS association, immediately afterward.

## Intellectual Phases

### Phase 1 — Archaeology (c. 1961–1969)
The archaeological method recovers the *episteme* — the unconscious discursive rules governing what can count as knowledge in a given era. Foucault's first major works map three successive Western epistemes: Renaissance similitude, Classical mathesis/taxonomy, and the modern human sciences built on the figure of "Man." Key works: *Folie et déraison* (1961), *Naissance de la clinique* (1963), *Les Mots et les choses* (1966), *L'Archéologie du savoir* (1969).

→ See [[Concepts/History of Madness (Foucault)]], [[Concepts/Episteme and the Order of Things (Foucault)]], [[Concepts/Archaeology of Knowledge (Foucault)]]

### Phase 2 — Genealogy (c. 1970–1979)
Influenced decisively by Nietzsche's *Untimely Meditations* (read in 1953 in Italy), genealogy replaces archaeological excavation with a Nietzschean "history of the present" — tracking not origins but the contingent power struggles through which norms become naturalized. Key works: *Surveiller et punir* (1975), *La Volonté de savoir* (1976), and the Collège de France lecture series. GIP prison activism (1971–) was both expression of and laboratory for this phase.

→ See [[Concepts/Genealogy (Foucault)]], [[Concepts/Power-Knowledge (Foucault)]], [[Concepts/Disciplinary Society and the Panopticon (Foucault)]], [[Concepts/Biopolitics and Governmentality (Foucault)]]

### Phase 3 — Ethics of the Self (c. 1980–1984)
The final phase pivoted toward Greco-Roman practices of self-fashioning: *askesis*, *chresis aphrodision* (use of pleasures), *parrhesia* (truth-telling courage). Foucault reframed ethics not as moral code but as *aesthetics of existence* — the giving of style to one's life. He understood the American gay scene as a laboratory for new relational forms beyond fixed identity. Key works: *L'Usage des plaisirs* (1984), *Le Souci de soi* (1984), and the final Collège lectures.

→ See [[Concepts/Ethics of the Self and Care of the Self (Foucault)]], [[Concepts/History of Sexuality (Foucault)]]

## Key Positions

- **Knowledge is produced, not discovered:** Each historical period's "archive" of possible statements precedes and conditions what can be said — including in science, medicine, and the human sciences.
- **Power is relational, not possessed:** Power is not held by a sovereign and wielded downward; it is exercised in relations, dispersed through institutions, and productive — it produces subjects, bodies, and knowledge, not merely suppresses them.
- **The subject is constituted, not given:** The "human" is a recent historical invention whose disappearance Foucault predicted (and celebrated) in *Les Mots et les choses*; subjectivity is produced through technologies of power and technologies of the self.
- **Genealogy over teleology:** History has no direction or progressive structure; what appears as progress (liberation of the mad, reform of punishment) typically conceals a transformation and intensification of control.

## Intellectual Lineage

**Masters:** Canguilhem (epistemological discontinuity, history of science), Hyppolite (Hegel), Althusser (anti-humanist structural Marxism), Nietzsche (genealogy, diagnostics).

**Contemporaries:** Collaborated closely with [[Thinkers/Gilles Deleuze]] (especially "Intellectuals and Power," 1972); engaged in sustained polemics with Sartre ("last philosopher of the nineteenth century") and Derrida (charged deconstruction with giving "limitless sovereignty to the master's voice").

**Institutional lineage:** Inherits the French philosophy-of-concepts tradition (Bachelard → Canguilhem → Foucault) against the philosophy-of-subjectivity (Husserl → Sartre → phenomenology).

## Key Contradictions

- Foucault's retrospective self-placement in a "philosophy of concepts" tradition conflicts with his earliest works, which are saturated with phenomenology and existentialism — Macey flags this as post-hoc narrative.
- He rejected "speaking on behalf of others" as shameful yet was the unambiguous public face of the GIP.
- He theorized liberation discourse as an instrument of normalization while pursuing limit-experiences in practice — and dismissed early AIDS reports while frequenting the bath-house culture where he was exposed.
- He championed universal human rights in the late 1970s while his genealogical work treated "universal" rights as instruments of normalizing power.

> [!warning] Tension with [[Thinkers/Jean-Paul Sartre]]: Foucault's "death of man" and anti-humanism directly attacks Sartre's existentialist subject; the two represented opposed poles of French intellectual life. See also [[Thinkers/Gilles Deleuze]] for the most productive collaboration and eventual rupture.
