# Parmenides

> Greek philosopher (c. 515 – 450 BCE) from the colony of Elea in southern Italy. Founder of the Eleatic school and the first to introduce deductive logic to metaphysics.

## Key Contributions

**The Way of Truth (Aletheia)**:
- Wrote a philosophical poem in hexameter verse detailing a revelation by a goddess.
- Formulated the fundamental axiom: **Being is, and Non-Being is not**. 
- What is not cannot be thought of, spoken of, or exist.
- Deduced that **Being is one, eternal, indivisible, homogeneous, and changeless**:
  - *Generateless*: Being cannot come from nothing (Non-Being) or from something else.
  - *Indestructible*: Being cannot pass into nothingness.
  - *Changeless*: Change requires something to become what it is not, which implies Non-Being.
  - *Full*: There is no empty space (void), since void is Non-Being.

**The Way of Belief (Doxa)**:
- Described the physical, changing world of sensory experience as a deceptive illusion (*doxa*).
- Sensation tells us that things change, move, are born, and die. However, because these processes are logically contradictory (requiring empty space and non-being), we must follow reason (*logos*) rather than our senses.

**Deductive Metaphysics**:
- First philosopher to use strict, formal deduction to arrive at a metaphysical system, starting from a single self-evident premise and deducing all subsequent qualities.

## Relevance to Vault

- **The Parmenidean Challenge**: Parmenides' logic created a major crisis in early Greek thought. If change is logically impossible, how do we explain the visible world? All subsequent Pre-Socratic systems (pluralists like Empedocles and Anaxagoras, and atomists like Democritus) were designed specifically to answer Parmenides by showing how change can occur without creating or destroying basic substances.
- **Root of Rationalism**: Initiated the rationalist tradition that holds reason and logic to be superior to sensory observation.
- **Influence on Plato**: Plato's realm of Forms inherits all the characteristics of Parmenidean Being (eternal, changeless, indivisible, accessible only to the intellect). Plato dedicates an entire dialogue (*Parmenides*) to examining these concepts.
- **As Plato's interlocutor** (in the *Parmenides* dialogue, dramatic date c. 450 BCE): Plato casts the venerable Eleatic — "reverend and awe-inspiring" — as the one who dismantles young Socrates' naive Theory of Forms (the [[Concepts/Participation Problems and the Critique of the Forms (Plato Parmenides)|participation critique]], the [[Concepts/The Third Man Argument|Third Man]]), then leads the eight-deduction gymnastic on the One (the [[Concepts/The Eight Deductions - Hypotheses about the One (Plato Parmenides)|hypotheses about the One]]). On Hermann's reading this is homage, not parricide: Plato tests his own Forms against the "father of disproof." The dialogue's first deduction — the One that is not even one — became the Neoplatonic **One beyond Being** ([[Concepts/The One (To Hen)]]).
