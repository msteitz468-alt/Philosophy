# Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778)

> Genevan philosopher, writer, and composer. His political philosophy influenced the progress of the Age of Enlightenment, the French Revolution, and the development of modern political, ethical, and educational thought.

## Life and Context

Born in Geneva, Switzerland, Rousseau led a nomadic, unstable life across Switzerland, Italy, and France. He rose to fame in Paris after winning the essay competition of the Academy of Dijon in 1750 with his *Discourse on the Arts and Sciences*, in which he argued that the progress of civilization had corrupted, rather than improved, human morals.

Rousseau's major works include *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality* (1755), *Julie, or the New Heloise* (1761), *Emile, or On Education* (1762), and *The Social Contract* (1762). Due to his critiques of traditional religion and monarchy, *Emile* and *The Social Contract* were banned in France and Geneva, forcing him to flee into exile (spending time in England with David Hume, with whom he had a famous falling out). He died in France, and his remains were later moved to the Panthéon in Paris during the French Revolution.

---

## Major Works

- *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality* (1755): Traces human development from a peaceful, solitary state of nature to civil society, showing how property and socialization created inequality and moral corruption.
- *The Social Contract* (1762): Outlines the foundations of a legitimate political order, proposing that sovereignty lies with the collective body of citizens acting through the "General Will."
- *Emile, or On Education* (1762): A philosophical novel detailing the natural education of a boy, emphasizing the preservation of natural virtue against social corruption.

---

## Key Philosophical Positions

* **The Noble Savage**: Rousseau argued that humans are naturally good, peaceful, and compassionate in the state of nature, but are corrupted by civil society and the introduction of private property.
* **The Social Contract**: Rousseau's political theory begins with the famous line: *"Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains."* The social contract is his solution: a form of association where citizens unite while remaining as free as before, by alienating their individual rights to the whole community.
* **The General Will**: The collective interest of the sovereign citizenry. Unlike the "will of all" (the sum of private self-interests), the General Will represents the common good and is the only legitimate basis for law.
* **Popular Sovereignty**: Rousseau rejected representative government, arguing that sovereignty is indivisible and inalienable, and must be exercised directly by the citizens in assembly.

---

## Influence and Legacy

Rousseau was a transitional figure between the Enlightenment and Romanticism. His emphasis on feeling, nature, and natural virtue against cold rationalism inspired the Romantic movement. Politically, his concepts of the General Will and popular sovereignty became central to the French Revolution (he was the favorite philosopher of the Jacobins). 

His educational theories laid the foundation for modern child-centered education. However, his political theories have also been critiqued by liberals (like John Stuart Mill) who warned that his emphasis on forcing citizens to conform to the General Will could lead to democratic tyranny or totalitarianism.

## Relevance to Vault

Rousseau provides a crucial counterpoint to the political realism of Thomas Hobbes:
- **State of Nature Contrast**: Rousseau's peaceful, pity-driven natural state directly opposes Hobbes' "nasty, brutish, and short" war of all against all. See [[Contradictions/Rousseau vs. Hobbes on the State of Nature|Rousseau vs. Hobbes on the State of Nature]].
- **The Social Contract Traditions**: Expands the contractarian cluster alongside [[Thinkers/Thomas Hobbes|Hobbes]] and [[Thinkers/Locke|Locke]], showing a radical democratic alternative to Hobbes' absolutism and Locke's constitutionalism.
- **Ethics of Compassion**: Rousseau's grounding of morality in natural pity (*pitié*) contrasts with Kant's duty-based rationalism.
