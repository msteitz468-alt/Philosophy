# A.J. Ayer (1910–1989)

> British philosopher; the principal English-language voice of logical positivism; author of *Language, Truth and Logic* (1936), which brought the Vienna Circle's verification program to an Anglo-American audience and sold hundreds of thousands of copies as one of analytic philosophy's defining manifestos.

## Life and Context

Alfred Jules Ayer was born in St. John's Wood, London. He won a King's Scholarship to Eton, then read "Modern Greats" (PPE) at Christ Church, Oxford, where Gilbert Ryle was his philosophy tutor (Ryle called him "the best philosopher I have yet been taught by, among my pupils"). After graduating with a first in 1932, Ayer spent four months in Vienna, attending meetings of the **Vienna Circle** — the logical positivists led by Moritz Schlick with Otto Neurath and Rudolf Carnap. He returned to Oxford intent on revolution.

*Language, Truth and Logic* was written between 1934 and 1935, when Ayer was 24–25, published 1936 (Gollancz). It was an immediate *succès de scandale*: Peter Strawson read it "in one absorbed sitting"; Oxford's conservative Master of Balliol threw a copy out his window. By the mid-1970s it had sold 400,000+ copies across Britain and America and become a standard undergraduate text. Ayer described it as "a young man's book… written with more passion than most philosophers allow themselves to show."

Career: Christ Church Lecturer (1933), Research Student (1935); wartime Military Intelligence (Welsh Guards); Fellow and Dean, Wadham College, Oxford (1945); **Grote Professor of Philosophy of Mind and Logic, University of London** (1946–59); **Wykeham Professor of Logic, Oxford** (1959–78); Fellow of New College then Wolfson College; Fellow of the British Academy; knighted 1970 (KBE); Chevalier de la Légion d'honneur. Later books include *The Foundations of Empirical Knowledge*, *The Problem of Knowledge*, *The Central Questions of Philosophy*, *Philosophy in the Twentieth Century*; also wrote on Russell, Hume, Wittgenstein, and Voltaire; two volumes of autobiography. Died June 1989.

## The Book and Its Sources

Ayer synthesizes three traditions into a single slender manifesto:

1. **Hume** ("always my master") — Hume's Fork (relations of ideas vs. matters of fact), the "flames" meaning-test, empiricism, mitigated scepticism.
2. **Vienna Circle** (especially Carnap) — the verification principle, the program of logical analysis as philosophy's proper method, the equation of meaningful discourse with the formal + the empirically testable.
3. **Russell and Wittgenstein** — Russell's theory of descriptions (paradigm for "definitions in use"), the program of philosophical analysis, Wittgenstein's *Tractatus* (logic as tautological, the "showing/saying" distinction in background).

## Core Positions (from *Language, Truth and Logic*)

### The Verification Principle
The central criterion: a sentence is *factually significant* iff some possible sense-experience would be relevant to determining its truth or falsehood. If a putative statement is consistent with every possible experience and is not a tautology, it is a *pseudo-proposition* — literally meaningless (not false). The weak form (relevance of possible experience) is required because the strong form (conclusive verifiability) condemns scientific laws as nonsense. See [[Concepts/Verification Principle (Ayer)]].

### Metaphysics as Literally Meaningless
Metaphysical sentences fail the verification test and are not tautologies; therefore they say nothing — they are neither true nor false. The charge is not that metaphysics is mere speculation or false, but that it is *senseless*. Sources of metaphysical confusion: linguistic grammar misleads us into positing substance, Being, subsistent entities. Transcendent theology ("there is a God") is metaphysical = meaningless; neither atheism nor agnosticism is appropriate since both presuppose the question is genuine. See [[Concepts/Metaphysics as Meaningless (Ayer)]].

### A Priori = Analytic = Tautological
All necessary and certain propositions (logic, mathematics, geometry) are analytic: "true solely in virtue of the definitions of the symbols they contain." They record our linguistic conventions; they are tautologies. They say *nothing about the world*. This defeats rationalism (no synthetic a priori) and Mill's empiricism (a priori truths are not inductive generalizations). They can surprise us through computational complexity, but their necessity is logical, not factual. See [[Concepts/Analytic Propositions as Tautologies (Ayer)]].

### Philosophy as Analysis
Philosophy's function is wholly critical and analytic — not to deliver first principles or speculative truths, but to provide *definitions in use*, to elicit the formal/logical consequences of our linguistic conventions. The philosopher is "not in a position to furnish speculative truths which would compete with the hypotheses of science." Philosophy should develop into the *logic of science*. Ayer endorses Russell's analysis program; [[Concepts/Philosophy of Logical Analysis (Russell)]] is the closest existing vault concept.

### The Emotive Theory of Ethics
Normative ethical symbols ("wrong," "good," "ought") are *pseudo-concepts*: adding them to a factual description adds no factual content. "You acted wrongly in stealing" says no more than "You stole" — the ethical word expresses moral disapproval, not a further assertion. Ethical judgments are *expressions and excitants of feeling*, neither true nor false. What passes for moral argument is really argument about *facts* (motives, consequences, circumstances); when facts are settled and attitudes still diverge, argument ends. The same analysis applies to aesthetics. See [[Concepts/Emotive Theory of Ethics (Ayer)]].

### Redundancy Theory of Truth
"'p is true' is logically superfluous — to say that p is true is just to assert p." There is no substantial analysis of truth to be given; the "problem of truth" is a pseudo-problem generated by grammar. This directly undercuts both the correspondence theory ([[Concepts/Correspondence Theory of Truth (Russell)]]) and the pragmatic theory ([[Concepts/Pragmatic Theory of Truth (James)]]) as analyses of a concept that does not require analysis.

### Pragmatic Dissolution of Induction
Ayer says the problem of induction is a *fictitious problem*: there is no solution, because no solution is needed. Inductive procedure is justified by its practical success; demanding a logical guarantee is asking for the impossible. Rationality is defined as following an accredited, self-consistent procedure. Compare with [[Thinkers/Karl Popper]], who treats the induction problem as genuine but bypasses it via falsifiability, and with [[Thinkers/Hume]], who regards it as a genuine and unanswerable sceptical challenge.

### Phenomenalism
Material things are *logical constructions* out of sense-contents: all sentences about material things are in principle translatable into sentences about actual and possible sense-contents. The self is similarly a logical construction out of sense-experiences, with personal identity grounded in bodily continuity. This connects to Russell's [[Concepts/Sense-Data vs. Physical Objects (Russell)|sense-data framework]] and resolves Berkeley's difficulties without Berkeley's theism.

## Relations to Other Vault Thinkers

- **[[Thinkers/Hume]]**: Ayer's closest intellectual ancestor. The Fork = direct ancestor of the analytic/empirical division; the "flames" passage = "a rhetorical version of our own thesis"; Ayer inherits causation-as-constant-conjunction, mitigated scepticism, and the Copy Principle's meaning-test.
- **[[Thinkers/Bertrand Russell]]**: supplies the logical-analysis program (theory of descriptions, "definitions in use," sense-data, philosophical analysis as anti-metaphysical elucidation) and the liberal empiricist tradition.
- **[[Thinkers/W.V.O. Quine]]**: Quine's "Two Dogmas" (1951) is the decisive internal critique of *everything* Ayer's program depends on — both the analytic/synthetic distinction and the verification theory of meaning. If Quine is right, Ayer's clean division cannot be maintained. The vault's primary confrontation between classical logical empiricism and its post-positivist dissolution.
- **[[Thinkers/Karl Popper]]**: a contemporary critic from within the anti-metaphysics camp. Popper disagrees that verifiability is the right criterion (use falsifiability for *demarcation*, not *meaning*), treats the induction problem as genuine (not fictitious), and rejects the identification of metaphysics with nonsense in principle (some metaphysical research programs are fruitful). See [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)]].
- **[[Thinkers/Kant]]**: Ayer's main theoretical opponent. Kant's synthetic a priori is the key target; Ayer argues all Kant's purported examples are analytic. The "transcendental aesthetic" falls with it.
- **[[Thinkers/Carnap]]** (not yet a vault entity): closest Vienna Circle ally. The *Aufbau* is the most serious prior attempt to reduce physical-object language to sense-data; Ayer adopts the spirit while liberalizing the criterion.
- **[[Thinkers/Locke]]**, **[[Thinkers/Berkeley]]**: part of Ayer's empiricist lineage; he claims they were primarily analysts, not metaphysicians (Locke "clearing the ground"; Berkeley correctly seeing material things must be defined in terms of sense-contents).

## 1946 Revisions (Appendix)

Ayer added a Preface to the second edition acknowledging four problems:
1. The original verification principle is too liberal (admits anything); revised to distinguish *directly* and *indirectly* verifiable statements.
2. Analytic propositions are not *descriptions* of linguistic usage (that would make them empirical), nor are they arbitrary linguistic rules (they are necessary, given the rules presupposed). They *elucidate* the use of logical constants.
3. The emotive theory needs a richer account of how moral language functions to arouse action and influence others.
4. The account of other minds remains problematic; Ayer leans toward a behaviourist reading.

## Relevance to Vault

- **For the Philosophy of Science leg**: Ayer is the canonical English-language positivist — the position that Popper, Kuhn, and Quine are all reacting against. Understanding Ayer is essential for understanding what those three are doing.
- **For epistemology/meaning**: the verification principle, the analytic/empirical divide, and the redundancy theory of truth are the backdrop for Quine's holism and the post-positivist turn.
- **For ethics**: emotivism is one of the three major 20th-c. metaethical positions (alongside moral realism and subjectivism); understanding Ayer's formulation sharpens contrast with Kant's deontology and Aristotle's eudaimonia.
