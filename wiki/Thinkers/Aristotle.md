# Aristotle

> Athenian philosopher (384–322 BCE), student of Plato, founder of the Lyceum (Peripatetic school). Author of the *Organon* (including *Prior Analytics* on logic and modal syllogistic), *Nicomachean Ethics*, *Rhetoric*, and works on metaphysics, biology, politics, and poetics. His thought profoundly shaped Western philosophy, science, and logic.

## Key Contributions from Ingested Sources

**Modal Logic (Organon / Prior Analytics, via Richard Patterson)**:
- Modal propositions use a **modal copula** (“necessarily applies”, “possibly applies”) rather than operators on whole propositions.
- Two readings of necessity (weak/extensional vs strong/essential) grounded in the metaphysics of essence vs accident.
- Logic is not independent of metaphysics; scientific demonstration (*apodeixis*) relies on *per se* (essential) predications.
- Critiques modern de dicto/de re frameworks as incomplete for capturing Aristotle’s system.

**Nicomachean Ethics (Book I)**:
- Every action/expertise aims at some good; there is a highest good: *eudaimonia* (happiness/flourishing) as activity of the soul in accordance with virtue (*arête*) over a complete life (*teleiotēs*).
- Function (*ergon*) argument: the human good lies in performing the characteristic human activity (rational soul) excellently.
- Political science is the most architectonic expertise, directing others and aiming at the good of the city (greater than for one individual).
- Critiques the lives of pleasure (vulgar), honor (political), and wealth (instrumental). Requires good upbringing/habituation for ethical understanding.

**Rhetoric (Book I)**:
- Rhetoric is the counterpart of dialectic: the art of observing the available means of persuasion in any given case.
- Artistic proofs: *logos* (argument), *ethos* (speaker character), *pathos* (audience emotion).
- Central tools: *enthymeme* (rhetorical syllogism from probabilities/signs) and *example* (induction).
- Three branches: deliberative (future, expedient), forensic (past, just/unjust), epideictic (present, praise/blame).
- Critiques earlier manuals for over-focusing on emotional manipulation at the expense of substance and the integrity of judgment.

## Skeptical Counterpoint (Sextus Empiricus / Pyrrhonism)

Sextus Empiricus opens the *Outlines of Pyrrhonism* by naming "the followers of Aristotle and Epicurus, the Stoics, and certain others" as the paradigmatic *Dogmatists* who "think that they have found [the truth]." Pyrrhonian Skepticism is the third philosophy (Skeptics continue to search), using systematic equipollent opposition (the 10 and 5 Modes) to produce epoché and ataraxia.

Direct engagements:
- Modes target essentialism, scientific demonstration (*apodeixis*), and modal claims (Organon): relativity, disagreement among "Dogmatists," regress on criteria of truth, hypothesis.
- God/cause critiques (Book III) undermine Aristotle's prime mover/unmoved mover and final causes (productive sources, teleology).
- Material sources disagreement explicitly includes Peripatetic elements + aether.
- Ataraxia via suspension (no resolution of anomaly) contrasts eudaimonia as excellent activity of soul in accordance with virtue over complete life.

See new [[Sources/Outlines of Pyrrhonism - Sextus Empiricus]], [[Thinkers/Sextus Empiricus]], [[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]], [[Concepts/Equipollence (Isosthenia) and the Modes]]. Supplies the ancient skeptical counter-synthesis to the Aristotelian thread (and the classical cluster as a whole). Enriches the good (eudaimonia vs. ataraxia-via-epoché) and method (opposition before commitment for scaffolds, lint, ingest). ## Medieval Synthesis and Elevation (Thomas Aquinas)

Thomas Aquinas is the great medieval commentator and completer of Aristotle for a Christian context (he wrote a full commentary on the *Nicomachean Ethics*). In *Summa Theologica* I-II he takes up the function/ergon argument, eudaimonia as excellent activity of the rational soul over a complete life, the architectonic role of political science, the critiques of pleasure/honor/wealth, and the need for good upbringing/habituation — and shows that natural happiness is real and good (via acquired virtues) but imperfect and incomplete.

- Ultimate last end/happiness (*beatitudo*) is the beatific vision: direct contemplation of God in His essence by the glorified intellect (supernatural, requiring grace).
- "Grace does not destroy nature but perfects it" — the natural (Aristotelian) order is elevated, not abolished.
- Natural law (elsewhere in I-II) grounds Aristotelian natural right in eternal law (rational creature's participation via "the light of natural reason").
- Theological virtues (faith, hope, charity) perfect the cardinal virtues.

This provides the medieval capstone to the vault's Aristotle material: eudaimonia completed as beatitude; natural ethics grounded in law and elevated by grace. See new [[Sources/Summa Theologica, Part I-II - Saint Thomas Aquinas]], [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]], [[Concepts/Beatific Vision - Last End (Aquinas)]], [[Concepts/Natural Law (Aquinas)]]. Enriches [[Concepts/Eudaimonia]] and the entire classical cluster with systematic scholastic synthesis and the nature/grace theme (highly relevant to the LLM Wiki pattern itself).

## Islamic Transmission and Defense (Ibn Rushd / Averroes)

The *Tahafut al-Tahafut* (Incoherence of the Incoherence) by Ibn Rushd (Averroes) is the primary Islamic source defending a rigorous, largely literal Aristotelian position against Ghazali’s Ash‘arite theological critique. It supplies the exact philosophical opponent that Aquinas later corrects on the soul and intellect while himself relying on Aristotelian causation and hylomorphism.

- **Eternity of the World**: Aristotle as the first to affirm an eternal ordered cosmos (no absolute beginning; time/motion eternal; God as eternal simultaneous cause). Averroes refutes Ghazali’s arguments from “eternal will” and infinite series as equivocal and dialectical; notes real tensions in Aristotle’s own system (eternal motion vs. finite causal series; potentiality vs. his logic of being/non-being).
- **Real Secondary Causation vs. Occasionalism**: The central natural-sciences discussion. Ghazali denies necessary connections — fire does not burn by nature; God creates every effect directly (custom/habit makes it predictable; miracles are easy because nothing has fixed power). Averroes calls this sophistry: it destroys essences, definitions, intellect (which knows things *with their causes*), logic, and science itself. Things have real specific natures/powers (four causes); observed regularities are genuine. Impediments or special divine action on dispositions can explain the unusual without abolishing nature. See dedicated [[Concepts/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation (Ghazali and Ibn Rushd)]] and [[Concepts/Eternity of the World (Ibn Rushd)]].
- **Soul, Knowledge, Resurrection**: Engages Avicenna on individuation and the active intellect. Defends forms of survival and the necessity of religious law (as political art from reason + inspiration) for mass virtue, while protecting demonstrative philosophy for the qualified. Philosophers publicly conform to religious principles.
- **Influence on Aquinas**: The Latin reception of Aristotle was heavily mediated by Averroes’ commentaries. Aquinas cites “the Commentator” constantly — adopting much of the causal and hylomorphic framework while explicitly refuting the unicity of the intellect and related errors that would undermine personal immortality, moral responsibility, and “Socrates understands” (see Summa I, Q. 76). The *Tahafut* now grounds those existing anti-Averroist notes in the vault.

This adds the crucial Islamic link in the transmission chain: Greek Aristotle → Neoplatonized Islamic *falasifa* (Avicenna et al.) → Averroes’ literal defense → Latin scholastic debate and Aquinas’ synthesis. Enriches the entire classical cluster with the faith/reason, elites/masses, and causation/nature debates. See new [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]], [[Sources/Incoherence of the Incoherence - Ibn Rushd]].
