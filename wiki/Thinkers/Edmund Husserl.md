# Edmund Husserl
> Founder of phenomenology — the rigorous, first-person science of conscious experience — whose philosophical system integrates logic, ontology, phenomenology, epistemology, and ethics through the central concept of intentionality.

## Summary

Edmund Husserl (1859–1938) was a Czech-Austrian-German philosopher who founded phenomenology as a systematic discipline. Born in Prossnitz, Moravia to a Jewish family (later converting to Lutheranism), he trained as a mathematician under Karl Weierstrass in Berlin before turning to philosophy under [[Thinkers/Franz Brentano]] in Vienna (1884–86). Brentano gave him the concept of intentionality and sent him to Carl Stumpf in Halle, where Husserl spent fifteen years (1887–1901) developing the foundations of his system.

His first book, *Philosophy of Arithmetic* (1891), attempted a genetic-psychological account of number and was charged by [[Thinkers/Gottlob Frege]] with psychologism (reducing logic to psychology). Husserl accepted the critique — more fully than the text strictly warranted — and responded with *Logical Investigations* (1900–01), a two-volume work that demolishes psychologism, develops a theory of ideal meanings, and lays the framework for all his subsequent philosophy. Smith calls this "his magnum opus" and the "enduring roadmap" of his system.

Husserl held chairs at Göttingen (1901–16) and Freiburg (1916–28). At Freiburg, [[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]] served as his assistant (1919–23) and succeeded him in the chair (1928). *Being and Time* (1927) was dedicated "in admiration and friendship" to Husserl; this dedication was later removed. When the Nazis came to power in 1933, Heidegger as Rektor enforced the ban excluding Husserl (as a Jew) from the university. Their relationship ended in estrangement.

Husserl left approximately 40,000 pages of shorthand manuscripts (the Nachlass), smuggled to the Husserl Archives in Louvain, Belgium, before Nazi confiscation. These posthumous texts — *Ideas II*, *On the Phenomenology of Internal Time-Consciousness*, *Experience and Judgment*, and *The Crisis of European Sciences* — substantially expand the picture of his philosophy.

## Life and Intellectual Development

**Three phases:**
- **Halle (1887–1901)**: Mathematics → logic → intentionality. *Philosophy of Arithmetic* (1891); *Logical Investigations* (1900–01).
- **Göttingen (1901–16)**: Systematic expansion. *Ideas I* (1913, the "transcendental turn"); drafts of *Ideas II* and *III*; lectures on time-consciousness, thing and space, ethics, Kant.
- **Freiburg (1916–38)**: Transcendental and genetic phenomenology; *Formal and Transcendental Logic* (1929); *Cartesian Meditations* (1931); *Crisis* (1935–38).

**Key intellectual influences:**
- [[Thinkers/Franz Brentano]]: intentionality, the priority of descriptive over explanatory method
- Bernard Bolzano: propositions-in-themselves as ideal objects; logic as theory of theories
- [[Thinkers/Gottlob Frege]]: anti-psychologism (via critique); parallel development of sense/reference
- Georg Cantor: set theory; mathematical influence on the theory of manifolds
- David Hilbert: formal axiomatics, inspiring the theory of definite manifolds
- [[Thinkers/Immanuel Kant]]: transcendental idealism as interlocutor from *Ideas I* onward

## Husserl's Philosophical System

Husserl's philosophy is *not* phenomenology standing alone but a tightly interdependent system in which **pure logic**, **formal and material ontology**, **transcendental phenomenology**, **epistemology**, and **ethics** mutually support one another. The common reduction of Husserl to phenomenology misrepresents the architecture. Smith's central thesis: the concept of **dependence** (Fundierung) is the spine that holds the system together — parts depend on each other, often mutually, just as colour and extension are mutually dependent moments of a thing.

The system has a **metatheory** (pure logic as theory of theories, following Bolzano), which applies recursively to all theories including phenomenology itself.

## Logic and Anti-Psychologism

*Logical Investigations* opens with a 300-page demolition of psychologism (the reduction of logical/mathematical laws to facts about how humans think). Husserl argues: (1) logical laws are not empirical generalizations; (2) they are objective, a priori laws about ideal meanings; (3) psychologism leads inevitably to relativism — if logic depends on species-specific psychology, there can be no absolute truth.

**Pure logic** (following Bolzano) is the "theory of theories" — a philosophical account of what any unified scientific theory is: an ideal system of propositions about a domain, governed by formal categories of meaning and formal categories of objects.

**The four-level correlation schema**: Sentences → linguistic meanings (Bedeutungen) → intentional senses (Sinne) → states of affairs (Sachverhalte) in the world. Language "stamps" sense, modifying it rather than transparently expressing an underlying judgment.

Husserl's pure logic anticipates later model theory: a **manifold** (Mannigfaltigkeit) is the form of a field of objects — the objective correlate of a theory's form, prefiguring Tarski's model-theoretic notion.

## Ontology: Formal and Material

Husserl's ontology begins from the Fact/Essence distinction: every object is either a concrete individual occurring contingently in space-time (Fact) or an ideal, nonspatiotemporal entity that "determines" concrete objects (Essence).

**Formal ontology**: Studies objects *qua* objects, under empty logical forms applying to *any* domain — Individual, Property, Relation, State of Affairs, Number, Whole, Part, Dependence, Manifold. "Arguably new in the history of metaphysics" (Smith). Contrasts with Aristotle's object-categories and Kant's concept-categories.

**Material ontology**: Studies substantive regions with specific properties. Three highest-level material regions: (1) **Nature** (spatiotemporal, causal); (2) **Consciousness** (intentional, lived experience); (3) **Culture/Geist** (persons, norms, institutions).

**Mereology** (theory of parts): Independent parts = *pieces* (can exist apart from the whole). Dependent parts = *moments* (cannot exist apart — e.g., this-whiteness in this vase). *Dependence/Fundierung*: A depends on B iff, by laws of essence, A cannot exist unless B does. This is a formal category, applicable across all regions.

**Mind-body**: "I" am one individual substrate whose moments fall under distinct regions — a Consciousness-moment (intentional experience), a Nature-moment (body/brain), and a Culture-moment (person/moral agent). These moments stand in dependence relations; the consciousness-moment depends on the Nature-moment without being identical to it.

See [[Concepts/Formal and Material Ontology (Husserl)]] and [[Concepts/Essences and Eidetic Variation (Husserl)]].

## Phenomenology: Method and Theory

**Definition** (*Ideas I*, §34): Phenomenology is "the science of the essence of consciousness." It is a transcendental, not naturalistic, science; it studies consciousness "from the first-person point of view."

**Intentionality**: Every act of consciousness is directed toward an object via an ideal noematic sense. Structure: *act (noesis) — noematic sense → object ) horizon*. Consciousness is always of the object, not of the sense; the sense becomes visible only in reflection. The noema has a thetic/positing character (perceiving, judging, desiring) and a sense-nucleus: the "pure X" (determinable subject) + predicate-senses attributing properties to X.

**Method — epoché / bracketing**: The phenomenological method is not Cartesian doubt but the suspension (*Einklammerung*) of the "general thesis of the natural attitude" — we bracket the question of the existence of objects in order to attend to the structure of consciousness of them. The positive device is "noematic quotation": shifting attention from "the tree" to "the tree as perceived." Husserl insists this is not skepticism or Berkeleyan idealism.

**Time-consciousness**: Every perceptual moment comprises a *primal impression* (current phase), *retentions* (just-past phases held in the present), and *protentions* (just-coming phases anticipated). "The flow of consciousness constitutes its own unity" — a "double intentionality" that constitutes both the object-in-time (the melody) and the stream of consciousness itself.

**Embodiment**: Space as experienced is a visual-kinesthetic field centered on the *lived body* (Leib). Kinesthetic sensations of movement partly constitute the visual field and the spatial orientation of objects. Husserl distinguishes Leib (body as lived from inside) from Körper (body as inanimate physical thing).

**Constitution**: Not idealist creation ex nihilo but the structured correlation between noematic sense (+ horizon of further possible experiences) and the intended object's structure. Consciousness "constitutes" objects in the sense that it provides the structure of meaning through which they are presented.

See [[Concepts/Intentionality and Noema (Husserl)]], [[Concepts/Phenomenological Reduction and Epoché (Husserl)]], [[Concepts/Transcendental Ego and Constitution (Husserl)]].

## Intersubjectivity and the Life-World

**Empathy (Einfühlung)**: Objects are constituted as "there for everyone" via empathy — the intuitive transfer of the sense "I" to another, experiencing the other as a bodily-psychic being. Empathy grounds intersubjectivity, which grounds objectivity. Edith Stein deepened Husserl's account of empathy.

**Lebenswelt (life-world)**: The pre-scientific, everyday world of lived experience presupposed by all scientific knowledge. The *Crisis* (1935–38) diagnoses a cultural malaise in which mathematical physics, since Galileo's "mathematization of nature," has lost contact with the life-world. This "crisis" is connected to the rise of irrationalism (including Nazism).

**Cartesian Meditations and solipsism**: The epoché appears to trap the phenomenologist in solipsism. Husserl tries to rebut this via the "Fifth Meditation" analysis of the other as an "analogical apperception," but remained unsatisfied with the argument.

See [[Concepts/Intersubjectivity and the Life-World (Husserl)]].

## Epistemology

Husserl positions himself as the "genuine positivist" — grounding all sciences in what is "originarily grasped," admitting all kinds of intuition as "equally valuable sources for the justification of knowledge." This goes beyond empiricism (which admits only sensory experience) while also going beyond logic-centered rationalism.

**Types of intuition**: (1) Sensory perception; (2) **Eidetic intuition** (Wesenserschauung — grasping essences through imaginative variation); (3) **Categorial intuition** (grasping formal structures like State of Affairs, Number, Individual); (4) Phenomenological reflection on one's own experience.

**Three grades of evidence**: (a) *Certainty* — the subject does not doubt the object's existence; (b) *Adequacy* — all aspects are evidently given (rarely achieved); (c) *Apodicticity* — non-being of the object is absolutely inconceivable. Perception is certain but inadequate and non-apodictic; phenomenological self-reflection is apodictic but not adequate.

**Truth as fulfillment**: A judgment is known (true) when the intention is "fulfilled" by an intuitive experience presenting the very state of affairs intended. This is the phenomenological correlate of verification.

**Against Kant's Ding an sich**: Husserl rejects the unknowable thing-in-itself. When perception is intuitively fulfilled, it reaches the existing thing itself — not merely a phenomenon.

See [[Concepts/Evidence and Truth (Husserl)]].

## Ethics

Husserl's ethics parallels his logic in structure. **Pure (formal) ethics** is an a priori theoretical discipline studying the forms of normative propositions, analogous to pure logic. **Material ethics** concerns substantive moral maxims. Every normative domain has a **ground norm** (Grundnorm) — not itself a normative proposition but the formal measure defining normativity within that domain.

Husserl's preferred ground norm (following Brentano): "Do the best that is attainable." He criticizes Kantian ethics for excluding love and compassion, utilitarian ethics for reducing ethics to a calculus, and Humean feeling-ethics for losing objectivity. In later lectures, moral values are "values of love" grounded in empathy with others.

The **life-world** is not a mere fact-world but a "value-world, goods-world, practical world" (*Ideas I*, §27). Values are features of the objects we experience, not merely projections of feeling.

See [[Concepts/Formal and Material Ethics (Husserl)]].

## Legacy

**Continental tradition**: Husserl's students and interlocutors include [[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]] (fundamental ontology), [[Thinkers/Jean-Paul Sartre]] (existential phenomenology), Maurice Merleau-Ponty (embodied perception), Emmanuel Levinas (ethics of the Other), Edith Stein (empathy), and Hans-Georg Gadamer (hermeneutics). Derrida's deconstruction is a radicalisation of Heidegger's *Destruktion* of phenomenology.

**Analytic tradition**: Husserl interacted with Cantor and Hilbert; Frege corresponded with him. Carnap attended his 1924–25 lectures; Tarski cites the *Logical Investigations* in his 1933 theory of truth; Gödel in his later years turned to Husserlian phenomenology to support mathematical Platonism. The analytic/continental divide obscured these connections.

**Philosophy of mind today**: Husserl's moment ontology — consciousness-moment and neural-moment as distinct dependent parts of one individual — is Smith's proposed response to the "hard problem of consciousness." His theory of intersubjectivity and embodiment has been taken up in "neurophenomenology" (Varela) and the embodied cognition movement (drawing on Merleau-Ponty).

See [[Contradictions/Husserl vs Naturalism — Phenomenal Consciousness and the Hard Problem]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Whether Husserl's mature transcendental idealism (*Ideas I* §49: the world's being is "relative" to consciousness) is compatible with his systematic realism (objective meanings directed toward mind-independent states of affairs) is the central unresolved controversy in Husserl scholarship. Smith reads the idealist passages as absorbed neo-Kantian idiom, not settled ontology.
- > [!warning] The epoché appears to generate solipsism; Husserl's intersubjectivity arguments in *Cartesian Meditations* were never regarded as fully satisfactory even by Husserl himself.
- > [!warning] Conflicts with [[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]] on consciousness: Husserl insists phenomenal character is irreducible to functional/computational processes; Dennett dissolves qualia and denies any first-person "given." See [[Contradictions/Husserl vs Naturalism — Phenomenal Consciousness and the Hard Problem]] and [[Contradictions/Dennett vs Nagel - Multiple Drafts vs Subjective Experience]].
