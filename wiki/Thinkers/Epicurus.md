# Epicurus

> Greek philosopher (341–271 BCE), founder of Epicureanism. Born in Athens (or Samos), studied with Platonists, taught in Mytilene and Lampsacus before establishing his school in Athens' "Garden" (306 BCE). Revived and systematized ancient atomism (Leucippus/Democritus) into a complete materialist philosophy: infinite atoms and void, multiple worlds formed by blind collisions (with "swerve" for indeterminacy/free will), empiricist epistemology (senses as criterion, verificationism), and ethics aimed at tranquility (ataraxia/peace of mind as absence of pain and fear). Famous for "death is nothing to us," "live unnoticed" (lathe biosas), friendship as highest pleasure, prudent hedonism (natural/necessary desires only), and gods as blissful but uninvolved models. The Garden was a community emphasizing simple living, mutual aid, and accessibility (anti-elitist, open to women/slaves).

## Key Contributions from the Source

**Physics/Cosmology (Letter to Herodotus, Letter to Pythocles)**:
- Fundamental reality: atoms (indivisible, finite types by size/shape/mass, inherent motion) + void (empty space, infinite).
- Infinite number of worlds, formed/dissolved accidentally by atomic collisions/sorting; our world is one among many, not unique, eternal, or purposeful.
- "Swerve" (clinamen): atoms occasionally deviate slightly from straight fall, enabling collisions (world formation) and human free will (against strict determinism).
- Anti-teleological: nature is blind/self-organizing, no divine purpose or final causes (contra Aristotle/Plato).

**Epistemology/Methodology (Herodotus, Pythocles)**:
- Robust empiricism: senses (and "clear views"/prolepsis) are the ultimate criterion of truth. Multiple explanations for obscure phenomena (e.g., celestial) are acceptable if consistent with evidence; reject only those contradicted by sensation.
- Error from adding unverified opinions to raw sensations. Verificationist/falsificationist leanings (multiple explanations for the obscure are acceptable if consistent with sensation; reject only those contradicted). Strong anticipation of modern scientific method, now grounded in [[Sources/The Logic of Scientific Discovery - Karl Popper (Routledge Classics)]] (see [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)]] and [[Thinkers/Karl Popper]]). Popper treats this as a proto-falsificationist attitude in ancient empiricism.
- All knowledge (even mental) ultimately from atomic interactions with senses.

**Ethics (Letter to Menoeceus, Principal Doctrines, fragments)**:
- Telos: pleasure as the natural good, but refined — absence of bodily pain (aponia) and mental disturbance (ataraxia/tranquility). Classify desires: natural/necessary (simple, prudent) vs. vain/unnecessary (avoid).
- "Death is nothing to us": when we exist, death does not; when death exists, we do not (no experience of it, no afterlife torments).
- Gods exist (fine atomic beings living in perfect bliss) but do not interfere in human affairs (to attribute care/malice is impious and disturbs their tranquility).
- Prudence as greatest virtue; friendship as highest (natural) pleasure. "Live unnoticed" — avoid public ambition/turmoil for a simple, private life of modest pleasures.
- Contractarian society: humans not naturally sociable (contra Aristotle); society arises for mutual security/benefit. Wise person withdraws to cultivate inner peace.
- Key maxims (Principal Doctrines): e.g., "Of all the things which wisdom provides for the blessedness of life as a whole, by far the greatest is the possession of friendship"; "A happy and blessed life is impossible without prudence, honor, and justice."

**Life and Reception (from introduction)**:
- Founded Garden as community (not commune; property private for trust). Emphasized memorization of principles for daily use. Died 271 BCE of painful illness but faced it with equanimity ("strangury and dysentery").
- Historical influence: revived by Newton (atoms/void in physics), contractarianism in Hobbes/Locke/Jefferson, anti-superstition rationalism. Often maligned as atheist/hedonist by critics (Platonists, Christians, etc.), but texts show noble, accessible philosophy of peace of mind.
- Anti-elitist: philosophy for all, based on common experience, not special insight or Forms.

**Primary elaborator — Lucretius**:
- [[Thinkers/Lucretius]] (c. 99–55 BCE), *De Rerum Natura* (*On the Nature of Things*), is the most important elaboration of Epicurean philosophy in any language. Lucretius calls Epicurus "our father and finder-out of truth" and "the man in genius who o'er-topped the human race." His six-book poem provides:
  - The fullest argument for the clinamen (swerve) and its connection to free will
  - The secondary qualities doctrine (atoms have no color/smell/taste — all emergent)
  - 14 distinct arguments for the mortality of the soul (Book III)
  - The symmetry argument: pre-birth = post-death nothingness
  - The interpretation of mythological hells (Sisyphus, Tantalus) as allegories for earthly psychological states
- Without Lucretius, most of Epicurean physics would survive only in hostile summaries. Lucretius was rediscovered in 1417 by Poggio Bracciolini and became a key text of the Renaissance and Enlightenment.

## Skeptical Counterpoint (Sextus Empiricus / Pyrrhonism)

Sextus Empiricus explicitly names "the followers of Aristotle and Epicurus, the Stoics, and certain others" as the paradigmatic *Dogmatists* who "think that they have found [the truth]" (opening of *Outlines of Pyrrhonism*). Pyrrhonian Skepticism is the third way: continue searching by generating equipollent oppositions (isosthenia) among claims/appearances, leading to epoché (suspension of judgment on non-evident) and ataraxia as its shadow.

Direct contrasts:
- Ataraxia achieved via *suspension* on the non-evident (including atoms, causes, gods, the nature of death) rather than correct *understanding* of atomist physics, swerve, mortal soul, and "death is nothing."
- Criterion for life is the *appearance* (phantasia) — follow the 4-fold ordinary regimen (nature, pathé, laws/customs, arts) without belief — rather than verification of a full materialist ontology.
- Book III critiques material sources (explicitly includes "Democritus and Epicurus, atoms" among the disagreed-upon options) and cause (circularity: cannot conceive cause without prior apprehension of effect as effect, and vice versa; relative nature prevents proper temporal ordering).

See new [[Sources/Outlines of Pyrrhonism - Sextus Empiricus]], [[Thinkers/Sextus Empiricus]], [[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]], [[Concepts/Equipollence (Isosthenia) and the Modes]]. This supplies the ancient skeptical counter to the Epicurean (and broader classical) thread, enriching ataraxia with a third Hellenistic route and providing strong methodological parallels for the wiki (report "what appears" from sources like a chronicler; oppose before integrating; suspend on underdetermined claims).
