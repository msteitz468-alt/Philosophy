# Richard Rorty
> One of the most influential late 20th-century American philosophers, known for his critique of the representationalist tradition and his promotion of neopragmatism and therapeutic philosophy.

## Biography and Historical Context
Richard Rorty (1931–2007) began his career in analytic philosophy, editing the pathbreaking anthology *The Linguistic Turn* (1967). However, he gradually broke with the analytic mainstream, culminating in his landmark work *Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature* (1979). He argued that the Cartesian-Lockean-Kantian tradition of treating mind and language as a "mirror of nature" (representing external reality) was a historical mistake. Rorty integrated the insights of John Dewey, Martin Heidegger, and Ludwig Wittgenstein to advocate for a post-epistemological neopragmatism, viewing language as a tool for social cooperation rather than an instrument of representation.

## Core Contributions

### The Critique of Representationalism
Rorty argues that the primary task of modern philosophy should be to abandon representationalist epistemology. Instead of seeking to match words to things or anchor beliefs to mind-independent reality, we should evaluate our beliefs and linguistic vocabularies by their practical utility in human life ("social practice").

### Therapeutic vs. Pragmatic Wittgensteinianism
In his contribution to *Wittgenstein's Philosophical Investigations: A Critical Guide* (2010), Rorty distinguishes two ways of reading the later Wittgenstein:
- **Therapeutic Wittgensteinianism**: The "resolute" reading (associated with James Conant and Cora Diamond) that views Wittgenstein's metaphilosophical goal as purely therapeutic, aiming to show that philosophical questions and metaphysical theories are plain, ungrammatical nonsense.
- **Pragmatic Wittgensteinianism**: The reading (associated with Donald Davidson and Rorty himself) that treats Wittgenstein as replacing the "picture theory" of meaning with a positive, naturalistic social-practice theory of meaning, showing how language works as a set of tools.

Rorty argues that while therapeutic readings capture Wittgenstein's own self-image, pragmatic readings are philosophically more useful because they help replace the "spectator theory of knowledge" with a pragmatist account of language.
