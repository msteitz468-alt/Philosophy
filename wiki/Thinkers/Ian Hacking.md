# Ian Hacking

> Ian Hacking (1936–2023), Canadian philosopher of science. In *Representing and Intervening* (1983) he reframed the realism debate around experimental *practice*: "If you can spray them, then they are real." Distinguished **realism about entities** (theoretical objects really exist and can be used as tools) from **realism about theories** (theories literally describe and aim at the truth). Argued that experiment has a life of its own, largely independent of high theory, and that working experimenters are committed to a robust form of entity realism through intervention and manipulation.

## Profile

- **Dates / life**: Canadian; at time of writing taught introductory philosophy of science at Stanford University; collaborated with experimental physicist Francis Everitt (joint paper "Which comes first, theory or experiment?"); engaged with Nancy Cartwright's contemporaneous work at Stanford. Later career included work on probability and statistics, "styles of reasoning," and historical ontology.
- **Major works**: ***Representing and Intervening*** (1983) — the primary source here; *The Emergence of Probability* (1975); *The Taming of Chance* (1990); *Rewriting the Soul* (1995); *The Social Construction of What?* (1999); later essays on "styles of scientific reasoning."
- **In the vault**: Primary source [[Sources/Representing and Intervening - Ian Hacking (1983)]]. Also referenced indirectly via the introductory essay he wrote for the Chicago 4th edition of Kuhn's *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (already ingested). Provides the experimentalist/practice turn that complements the representation- and rationality-focused cluster (Popper, Kuhn, Feyerabend, Quine, Ayer).

## Core Positions (from *Representing and Intervening*)

- **Entity realism vs. theory realism**: Two distinct questions. Realism about theories: our theories are (or aim to be) literally true or approximately true descriptions. Realism about entities: the objects, processes, or structures postulated by theories (electrons, fields, etc.) really exist in the world. One can defend the latter while remaining agnostic or anti about the former.
- **"If you can spray them, then they are real"**: The practical criterion. In actual laboratory work (e.g., using positron and electron emitters as standard tools to charge a superconducting niobium ball in a quark-hunting experiment), entities are accepted as real because they are reliably used to intervene in other systems and produce further effects whose causes and consequences are understood. It is the *use as tool*, not belief in a full theoretical story, that commits the experimenter. "It is not thinking about the world but changing it that in the end must make us scientific realists."
- **Experiment has a life of its own**: Theory and experiment do not stand in a fixed hierarchical or temporal relation. In many sciences and periods, experiment, instrumentation, and technology develop with substantial independence from "high theory." "Which comes first?" has no single answer; illustrations from optics, thermodynamics, radio astronomy, and solid-state physics.
- **Observation is a skill, not (always) theory-laden seeing**: Contra strong readings of Hanson or Kuhn, not all observation is constituted by theory. There are pre-theoretical or minimally theoretical observations; observation is trained skill; we often "observe" effects transmitted by theoretically postulated but trusted instruments. Convergence across physically independent systems (different microscope types, including acoustic) underwrites belief.
- **Creation of phenomena**: Many experiments do not merely record pre-existing natural regularities; they create stable, pure, repeatable phenomena by refining apparatus and conditions until the effect can be produced on demand. "Repeating an experiment" is usually improving it.
- **Pivot from representation to intervention**: Debates about truth, reference, and incommensurability at the level of theories and representations are perennial and often inconclusive (anthropological parable in the "Break" chapter: humans as *homo depictor*, makers of representations). Modern natural science since the 17th century is the interlocking of representing *and* intervening; philosophy had neglected the intervening side. Realism is better grounded in what we can manipulate than in what we can picture.

## Influence, Reception, and Contrasts in the Vault

- **With/against [[Thinkers/Thomas Kuhn]]**: Accepts much of the historical/sociological picture and the force of incommensurability as a problem for theory-comparison and representational realism. Uses it as motivation to change the question: even if paradigms are incommensurable at the level of representation, experimental practice provides a more direct and robust route to entity realism. Hacking wrote the introductory essay for the Chicago 4th ed. of *Structure* used in the vault.
- **With/against [[Thinkers/Paul Feyerabend]]**: Shares skepticism of "law and order" methodologies and fixed rules; provides rich, concrete experimental histories that show practice has its own structure and constraints without restoring a universal method. Hacking's experimenter-realism is a partial counterweight to "anything goes."
- **With [[Thinkers/Karl Popper]]**: Shares a realist temperament (Popper's conjecturalism/scientific realism) but grounds it in experimental use rather than (or in addition to) severe testing and verisimilitude. Hacking's treatment of Lakatos (surrogate for truth without correspondence) engages the Popperian tradition's attempts to retain objectivity after Kuhn.
- **Positivism and pragmatism**: Direct engagement with the anti-entity, anti-cause, instrumentalist strands (Comte, Mach, van Fraassen; Peirce/Putnam/James/Rorty variants). The spray/use argument is offered as a practical refutation of strong anti-realism about entities.
- **Parallel with Nancy Cartwright** (not yet primary-sourced): Both (independently) downplay literal truth of fundamental theories while defending selected theoretical entities (Cartwright via phenomenological laws and models that "lie"; Hacking via entities that function as experimental tools). Hacking acknowledges the convergence explicitly.
- **Lakatos**: Ch. 8 treats the methodology of scientific research programmes less as a solution to rationality problems and more as an account of how scientific objectivity and a form of realism can survive without a correspondence theory of truth.

## Relevance to the Vault and Meta

- **Wiki as intervention as well as representation**: The vault is not only a set of representations (pages, wikilinks, syntheses) but a site of *intervention* — ingests change the structure, create new stable "phenomena" (concepts, contradictions), and use existing pages as tools to produce further understanding. "If you can usefully manipulate it to affect other pages, count it as real in the wiki."
- **AI/agentic**: Distinguishes model-as-picture (representation) from model-as-tool (intervention). Agents can be "entity realists" about their tools (retrievers, simulators, verifiers) even when skeptical of any single overarching "theory" the system embodies. Experiment-style iteration (create stable phenomena by refining prompts/apparatus) is often more powerful than pure representational refinement.
- **Philosophy of science leg**: Completes a crucial missing practical/experimental dimension alongside the rationality debates (Popper–Kuhn–Feyerabend) and the critique of positivism (Ayer–Quine). Shifts emphasis from "is the theory true?" to "what can we do with the posited entities?"
