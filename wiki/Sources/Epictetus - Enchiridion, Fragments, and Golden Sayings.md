# Epictetus — Enchiridion, Fragments, and Golden Sayings

> Three complementary texts by or attributed to Epictetus (c. AD 50–135): the *Enchiridion* (Handbook, 53 sections — a compressed daily manual), *Fragments Attributed to Epictetus* (gnomic axioms), and *The Golden Sayings* (86 sayings on the philosopher's vocation and divine order). Together they form the most accessible expression of Epictetan Stoicism: the foundational division between what is in our power and what is not, and everything that follows from it.

**Editions**: P. E. Matheson translation (Enchiridion, first published 1916); Hastings Crossley translation (Fragments and Golden Sayings, first published 1909). Provided in RAG-formatted markdown (epictetus_enchiridion_rag.md, epictetus_fragments_rag.md, epictetus_golden_sayings_rag.md).

## The *Enchiridion* (Handbook)

Compiled by Arrian from his notes on Epictetus' oral teaching. Intended as a portable manual — something to hold in the hand and keep in the mind. The 53 sections move from the foundational division through specific disciplines of desire, impression, role, piety, and the philosopher's identity.

### The Foundational Division (§1)

**"Of all existing things some are in our power, and others are not in our power."**

| In our power (*eph' hēmin*) | Not in our power |
|---|---|
| Thought, impulse | Body, property |
| Will to get and will to avoid | Reputation, office |
| Everything which is our own doing | Everything which is not our doing |
| By nature free, unhindered | By nature weak, servile, hindered |

This single distinction generates the entire Epictetan discipline. Attaching happiness to what is not in our power (as most people do) produces frustration, blame, and misery. Confining desire and aversion to what is in our power produces freedom, equanimity, and invincibility.

**"If you imagine that what is naturally slavish is free, and that what is naturally another's is your own, you will be hampered, you will mourn, you will be put into confusion, you will blame gods and men."**

### The Discipline of Desire and Aversion (§2)

- Direct aversion *only* toward things in one's own power that are contrary to nature.
- Suspend desire altogether from things not in one's power for now; otherwise bound to be unfortunate.
- Not the absence of desire, but the proper direction of it.

### The Formula of Description (§3)

When drawn to any attractive thing, immediately name its nature: "I am fond of a jug" (not simply "I am fond of this"). Then its loss will not disturb you. Applied to persons: "I am kissing a human being." Stoic love is not cold — it is clear-sighted.

### The Impression Test (§5, §27–28)

**"Men are disturbed not by the things which happen, but by the opinions about the things."**

Every impression that disturbs should be tested: Is this concerned with what is in our power, or with what is not? If the latter: **"It is nothing to me."** The site of philosophy is the moment of impression — before assent, before reaction.

### Role and Duty (§17)

**"Remember that you are an actor in a play, the character of which is determined by the Author... For this is your duty, to act well the part that is given to you; but to choose the part belongs to Another."**

The theatrical analogy: God/Nature assigns the role (long or short, of king or pauper); the actor's excellence consists in playing it as well as possible, not in demanding a different role. This is not resignation — it is the full exercise of what is genuinely one's own (the quality of the acting) within constraints that are not one's own (the role and its length).

### Invincibility (§19)

**"You can be invincible if you never enter any contest where it is not in your power to win."**

Not laziness but strategic allocation of will: never contest where the outcome is not in your power. The only genuinely unloseable contest is the governance of one's own rational faculty.

### Piety and Providence (§31)

*Lead me, O Zeus, and thou O Destiny, wherever thy decrees have fixed my lot; I follow cheerfully, and did I not, wicked and wretched, I must follow still.*

Fate is not blind chance but the rational order of the universe. Cheerful alignment with what providence assigns is both freedom (the rational being's unique capacity to agree with what it must undergo) and piety.

### The Philosopher's Identity (§46, §51)

Do not claim the title; enact the practice. The philosopher does not announce principles at dinner — he eats as one ought. When someone sought Socrates' recommendation to philosophers, Socrates took them to the philosophers; he never called himself one in conversation. Progress in philosophy is measured not by demonstrations but by the consistency of daily conduct.

## The *Fragments Attributed to Epictetus*

Brief gnomic axioms, Crossley translation. More compressed than the *Enchiridion*, functioning as standalone moral axioms.

**Core contrasts:**
- *"A life entangled with Fortune is like a torrent. It is turbulent and muddy; hard to pass and masterful of mood: noisy and of brief continuance."* vs. *"The soul that companies with Virtue is like an ever-flowing source. It is a pure, clear, and wholesome draught."*
- Honey sweetens the palate; vice embitters the rational faculty — God's gift to us. We protect the trivial while corrupting the essential.
- Flattery blinds the soul's eyes: more dangerous than open injury because it corrupts from within.
- **"Thou art a little soul carrying a corpse."** The body is the vessel; the soul is the content. Preoccupation with the body is misidentification of self.
- **"No great thing is created suddenly, any more than a bunch of grapes or a fig."** Growth takes the time it takes; impatience is the error of those who want the fruit before the blossom.

## *The Golden Sayings of Epictetus*

86 sayings, Crossley translation. Organized numerically; vary from one sentence to a short paragraph. More explicitly theological than the *Enchiridion* — the philosopher's vocation is to hymn the divine order.

**The Philosopher as Singer (I)**

Providence has given us breath, hands, digestion — the whole apparatus of life. *If we had understanding, we should never cease hymning and blessing the Divine Power, both openly and in secret.* Since most people are blind to this, someone must fill the role. That is the philosopher's specific vocation: to hymn the divine on behalf of those who cannot see it.

**The Inn and the Journey (II–III)**

The man who stops at a beautiful inn and decides to live there has forgotten his destination. Life is the road, not the inn. *Try to enjoy the great festival of life with other men* — but remember the journey's end. This image of life as festival-and-journey is the corrective to both grim asceticism and careless worldliness.

**Acting According to Nature**

The nightingale sings as a nightingale; the swan behaves as a swan. The rational being must act according to rational nature — which is also to act according to God's intention. The question in every situation: *What is my nature? What does it require here?*

**On Not Seeking Praise**

Desire for praise is servitude to the opinions of others. True freedom requires acting rightly whether observed or not. The proof of philosophical progress is the interior condition of the ruling faculty, not the testimony of others.

## Key Quotes

- **"Of all existing things some are in our power, and others are not in our power."** (Ench. §1)
- **"Men are disturbed not by the things which happen, but by the opinions about the things."** (Ench. §5, echoed throughout Fragments)
- **"Ask not that events should happen as you will, but let your will be that events should happen as they are, and you will have a tranquil flow of life."** (Ench. §8)
- **"Never say about anything, I have lost it; but, I have restored it."** (Ench. §11)
- **"You can be invincible if you never enter any contest where it is not in your power to win."** (Ench. §19)
- **"No man is free who is not master of himself."** (Fragments)
- **"Thou art a little soul carrying a corpse."** (Fragments)
- **"No great thing is created suddenly."** (Fragments)
- *Lead me, O Zeus, and thou O Destiny...* (Ench. §31 — the Cleanthes hymn)

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **The Stoic sage** (*sophos*) is acknowledged even by Stoics to be very rare or nonexistent in practice. Seneca, Marcus Aurelius, and Epictetus himself all speak as *proficients* (making progress) rather than as sages. The standard is aspirational, not a claim of achievement.
- **Emotions**: Epictetus distinguishes proper emotional responses from passions based on false beliefs. Stoic *eupatheia* (good emotions like joy, caution, well-wishing) are not suppressed but purified. This is often misread as emotionlessness; see Seneca's letters for the nuanced position.
- **Providence vs. mechanism**: Stoic physics grounds ethics in a providential rational cosmos (logos). Pyrrhonian skepticism challenges this dogmatic commitment; Epicurean physics offers an alternative (chance swerve). Augustine challenges the entire framework from a theological direction (grace, not providence-as-logos, is the operative principle).
