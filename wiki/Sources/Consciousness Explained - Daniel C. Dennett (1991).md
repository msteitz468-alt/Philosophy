# Consciousness Explained — Daniel C. Dennett (1991)

> Dennett's comprehensive theory of human consciousness: an empirical, functionalist, and evolutionary account that dismantles the "Cartesian Theater" (the idea that consciousness happens at a single central place in the brain) and replaces it with the Multiple Drafts model (parallel, distributed editorial processes with no canonical "final draft"). Qualia are deflationary-explained as complex reactive dispositions; the self is a Center of Narrative Gravity; consciousness itself is a virtual machine (the "Joycean machine") culturally installed on the brain's parallel hardware.

## Structure

**Preface** — 30-year inquiry into the mind-body problem; promises a scientific theory, not a mystery-preservation exercise.

**Part I — Problems and Methods (Chs. 1–4)**
- Ch. 1 *Prelude: How Are Hallucinations Possible?* — Brain-in-the-vat thought experiment establishes combinatorial explosion limits; party-game analogy shows how narrative content can be generated without an author (generate-and-test hallucination model).
- Ch. 2 *Explaining Consciousness* — Mind stuff: four reasons dualism is tempting (medium for purple cow, thinking thing, appreciator, moral agent). Why dualism is forlorn (conservation-of-energy problem; "Casper the Friendly Ghost" paradox). Challenge: no wonder tissue, no feigning anesthesia. Introduction of the Cartesian Theater as the target.
- Ch. 3 *A Visit to the Phenomenological Garden* — Inventory of consciousness: outer (taste/smell, touch/kinesthesia, hearing, vision), inner (mental imagery, inner speech, comprehension), affect (pain, laughter). Qualia as "intrinsic properties of conscious experiences" introduced as the object of future critique.
- Ch. 4 *A Method for Phenomenology* — Problems with first-person-plural presumption (introspection unreliable; Wundt/Husserl traditions diverge). Third-person alternative (behaviorism too austere). **Heterophenomenology** as the solution: extract texts from subjects → adopt intentional stance → generate a theorist's fiction (the "heterophenomenological world") without presupposing its real-world correlates. Shakey the robot as illustration of how a system can have "mental images" in a metaphorical but truth-preserving sense.

**Part II — An Empirical Theory of the Mind (Chs. 5–9)**
- Ch. 5 *Multiple Drafts vs. the Cartesian Theater* — The central thesis: no place in the brain is the "finish line" for consciousness. Cartesian materialism (still expecting a neural bottleneck even after ditching dualism) is exposed via color-phi phenomenon (Kolers/Goodman: spot seems to change color mid-trajectory before the second spot is even perceived). **Orwellian vs. Stalinesque revisions**: at millisecond timescales the distinction between pre-experiential and post-experiential content fixation loses force — there is no fact of the matter.
- Ch. 6 *Time and Experience* — Libet's "backwards referral" experiments and Grey Walter's precognitive carousel. Both interpreted through Multiple Drafts: subjective time is represented time, not the time of representing. The "absolute timing" of a conscious experience is a category error (British Empire and the War of 1812 analogy).
- Ch. 7 *The Evolution of Consciousness* — Three evolutionary processes: (1) genetic evolution; (2) phenotypic plasticity/postnatal design-fixing (Baldwin Effect: learned good tricks feed back into gene pool); (3) memetic/cultural evolution. Consciousness = convergence of primate pandemonium architecture (Selfridge) + language + cultural meme-complexes. Orienting responses → curiosity → informavores. Dorsal/ventral brain specialization → hemisphere asymmetries. Human brains at full size 150,000 years before language.
- Ch. 8 *How Words Do Things with Us* — Speech production demystified: Levelt's "Conceptualizer" dissolves under scrutiny into pandemonium of word-demons competing for expression. No Central Meaner behind utterances; meaning is distributed. Von Neumann machine architecture as the inadvertent model of serial consciousness.
- Ch. 9 *The Architecture of the Human Mind* — **Joycean machine**: human consciousness is a more-or-less serial virtual machine (inspired by James Joyce's "stream of consciousness") implemented inefficiently on the brain's massively parallel hardware. The program is culturally installed (via talking-to-yourself habits, writing, diagrams). Memes as the software units. Consciousness is too recent to be hardwired; good tricks (Baldwin Effect) are still migrating into the genome.

**Part III — The Philosophical Problems (Chs. 10–14)**
- Ch. 10 *Show and Tell* — Reportability and the distinction between conscious and unconscious content. Zombies and zimboes. Folk psychology's over-determined self.
- Ch. 11 *Dismantling the Witness Protection Program* — Blindsight (partial zombiehood?), prosthetic vision (Bach-y-Rita), "filling in" vs. finding out. The persistent myth of a Witness who needs to be shown everything.
- Ch. 12 *Qualia Disqualified* — **The elimination of qualia** in the strong (intrinsic, ineffable, private, directly apprehensible) sense. Colors coevolved with color vision — no independent substrate "for" colors; they are dispositional properties shaped by ecological history. Mary the color scientist (Frank Jackson): "all the physical information" entails advance knowledge of reactive dispositions, so Mary's exclamation "So that's what red looks like!" results from imagining her with *less* than all the information. Inverted qualia thought experiments presuppose a Cartesian Theater; the Multiple Drafts model shows no coherent story can be told that detaches qualia from dispositions. Epiphenomenal qualia (philosophical sense) are incoherent (undetectable in principle ⇒ no evidence ⇒ gremlins parity). "We're all zombies" — nobody is conscious in the systematically mysterious sense.
- Ch. 13 *The Reality of Selves* — **Center of Narrative Gravity**: the self is not a brain-pearl or soul-atom but an abstraction — like a center of gravity — useful for organizing attributions. We spin narrative webs (like spiders, bowerbirds, beavers, but in language). Multiple Personality Disorder: two selves per body is no more metaphysically extravagant than one. Split-brain patients: brief bifurcations of narrative center, not sustained selves. Self is substrate-independent — could survive physical death as information ("if what you are is the program that runs on your brain's computer…").
- Ch. 14 *Consciousness Imagined* — Can a robot be conscious? Yes in principle — imagining it is child's play (R2D2, Hal); the real difficulty is imagining *how*, which is equally hard for biological brains. Searle's Chinese Room: the Systems Reply is the correct response — we are not entitled to imagine only a simple table-lookup system and then declare genuine understanding absent; the full complexity of a Turing-test-passing program brimming with world knowledge and meta-knowledge cannot be dismissed. Nagel's "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" overstated pessimism — we can investigate the differences between bat consciousness and human consciousness in third-person terms. Minding and mattering: suffering requires sufficient cognitive complexity to have thwarted desires; Bentham's "Can they suffer?" operationalized via Marian Stamp Dawkins's preference experiments. Final: **Consciousness Explained, or Explained Away?** — all successful explanations leave something out; the illusion of inexplicability is a failure to understand that feature. Multiple Drafts replaces one family of metaphors (Theater, Witness, Figment) with a better one (Software, Virtual Machines, Pandemonium, Narrative Gravity).

**Appendices**: A (for philosophers — identity theory, eliminativism, functionalism, verificationism) and B (for scientists — experimental predictions).

## Key Arguments and Contributions

| Argument | Core Claim |
|---|---|
| Multiple Drafts | No canonical final representation; distributed, parallel content-fixation without Cartesian Theater |
| Heterophenomenology | Neutral third-person method for phenomenology: subject's reports = theorist's fiction, not presupposed truth |
| Orwellian/Stalinesque | At millisecond scales the pre/post-experiential distinction is incoherent; no fact of the matter |
| Qualia elimination | No intrinsic, ineffable properties; secondary qualities are ecological/dispositional; Mary's knowledge is already complete |
| Color coevolution | Colors and color vision were "made for each other" — no independent color-reality prior to observers |
| Inverted qualia | The thought experiment presupposes Cartesian Theater; without it there is no coherent story |
| Epiphenomenal qualia | Philosophical sense is incoherent; Huxley's sense is trivially physicalist and uninteresting |
| Center of Narrative Gravity | Self = abstraction like center of gravity; no brain-pearl; substrate-independent in principle |
| Joycean Machine | Consciousness = serial virtual machine on parallel brain hardware; culturally programmed |
| Systems Reply | Searle's Chinese Room fails because it discourages imagining the full complexity required |
| Minding and Mattering | Suffering ∝ cognitive complexity; no mysterious qualia needed for moral standing |

**Minsky Society of Mind connection**: Dennett's pandemonium of word-demons, distributed content fixation, and rejection of any Central Meaner or Cartesian Theater is strongly convergent with Marvin Minsky's 1986 model of mind as a *society of many simple, mindless agents*. Minsky's K-lines (reactivating sets of agents to restore partial prior mental states) and B-brains (higher-level monitoring agencies) offer concrete mechanisms that support Multiple Drafts without a finish line. Both treat the unified "self" as a constructed abstraction rather than a real inner entity. See new [[Sources/The Society of Mind - Marvin Minsky (1986)]] and [[Thinkers/Marvin Minsky]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Dennett vs. Nagel on subjective experience: Nagel insists there is irreducibly subjective phenomenology that third-person science cannot capture ("What Is It Like to Be a Bat?"); Dennett's heterophenomenology is designed to sidestep and dissolve this. See [[Contradictions/Dennett vs Nagel - Multiple Drafts vs Subjective Experience]].
- > [!warning] Dennett vs. Searle on syntax/semantics: Dennett's Systems Reply challenges the Chinese Room; Searle's argument specifically targets the functionalism Dennett defends. See [[Contradictions/Hofstadter vs Searle - Strange Loops vs Chinese Room]] (Dennett as third voice).
- > [!warning] Dennett vs. Chalmers (not yet in vault): Chalmers' "hard problem" (why does physical processing give rise to subjective experience at all?) is the post-Dennett articulation of what Dennett's theory allegedly explains away rather than explains.
