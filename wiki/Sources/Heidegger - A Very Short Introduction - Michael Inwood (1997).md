# Heidegger: A Very Short Introduction — Michael Inwood (1997)
> A compressed but philosophically rigorous survey of Heidegger's entire corpus, centered on *Being and Time* (1927) but extending through the later philosophy of art, language, technology, and the Turn.

## Argument Spine

Inwood presents Heidegger's philosophy as driven by a single question: *What is Being?* — a question the Western tradition once asked (Aristotle, the Pre-Socratics) but subsequently forgot, burying it under epistemology (Kant), subjectivism (Descartes), and science. To recover the question, Heidegger analyses the entity that asks it — *Dasein* (human being understood as the being whose own being is an issue for it). The first division of *Being and Time* shows that Dasein is essentially *being-in-the-world*: not a subject confronting objects but always already practically engaged with equipment, others, and meaning. Its being is *care* (*Sorge*). The second division grounds care in *temporality*: Dasein is ecstatically temporal — projecting toward its own death (future), thrown into a past it did not choose, and falling into the present of everyday absorption. Authentic temporality (being-toward-death, resoluteness, historicality) reveals the horizon for understanding Being as such. The later Heidegger shifts from Dasein's active questioning to a receptive openness in which Being itself discloses through art, language, and thinking — the "Turn" (*Kehre*).

## Whole-Work Assessment

Inwood's *Very Short Introduction* provides the vault's first dedicated treatment of Heidegger — arguably the most consequential continental philosopher of the twentieth century and the figure whose question of Being restructures how every other thinker in the vault (Aristotle, Plato, Descartes, Kant, Hegel, Nietzsche) can be read. The ingest adds a complete arc from the ontological difference through Dasein, being-in-the-world, care, temporality, death, and historicality to the later philosophy of art and truth — filling the vault's largest single gap in 20th-century philosophy.

The most productive tensions this ingest opens: (1) Heidegger vs. the entire correspondence-theory tradition on truth (Aristotle through Ayer); (2) Heidegger's anti-Cartesian being-in-the-world vs. Descartes's *res cogitans/extensa* (already a major vault axis); (3) the ready-to-hand/present-at-hand distinction as a challenge to Aristotle's categorial ontology; (4) Heidegger's ecstatic temporality vs. Augustine/Aristotle/Kant/Bergson on time; (5) the absence of normative criteria in resoluteness, which bears on the vault's existing authenticity-vs-ethics tensions.

The Phenomenology school page establishes a new school category, connecting Husserl, Heidegger, and the French tradition (Sartre, Merleau-Ponty, Levinas, Derrida) to existing vault entries (Dennett's heterophenomenology, Ayer's positivist critique).
