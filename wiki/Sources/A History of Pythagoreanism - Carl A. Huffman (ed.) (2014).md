# A History of Pythagoreanism
> Carl A. Huffman (ed.), Cambridge University Press (2014). Twenty-one chapters by leading scholars covering Pythagoras and Pythagoreanism from the sixth century BC to the seventeenth century AD — the current authoritative synthesis that sits above the [[Sources/Lore and Science in Ancient Pythagoreanism - Walter Burkert (1972)]] ↔ [[Sources/Pythagoras and the Early Pythagoreans - Leonid Zhmud (2012)]] debate.

## Argument Spine
Pythagoras "eludes interpretation" more than any other Presocratic: the earliest sources are thin and the rich later (Neopythagorean/Neoplatonic) sources are suspect, so every reconstruction is value-laden. The volume frames itself against the two great 1962 books — Guthrie (Pythagoras the philosopher-mathematician) and Burkert (Pythagoras the religious charismatic; the mathematics belongs to [[Thinkers/Philolaus]], [[Thinkers/Archytas]] and the Academy). Rather than impose one model (mystic / sage / shaman / scientist), the collection disaggregates "Pythagoreanism" into many strands and historical layers — early figures, society and politics, way of life, religion, mathematics, harmonics — and then traces how each later tradition (Plato, Aristotle, the Academy, the Peripatetics, the historians, the pseudo-Pythagorean forgers, Rome, the three ancient Lives, the Middle Ages and Renaissance) reconstructed Pythagoras for its own ends. The recurring lesson: most of what "everyone knows" about Pythagoras is reception, and distinguishing the sixth-century man from the accreted image is the central methodological problem of the field.

## Whole-Work Assessment
Huffman's volume is now the vault's **keystone Pythagorean source** — the authoritative synthesis that adjudicates the [[Sources/Lore and Science in Ancient Pythagoreanism - Walter Burkert (1972)|Burkert]] ↔ [[Sources/Pythagoras and the Early Pythagoreans - Leonid Zhmud (2012)|Zhmud]] debate already held in the wiki. Its decisive move is *disaggregation*: rather than choosing between "Pythagoras the scientist" and "Pythagoras the charismatic," it dissolves the global question into specialist strands, which let the vault grow from a Pythagoras/Philolaus/Hippasus/Alcmaeon cluster into a full map of Pythagoreanism across a millennium. Net effects on the vault: (1) it **confirms the deflationary consensus on Pythagoras himself** (the hard mathematics belongs to [[Thinkers/Philolaus]] and the new [[Thinkers/Archytas]]; the "number-doctrine" is Aristotle's reconstruction of Philolaus — Primavesi, Netz), while keeping the *persona* question pointedly open (Lloyd); (2) it adds the entire **reception arc** the vault lacked — [[Schools/Neopythagoreanism]], the [[Concepts/Pseudo-Pythagorean Writings]], [[Concepts/The Three Ancient Lives of Pythagoras]], and [[Concepts/Pythagoras in Medieval and Renaissance Thought]] — showing that almost everything "known" about Pythagoras is later construction, and tracing the "reality as number" program forward to Boethius, Ficino, and Kepler; (3) it opens a **fresh contradiction front** — Gemelli Marciano defends the genuinely religious *bios* against Zhmud's secular *hetairia* (see [[Concepts/Pythagorean Way of Life and Ethics]] vs [[Schools/Pythagoreanism]]); and (4) it sharpens the science pages by separating genuine, testable [[Concepts/Pythagorean Harmonics|harmonics]] (Barker) from the more skeptical verdict on [[Concepts/Pythagorean Mathematics|mathematics]] (Netz: "there were no cards"). The Argument Spine written in Step 1 held up: the volume's own thesis is that disaggregation, not a single model, is the right response to a figure who "eludes interpretation."
