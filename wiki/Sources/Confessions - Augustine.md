# Confessions - Augustine

> Newly translated and edited by Albert C. Outler (1955, public domain; updated language/titles/re-formatting by William H. Gross, 2007). Augustine's most personal and widely read work: a sustained prayer addressed to God that is simultaneously confession of sins and confession/praise of God's grace. Books 1–9 trace his "pilgrimage of grace" from childhood through Manicheanism, Academic skepticism, Neo-Platonism, the dramatic garden conversion ("tolle, lege"), baptism, and his mother Monica's death. Books 10–13 turn to philosophical self-analysis (memory, current temptations) and exegesis of creation/time/Genesis 1. Central motif: "You have made us for yourself and restless is our heart until it comes to rest in you." Major patristic source shaping Western Christianity, psychology, philosophy of history, and especially Aquinas.

## Summary

Augustine (354–430 CE, Bishop of Hippo Regius) presents not a conventional autobiography but a theological "confession" in two senses: acknowledgment of his own evil (sins of youth, pride, incontinence, intellectual errors) and praise/glorification of the sovereign God of grace who pursued, converted, and sustains him. The work models radical interior self-examination ("plumb the depths of his memory") in the presence of God. Grace is prevenient and transforming; the will is divided until grace unites it; evil is privation of good (not a positive substance); the soul finds rest only in God; time is a distention of the mind (memory/present attention/expectation) while God is eternal; Scripture (especially Genesis) yields rich literal + allegorical meaning. The *Confessions* stands as the first great work of introspective psychology and a bridge from classical philosophy (especially Neo-Platonism) to Christian theology.

Outler's Introduction emphasizes Augustine as "last patristic and first medieval," "Doctor Gratiae," psychologist of the will and self, philosopher of history ("city of earth" and "city of God"), and pervasive influence on the Middle Ages ("even Aquinas is more of an Augustinian at heart than a 'proper' Aristotelian").

## Structure (13 Books)

**Books 1–9: Pilgrimage of Grace (autobiographical confession of God's mercies)**

- **Book 1 – Pilgrimage of Grace**: Infancy, learning to speak (by imitation, memory, gestures, authority of elders), early sins (lies for praise, cheating in games/school), opening philosophical theology of God (omnipresent, immutable, "most high, most excellent..."), famous invocation: "Great are you, O Lord... restless is our heart until it comes to rest in you."
- **Book 2 – Adolescent Mischief**: The famous pear theft (age 16): stole not for hunger or beauty of the fruit (had better at home) but for the sin itself, "the act of stealing," enhanced by "friendship all unfriendly" and peer pressure ("we are ashamed not to be shameless"). Profound analysis of sin as perverse imitation of God (counterfeit liberty, rebellion against law for its own sake), love of evil for evil's sake, privation.
- **Book 3–6**: Student days in Carthage (theater, lust, "cauldron of illicit loves"), Manicheanism (materialism, dualism, "fantasies" as food that did not nourish), move to Rome/Milan, Academics (skepticism loosening Manichean dogmatism), Ambrose (majesty of Scripture, Christian learning).
- **Book 7 – Conversion to Neo-Platonism**: Intellectual resolution via "the Platonists" (Plotinus/Neo-Platonism): immaterial reality, God without bodily extension/shape, Plotinian ecstasy ("I was snatched up... to that which is"), but unable to sustain or reach continence. Evil as privation of good ("evil was nothing but a privation of good").
- **Book 8 – Song of True Conversion**: Divided will (two wills at war: "the new will... was not yet strong enough to overcome the old"); examples inflame desire (Victorinus' humble baptism, Antony, two imperial agents in garden at Treves). Climax in Milan garden: child's voice chanting "tolle, lege" ("take and read"); opens Paul (Rom. 13:13–14: "Not in rioting and drunkenness, not in chambering and wantonness... but put ye on the Lord Jesus Christ..."). Resolution; Alypius also converts. Informs rejoicing Monica.
- **Book 9 – Baptism and the Return Home**: Baptism with Alypius and Adeodatus; return to Africa; Monica's illness and death at Ostia (ecstatic vision of "eternal life" together, "the first fruits of the Spirit"); grief and consolation; "we were but one soul in two bodies."

**Books 10–13: Self-analysis, memory, time, creation, and hermeneutics (philosophical/theological)**

- **Book 10 – Motivations and Appetites**: Turns from past to present self. "Let me know you, O my Knower; let me know you even as I am known." Exploration of memory ("vast fields... storehouses... plains, and caverns... innumerable and innumerably full of innumerable kinds of things") as path to God/self-knowledge. Analysis of current temptations (lust of the flesh, lust of the eyes, pride of life). Profit of public confession: stirs others to mercy and praise; brotherly love.
- **Book 11 – Creator, Creation, and Time**: "In the beginning God created..." Time and creation co-temporal. Famous analysis: "What then is time? If no one asks me, I know; if I wish to explain it to one that asketh, I know not." Time as distention of the soul/mind (past = memory, present = attention, future = expectation); God abides in eternity; creation ex nihilo without change in God.
- **Books 12–13 – Truth and Interpretation / Hermeneutics and Creation**: Detailed allegorical + literal exegesis of Genesis 1 (formless matter, "heaven of heavens," firmament as Scripture, lights, creatures, "increase and multiply," Sabbath rest). Multiple senses of Scripture; Creator is Redeemer; man's end and beginning meet in God.

## Key Themes and Extracts

- **Grace and the Will**: Prevenient, transforming, irresistible yet not destroying freedom. "Give what you command and command what you will" (repeated; cited by Pelagius' opponents). Divided will healed only by grace. "You have melted away my sin as if it were ice."
- **Evil as Privation**: "Evil was nothing but a privation of good." Sin as turning from the higher good (God) toward lower goods or the act of turning itself. Pear theft as paradigm of gratuitous evil and perverse imitation.
- **Interiority and Memory**: Truth dwells within ("in interiore homine habitat veritas"). Confession as self-knowledge in God's presence. Memory as the inner space where God may be sought/found or the soul's restlessness exposed.
- **Rest in God**: "Restless is our heart until it comes to rest in you." Ultimate happiness = rest in the unchangeable Good (ties directly to Aquinas' beatific vision as elevation/completion).
- **Time and Eternity**: Time is creaturely, subjective distention; God is eternal present. Creation and time begin together.
- **Hermeneutics**: Scripture is "chaste delight"; veiled in mysteries yet sublime; literal + spiritual senses (e.g., "increase and multiply" as spiritual fecundity of the mind).
- **Double Confession**: "To confess... is to praise and glorify God; it is an exercise in self-knowledge and true humility in the atmosphere of grace."

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- No internal contradictions in the *Confessions*; the work is self-consistent as a prayerful narrative of grace. Strong alignment with Aquinas (primary source for grace, will, *imago Dei*, beatific rest) and Plotinus (Neo-Platonism as stepping stone in Book VII, shared privation language and ascent, but transposed by Christ/Mediator and grace). Contrasts with materialist views (Lucretius/Epicurus on soul/mortality) and pure philosophical paths (Stoic control, Epicurean ataraxia, Pyrrhonian suspension) by emphasizing prevenient/transforming grace and the divided will's need for divine aid.
- Open questions: Full *De Civitate Dei* for the two cities, philosophy of history, and more on original sin/predestination to compound with current classical cluster. *Enchiridion* for mature summary of faith/hope/love. Potential future synthesis: "Four Paths to Tranquility/Rest" (Epicurean ataraxia, Stoic equanimity, Plotinian henosis, Augustinian rest in God via grace) with vault parallels (different "means" for knowledge integration/compounding).
