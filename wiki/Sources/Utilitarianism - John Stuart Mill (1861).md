# Utilitarianism - John Stuart Mill (1861)

> **Utilitarianism**. Originally published as a series of three articles in *Fraser's Magazine* in 1861, Mill's essay remains the most famous defense and exposition of utilitarian ethics, seeking to refine Bentham's quantitative utility by introducing qualitative distinctions of pleasure and reconciling utility with the demands of justice.

## Core Thesis: The Greatest Happiness Principle

Mill argues that the supreme standard of morality is the **Greatest Happiness Principle**: actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, and wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness. Happiness is defined as pleasure and the absence of pain. Mill asserts that moral rules and duties derive all their validity from their tendency to maximize the aggregate happiness of all sentient beings.

## Section Summary

### Chapter 1: General Remarks
-   **The Search for a First Principle**: Mill observes that moral philosophy has made little progress since antiquity, still lacking a universally accepted first principle. He argues that the concept of utility has unconsciously guided moral judgments throughout history, even within deontological systems.

### Chapter 2: What Utilitarianism Is
-   **Qualitative Pleasures**: Responds to critics who brand utilitarianism as a "doctrine worthy of swine" by claiming it reduces human life to base pleasures. Mill distinguishes between **higher pleasures** (intellectual, artistic, and moral) and **lower pleasures** (physical sensations).
-   **The Competent Judges**: To prove that higher pleasures are superior, Mill points to "competent judges"—those who have experienced both and consistently prefer higher pleasures. Famous quote: "It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied." See [[Concepts/Greatest Happiness Principle (Mill)]].
-   **Self-Sacrifice**: Notes that utilitarianism does not reject self-sacrifice; indeed, sacrificing one's own happiness is noble, but only if it contributes to the overall happiness of others.

### Chapter 3: Of the Ultimate Sanction of the Principle of Utility
-   **Moral Motivation**: Explores what motivates individuals to act according to utilitarianism. Mill identifies:
    1.  *External Sanctions*: The hope of favor and fear of displeasure from our fellow humans or God.
    2.  *Internal Sanctions*: The feeling of duty or conscience, which is a subjective feeling in our own minds. Mill argues that humans possess natural social feelings that make them desire to be in harmony with their fellow creatures.

### Chapter 4: Of What Sort of Proof the Principle of Utility Is Susceptible
-   **The Proof of Utility**: Mill admits that first principles cannot be proven in the traditional logical sense. Instead, he offers an empirical argument: the only proof that an object is visible is that people actually see it. Similarly, the only proof that happiness is desirable is that people actually desire it. Because each person desires their own happiness, the general happiness is desirable to the aggregate of all persons.

### Chapter 5: On the Connection between Justice and Utility
-   **Reconciling Justice**: Addresses the strongest objection to utilitarianism: that it violates absolute principles of justice (e.g., individual rights). Mill analyzes the concept of justice and argues that its rules are not independent, mystical truths. Rather, justice consists of those moral rules that protect our most vital interest: security. The duties of justice are simply the most essential social utilities, and they derive their unique emotional force from our natural impulse for self-defense and sympathy.

## Integration and Vault Value

-   **Consequentialist Axis**: Serves as the central text for consequentialist ethics in the vault, establishing the main tension with Kant's duty-based ethics. See [[Contradictions/Utilitarian Consequentialism vs. Kantian Deontology]].
-   **Refinement of Hedonism**: Shifts utilitarianism from Jeremy Bentham's purely quantitative calculus ("push-pin is as good as poetry") to a qualitative framework that values intellectual cultivation.
-   **Ethical Foundation for Liberalism**: Connects directly to Mill's political thought in *On Liberty*, where he defends individual freedom on the grounds of "utility in the largest sense, grounded on the permanent interests of man as a progressive being."
