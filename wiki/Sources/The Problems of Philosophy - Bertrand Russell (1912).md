# The Problems of Philosophy - Bertrand Russell (1912)

> **The Problems of Philosophy** (1912). By Bertrand Russell (1872–1970). Russell's short, classic introduction to philosophy — a *primary* work (unlike his 1945 survey), and one of the most widely read first books in analytic epistemology. Written for the Home University Library; acknowledges G. E. Moore (sense-data/physical objects) and J. M. Keynes (probability/induction). Where the *History* surveys others, *Problems* states Russell's *own* theory of knowledge: sense-data vs. physical objects, knowledge by acquaintance vs. by description, the problem of induction and the a priori, realism about universals, the correspondence theory of truth, and the famous closing on the value of philosophy.

## Nature of the source

This is the **companion primary text** to the already-ingested [[Sources/A History of Western Philosophy - Bertrand Russell (1945)]]. Together they give the vault both Russell-the-historian (verdicts, the social-circumstances method, "cosmic impiety") and **Russell-the-epistemologist** (constructive theory of knowledge). The Preface: "I have confined myself in the main to those problems of philosophy in regard to which I thought it possible to say something positive and constructive… theory of knowledge occupies a larger space than metaphysics." Notably, *Problems* (1912) already contains Russell's anti-pragmatist **realism about truth** that the *History* (1945) would restate as "cosmic impiety" — his opposition to "truth is man-made" was lifelong.

## Chapter-by-chapter

- **I. Appearance and Reality.** The table: its colour, shape, texture, hardness all vary with viewpoint, light, and observer, so "the real table, if there is one, is not the same as what we immediately experience." Introduces **sense-data** ("the things immediately known in sensation — colours, sounds, smells, hardnesses…") vs. **sensation** (the awareness of them), and the **physical object** ("the real table, if it exists") whose collection is **matter**. The appearance/reality distinction; "the one thing we know about [the table] is that it is not what it seems."
- **II. The Existence of Matter.** Descartes' method of doubt; the cogito refined ("what is quite certain… is not 'I am seeing a brown colour,' but rather, 'a brown colour is being seen'"). We cannot *prove* an external world (no logical absurdity in the dream-hypothesis), but the realist hypothesis is *simpler* (the cat that gets hungry between meals). Belief in an independent world is an **instinctive belief**; philosophy's job is to organize instinctive beliefs into a "harmonious system," diminishing (not eliminating) the chance of error.
- **III. The Nature of Matter.** Physical science reduces matter to motion in a public **physical space**, distinct from the private spaces of sight and touch. We can know the *relations/structure* of physical objects (geometry, order) but not their *intrinsic* nature — "the physical objects themselves remain unknown in their intrinsic nature." (Seeing the sun = seeing "the sun of eight minutes ago.")
- **IV. Idealism.** Refutes Berkeley's *esse est percipi*. The key diagnosis: Berkeley equivocates on "idea" — conflating the **act of apprehension** (which is mental) with the **object apprehended** (which need not be). "To argue that the tree itself must be in our minds is like arguing that a person whom we bear in mind is himself in our minds." Acquaintance is "a relation between the mind and something other than the mind"; its objects need not be mental.
- **V. Knowledge by Acquaintance and Knowledge by Description.** The signature distinction (see [[Concepts/Knowledge by Acquaintance and Knowledge by Description (Russell)]]). **Acquaintance** = direct awareness without inference (sense-data, memory, introspection, probably the Self, and **universals**). **Description** ("the so-and-so") = knowledge of things we are *not* acquainted with, via truths connecting them to things we are. The **fundamental principle**: "Every proposition which we can understand must be composed wholly of constituents with which we are acquainted" (the Bismarck and Julius Caesar examples; ordinary proper names are really descriptions). Description "enables us to pass beyond the limits of our private experience."
- **VI. On Induction.** Restates **Hume's problem** (see [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]]) with the famous **chicken**: "The man who has fed the chicken every day throughout its life at last wrings its neck." Past uniformities *cause* expectation but do not *justify* it; the **inductive principle** (more associations → higher probability, approaching but never reaching certainty) "is incapable of being proved by an appeal to experience" without circularity. So we "must either accept the inductive principle on the ground of its intrinsic evidence, or forgo all justification of our expectations about the future."
- **VII. On Our Knowledge of General Principles.** The inductive principle is one of several **a priori** principles (the laws of logic — identity, non-contradiction, excluded middle — and "anything implied by a true proposition is true"). Russell's verdict on the empiricist/rationalist controversy: on logical principles "the **rationalists were in the right**" (logic can't be proved by experience, since all proof presupposes it); but on existence "the **empiricists were in the right**" — "nothing can be known to exist except by the help of experience." All a priori knowledge of existence is *hypothetical*. Includes a priori knowledge of **ethical value** (intrinsic goods); and pure mathematics as a priori ("two and two are four" holds in "any possible world," unlike "all men are mortal").
- **VIII. How A Priori Knowledge is Possible.** Engages Kant. Russell rejects Kant's view that a priori truth reflects the *constitution of the mind* (which would make it contingent on us, and unable to guarantee it for "things in themselves"). His own view: a priori knowledge is knowledge of **relations among universals**, not about the mind.
- **IX–X. The World of Universals / On Our Knowledge of Universals.** Realism about **universals** (Plato's "ideas" reinterpreted): qualities and especially **relations** (e.g., "north of," "before") are real but do not *exist* in time — they "**subsist**" or "have being." Empiricism neglected relations; Russell rehabilitates a qualified Platonism. We have acquaintance with some universals, knowledge of others by description.
- **XI. On Intuitive Knowledge.** Self-evidence comes in **degrees** (the trotting horse fading out of hearing); some truths are self-evident with absolute certainty (acquaintance with a fact), others only partially.
- **XII. Truth and Falsehood.** The **correspondence theory** (see [[Concepts/Correspondence Theory of Truth (Russell)]]). Three requisites: truth must admit an opposite (falsehood); truth/falsehood are properties of *beliefs*; but they depend on something *outside* the belief. Rejects **coherence** as the *definition* of truth (two reasons: more than one coherent system is possible; coherence presupposes the laws of logic) — though coherence is "often a most important *test*." The Othello/Desdemona multiple-relation theory of judgment; "minds do not *create* truth or falsehood."
- **XIII. Knowledge, Error, and Probable Opinion.** Knowledge is not simply "true belief" (the true belief luckily derived from a false premise, or by fallacious reasoning, is not knowledge). Derivative vs. intuitive knowledge; "knowledge" shades into "probable opinion"; "all our knowledge of truths is infected with some degree of doubt."
- **XIV. The Limits of Philosophical Knowledge.** Critique of grand metaphysics (esp. Hegel's claim to deduce reality's nature from logic alone); philosophy cannot establish sweeping results about "the universe as a whole." A modest, piecemeal scope (anticipating the *History*'s logical-analysis chapter).
- **XV. The Value of Philosophy.** The famous close (see [[Concepts/The Value of Philosophy (Russell)]]): philosophy is to be studied "not for the sake of any definite answers… but rather for the sake of the questions themselves," which "enlarge our conception of what is possible" and free us "from the tyranny of custom." Knowledge as "enlargement of the Self" via union with the **not-Self**; the **free intellect** "will see as God might see… without the trammels of customary beliefs." Explicitly rejects "the view which tells us that Man is the measure of all things, that truth is man-made" — it "fetters contemplation to Self." "Citizens of the universe, not only of one walled city."

## Key Quotes

- "The real table, if there is one, is not immediately known to us at all, but must be an inference from what is immediately known."
- "What is quite certain at once is not 'I am seeing a brown colour,' but rather, 'a brown colour is being seen.'"
- "Every proposition which we can understand must be composed wholly of constituents with which we are acquainted."
- "The man who has fed the chicken every day throughout its life at last wrings its neck."
- "Coherence cannot be accepted as giving the *meaning* of truth, though it is often a most important *test* of truth."
- "Minds do not *create* truth or falsehood. They create beliefs… What makes a belief true is a *fact*."
- "Philosophy is to be studied, not for the sake of any definite answers… but rather for the sake of the questions themselves."
- "The free intellect will see as God might see, without a 'here' and 'now,' without hopes and fears."

## Provenance & Cautions

- A 1912 introduction: clear and canonical, but Russell's positions evolved (he later abandoned the multiple-relation theory of judgment, and "sense-data" fell out of favour; his realism about universals remains contested). Read as Russell's *early* analytic epistemology.
- The **correspondence theory** and **anti-pragmatism** are stable across his career — *Problems* (1912) and the *History* (1945, "cosmic impiety") agree.
- Acknowledged debts to **Moore** and **Keynes**; written before the full development of Russell's later logical atomism.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **Russell's correspondence truth vs. [[Concepts/Pragmatic Theory of Truth (James)]] / [[Concepts/Humanism - Reality as Malleable (Schiller and James)]]**: head-on. James: "truth happens… is made true"; Russell: "minds do not create truth… what makes a belief true is a fact." The vault now holds both the constructivist and realist poles, plus Russell's "cosmic impiety" critique.
- **Russell's inductive principle vs. [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]]**: Russell agrees induction can't be justified by experience, but (unlike Hume's pure scepticism) proposes accepting it as an **a priori principle** of "intrinsic evidence" — a constructive move Hume would resist.
- **Realism about universals** vs. the vault's nominalist/empiricist strands ([[Concepts/Nominal and Real Essences (Locke)]], Hume): Russell rehabilitates a qualified Platonism that Locke/Hume sought to dissolve.
- **"Knowledge ≠ true belief"** (ch. XIII) anticipates the 20th-c. "justified true belief" debates (Gettier, 1963) — a forward-looking open thread.
