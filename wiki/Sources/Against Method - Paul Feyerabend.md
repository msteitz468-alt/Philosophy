# Against Method - Paul Feyerabend

> Paul Feyerabend's 1975 polemic (here the 1993 third edition), subtitled *Outline of an Anarchistic Theory of Knowledge*. The radical culmination of the Popper→Kuhn→Lakatos debate: **there is no fixed scientific method** — every methodological rule (including falsificationism) has been violated in the great advances, and *had to be*, for science to progress. "The only principle that does not inhibit progress is: **anything goes**." Built on a detailed re-reading of **Galileo** (who triumphed by propaganda, rhetoric, ad hoc moves, and counter-inductive defiance of the evidence), it ends as a **political** thesis: science is one tradition among many, not the one road to truth, and in a free society it should be **separated from the state** as the churches are.

**Full title / edition**: *Against Method: Outline of an Anarchistic Theory of Knowledge*, Paul Feyerabend (1924–1994). First published 1975 (NLB/Verso), growing out of a planned debate-book with **Imre Lakatos** (to whom it is dedicated — "friend and fellow-anarchist" — and who died in 1974 before writing his reply); the 1993 **third edition** merges in material from *Science in a Free Society* (1978) and adds chapters on the trial of Galileo and on reality. ~2868 lines in the ingested .txt. The book is deliberately unsystematic — "a letter to a friend," ironic in tone, addressed to Lakatos's rationalism and admiration of Popper.

## Summary

Feyerabend, a former student of Popper and Berkeley colleague of Kuhn, argues that all proposed universal methodologies are both *false* (science has never obeyed them) and *harmful* (obeying them would have strangled science). His case is two-pronged: an **abstract** argument (rules + the theory-ladenness of facts mean any rule can be the wrong one) and a **historical** argument (the Copernican Revolution, especially Galileo, breaks every empiricist and falsificationist rule). The famous slogan **"anything goes"** is, he insists in the preface, *not* a positive principle he endorses but "the terrified exclamation of a rationalist who takes a closer look at history" — there is no single rule that holds always, so the only "rule" surviving universal scrutiny is the empty one.

Key moves:
- **Counterinduction**: progress often requires advancing hypotheses that *contradict* well-confirmed theories and well-established facts. Counter-evidence and the hidden assumptions in our "facts" can be unearthed only from the standpoint of an alternative that conflicts with them.
- **Theory-ladenness via natural interpretations**: "facts" are constituted by older theories sedimented into our perception and language ("natural interpretations"). Galileo's real achievement in the **tower argument** was to *replace* the natural interpretation of "real motion" (naïve operative realism) with the relativity of motion and circular inertia — and to disguise the change as mere "reminiscence" (anamnesis). He also "changed sensations" with the telescope while offering no theory of why the telescope should be trusted (its first sky images were "indistinct, indeterminate, contradictory"). Galileo won by rhetoric, style, and propaganda as much as by argument.
- **Proliferation + tenacity (pluralism)**: invent and develop theories inconsistent with the accepted view (proliferation) *and* hold on to theories despite anomalies (tenacity) — both are rational. (Explicitly drawn from **J. S. Mill's *On Liberty*** and from Niels Bohr's practice.)
- **Against the privilege of science**: science is "objectively" on a par with other traditions (myth, religion, magic, "traditional" medicine); its excellence is real only *relative* to certain values. Hence the **political** conclusion: **separation of science and state**, science taught "as one view among many," public/democratic control of research, and the defence of non-scientific cultures' right "to stand on their own feet." Feyerabend frames his motive as **humanitarian, not intellectual** — anger "at the wanton destruction of cultural achievements."

## Key Claims (the Introduction's 20 theses, condensed)

1. **Science is essentially anarchic**; theoretical anarchism is more humane and more progress-friendly than "law-and-order" methodology. "The only principle that does not inhibit progress is: anything goes."
2. **Counterinduction**: use hypotheses that contradict well-confirmed theories and/or established facts.
3. The **consistency condition** (new theories must agree with accepted ones) is unreasonable — it preserves the *older*, not the *better*, theory. **Proliferation** aids science; **uniformity** impairs its critical power and endangers the free individual.
4. "There is no idea, however ancient and absurd, that is not capable of improving our knowledge." Even political interference may be needed against the "chauvinism of science."
5. **No theory agrees with all the facts**; facts are constituted by older ideologies, so a clash may signal *progress*.
6–11. The **Galileo / tower** case: natural interpretations; the new relativity-of-motion observation language introduced by "anamnesis"; the **telescope** without a supporting theory; "irrational" methods of support were *needed* because of the "uneven development" of science. "Copernicanism… survived only because reason was frequently overruled in [its] past."
12–14. Galileo's method generalizes (e.g. the mind/body problem); the Church's case against Galileo was, by the standards of the time, "rational"; the full Copernican Revolution fits no clean rule.
15. Abolish the distinctions discovery/justification, norms/facts, observation/theory terms. **"Popper's critical rationalism fails for the same reasons."**
16–19. Methodological *comparison* breaks down for non-scientific vs. scientific views and for the most general "mythological" parts of science. **Neither science nor rationality is a universal measure of excellence**; both are *particular traditions*. Science is "neither a single tradition, nor the best tradition there is" — in a democracy it "should be separated from the state just as churches are."
20. The driving motive is moral: anger at cultural destruction and intellectual conceit.

## Contributions and Integration with Existing Wiki Content

This is the **radical pole** of the vault's Philosophy of Science leg — the position that *no* methodology (Popper's, the positivists', even Lakatos's) survives contact with real history.

- **Completes the Popper→Kuhn→Lakatos→Feyerabend arc**: Feyerabend turns the [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)|falsifiability]] criterion against itself (strict falsificationism would have killed Copernicanism in its cradle) and pushes [[Concepts/Incommensurability (Kuhn)|Kuhn's incommensurability]] further (he co-originated the term, independently, in 1962). He attacks **Lakatos** most intimately: the *Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes* is "anarchism in disguise," since its standards can only be applied with hindsight and so give no rules for the present. → the contradiction page is broadened: [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]] now spans the full rationalism↔anarchism spectrum.
- **Deepens the Quine/holism thread**: Feyerabend's "no theory agrees with all the facts" and the constitution of facts by theory are kin to [[Concepts/Confirmation Holism (Duhem-Quine Thesis)]] and underdetermination — but Feyerabend draws an *anarchist* moral where Quine draws a *holist-pragmatist* one. See [[Thinkers/W.V.O. Quine]], [[Sources/Two Dogmas of Empiricism - W.V.O. Quine (1951)]].
- **Theory-ladenness**: his **natural interpretations** are the strongest version in the vault of the rejection of the neutral "given" — compare [[Concepts/Basic Statements and the Empirical Basis (Popper)]] (Popper) and Kuhn; and contrast with the empiricist "given" of [[Concepts/Sensation and Reflection as the Two Fountains of Ideas (Locke)]] and [[Concepts/Impressions and Ideas - Copy Principle (Hume)]].
- **Counterinduction vs. induction/falsification**: an explicit inversion of inductive method and a radicalization of Popper's "bold conjectures"; relates to [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]].
- **Proliferation/pluralism** ties to **Mill's liberty** and to [[Concepts/Social Cohesion vs. Individual Liberty (Russell)]] and [[Concepts/Toleration (Locke)]]; the **separation of science and state** echoes liberal anti-establishment arguments (Locke's toleration, the Federalist's distrust of any single faction — [[Concepts/Faction (Federalist 10)]]).
- **Relativism / science-as-tradition** resonates with the constructivist pole — [[Concepts/Perspectivism (Nietzsche)]], [[Concepts/Pragmatic Theory of Truth (James)]], [[Concepts/Humanism - Reality as Malleable (Schiller and James)]] — and with the sceptical traditions ([[Concepts/Mitigated Scepticism (Hume)]], [[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]]). It is the sharpest opponent of Popper's realist [[Concepts/Verisimilitude (Truthlikeness) (Popper)]] and of [[Concepts/Correspondence Theory of Truth (Russell)]] (though Feyerabend denied being a relativist "for whom there are no truths as such").

**Vault / AI / personal relevance** (meta):
- A caution against **method-worship**: a single fixed pipeline (one ingest rule, one prompt pattern, one "scientific" workflow) can suppress the moves that actually produce breakthroughs. *Proliferation* (try inconsistent approaches), *tenacity* (don't drop a promising line at the first anomaly), and *counterinduction* (develop the view that contradicts the consensus to expose hidden assumptions) are usable heuristics — bounded by judgment, not elevated into new dogmas (Feyerabend explicitly refuses to replace old rules with new "libertarian" ones).
- AI/agentic: the theory-ladenness of "facts" warns that an agent's framing constitutes what it counts as data; deliberate generation of *rival* framings surfaces hidden assumptions. "Anything goes" is **not** a license for sloppiness — it's a denial that any one rule is always right.
- Career/CPCU/strategy: expert consensus and "best practice" are traditions, not the only road; edge can come from the counter-inductive move and from taking non-standard views seriously — while remembering Feyerabend's own warning that "an increased liberalism in the definition of 'fact' can have grave repercussions."

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **Rationalism vs. anarchism** — the core tension, folded into [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]] (Feyerabend as the radical pole; Lakatos as the failed mediation).
- **The self-reference / relativism charge**: if "anything goes," what authority has *Against Method*? Feyerabend's reply: it is irony and history, not a counter-method; and he denies being a populist or relativist "for whom there are no truths as such" — but critics (and admirers) read him as a relativist anyway.
- **Does the Galileo case generalize?** Feyerabend himself says his account is "not a universal account but a description of prominent aspects of a special period."
- **Is "separation of science and state" workable / desirable?** Live and contested; not adjudicated here.
- Lakatos's reply was never written; the debate is permanently one-sided in the text.
