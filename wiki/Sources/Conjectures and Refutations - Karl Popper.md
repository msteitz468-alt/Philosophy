# Conjectures and Refutations - Karl Popper

> Popper's 1963 collection of essays and lectures (subtitle *The Growth of Scientific Knowledge*) elaborating critical rationalism beyond the technical 1934 *Logik der Forschung*: all knowledge grows by **bold conjectures controlled by attempted refutations**; there are **no authoritative sources** of knowledge (neither observation nor reason); science is demarcated from pseudo-science by **refutability**, illustrated by the famous contrast between Einstein's risky eclipse prediction and the all-explaining theories of Marx, Freud, and Adler; and the aim of science is growing **verisimilitude** (truthlikeness), not probability.

**Full title / edition**: *Conjectures and Refutations: The Growth of Scientific Knowledge* (first published 1963 by Routledge & Kegan Paul; Routledge Classics 2002). Dedicated to F. A. von Hayek. ~9366 lines in the ingested .txt. Two parts — **Conjectures** (chs. 1–10) and **Refutations** (chs. 11–20) — plus an Introduction ("On the Sources of Knowledge and of Ignorance") and technical Addenda. Companion to the already-ingested [[Sources/The Logic of Scientific Discovery - Karl Popper (Routledge Classics)]]; this is the more accessible, wider-ranging, and historically/politically richer statement of the same philosophy.

## Summary

Where *LSD* gave the formal logic of testing, *C&R* gives the **philosophy of the growth of knowledge** and extends critical rationalism into the history of philosophy, metaphysics, the body-mind problem, the social sciences, and politics. The unifying thesis (dust-jacket): *"our knowledge, and especially our scientific knowledge, progresses by unjustified (and unjustifiable) anticipations, by guesses, by tentative solutions to our problems, in a word by conjectures."* Conjectures are controlled by criticism — by attempted refutations including severe tests. They can never be positively justified (neither proven true nor shown "probable"), but their refutation is itself **a step toward the truth**: this is how we learn from our mistakes (epigraphs: Wilde, "Experience is the name every one gives to their mistakes"; Wheeler, "make the mistakes as fast as possible").

## Key Essays and Claims

**Introduction — On the Sources of Knowledge and of Ignorance**: The old quarrel between classical empiricism (Bacon/Locke/Berkeley/Hume/Mill — source of knowledge is observation) and classical rationalism (Descartes/Spinoza/Leibniz — source is intellectual intuition) is *less important than its shared error*: the assumption that knowledge has an authoritative **source** at all. Both are versions of the doctrine that **"truth is manifest"** — that truth, if only we strip away prejudice, reveals itself. This optimistic epistemology breeds its shadow: the **conspiracy theory of ignorance** (if truth is manifest, error must be the work of sinister powers/interests that hide it). Popper's reply: there are no ultimate sources; *every* source is welcome and *every* source is open to critical examination. "The proper question is not 'What are the best sources?' but 'How can we hope to detect and eliminate error?'" Neither observation nor reason is an authority. (→ [[Concepts/No Authoritative Sources of Knowledge (Popper)]])

**1. Science: Conjectures and Refutations** (the celebrated Cambridge lecture): How Popper arrived at the demarcation problem in 1919–20. He contrasts **Einstein's general relativity** — which made the *risky, prohibitive* prediction (light bent by the sun) tested by **Eddington's 1919 eclipse** expedition and which could have been refuted — with **Marx's theory of history, Freud's psychoanalysis, and Adler's individual psychology**, whose adherents saw *confirmations everywhere*. ("With this new case, I suppose, your experience has become thousand-and-one-fold.") The very fact that these theories explained *any* conceivable event was their weakness, not their strength. The seven conclusions (reformulated): (1) it is easy to get confirmations for nearly any theory; (2) **confirmations count only if they result from risky predictions**; (3) every good theory is a **prohibition**; (4) **irrefutability is a vice, not a virtue**; (5) every genuine test is an attempt to refute; (6) confirming evidence counts only as a *failed* serious attempt at refutation ("corroborating evidence"); (7) theories rescued from refutation by ad hoc "**conventionalist twists/stratagems**" lower their scientific status. (→ updates [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)]])

**2. The Nature of Philosophical Problems and their Roots in Science**: Genuine philosophical problems are rooted in problems outside philosophy (in science, mathematics, politics, religion) and "die" if cut off from those roots. Against the linguistic-analysis view that there are only philosophical *puzzles*, not problems.

**3. Three Views Concerning Human Knowledge**: Against (1) **essentialism/"ultimate explanation by essences"** (Galileo's "betrayal"; science as final knowledge of the natures of things) and (2) **instrumentalism** (theories as mere calculating instruments/tools, neither true nor false — Berkeley, Mach, Duhem, Poincaré, Bohr's Copenhagen turn). Popper defends (3) **conjecturalism**: theories are genuine *conjectures about reality*, true or false, irreducible to instruments, capable of real explanatory discovery (the prediction of *new kinds* of events that instrumentalism cannot account for). (→ [[Concepts/Three Views Concerning Human Knowledge (Popper)]])

**4. Towards a Rational Theory of Tradition**; **5. Back to the Presocratics**: The **critical tradition** — the freedom to challenge and improve on one's master's doctrine — was *invented by the Ionian/Milesian school* (Thales → Anaximander → Anaximenes). Anaximander's conjecture that the earth hangs unsupported in space, held by equilibrium, is "one of the boldest, most revolutionary, and most portentous ideas in the whole history of human thought." Science did not begin with cautious collection of observations (the "Baconian myth") but with **bold theories** about the cosmos. "All science is cosmology." Heraclitus defended against Kirk & Raven.

**6. A Note on Berkeley as Precursor of Mach and Einstein**; **7. Kant's Critique and Cosmology**; **8. On the Status of Science and of Metaphysics**: A sympathetic re-reading of **Kant**: Kant's real problem was the cosmological one (how is Newton's science possible?), and his error was *believing Newton's theory was certainly true* (a forgivable error before Einstein). Popper's critical rationalism "merely puts the finishing touch to Kant's own critical philosophy," extending Kant's autonomy and his insight that the scientist must *interrogate* nature rather than wait passively for her to reveal herself. The **irrefutability of philosophical (metaphysical) theories** is examined: a theory can be both **false and irrefutable** (e.g. determinism vs indeterminism are both irrefutable yet incompatible; pure existential statements like "there exists a curative Latin formula" are irrefutable but reasonably held false). Irrefutability therefore entails neither truth nor meaninglessness.

**9. Why are the Calculi of Logic and Arithmetic Applicable to Reality?**; **10. Truth, Rationality, and the Growth of Scientific Knowledge**: Endorses **Tarski's** rehabilitation of the objective (correspondence) theory of truth — "science as the search for truth," with truth as a **regulative idea** (the standard we may fall short of, which gives sense to *error* and *learning*). Introduces **verisimilitude** = **truth-content minus falsity-content**; we prefer the theory that, though still fallible (and perhaps false), corresponds *better* to the facts. Verisimilitude, not probability, is what we value: a high-content/bold theory is *logically improbable* yet more valuable. **Three requirements for the growth of knowledge**: a new theory should (1) proceed from a *simple, new, unifying idea*; (2) be **independently testable** (yield new, preferably new-kind, predictions — not merely ad hoc); and (3) **pass some new and severe tests**. (→ [[Concepts/Verisimilitude (Truthlikeness) (Popper)]], updates [[Concepts/Corroboration (Popper)]])

**11. The Demarcation Between Science and Metaphysics**: Detailed critique of **Carnap's** successive theories of meaninglessness and verificationism; falsifiability is for demarcating the *empirical*, not for branding metaphysics meaningless. **12–14. Language and the Body-Mind Problem**; four functions of language (expressive, signalling, descriptive, argumentative) — the higher (descriptive, argumentative) functions are what make criticism and objective knowledge possible.

**15. What is Dialectic?**: Hegelian/Marxist **dialectic** (thesis → antithesis → synthesis) is at best a loose, narrower redescription of trial-and-error (conjecture and its criticism). Its fatal move is the claim that **contradictions are fertile and need not be avoided** — abandoning the law of non-contradiction. Popper: contradictions are fertile *only because* we refuse to tolerate them and are thereby driven to change our theories; a system that accepts contradiction can prove anything and so says nothing. (→ [[Concepts/Critique of Historicism and Dialectic (Popper)]])

**16. Prediction and Prophecy in the Social Sciences**; **18. Utopia and Violence**; **19. The History of Our Time**; **20. Humanism and Reason**: The methodological core of Popper's politics (developed in *The Poverty of Historicism* and *The Open Society*). Distinguish **conditional scientific prediction** ("if conditions C, then E") from **unconditional historical prophecy**. Long-term prophecy is only possible for *isolated, stationary, recurrent* systems (the solar system) — society is not one. **Historicism** (the doctrine that history obeys laws permitting prophecy of its course — Marx, etc.) is therefore baseless; "the fact that we can predict eclipses does not provide a valid reason for expecting that we can predict revolutions." Against **utopian** social engineering enforced by violence; for **piecemeal** reform, free discussion, and the open society. (→ [[Concepts/Critique of Historicism and Dialectic (Popper)]])

## Contributions and Integration with Existing Wiki Content

This source completes the Popper picture begun with *LSD* and pushes the vault decisively into its **Philosophy of Science / Epistemology** leg. It also opens, for the first time in primary form, **Popper's political philosophy** (anti-historicism, the open society, piecemeal engineering) and his **historiography of philosophy** (the critical tradition of the Presocratics).

**Agreements / extensions of existing pages**:
- Grounds and enriches [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)]] with the Marx/Freud/Adler vs. Einstein illustration and the seven conclusions (now the canonical popular statement).
- Supplies the primary text for [[Concepts/Corroboration (Popper)]]'s forward-references to **verisimilitude** and the **three requirements for growth**.
- Engages [[Concepts/Correspondence Theory of Truth (Russell)]]: Popper, like Russell, defends an objective correspondence theory (now via Tarski) and rejects truth-as-coherence and truth-as-usefulness. Direct ally against [[Concepts/Pragmatic Theory of Truth (James)]] — Popper explicitly links epistemological *pragmatism* and *relativism* to authoritarian/totalitarian ideas (crediting Russell).
- Engages [[Thinkers/Kant]]: a generous reading making Popper the "finishing touch" to Kant's critical philosophy and autonomy — complements the demarcation-as-"Kant's problem" theme already in the vault.
- Engages the **Presocratics** and [[Thinkers/Plato]] / [[Thinkers/Aristotle]]: locates the origin of rational criticism in Ionia; criticizes Aristotelian **essentialism** (definitions as statements of essence) — connecting to [[Concepts/Modal Copula (Aristotelian Modality)]] and [[Concepts/Nominal and Real Essences (Locke)]].

**Contrasts / productive tensions** (flagged, not silently reconciled):
- Vs. **instrumentalism** (Berkeley/Mach/Duhem/Poincaré, and the Copenhagen turn): theories are conjectures about reality, not mere tools — a realist commitment.
- Vs. **essentialism** (Aristotle, Descartes/Kant on "essential" definitions): science seeks structural conjectures, never ultimate essences; definitions add no factual knowledge.
- Vs. **Hegelian/Marxist dialectic** and **historicism**: the vault's first sustained critique of Hegel and Marx (neither yet a primary source) — see [[Concepts/Critique of Historicism and Dialectic (Popper)]].
- Vs. **pragmatic/coherence theories of truth** ([[Thinkers/William James]]): correspondence + verisimilitude as the realist alternative.
- With [[Concepts/Mitigated Scepticism (Hume)]] and Pyrrhonism ([[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]]): Popper is a *fallibilist* but an anti-sceptic about *growth* — we never have certainty, yet we can get nearer the truth.

**Vault / AI / personal relevance** (meta):
- The wiki as a critical-rationalist artifact is reinforced: pages are conjectures; ingests, lint, and contradiction pages are attempted refutations; **no authoritative source** (no raw file, no prior page) is beyond criticism; the aim is rising verisimilitude of the synthesis, not mere accumulation of confirmations.
- AI/agentic work: prefer **risky, prohibitive** claims; treat confirmations as failed refutation attempts; avoid **conventionalist twists** (ad hoc patches that immunize a failing answer); distinguish conditional prediction from prophecy when forecasting.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The objectivity of accepting **basic statements** by decision (see [[Concepts/Basic Statements and the Empirical Basis (Popper)]]) — unchanged tension carried from *LSD*.
- The **formal definition of verisimilitude** given here was later shown (by Tichý and Miller, 1974) to be technically flawed for false theories; the *intuition* (truth-content vs falsity-content) survives, the measure does not. Noted as an open question on the verisimilitude page.
- Whether Popper's anti-historicism does justice to genuine large-scale social regularities (a live debate; Marx/Hegel not yet primary-sourced for the other side).
- **Kuhn's critique of naive falsificationism is now primary-sourced** ([[Sources/The Structure of Scientific Revolutions - Kuhn (Chicago, 4th ed.)]]; see [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]]). Lakatos and Feyerabend remain outside the vault.
