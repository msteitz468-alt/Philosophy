# Break 'Em Up — Zephyr Teachout (2020)
> Monopoly is tyranny: concentrated corporate power is a new form of private government that subverts democracy, and breaking it up is the central task of our time.

## Argument Spine

Teachout argues that modern monopolies are not merely economic inefficiencies but *political* entities — a new form of quasi-governmental private power that extracts wealth, silences dissent, surveils workers, and subverts democratic self-governance. The consumer welfare standard (low prices = no problem) is the ideological error that allowed this accumulation. The solution requires not boycotts or ethical consumerism but a mass anti-monopoly movement using existing antitrust tools, new legislation, and structural breakup of dominant firms across every sector of American life.

Two theses: (1) monopoly is tyranny — a democratic republic cannot survive corporations with governmental ambitions; (2) strategy — the left must build an anti-monopoly movement, not just demand redistribution or individual corporate accountability.

## Whole-Work Assessment

*Break 'Em Up* is the vault's primary text on political philosophy of corporate power and anti-monopoly thought. Its contribution is twofold: (1) it bridges economic analysis (monopsony, consumer welfare standard) and political philosophy (tyranny, private government, republican freedom) in a way that enriches both; (2) it extends the vault's classical political philosophy — Aristotle's political animal and tyrant distinction, Madison's faction theory, Hobbes's Leviathan — into the 21st-century corporate context, making those frameworks live rather than historical.

The book significantly shifts existing pages: [[Thinkers/Aristotle]] gains a modern application of the tyranny analysis; [[Thinkers/James M. Buchanan]]'s public choice framework gains a major counter-position; [[Thinkers/Mancur Olson]]'s collective action theory gains a real-world test case (can the anti-monopoly movement succeed against the Olsonian logic?). New pages created: Teachout (thinker), Consumer Welfare Standard, Private Government, Chickenization of Labor, Surveillance Capitalism and Platform Power, Forced Arbitration as Private Justice, Monopsony and Wage Suppression, Moral Economy (Teachout), Anti-Monopoly Movement (Teachout), and a contradiction page (Teachout vs. Buchanan).

The book is less philosophically systematic than other vault sources — it is polemical and activist — but philosophically significant for its implicit claim that political philosophy must theorize *private* power, not just state power. Foucault's biopolitics and governmentality are the natural complement from within the vault; Hobbes's Leviathan and Aristotle's Politics are the classical interlocutors.

**Cross-link of highest value:** [[Contradictions/Teachout vs Buchanan — Private vs Government Sources of Monopoly]] — this is the vault's clearest example of two anti-monopoly thinkers arriving at opposite prescriptions from opposed diagnoses of how monopoly originates.
