# Aristotle - Nicomachean Ethics (Complete, W.D. Ross)

> The full ten-book treatise (W. D. Ross translation) on how one should live. Supersedes the vault's prior partial Book-I-only summary ([[Sources/Aristotle - Nicomachean Ethics (Book I)]], from secondary notes) with a direct reading of the complete text.

## Argument Spine

The human good is *eudaimonia* — activity of the soul in accordance with virtue over a complete life — established via the function (*ergon*) argument and defended against the rival lives of pleasure, honor, and contemplation-only in Book I. Virtue of character (*arête*) is a stable disposition to choose the mean between excess and deficiency, acquired by habituation, not nature or teaching alone (Book II), and is exercised through voluntary choice (*prohairesis*) following deliberation about means (Book III). Books III–IV survey the particular moral virtues (courage, temperance, liberality, magnificence, magnanimity, mildness, wit, friendliness, and shame) as a series of such means. Book V isolates justice as a special virtue, both universal (lawfulness) and particular (fair distribution and rectification), with equity (*epieikeia*) as law's necessary corrective. Book VI turns to the intellectual virtues, crowning practical wisdom (*phronēsis*) as the virtue that makes correct deliberation and right action possible, alongside theoretical wisdom (*sophia*). Books VII–VIII address the puzzle of *akrasia* (incontinence — acting against one's own better judgment) and open the extended treatment of friendship (*philia*), continued through Book IX, as indispensable to the good life. Book X completes the account of pleasure (as supervening on unimpeded activity, not identical with it) and closes by arguing that the contemplative life (*theōria*), as activity of the highest part of the soul in accordance with the highest virtue, is the most complete happiness — with the practical/political life of virtue as happiness in a secondary sense — before transitioning to the *Politics* as the sequel inquiry into how such character is produced by law and education.

## Whole-Work Assessment

Reading the full ten books (rather than Book I alone, previously the vault's only primary window into the *Ethics*) reveals a tightly architectonic structure that a Book-I-only summary could not show: the mean-doctrine of Book II is not a standalone thesis but a method instantiated across every particular virtue in Books III–IV, re-derived in a modified proportional form for justice in Book V, grounded epistemically in the practical wisdom of Book VI, stress-tested against the puzzle of akrasia in Book VII, extended relationally in the friendship books (VIII–IX), and finally subordinated to — while also being distinguished in degree from — the contemplative life in Book X. The work's own explicit conclusion (habituation and law, not argument, form most people's character) is the direct textual bridge to the *Politics*, making the *Ethics* and *Politics* a genuine diptych rather than two independent treatises — a structural point invisible from Book I alone. The text also opens (rather than closes) several genuine interpretive tensions the vault now records rather than resolves: the inclusivist/intellectualist debate over whether contemplative happiness includes or supersedes practical virtue (Book X vs. Books II–IX), and the relation between the magnanimous person's self-sufficient disdain for dependency (Book IV) and the mature claim that even the happiest person needs friends (Book IX). This ingest also directly deepens the vault's existing Plato-Aristotle classical cluster (virtue as knowledge/teachable vs. habituated, Socratic akrasia denial vs. Aristotle's having/using resolution) and sets up Aquinas's medieval synthesis (already linked from [[Concepts/Eudaimonia]]) on firmer primary-source footing.
