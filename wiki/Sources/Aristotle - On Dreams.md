# Aristotle - On Dreams

> Second of the *Parva Naturalia* (*De Insomniis*, 458a). Asks which faculty of soul dreaming belongs to. Answer: neither sense-perception proper (the senses are inactive in sleep) nor opinion/intellect alone, but the **presentative faculty** (*phantasia*) — dreaming is a "movement" left over from sense-impressions, exercised while the controlling sense-organ is quiescent, and mistaken by the dreamer for a present perception.

## Argument Spine

Sense-perception cannot be the seat of dreaming, since the eyes are shut and no external object stimulates the sleeper — yet dreaming is more than bare opinion, since dreamers judge not just "a man" but "a white, beautiful man," a judgment opinion alone (without perception) cannot make. The resolution: perceptual stimulation leaves behind a persisting "movement" in the sense-organs even after the object departs (proven by afterimages, sustained color/motion illusions, and — strikingly — the "cloudy mirror" phenomenon during menstruation). By day these residual movements are swamped by fresh, stronger waking perceptions; by night, as blood recedes inward toward the heart during sleep, the residual movements become disproportionately vivid and are received by the primary/common sense-organ as if they were fresh perceptions. A dream is this presentation (*phantasma*) mistaken, under sleep's peculiar conditions, for the real thing — "though not actually Koriskos, it is like Koriskos."

## Key Claims / Positions

- Dreaming belongs to **phantasia** (the presentative faculty), not to sense-perception simply nor to intellect/opinion simply — it is "an activity of the faculty of sense-perception... qua presentative."
- **Persistence-of-stimulation** physics: sensory qualitative change, like projectile motion or heat conduction, continues propagating after its original cause ceases — grounding afterimages, color-adaptation illusions, and (famously) the claim that a menstruating woman's gaze can cloud a mirror's surface via an atmospheric medium (Aristotle's own emission-in-reverse theory of vision as reciprocal action).
- **Emotional/pathological bias**: fear, desire, fever, and intoxication all lower the threshold of resemblance needed to trigger a false identification (the coward "sees" enemies, the lover "sees" the beloved) — the same mechanism, intensified, produces dream-images from faint residual movements.
- Sleep amplifies trifling internal movements into vivid presentations because daytime "noise" (fresh perception and thought) is absent — an early **signal-to-noise** account of why dream-images seem so impressive despite trivial causes.
- **Not every night-time presentation is a dream** proper: the treatise excludes faint real perceptions during light sleep (glimpsed lamplight, heard cock-crow later recognized as real) and true thoughts occurring in sleep — the dream proper is specifically a presentation grounded in residual sense-movement, occurring in sleep taken strictly.
- Excessive internal movement (after heavy meals, in infants) can obliterate or badly distort dream-images, just as violently disturbed water gives no clear reflection — anticipates the "no dreams in infancy/right after eating" claim developed further in *On Divination in Sleep*.
- Self-aware dreaming: some sleepers retain "something within" declaring the presentation a mere dream, distinguishing lucid from non-lucid dream-states.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The extramission-adjacent claim that the eye "produces a certain effect" upon what it sees (the mirror-clouding example) sits oddly beside Aristotle's generally intromissive account of vision in [[Concepts/Theory of Perception (Aristotle)]]; flagged here as an internal tension rather than resolved.
