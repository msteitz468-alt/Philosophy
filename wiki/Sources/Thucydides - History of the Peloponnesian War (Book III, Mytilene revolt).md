# Thucydides - History of the Peloponnesian War (Book III, Mytilene revolt)

> Narrative of a critical phase in the Peloponnesian War: Peloponnesian invasion of Attica, the revolt of Mytilene (Lesbos) from Athens, Athenian military and diplomatic response, the shift to siege warfare, wider campaigns, and the Mytilenaean appeal to the Peloponnesian allies at Olympia. Explores the dynamics of empire and subject revolt, the fragility of alliances under power imbalance, the interplay of strategy, diplomacy, honor, and necessity in Greek interstate relations.

## Summary

In the context of ongoing war and plague-weakened Athens, Lesbos (led by Mytilene) revolts. The Athenians, initially disbelieving, dispatch a fleet. Mytilene attempts defense and negotiation while secretly seeking Peloponnesian aid. The conflict becomes a siege with divided control (Athenians at sea, Mytileneans inland). Mytilenaean envoys appeal to the Peloponnesian coalition at Olympia, arguing that revolt from an oppressive empire is justified when alliances are no longer equal and mutually beneficial.

## Key Narrative and Themes

### Peloponnesian Invasion and the Revolt
During grain ripening, Peloponnesians under Archidamus invade Attica, ravage the land. Athenian cavalry limits damage near the city. After provisions run out, invaders withdraw.

Immediately after, all Lesbos except Methymna revolts from Athens. The Lesbians had long wished to revolt but were earlier refused by Lacedaemonians. Now they accelerate preparations: harbor defenses, walls, ships, supplies, archers, in coordination with Lacedaemonians and Boeotians.

### Athenian Response
Athens, weakened by plague and war, sees a new front against a strong naval power as serious. Initially skeptical of the reports. When envoys fail to stop Mytilene’s preparations, they dispatch forty ships under Cleippides to preempt.

Mytilene, forewarned, halts festival at Apollo Maleis temple, strengthens defenses, and when Athenians demand surrender of ships and dismantling of walls, refuse. Limited fighting occurs; Mytileneans avoid full engagement and seek to negotiate for time.

### Escalation to Siege and Wider Operations
Negotiations fail. Revolt spreads across Lesbos (except Methymna); Imbrians and Lemnians join. A battle near the Athenian camp favors Mytilene initially, but they withdraw.

Athenians summon reinforcements, establish a base at Malea, and blockade both harbors by sea while Mytilenaeans hold inland. It becomes a classic siege.

Simultaneously, Athens sends thirty ships under Asopius to ravage Laconia coasts, operate toward Naupactus, and conduct combined expeditions (with mixed results).

### The Appeal to Peloponnesian Allies
Mytilenaean envoys reach Lacedaemon and are instructed to present their case before the allied assembly at Olympia.

They argue before the Peloponnesian coalition that Greeks traditionally distrust those who abandon alliances, but such distrust applies only to alliances freely entered among equals. When an alliance becomes domination by the stronger party (as with Athenian empire), revolt is not unjust but a return to proper relations based on mutual goodwill and equality of power.

The appeal mixes pragmatic calculation (power balance) with moral/political claims (honor, justice of resistance to oppression).
