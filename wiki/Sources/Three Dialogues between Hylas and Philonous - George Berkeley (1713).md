# Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous - George Berkeley (1713)

> A classic philosophical dialogue presenting a defense of immaterialism (subjective idealism). Written by [[Thinkers/George Berkeley|George Berkeley]] in response to critiques of his earlier *Principles of Human Knowledge*.

## Summary

The *Three Dialogues* is structured as a conversation between two characters:
1. **Hylas** (derived from the Greek word for "matter"): Represents the common-sense material realist who believes in mind-independent physical objects.
2. **Philonous** ("lover of mind"): Represents Berkeley's own view, arguing that there is no material substance and that reality consists entirely of minds and ideas.

Over three successive days, Philonous systematically deconstructs Hylas's belief in matter, showing that immaterialism is not a skeptical denial of the physical world but rather its truest, most common-sense defense against skepticism and atheism.

---

## Dialogues Breakdown

### First Dialogue: The Deconstruction of Matter
Philonous begins by arguing that the objects of sensory perception are nothing but "sensible qualities" (heat, cold, taste, color, shape). 
* **Pain and Pleasure**: Philonous shows that intense heat or cold is indistinguishable from pain or pleasure, which exist only in the mind. Therefore, heat and cold exist only in the mind.
* **Secondary Qualities**: Through various illustrations (e.g., how the same water can feel warm to one hand and cold to another), Philonous proves that secondary qualities (tastes, colors, sounds) are mind-dependent.
* **Primary Qualities**: Philonous extends this logic to primary qualities (extension, figure, motion). He shows that size and shape are relative to the perceiver (e.g., a small insect perceives a mite as large, and a shape changes depending on the angle of view). Thus, primary qualities are also mind-dependent.
* **Conclusion**: Material substance—defined as an unperceived support for these qualities—is an empty, contradictory concept.

### Second Dialogue: The Mind of God
Having convinced Hylas that sensible qualities cannot exist in a mind-independent matter, Philonous addresses the metaphysical implications:
* **The Existential Argument**: If sensible things cannot exist unless perceived, and they exist independently of human minds (as they plainly do), there must be an infinite, omnipresent mind that perceives them: **God**.
* **Causation**: Passive ideas cannot cause anything. Physical "causation" is merely the regular order in which God displays ideas to our minds. God is the sole active cause in the universe.

### Third Dialogue: Objections and Common Sense
The final dialogue is dedicated to resolving Hylas's lingering objections:
* **The Reality of the World**: Hylas objects that this makes the world an illusion. Philonous replies that immaterialism preserves the reality of the table, chair, and cherry; it only rejects the philosopher's abstract concept of "matter." The cherry is real because it is seen, felt, and tasted.
* **Scientific Language**: Philonous explains that scientific talk of "atoms," "forces," and "corpuscles" is useful for describing the regularities of God's ideas, but they do not represent mind-independent substances.
* **Creation**: In creation, God makes ideas perceptible to created spirits, bringing them into the public phenomenal world.

## Key Tensions / Contradictions

> [!WARNING]
> The central claim of the *Three Dialogues*—that mind-independent material substance does not exist—stands in direct contradiction to Cartesian and Lockean physical realism. See [[Contradictions/Subjective Idealism vs. Physical Realism|Subjective Idealism vs. Physical Realism]].
