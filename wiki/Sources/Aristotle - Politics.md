# Politics (Aristotle)

## Argument Spine
The polis (city-state) is a natural community, the highest and most self-sufficient partnership, existing for the sake of the good life, not mere life. Man is by nature a political animal (*zōon politikon*); the state is prior in nature to the individual and household because the whole is prior to the part. Having established the household (including the contested question of natural slavery and property) as the polis's building block, Aristotle surveys and critiques prior and actual constitutions, classifies constitutions by who rules and for whose benefit (true forms: kingship, aristocracy, polity; deviations: tyranny, oligarchy, democracy), analyzes what causes constitutions to be preserved or overthrown (*stasis*), and concludes with an account of the best/ideal constitution and the education proper to it.

## Whole-Work Assessment

The *Politics* completes the Ethics→Politics arc the vault has anticipated since its first Aristotle ingest: eudaimonia as virtuous activity, established in the *Nicomachean Ethics*, is here shown to require the polis as its necessary context, since leisure, self-sufficiency, and the rule of law are all conditions the individual cannot secure alone. The Argument Spine held up well against the text: the natural-teleological reading of the polis (Book I) is vindicated and directly completed by Book VII's identity of individual and political good. The most productive tension the work opens is not internal but comparative — it supplies the primary ancient text for the private-property-vs-communism debate (against Plato) and, via the usury/chrēmatistikē analysis, an unexpected ancient anticipation of the Marxian critique of self-expanding capital, tightening the three-way [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]/Plato/Aristotle triangle on property that the vault's Teachout ingest had only gestured at. The natural-slavery doctrine remains the work's least defensible and most historically consequential claim, flagged rather than reconciled. Eight new concept pages and one new contradiction page extend the Aristotle hub to its ninth major source cluster.
