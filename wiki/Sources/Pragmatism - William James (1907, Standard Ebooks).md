# Pragmatism - William James (1907, Standard Ebooks)

> **Pragmatism: A New Name for Some Old Ways of Thinking** (1907). By William James (1842–1910). Eight popular lectures delivered at the Lowell Institute, Boston (Nov–Dec 1906) and Columbia University, New York (Jan 1907); "printed as delivered." Dedicated "To the Memory of John Stuart Mill, from whom I first learned the pragmatic openness of mind." The single most influential statement of American pragmatism: it generalizes C. S. Peirce's principle ("How to Make Our Ideas Clear," 1878) into both a **method** for settling metaphysical disputes and a **genetic theory of truth** (truth as what "works," as a process of verification), and applies them to substance, materialism vs. theism, design, free will, the one and the many, common sense, humanism, and religion.

## Summary

James offers pragmatism explicitly as a **mediator** between two enduring philosophical temperaments — the "tender-minded" (rationalist, intellectualist, idealist, optimistic, religious, dogmatic, monistic) and the "tough-minded" (empiricist, sensationalist, materialist, pessimistic, irreligious, sceptical, pluralistic). The fact-loving modern person, he argues, is caught in a dilemma: empiricism gives facts but no religion; rationalism gives a religion that "keeps no contact with concrete facts." Pragmatism resolves the dilemma by keeping "the richest intimacy with facts" while remaining open to religious and metaphysical hypotheses — judged not by their pedigree but by their consequences.

Two doctrines run through the book:

1. **The pragmatic method** — to settle a dispute, trace the *practical consequences* of each alternative being true. "There can be no difference anywhere that doesn't make a difference elsewhere." If no concrete difference can be found, the dispute is idle/verbal. Theories become "instruments, not answers to enigmas in which we can rest"; their "cash-value" is what they do for us within "the stream of experience."

2. **The pragmatic theory of truth** — truth is not a static "copying" relation or an inert agreement with a ready-made reality. "Truth *happens* to an idea. It *becomes* true, is *made* true by events" — by the process of its **verification**. "The true is only the expedient in the way of our thinking, just as the right is only the expedient in the way of our behaving." Truth lives "on a credit system"; ideas "pass" until challenged; all true processes ultimately cash out in "directly verifying sensible experiences somewhere."

The book closes by moving from method and truth toward metaphysics and faith: a defense of **pluralism** over absolute monism, of **humanism** (we contribute to and "build out" reality; truths are "man-made products"), and of **meliorism** in religion (the world's salvation is neither inevitable [optimism] nor impossible [pessimism] but a *possibility* whose probability rises as the actual conditions — including our own acts — accumulate). James's pragmatic theism is pluralistic: God as "primus inter pares," one helper among the shapers of the world's fate, in a universe "still in the making."

### The eight lectures

- **Lecture I — The Present Dilemma in Philosophy**: The history of philosophy as a "clash of human temperaments." The "potentest of all our premises" — temperament — is never avowed. The famous two-column table of the **tender-minded vs. tough-minded** ([[Concepts/Tender-Minded and Tough-Minded (James)]]). The modern fact-lover's dilemma; Leibniz's *Théodicée* on the damned as "a cold literary exercise" exemplifying rationalism's "feeble grasp of reality"; Morrison Swift's anarchist protest against absolutist optimism amid real human misery. Pragmatism offered as the philosophy that can satisfy "both kinds of demand."

- **Lecture II — What Pragmatism Means**: The squirrel-and-tree anecdote ("which party is right depends on what you practically mean by 'going round'"). The **pragmatic method** ([[Concepts/Pragmatic Method (James)]]); Peirce's principle; Ostwald's chemistry. Pragmatism as "a method only," "no dogmas, no doctrines save its method" — "like a corridor in a hotel" (Papini). Theories as instruments. First statement of the truth theory (Dewey/Schiller's instrumentalism): an idea is true "in so far as it helps us to get into satisfactory relation with other parts of our experience." New truth as a "go-between" that marries old opinion to new fact "with a minimum of jolt." The Absolute granted truth "in so far forth" (it gives "moral holidays"), but rejected for its "supernumerary features."

- **Lecture III — Some Metaphysical Problems Pragmatically Considered**: **Substance** (the Eucharist as its one real pragmatic application); **matter vs. spirit / materialism vs. theism** (no difference retrospectively, but prospectively the difference is everything — materialism = "the denial that the moral order is eternal," spiritualism = "the affirmation of an eternal moral order and the letting loose of hope"); **design** ("the word 'design' is a blank cartridge" — the real question is *what sort* of designer, answerable only by facts); **free will** ("freewill means novelties," a "doctrine of relief," a "general cosmological theory of promise," not a basis for the "contemptible right to punish"). All these abstract terms (God, Matter, Design, Free Will, the Absolute) have meaning only as "terms of promise" we "bear into life's thicket."

- **Lecture IV — The One and the Many**: James's "most central of all philosophic problems." Pragmatic enumeration of the senses in which the world is "one" (unity of discourse, continuity, lines of influence, causal origin, generic kinds, purpose, aesthetic union, the one Knower) — each coordinate with a corresponding manyness. Oneness and manyness are "absolutely coordinate"; the world is "neither a universe pure and simple nor a multiverse pure and simple." Absolute monism (Vedanta's Vivekananda quoted; "there is no many") rests on mystical rather than intellectual grounds and is "shattered" by "the slightest modicum" of real separation. Pragmatism must "range herself upon the pluralistic side"; perhaps the "Absolute" should be replaced by the "Ultimate" (oneness at the *end* of things, not their origin). See [[Concepts/The One and the Many (James)]].

- **Lecture V — Pragmatism and Common Sense**: Knowledge "grows in spots… and like grease-spots, the spots spread." Three "stages, or types of thought": **common sense** (its inherited *denkmittel* — thing, same/different, kinds, minds, bodies, one time, one space, subject/attribute, the real, cause), **science** (corpuscles, ether, laws — bursting the bounds of common sense), and **critical philosophy** (Hume/Berkeley/Kant/Hegel — making "havoc of everything"). The common-sense categories are not eternal intuitions but "extraordinarily successful hypotheses" discovered by "prehistoric geniuses" and frozen into language. No stage is "absolutely more true" than another; each is best "for one sphere of life." This restlessness itself "awakens a presumption favorable to the pragmatistic view that all our theories are instrumental."

- **Lecture VI — Pragmatism's Conception of Truth**: The core chapter. "The particular *go* of it" (Clerk Maxwell). Truth = "agreement with reality," but pragmatism analyzes "agreement" as a *process of leading*: true ideas "lead us… into or up to or towards other parts of experience" "progressively, harmoniously, satisfactorily." "True ideas are those that we can assimilate, validate, corroborate and verify." Truth "happens to an idea." Truth "lives on a credit system" (indirect/potential verification "passes muster"). Realities to agree with = concrete facts, abstract relations (mathematics/logic, "eternal" truths), and "the whole body of other truths already in our possession." "The 'true'… is only the expedient in the way of our thinking." The "absolutely true" is an "ideal vanishing-point." Truth is like "wealth," "health," "strength" — a name for processes *in rebus*, not a static essence *ante rem*. Refutes the charge that pragmatism licenses believing "whatever you find it pleasant to say" ("an impudent slander" — the pragmatist feels "the immense pressure of objective control"). See [[Concepts/Pragmatic Theory of Truth (James)]].

- **Lecture VII — Pragmatism and Humanism**: Against "*the* Truth" with a capital T as "a perfect idol of the rationalistic mind." Law, Latin, and language are *results*, not antecedent principles; so is truth. Schiller's **Humanism**: "to an unascertainable extent our truths are man-made products." We add to reality both subject and predicate: we "carve out" constellations (Great Bear / Charles's Wain / Dipper), take 27 as "26 + 1" or "3 cubed," "carve the statue" from the marble block of sensation. Reality has three "resisting" parts (the flux of sensation, the relations among sensations, and previous truths), but "the *that* of it is its own; the *what* depends on the *which*; and the *which* depends on us." Lotze: our descriptions may be "important additions to reality." "The world stands really malleable, waiting to receive its final touches at our hands." This reopens the monism/pluralism question as a question about "the structure of the universe itself": rationalism's "edition de luxe, eternally complete" vs. pragmatism's "one edition of the universe, unfinished, growing." See [[Concepts/Humanism - Reality as Malleable (Schiller and James)]].

- **Lecture VIII — Pragmatism and Religion**: On pragmatic principles "we cannot reject any hypothesis if consequences useful to life flow from it." Two readings of Whitman's "To You": the monistic/mystical (you are *already* safe, absorbed in the One — "quietism," "spiritual opium") vs. the pluralistic/moralistic (your "better possibilities," your loyalty, "picks its way"). The pragmatic analysis of "**possible**": a "concretely grounded" possibility means *some of the conditions of production actually exist*. **Meliorism** between optimism and pessimism: salvation as a "possibility which becomes more and more of a probability the more numerous the actual conditions of salvation become"; "our acts… the turning-places and growing-places… of the world." The "world's author" scenario: a universe "not certain to be saved," "a real adventure with real danger," offered for our *fiat*. James sides with the "moralistic" view, "willing that there should be real losses and real losers, and no total preservation of all that is." A pluralistic theism: God as "but one helper, *primus inter pares*, in the midst of all the shapers of the great world's fate"; we stand to the universe "as our dogs and cats" do to human life. See [[Concepts/Meliorism (James)]].

## Key Quotes

- "The pragmatic method… is to try to interpret each notion by tracing its respective practical consequences."
- "There can be no difference anywhere that doesn't make a difference elsewhere — no difference in abstract truth that doesn't express itself in a difference in concrete fact."
- "Theories thus become instruments, not answers to enigmas, in which we can rest."
- "Truth *happens* to an idea. It *becomes* true, is *made* true by events."
- "The 'true,' to put it very briefly, is only the expedient in the way of our thinking, just as 'the right' is only the expedient in the way of our behaving."
- "Truth lives, in fact, for the most part on a credit system."
- "The *that* of it is its own; but the *what* depends on the *which*; and the *which* depends on us."
- "The world stands really malleable, waiting to receive its final touches at our hands. … Man engenders truths upon it."
- "We receive in short the block of marble, but we carve the statue ourselves."
- "On the pragmatist side we have only one edition of the universe, unfinished, growing in all sorts of places, especially in the places where thinking beings are at work."
- "Meliorism treats salvation as neither inevitable nor impossible. It treats it as a possibility, which becomes more and more of a probability the more numerous the actual conditions of salvation become."

## Provenance & Cautions

- James is reporting and unifying a movement: he leans heavily on **C. S. Peirce** (the originating principle), **John Dewey** and **F. C. S. Schiller** (the instrumental/humanist theory of truth), and **Papini** ("corridor in a hotel"; man's "divinely-creative functions"). He explicitly distinguishes pragmatism from his own separate doctrine of "radical empiricism" (Preface).
- The lectures are popular and polemical ("dealing in broad strokes, avoiding minute controversy"); James concedes the tender/tough table is "a brutal caricature." The truth theory was immediately and "ferociously attacked" by rationalists (Russell, Moore, Bradley, Rickert); James devotes much of VI–VII to rebutting the "impudent slander" that pragmatism means "whatever is pleasant to believe is true."
- Period framing: "darwinism," "energetics" (Ostwald/Mach/Duhem), radium/helium as the era's live science; some dated ethnographic/colonial asides ("Filipinos fit for self-government," "ignorant and barbarous nations") are of 1907.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **Does "true = what works" collapse truth into mere usefulness?** The standing objection (Russell): a belief can be useful yet false, or true yet useless. James's reply: usefulness is *in the long run and on the whole*, under "the immense pressure of objective control" from sensation and prior truth — but critics find the equation still slippery.
- **Self-application**: is the pragmatic theory of truth *itself* true pragmatically or absolutely? (Parallels the [[Concepts/Mitigated Scepticism (Hume)]] self-application worry.)
- **Relation to the vault's realist sources**: James's "humanism" (we partly *make* reality and truth) is in direct tension with Aristotle/Aquinas's adequation theory of truth, Spinoza's *more geometrico* intelligibility of a mind-independent Nature, and Descartes's God-guaranteed clear-and-distinct *scientia*. Flag rather than reconcile.
- **Pragmatic theism vs. the classical/medieval God**: James's finite, "primus inter pares" God who is "one helper" is incompatible with the omnipotent, simple, eternal God of [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]] and [[Thinkers/Augustine]]; it also differs sharply from Spinoza's *Deus sive Natura* and Kant's postulate.
