# The Structure of Scientific Revolutions - Kuhn (Chicago, 4th ed.)

> Thomas Kuhn's 1962 classic — the most influential book in 20th-century history and philosophy of science. It replaces the image of science as the steady, cumulative accumulation of truth (and Popper's image of permanent revolution by falsification) with a cyclical structure: **normal science** under a **paradigm** (puzzle-solving) → accumulating **anomaly** → **crisis** → **revolution** (a **paradigm shift**, a gestalt-like "conversion") → a new normal science. Across the revolutionary divide rival paradigms are **incommensurable**, and scientific progress is **evolution *from* primitive beginnings, not evolution *toward* a fixed truth set by nature in advance.**

**Full title / edition**: *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*, Thomas S. Kuhn (1922–1996), University of Chicago Press. First published 1962; 2nd ed. 1970 (adding the **Postscript—1969**); this is the **Fourth Edition (2012)** with an **Introductory Essay by Ian Hacking**. ~3004 lines in the ingested .txt. Thirteen short sections (Kuhn called them sections, not chapters) plus the Postscript. Hacking's essay (a model of compression and historical context, including the Kuhn↔Popper relation) is used heavily below alongside Kuhn's own text. (Hacking's own major work, *Representing and Intervening* (1983), later engages Kuhn's incommensurability and "different worlds" themes as drivers of irrealism about representation and pivots to experimental intervention as a more robust ground for entity realism — see new primary source [[Sources/Representing and Intervening - Ian Hacking (1983)]].)

## Summary

Kuhn argues — as a historian of science (he came to philosophy from physics) — that the textbook image of science as a continuous, cumulative march toward truth is a myth manufactured by the textbooks themselves. Actual science has a **structure**:

1. **Pre-paradigm / immature** stage: competing schools, no shared framework, fact-gathering near-random.
2. A **paradigm** emerges — a concrete achievement (Newton's *Principia*, Lavoisier's chemistry) "sufficiently unprecedented to attract an enduring group of adherents" and "open-ended" enough to leave problems to solve.
3. **Normal science**: the community does **puzzle-solving** within the paradigm, articulating and extending it. "How little [research problems] aim to produce major novelties." Normal science is *not* a test of the paradigm; the paradigm is presupposed (the scientist is like a chess player trying moves *within* the rules, not testing the rules).
4. **Anomalies** accumulate — results the paradigm cannot assimilate. They are not instant refutations; an anomaly must be *recognized* as significant, which is itself a complex historical event.
5. **Crisis**: persistent, intractable anomalies provoke "extraordinary research" — proliferation of competing versions, recourse to philosophy, debate over fundamentals.
6. **Revolution**: a new candidate paradigm resolves the crisis. "To reject one paradigm without simultaneously substituting another is to reject science itself." The transfer of allegiance is a **gestalt switch / conversion**, made "all at once (though not necessarily in an instant)," not forced step-by-step by logic and neutral experience.
7. A new **normal science** begins; the cycle repeats. History is then rewritten by textbooks to look cumulative (**the invisibility of revolutions**).

Two further theses are the most contested. **Incommensurability**: across a revolution the very meanings of terms (mass, space, earth, element) change; rival paradigms partly "talk through each other," disagree on which problems matter and on standards, and in a sense "practice their trades in different worlds." Choice between paradigms "cannot be made a step at a time, forced by logic"; it rests partly on faith, future promise, and even aesthetic judgment. **Progress without a goal**: science is a "process of evolution *from* primitive beginnings," driven (like Darwinian natural selection) by competition within the community for the fittest practice — but "*toward no goal*," with no "permanent fixed scientific truth, of which each stage in the development of scientific knowledge is a better exemplar."

The **Postscript—1969** answers critics: it splits "paradigm" into the broad **disciplinary matrix** (symbolic generalizations, models, values, **exemplars**) and the narrow **exemplar** (shared concrete problem-solutions — "the most novel and least understood aspect of this book"); ties paradigms to the **scientific community** (sociology); softens (and partly recants) the pre-paradigm idea; and denies that incommensurability means irrationality or that he is a relativist.

## Key Concepts and Structure

- **§§II–V — Normal science, puzzle-solving, paradigm**: paradigm as a shared achievement constituting a community; normal science as puzzle-solving (three problem types: determining significant facts, matching fact to theory, articulating theory); the **priority of paradigms** (scientists agree on exemplars even when they cannot agree on explicit rules). → [[Concepts/Paradigm (Kuhn)]], [[Concepts/Normal Science and Puzzle-Solving (Kuhn)]]
- **§§VI–VIII — Anomaly, crisis, response**: discovery comes from "something going awry"; anomaly recognized only against paradigm expectations; crisis as the loosening that permits novelty. → [[Concepts/Anomaly and Crisis (Kuhn)]]
- **§§IX–XII — Revolutions, world-change, invisibility, resolution**: necessity of revolutions for novelty; "scientists work in a different world" after a revolution (Kuhn's cautious "try-out" mode); gestalt switch; resolution by persuasion/conversion, not proof. The explicit engagement with **Popper** is here (§XII). → [[Concepts/Scientific Revolutions and Paradigm Shifts (Kuhn)]], [[Concepts/Incommensurability (Kuhn)]]
- **§XIII — Progress through revolutions**: cumulative *within* normal science; non-cumulative *across* revolutions; **evolution-from not evolution-toward**; "Somewhere in this maze… must lie the problem of induction."
- **Postscript—1969**: disciplinary matrix vs. exemplar; scientific community; values of theory-choice (accuracy, consistency, scope, simplicity, fruitfulness — shared even as their application is judgment, not algorithm); reply to charges of irrationalism/relativism.

## Contributions and Integration with Existing Wiki Content

This is the **decisive counterweight to Popper** in the vault's Philosophy of Science leg and the primary source for the historical/sociological turn in philosophy of science.

**The central tension — Popper vs. Kuhn** (given its own page): Kuhn engages Popper by name (§XII). He agrees Popper's *anomaly/falsifying instance* matters, but argues (a) there is "**no such thing**" as a bare falsifying experience — every theory always faces unsolved puzzles and imperfect fits, so "if any and every failure to fit were ground for theory rejection, all theories ought to be rejected at all times"; (b) scientists do **not** abandon a paradigm merely because of anomalies — "**the decision to reject one paradigm is always simultaneously the decision to accept another**"; and (c) "falsification" really names the *later* triumph of a new paradigm (a joint verification–falsification, two-stage process). Where Popper makes *permanent critical revolution* the norm and dogmatism a vice, Kuhn makes **uncritical normal science** the norm — and the dogged "assurance that the older paradigm will ultimately solve all its problems" a *functional necessity* (it is what lets the community press the paradigm hard enough to expose the anomaly that breaks it). And against Popper's **verisimilitude** (science approaching truth), Kuhn denies any goal "set by nature in advance." → [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **Popper vs. Kuhn** — the major tension, given a dedicated [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]] page.
- **Relativism / rationality charge**: Does incommensurability + conversion-not-proof make theory-choice irrational? Kuhn says no (shared values: accuracy, consistency, scope, simplicity, fruitfulness), but the charge dogged him; Feyerabend's "anything goes" pushed it further. Lakatos's "research programmes" tried to mediate Popper and Kuhn. (Feyerabend, Lakatos not yet in the vault.)
- **"Different worlds" / world-change**: literal or metaphorical? Hacking flags Kuhn's deliberately hedged "try-out mode."
- **The word "paradigm"**: Kuhn admitted he "lost control" of it (Masterman found 21 uses); the Postscript's disciplinary-matrix/exemplar split is the repair.
- **Scope**: Kuhn (like Popper) took *physics* as the prototype; fit to biology (Darwin's own revolution is never discussed in *Structure*), the experimental/instrumental traditions ([[Sources/Representing and Intervening - Ian Hacking (1983)]] / Galison), and the social sciences is contested. Hacking later used Kuhn's representation troubles as motivation to ground realism in experimental intervention rather than theory-comparison.
