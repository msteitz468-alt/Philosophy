# Meditations — Marcus Aurelius

> The private philosophical journal (*Ta eis heauton*, "To Himself") of the Roman emperor Marcus Aurelius (AD 121–180), written in Greek while on campaign against Germanic tribes on the Danube. Twelve books of short, repetitive reflections on governing one's ruling faculty (the daemon or inner citadel), living in accordance with nature and providence, the social/co-operative nature of the rational being, contempt for fame, the body, and externals, and the practice of doing every act as if it were the last. The primary source for Marcus' own voice and daily self-correction — not a systematic treatise but the living record of an emperor attempting to remain a philosopher under the extreme pressures of power, war, and mortality.

**Edition**: George Long translation (first published 1862). Included in the "Stoic Six Pack" anthology compilation (Enhanced Media edition, along with Epictetus' Golden Sayings, Fragments, Discourses, Enchiridion; Seneca's Letters; and reference to the John Lord biography). The raw file is the complete anthology; the Meditations occupy the central portion (Books 1–12).

## The Text and Its Character

- Not published by Marcus; discovered and circulated after his death.
- Composed in the field ("Among the Quadi at the Granua" at the end of Book 1; "This in Carnuntum" at end of Book 2).
- Book 1 is unique: a catalogue of gratitude and what Marcus learned from each important person in his life (grandfather, father, mother, Rusticus who gave him Epictetus, Apollonius, Sextus, etc.), ending in thanks to the gods for the circumstances that allowed him to live according to nature.
- Books 2–12: recurring, aphoristic notes on the same handful of themes — the nature of the whole and one's part in it; the ruling faculty; acceptance; impermanence; justice and social duty; freedom from opinion and desire for fame.

The form itself is the practice: brief, repeated reminders written to steady the mind amid the "smoke" of camp life, court, and war.

## Key Themes and Claims from the Meditations

**The Ruling Faculty (Daemon / Inner Citadel) and the Little Soul**
- "Whatever this is that I am, it is a little flesh and breath, and the ruling part."
- "Thou art a little soul bearing about a corpse, as Epictetus used to say."
- "It is sufficient to attend to the daemon within him, and to reverence it sincerely. And reverence of the daemon consists in keeping it pure from passion and thoughtlessness, and dissatisfaction with what comes from gods and men."
- "The ruling principle is that which rouses and turns itself... it also makes everything which happens appear to itself to be such as it wills."
- Philosophy's sole task: "keeping the daemon within a man free from violence and unharmed, superior to pains and pleasures, doing nothing without purpose, nor yet falsely and with hypocrisy... accepting all that happens... waiting for death with a cheerful mind."

**Morning Reflection and the Nature of the Good**
- Book 2 opening: "BEGIN the morning by saying to thyself, I shall meet with the busy-body, the ungrateful, arrogant, deceitful, envious, unsocial. All these things happen to them by reason of their ignorance of what is good and evil. But I who have seen the nature of the good that it is beautiful, and of the bad that it is ugly, and the nature of him who does wrong, that it is akin to me, not only of the same blood or seed, but that it participates in the same intelligence and the same portion of the divinity, I can neither be injured by any of them... For we are made for co-operation, like feet, like hands, like eyelids, like the rows of the upper and lower teeth. To act against one another then is contrary to nature..."

**Every Act as if the Last; Limit of Time**
- "Since it is possible that thou mayest depart from life this very moment, regulate every act and thought accordingly."
- "Every moment think steadily as a Roman and a man to do what thou hast in hand with perfect and simple dignity, and feeling of affection, and freedom, and justice; and to give thyself relief from all other thoughts. And thou wilt give thyself relief, if thou doest every act of thy life as if it were the last..."
- "Remember how long thou hast been putting off these things, and how often thou hast received an opportunity from the gods, and yet dost not use it. Thou must now at last perceive of what universe thou art a part, and of what administrator of the universe thy existence is an efflux, and that a limit of time is fixed for thee..."

**The Whole, Providence, and One's Part**
- "This thou must always bear in mind, what is the nature of the whole, and what is my nature, and how this is related to that, and what kind of a part it is of what kind of a whole..."
- "All that is from the gods is full of Providence. That which is from fortune is not separated from nature or without an interweaving and involution with the things which are ordered by Providence."
- "The intelligence of the universe is social. Accordingly it has made the inferior things for the sake of the superior, and it has fitted the superior to one another."
- "Live with the gods. And he does live with the gods who constantly shows to them, his own soul is satisfied with that which is assigned to him, and that it does all that the daemon wishes, which Zeus hath given to every man for his guardian and guide, a portion of himself."

**Transformation, Opinion, and Contempt for Externals**
- "The universe is transformation: life is opinion."
- "All existing things soon change, and they will either be reduced to vapour... or they will be dispersed."
- "How quickly all things disappear... the whole earth is a point... retire into this little territory of thy own... look at things as a man, as a human being, as a citizen, as a mortal."
- "Nothing is more wretched than a man who traverses everything in a round... without perceiving that it is sufficient to attend to the daemon within him..."

**Social Duty and Not Becoming Like the Wrongdoer**
- "The best way of avenging thyself is not to become like the wrong doer."
- "Judge every word and deed which are according to nature to be fit for thee; and be not diverted by the blame which follows from any people... but go straight on, following thy own nature and the common nature; and the way of both is one."
- "How hast thou behaved hitherto to the gods, thy parents, brethren, children, teachers... Consider if thou hast hitherto behaved to all in such a way that this may be said of thee: Never has wronged a man in deed or word."

**Death and Cheerful Acceptance**
- Death is "an operation of nature"; "if a man looks at it in itself... he will then consider it to be nothing else than an operation of nature; and if any one is afraid of an operation of nature, he is a child."
- "Waiting for death with a cheerful mind, as being nothing else than a dissolution of the elements..."
- "Though thou shouldst be going to live three thousand years... still remember that no man loses any other life than this which he now lives... the present is the only thing of which a man can be deprived."

## What This Source Adds (Beyond the Biography and Other Stoics)

The Lord biography supplies the external historical framing (philosopher-king, pedagogical lineage via Rusticus to Epictetus, "best of the pagan world," consistency of private and public life, the tragedy of Commodus). This source supplies **Marcus' own interior voice** — the actual daily work of maintaining philosophical discipline while bearing the empire.

It enacts the Epictetan and Senecan teachings in the most demanding circumstances (not as teacher or advisor, but as the man who must decide wars, law cases, succession, and taxes while writing these notes in camp). The gratitude of Book 1, the explicit "ruling part" language, the strong cosmic/social framing ("we are made for co-operation"), and the "little territory of thy own" as refuge are distinctive.

It provides the primary textual grounding for applying Stoicism to leadership, power, and the "examined life under extreme responsibility."

## Agreements, Nuances, and Contrasts with Prior Sources

- **Epictetus**: Direct continuity. Marcus credits Rusticus with introducing him to the *Discourses*. Uses the same division, impression test, role, and the Cleanthes hymn. Explicitly quotes "Thou art a little soul bearing about a corpse, as Epictetus used to say." The emperor's version adds the burden of supreme power and the need for the daemon to remain undisturbed amid "sharp pains, on the occasion of the loss of a child, and in long illness."
- **Seneca**: Shared emphasis on daily practice, death-rehearsal, philosophy as medicine, and cheerful acceptance. Marcus' journal is the private counterpart to Seneca's public letters to Lucilius. Both treat virtue as sufficient; Marcus lives it while commanding armies and ruling the known world.
- **Plato (Republic)**: The attempted historical fulfillment of the philosopher-king ideal. The bio emphasizes the cost and the near-tragedy; the *Meditations* show the interior cost and the resources (the daemon, the view of the whole) used to bear it.
- **Augustine / Christian contrast** (via the bio): Marcus represents the highest reach of pagan ethics — duty, justice, simplicity, and cosmic piety grounded in reason alone, without grace, resurrection, or the City of God. "The best of the pagan world" (Lord) is precisely Augustine's category for the earthly city at its finest.
- **Thucydides / power**: Marcus demonstrates that philosophical virtue in power is possible but does not guarantee historical continuity or succession (Commodus immediately undoes much of it). Virtue is not enough to control fortune or the next generation.
- **Vault meta**: The *Meditations* are the ancient exemplar of the personal use case of this wiki — private, repeated self-examination and bookkeeping of principles amid the "smoke" of daily life and work. The journal as persistent artifact of the ruling faculty.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The bio's Christian framing ("best of the pagan world" as tragic limitation) sits alongside the primary text's own confident, non-tragic piety and cheerfulness toward death and duty. The primary text does not present itself as lacking; it presents itself as sufficient for a good human life.
- Marcus' handling of Christians (local persecutions occurred without imperial protection) is noted in the bio as a failure of philosophical imagination. The text itself does not address the issue directly.
- Succession: Marcus' choice of Commodus is the one great practical failure acknowledged even by admirers. Philosophy strengthened the man but did not solve the problem of transferring character across generations.
