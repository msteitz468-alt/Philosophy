# Sophist - Plato

> **The Sophist** (c. 360 BCE). By Plato. Translated by Benjamin Jowett. A late, highly technical dialogue featuring the **Eleatic Stranger** and Theaetetus (with Socrates listening silently). The primary goal is to define what a **sophist** is and distinguish him from the philosopher and statesman. To do this, the dialogue introduces the **Method of Division** (*diairesis*) and grapples with the Parmenidean problem of **Non-Being**. In a famous philosophical "parricide," the Eleatic Stranger refutes his master Parmenides by proving that Non-Being must exist in a certain sense to explain falsehood, deception, and negative statements, culminating in the doctrine of the **Five Greatest Kinds** (*Megista Gene*).

---

## The Method of Division and Defining the Sophist

The dialogue demonstrates the **Method of Division**—the logical process of defining a concept by taking a broad genus and repeatedly dividing it into two sub-species until the specific target is isolated:
- The interlocutors use the angler (fisherman) as a simple warm-up exercise.
- They then apply it to the sophist, generating multiple definitions (e.g., a hunter of wealthy youth, a wholesaler of knowledge, an athlete in debate).
- The final definition is isolated: the sophist is a **creator of false images (semblances)**, who operates in the realm of appearance rather than reality.

---

## The Problem of Non-Being and the Eleatic Parricide

Defining the sophist as an "image-maker who speaks falsehoods" creates a major logical trap inherited from Parmenides:
- **Parmenides's Axiom**: Non-Being (what is not) is impossible and cannot be thought or spoken of.
- **The Sophist's Escape**: If Non-Being cannot exist, then **falsehood and deception are impossible**. A false statement is speaking of "what is not." If we speak of what is not, we are speaking of nothing, which is not speaking at all.
- **The Parricide**: To trap the sophist, the Eleatic Stranger is forced to commit "philosophical parricide" against his teacher Parmenides. He proves that **Non-Being exists** not as the absolute opposite of Being (nothingness), but as **Difference** (*Otherness*). When we say "not-large," we are not stating the existence of nothing; we are simply stating the existence of something *other* than large.

---

## The Five Greatest Kinds (Megista Gene)

To explain how Forms can combine, participate in, or exclude each other, the Eleatic Stranger identifies the five most fundamental and universal Forms:
1. **Being** (*On*)
2. **Motion** (*Kinesis*)
3. **Rest** (*Stasis*)
4. **Sameness** (*Tauton*)
5. **Otherness / Difference** (*Thateron*)

- **The Logic of Combination**: Motion and Rest cannot blend with each other, but both participate in Being (since both exist). Both are the *same* as themselves, and *other* than each other.
- **Otherness as Non-Being**: Otherness is distributed throughout all things: every single Form is itself (Sameness), but is *not* (Otherness) any of the other infinite Forms. Thus, "Non-Being" is simply the Form of **Otherness/Difference** in action, allowing us to make negative, meaningful, and false statements.

---

## Integration and Vault Value

- ** epistemological shift**: Marks Plato's transition from the early Socratic elenchus and intuitive myths to a formal, logical, and structural analysis of language and metaphysics.
- **Direct Answer to Parmenides**: Directly addresses [[Thinkers/Parmenides]] and [[Concepts/Parmenidean Being and the One (Parmenides)]], refuting Parmenides's monism by introducing Difference as a valid metaphysical category.
- **Anticipation of Aristotle**: Paves the way for Aristotle's Categories and his logic of definition by genus and difference.
