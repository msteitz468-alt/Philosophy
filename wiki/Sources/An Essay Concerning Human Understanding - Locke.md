# An Essay Concerning Human Understanding - Locke

> The mind at birth is an empty cabinet or white paper, void of all characters, without any innate principles or ideas. All our knowledge and ideas are derived from experience — from sensation (external objects) and reflection (operations of the mind itself). The foundation of modern empiricism and a direct precursor to Hume.

**This source summary covers the front matter, dedicatory epistle, epistle to the reader, introduction, and Book I of the Essay (the refutation of innate principles and ideas). Subsequent Books (II: Of Ideas; III: Of Words; IV: Of Knowledge and Probability) will be ingested in later chunks from the same raw file.**

## Edition and Provenance

From *The Complete Works of John Locke* (Delphi Classics, 2017). The text used is the Second Edition (1694, with later corrections noted), the one Locke himself revised and expanded. The volume is a large collection containing the *Essay* plus *A Letter Concerning Toleration* and sequels, *Two Treatises of Government*, *Some Thoughts Concerning Education*, *The Reasonableness of Christianity* and vindications, a paraphrase on St. Paul's epistles, *Some Thoughts on the Conduct of the Understanding*, miscellaneous works, letters, poems, and biographies of Locke.

The *Essay* first appeared in 1689/1690. Locke describes its origin in the Epistle to the Reader: a discussion among friends on a "subject very remote from this" reached an impasse; Locke proposed first examining "what OBJECTS our understandings were, or were not, fitted to deal with." The work grew from hasty notes through long intervals of neglect into the published form. It was "written by incoherent parcels."

## Design and Method (from the Introduction and Epistle)

Locke's explicit purpose: "to inquire into the original, certainty, and extent of HUMAN KNOWLEDGE, together with the grounds and degrees of BELIEF, OPINION, and ASSENT."

He adopts an "historical, plain method" — not a physical investigation of the mind's essence or the bodily causes of sensation, but a consideration of the discerning faculties as employed about their objects. He sets aside questions of whether ideas depend on matter.

**Three-part method**:
1. Original of our ideas (how the understanding comes to be furnished with them).
2. Knowledge the understanding has by those ideas; its certainty, evidence, and extent.
3. Nature and grounds of faith or opinion (assent to propositions of whose truth we have no certain knowledge); reasons and degrees of assent.

**Utility**: Knowing the powers and limits of the understanding prevents us from meddling with things exceeding our comprehension, raises fewer disputes about matters beyond our capacities, and cures both idle scepticism ("we can know nothing") and overreaching dogmatism. Our capacities are proportioned to our state and concerns ("The Candle that is set up in us shines bright enough for all our purposes").

He begs pardon for frequent use of "IDEA" — the term that "serves best to stand for whatsoever is the OBJECT of the understanding when a man thinks" (whatever is meant by phantasm, notion, species, or whatever the mind can be employed about in thinking). First inquiry: how ideas come into the mind.

## Book I: Neither Principles Nor Ideas Are Innate

The entire first Book is a sustained argument against the "established opinion amongst some men, that there are in the understanding certain INNATE PRINCIPLES; some primary notions, KOIVAI EVVOIAI, characters, as it were stamped upon the mind of man; which the soul receives in its very first being, and brings into the world with it."

### Chapter I: No Innate Speculative Principles

- **Universal assent is neither necessary nor sufficient**: Even if certain truths were universally assented to, that would not prove them innate (there could be another way to the agreement). In fact, there are no such universally assented speculative principles.
- The classic candidates — "Whatsoever is, is" and "It is impossible for the same thing to be and not to be" — are not known or assented to by all mankind (children and idiots have no apprehension of them; many adults and "savages" live long without them).
- Imprinting without perception is unintelligible: "To say a notion is imprinted on the mind, and yet at the same time to say, that the mind is ignorant of it, and never yet took notice of it, is to make this impression nothing."
- The common evasion "men know and assent to them *when they come to the use of reason*" fails on multiple grounds: (a) it concedes they are not known before reason, undermining "imprinted from the beginning"; (b) reason is the faculty of deducing unknown truths from known ones — if these maxims require reason to be discovered, they are not the "original" inscriptions they are claimed to be; (c) the use of reason does not suddenly reveal them as previously imprinted; general abstract ideas (the materials of these maxims) are themselves acquired later through experience and abstraction.
- Ready assent upon first hearing and understanding the terms is not unique to supposed innate maxims. Mathematical truths ("one and two are equal to three"), "white is not black," "a square is not a circle," and countless others are assented to immediately once terms are understood. By the same logic almost every proposition we can clearly think would be "innate."
- Recapitulation of Ch. I: These "first principles" are not universally assented to; the assent they receive is produced another way (by the same steps as other acquired truths); therefore no other speculative maxims have a better claim to innateness.

### Chapter II: No Innate Practical Principles

Moral/practical principles are even less plausibly innate than speculative ones.

- No moral rule has as general and ready an assent as the speculative maxims (or even as "the whole is bigger than the part").
- "Justice and keeping of contracts" is widely practiced within groups (even thieves and outlaws keep faith among themselves), but as a rule of convenience or interest within the group, not as an innate law of nature recognized as binding toward outsiders. Highwaymen who are just among comrades still plunder honest men.
- Actions are the best interpreters of thoughts. Professions of veneration for moral rules are common; consistent practice is rarer. Self-interest and the conveniences of life lead men to pay outward respect to rules whose violation would harm themselves, without inward full persuasion or treating them as inviolable.
- **Conscience is no proof**: Conscience is "nothing else but our own opinion or judgment of the moral rectitude or gravity of our own actions." It is shaped by education, company, and customs of country. The same "bent of conscience" can lead some to prosecute what others avoid. Contraries cannot both be innate.
- Historical and ethnographic examples of enormities practiced with serenity and without remorse or scruple: armies sacking towns (robbery, murder, rape as sport); exposure or killing of infants/children in various cultures; killing or eating parents at certain ages; cannibalism of captives or even own children; burying the sick alive; societies without any name for God or religion. Even among professing Christians (e.g., Mingrelians).
- Moral rules require proof and reasoning; they are not self-evident like "what is, is." One can always justly demand a reason for a moral rule (e.g., the Golden Rule "do as you would be done unto"). Different people ground the same rule differently (God's command + rewards/punishments for the Christian; public good/Leviathan for the Hobbist; honesty and dignity of human nature for the ancient philosopher). This variety of grounds and of opinions about what is virtuous shows the rules are not directly imprinted.
- "Virtue generally approved not because innate, but because profitable": God has connected virtue and public happiness; observance is visibly beneficial to society and to those who deal with the virtuous man. Men therefore recommend the rules (from interest as well as conviction), but this outward acknowledgment does not prove inward innate persuasion.

### Chapter III: Other Considerations Concerning Innate Principles, Both Speculative and Practical

- **Ideas must be innate if the propositions composed of them are to be innate**: "If the IDEAS which made up those truths were not [innate], it was impossible that the PROPOSITIONS made up of them should be innate."
- Children bring very few (if any) settled ideas into the world (faint sensations of hunger, thirst, warmth, pain in the womb at most). Ideas come in by degrees through experience and observation of things that come in their way.
- The component ideas of the supposedly innate maxims are themselves not early or innate: "impossibility" and "identity" (a child does not have a clear idea of identity before white/black or sweet/bitter; questions of bodily change, transmigration of souls (Pythagoras/Euphorbus/the cock), or resurrection show the idea of "same man" is far from obvious or universally clear). "Whole" and "part" are relative ideas founded on extension and number (which must themselves be acquired).
- The idea of God (and of worship) is not innate: history and exploration record whole nations with no notion or name for God (examples from Soldania, Brazil, Caribbee islands, Paraguay, etc.). Even in more civilized countries, impressions of a Deity are often weak or absent; complaints of atheism from the pulpit are not without reason. The name and some imperfect notion spread by education, language, and the use of reason reflecting on the visible marks of wisdom and power in creation — not by original imprint. If the idea were innate it would be uniform, clear, early, and irresistible; instead it is variable, often obscure, and late.
- If innate principles existed, they would be the most known, clearest, earliest, and least disputed of all our thoughts. The actual variety, obscurity, late acquisition, and cultural/historical disagreement refute the claim.
- Recapitulation of Book I: No practical principles enjoy universal agreement; therefore none are innate. The component ideas of the speculative maxims are not innate. The method by which most men arrive at firm assurance of their principles (education, custom, borrowed authority, long familiarity turning absurdities into "divine" truths) explains the diversity of what different groups treat as self-evident. Universal consent (the only mark offered) is insufficient. Principles must be examined; the demand for marks to distinguish genuine innate ones from pretenders is reasonable and unmet.

## Key Insights from This Portion

- Locke's "historical, plain method" is itself a model of careful, non-speculative epistemology: begin with what is given in experience and trace the actual steps by which the mind is furnished.
- The attack on innatism is not a denial of all early or universal knowledge; it is a denial that such knowledge arrives as pre-stamped characters independent of sensation and reflection. Children know the difference between sweet and bitter long before they know general maxims — because the particular ideas are acquired first.
- The argument is cross-cultural, developmental (children, "idiots," "savages"), and phenomenological (what is actually present to the mind at different stages). It anticipates modern cognitive science and learning theory.
- Strong implications for education (the *Thoughts Concerning Education* in the same volume) and for any "blank slate" or experience-driven model of mind or artificial agents.

## Book II: Of Ideas (Chunk 2 — Ideas in General, Simple Ideas, and Primary/Secondary Qualities)

**This chunk covers the positive account of the origin of ideas (Ch. I), the nature of simple ideas (Ch. II), and the crucial distinction between ideas in the mind and qualities in bodies, with primary vs. secondary qualities (relevant sections of Ch. VIII).**

### All Ideas from Sensation or Reflection (Ch. I)
The mind is supposed "white paper, void of all characters, without any ideas." It is furnished from EXPERIENCE alone — the two fountains:
- **SENSATION**: Senses convey perceptions from external sensible objects → ideas of sensible qualities (yellow, white, heat, cold, soft, hard, bitter, sweet, etc.). "This great source of most of the ideas we have, depending wholly upon our senses... I call SENSATION."
- **REFLECTION**: Perception of the mind's own internal operations (perception, thinking, doubting, believing, reasoning, knowing, willing, and passions like satisfaction/uneasiness) → "internal sense." "I call this REFLECTION."

"These two, I say, viz. external material things, as the objects of SENSATION, and the operations of our own minds within, as the objects of REFLECTION, are to me the only originals from whence all our ideas take their beginnings."

Evidence: children acquire ideas by degrees as senses furnish them; reflection requires attention and comes later ("pretty late before most children get ideas of the operations of their own minds"). Against "soul always thinks" independent of body/sensation: no evidence from experience or consciousness; leads to absurdities (two persons in one man; soul thinking without the man knowing). Ideas are coeval with sensation/perception. The foetus/infant passes much time without perception/thought until sensation provides matter. Understanding is passive in receiving these initial simple materials ("whether or no it will have these beginnings... is not in its own power").

### Simple Ideas (Ch. II)
Ideas are either SIMPLE or COMPLEX. Simple ideas are "uncompounded appearances": each "contains in it nothing but ONE UNIFORM APPEARANCE, OR CONCEPTION IN THE MIND, and is not distinguishable into different ideas" (e.g., coldness/hardness in ice as distinct as smell/whiteness of lily).

- Enter the mind "simple; and unmixed."
- Furnished only by the two fountains above.
- Mind is passive: "the objects of our senses do, many of them, obtrude their particular ideas upon our minds whether we will or not."
- Mind "can neither make nor destroy them": cannot invent or frame one new simple idea not received via sensation/reflection; cannot destroy those present. Power only to "repeat, compare, and unite them" into complex (see future concepts).
- Only qualities affecting the (five) senses are imaginable as simple ideas (speculation on other possible senses/creatures).

### Ideas in the Mind vs. Qualities in Bodies; Primary and Secondary (Ch. VIII)
To discourse intelligibly: distinguish "AS THEY ARE IDEAS OR PERCEPTIONS IN OUR MINDS" from "AS THEY ARE MODIFICATIONS OF MATTER IN THE BODIES THAT CAUSE SUCH PERCEPTIONS IN US."

- IDEA: whatever the mind perceives in itself (immediate object of perception/thought/understanding).
- QUALITY: the power in a body to produce an idea in our mind.

**Primary (original/real) qualities**: Solidity, extension, figure, number, motion or rest (or situation). "wholly inseparable from it"; persist through all alterations/division; "sense constantly finds in every particle"; mind finds inseparable even in insensible particles. Ideas of them are resemblances: "the ideas of primary qualities of bodies are resemblances of them, and their patterns do really exist in the bodies themselves." (E.g., circle/square in idea = in body; motion represents real motion.)

**Secondary (sensible/imputed) qualities**: Powers (arising from primary qualities/insensible particles' bulk, figure, texture, motion) to produce in us ideas of colors, sounds, smells, tastes, etc. "in truth nothing in the objects themselves, but powers to produce various sensations in us; and depend on those primary qualities." "There is nothing like our ideas, existing in the bodies themselves." They "vanish and cease" without a perceiver (reduced to primary causes).

Examples: Same fire produces warmth (one distance) or pain (nearer) — warmth not "actually in the fire" more than pain is. Porphyry has no color in dark (only particle configuration apt to produce with light). Almond pounded: "color" and "taste" change with texture (affects powers). Same water hot to one hand, cold to other (different nerve motion). Snow/ fire/ manna denominated from ideas they produce.

Three sorts of qualities: (1) primary (in bodies whether perceived); (2) powers to produce sensible ideas in us (secondary); (3) powers to alter other bodies' primary qualities (e.g., sun on wax, fire on lead). Secondary depend on primary.

## Updated Key Insights (Incorporating This Chunk)

- Locke's "historical, plain method" traces every idea back to its experiential origin in the two fountains, providing a rigorous empiricist alternative to innatist or purely rationalist foundations.
- The attack on innatism (Book I) + positive account (Book II) together give the full "blank slate" picture: mind starts empty; simple ideas (uniform appearances) are the passive materials from sensation/reflection; mind actively compounds them.
- Primary/secondary distinction explains both the objectivity of bodies (primary resemblant) and the subjectivity/variability of much sensory experience (secondary as powers/effects), while grounding everything in experience. Strong implications for philosophy of science, perception, and color/quality realism.

## Book II Continued: Complex Ideas, Substances, Relations, Identity, Association (final Book II chunk)

**Complex Ideas (Ch. XII)**: Mind actively makes complex ideas by repeating, comparing, uniting simple ones (voluntarily, beyond what sensation/reflection directly gave). Examples: beauty, gratitude, man, army, universe. Reduced to three heads: 1. MODES (dependences/affections of substances; simple modes variations of one simple idea like dozen/score; mixed modes of several kinds like beauty, theft). 2. SUBSTANCES (combinations representing particular things subsisting by themselves; idea of substance + simple ideas, e.g., lead or man; single or collective like army/flock). 3. RELATIONS (comparing one idea with another).

Even abstruse ideas (space, time, infinity) derived from repeating/joining simple from the two fountains.

**Space, Extension, etc. (Ch. XIII+ simple modes of space)**: Space from sight/touch (distance, capacity, extension as affection of matter). Immensity (power to repeat distance without end). Figure (relation of parts of termination). Place (relative position to fixed points). Extension not the same as body/solidity (space can be without solid matter; parts of space inseparable/immovable).

**Identity and Diversity (Ch. XXVII)**: Identity when ideas attributed vary not from what they were at a determined time/place of existence. One thing cannot have two beginnings; two things one beginning. For substances: God (eternal, everywhere); finite spirits (determined time/place of beginning); bodies/particles (same if no addition/subtraction; division creates distinct). For modes/relations (actions like motion/thought perish instantly, so diversity easy). Principium individuationis = existence itself (determines to particular time/place).

For living things (vegetables, animals): not same mass of particles but continued organization/life (oak from plant still same oak; colt to horse same horse). "An animal is a living organized body; and consequently the same animal... is the same continued LIFE communicated to different particles of matter, as they happen successively to be united to that organized living body."

For man: same continued life in organized body (shape important; not just soul — embryo to adult same man; prince soul in cobbler body would be same person but not same man to others).

**Personal Identity**: "a thinking intelligent being, that has reason and reflection, and can consider itself as itself, the same thinking thing, in different times and places" — by consciousness (inseparable from thinking; "it is impossible for any one to perceive without PERCEIVING that he does perceive"). Consciousness makes personal identity; extends as far as it reaches back to past actions/thoughts. Different substances can be the same person if same consciousness unites them (like different bodies same animal by life). Body goes to making the man in ordinary language. Prince/cobbler thought experiment: same person (accountable for prince's actions) but not same man. For resurrection: same consciousness with (possibly different) body makes same person.

**Association of Ideas (Ch. XXXIII)**: Besides natural correspondence, custom or chance creates "wrong connexion" of ideas not allied by nature (they "always keep in company"; "the whole gang, always inseparable"). Leads to unreasonable opinions, obstinacy, antipathies, errors in moral/natural actions, "a degree of madness" in most men. Examples: goblins with darkness (from maid's inculcation); injury with the man (aversion transfers); pain/sickness with place. Education should watch to prevent undue connexions in children (most susceptible). "This wrong connexion in our minds of ideas in themselves loose and independent of one another, has such an influence... that perhaps there is not any one thing that deserves more to be looked after."

## Book III: Of Words (Language)

**Words/Language in general (Ch. I)**: Man sociable; fitted for articulated sounds (words). Use as signs of internal ideas (to communicate thoughts; voluntary imposition, not natural connexion — else one language). General signs (to avoid multiplication of names for particulars; stand for general ideas). Negative/privative words (signify absence). Ultimately derived from sensible ideas (even abstract words like imagine, spirit, angel transferred from sensation).

**Signification of words (Ch. II)**: Words are sensible signs of the ideas in the mind of the speaker (primary/immediate signification). Secret references: (1) supposed to mark ideas in other men's minds (for communication; common acceptation); (2) to the reality of things (especially substances). By use, words readily excite ideas. Often used without clear signification (learned before ideas; parrots; set thoughts on words not things). Signification perfectly arbitrary (no natural connexion between sounds and ideas).

(Full Book III continues with names of simple ideas, mixed modes, substances (real essences, nominal essences, species/genera by abstract ideas), names of relations, abstract and concrete terms, particles, language imperfections/abuse of words (equivocation, etc.), remedies (definitions, etc.). Language as great instrument of society and knowledge; general terms for sorts; words stand for ideas in speaker's mind; importance of clear, determinate ideas for signification.)

## Book IV: Of Knowledge and Probability

**Knowledge in general (Ch. I)**: "Knowledge then seems to me to be nothing but THE PERCEPTION OF THE CONNEXION OF AND AGREEMENT, OR DISAGREEMENT AND REPUGNANCY OF ANY OF OUR IDEAS. In this alone it consists." Where this perception is, there is knowledge; else fancy/guess/believe but short of knowledge.

Four sorts of agreement/disagreement:
1. IDENTITY or DIVERSITY (first act of mind; white not black; what is, is).
2. RELATION (abstract relations between ideas, e.g., math: triangles equal).
3. CO-EXISTENCE or NECESSARY CONNEXION (in same subject, especially substances; e.g., gold is fixed — fixedness accompanies yellowness etc.).
4. REAL EXISTENCE (agreeing to any idea; God is; I exist).

**Degrees of knowledge (Ch. II+)**: 
- Intuitive (immediate, without intervention; highest certainty, irresistible light; e.g., white not black; a circle not a triangle; 1+2=3).
- Demonstrative (by intermediate ideas/ proofs; as in geometry; each step intuitive; certainty but less easy; requires memory of proofs).
- Sensitive (of particular existence of external objects, by sensation; lower degree; "an assurance that deserves the name of knowledge"; but limited — not as certain as intuitive/demonstrative; e.g., this fire exists now because it produces ideas in me).

Knowledge of existence: 
- Self: intuitive ( "I think, I reason, I feel pain" — certain as any demonstration).
- God: demonstrative (from own existence + idea of necessary being; cannot come from nothing; eternal, most powerful, etc.).
- Other things: sensitive (particular finite existences outside us, via senses; with practical assurance for life, though not the highest certainty; "I have not that certainty of them which we strictly call knowledge"; but sufficient).

**Other topics (Ch. III+)**: Extent of knowledge (limited to our ideas and their agreements; not to real essences of substances fully; trifling propositions; maxims; universal propositions). Truth (properly belongs to propositions; verbal/mental; real truth when agrees with things). Faith and reason (reason = natural revelation; faith = assent to proposition as from God on His authority; where reason ends, faith may begin for things above reason but not contrary; revelation must be judged by reason). Enthusiasm (immediate revelation without reason/evidence; "a strong and firm persuasion" not sufficient; "reason must be our last judge and guide"; enthusiasm often from "the conceits of a warmed or overweening brain"; dangerous as it sets up private opinion as rule). Wrong assent, error (from want of proofs, wrong measures, etc.). Division of the sciences (physica, practica, semeiotike — nature of things, actions for happiness, signs/words).

**Key closing**: Knowledge limited; probability for the rest (for conduct of life); "the candle that is set up in us shines bright enough for all our purposes."

Foundational for liberal democracy, property, consent of governed. (Ties to personal/career agency, education, Reasonableness.)

- **Some Thoughts Concerning Education (1693)**: Practical: health first (air, diet, clothing, exercise, sleep); habits over rules (early custom shapes mind more than precepts); virtue (wisdom, good breeding, courage, hardiness, justice); learning (Latin/Greek by use/conversation not rote; reading, writing, drawing, French, arithmetic, geometry, astronomy, geography, history, ethics, law, natural philosophy; travel for gentlemen). Vindications defend against charges of Socinianism etc. (Ties to toleration, education, anti-enthusiasm; contrasts with "school divinity.")
