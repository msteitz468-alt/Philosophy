# Phaedrus - Plato

> **The Phaedrus** (c. 370 BCE). By Plato. Translated by Benjamin Jowett. A rich, dialogic exploration of love (*Eros*), rhetoric, the soul's nature, and the limits of the written word. Set outside the city walls of Athens, Socrates and Phaedrus read and critique a speech by the orator Lysias on why a young man should yield to a non-lover rather than a lover. Socrates counters with two speeches: the first criticizing love, and the second (his famous recantation or *Palinode*) defending love as a form of **divine madness** that purifies the soul, introducing the **Chariot Allegory** to explain the soul's tripartite nature and its cosmic journey.

---

## Speeches on Love

1. **Speech of Lysias**: Lysias argues that the non-lover is rational, self-controlled, and reliable, whereas the lover is irrational, jealous, blind, and socially destructive. 
2. **Socrates' First Speech**: Socrates reluctantly agrees to Lysias's premise, defining love as an irrational desire for pleasure that overthrows reason, leading to the ruin of the beloved's intellect, body, and estate.
3. **Socrates' Second Speech (The Palinode)**: Socrates repents, fearing he has offended the god Eros. He argues that **love is a divine madness** (*mania*), which is a gift from the gods (like prophecy, mysteries, and poetry). 
   - Divine madness is the soul's recollection of the eternal truths (Beauty, Justice, Temperance) it saw before incarnating into a physical body.

## The Chariot Allegory and Metaphysics

Socrates describes the soul using the image of a **charioteer and two winged horses**:
- **The Charioteer**: Represents **Reason** (*Logos*), which must guide and control the horses.
- **The Noble White Horse**: Represents **Spirit** (*Thumos*)—honor, courage, and righteous ambition. It obeys the charioteer.
- **The Ignoble Black Horse**: Represents **Appetite** (*Epithumia*)—physical desires, lust, and greed. It is unruly and struggles against control.

### The Heavenly Journey and the Fall
- The souls of the gods fly smoothly in heaven, contemplating the Forms in the "plain of truth" beyond the sky.
- Human souls struggle because the black horse drags them down. If the charioteer cannot control the black horse, the wings are broken, and the soul falls to earth, taking on a physical body.
- Sighting physical beauty on earth triggers the recollection of the Form of Beauty, causing the soul's wings to begin growing back, which manifests as the intense, painful desire of love. See [[Concepts/Chariot Allegory and Divine Madness (Plato Phaedrus)]].

## Critique of Rhetoric and Writing

### Rhetoric for Truth
- Socrates critiques sophistic rhetoric (persuasion based on opinion or flattery). True rhetoric must be based on a philosophical knowledge of **truth** and an understanding of the **nature of the human soul**, adapting speech to different soul types.

### The Myth of Theuth and Thamus (Critique of Writing)
- Socrates tells a story of the Egyptian god **Theuth** (who invented writing) presenting his invention to King **Thamus**.
- Theuth claims writing will make the Egyptians wiser and improve their memory.
- Thamus refutes him, declaring that writing will do the opposite: it will **destroy memory** (leading to forgetfulness because people rely on external signs rather than internal recall) and create the **illusion of wisdom** (giving readers information without real understanding).
- **Living Speech vs. Dead Writing**: Writing is like a painting: it stands silent, and if you ask it a question, it always repeats the same thing. Writing cannot defend itself, nor does it know whom to speak to. True philosophy must be written in the **living soul** of the learner through dialogue.
