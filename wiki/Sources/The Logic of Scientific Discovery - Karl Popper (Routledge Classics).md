# The Logic of Scientific Discovery - Karl Popper (Routledge Classics)

> Karl Popper's 1934/1959 classic that proposes **falsifiability** (refutability by experience) as the criterion of demarcation between empirical science and metaphysics/pseudo-science, replaces inductive justification with a deductive methodology of bold conjectures subjected to severe attempted refutation, and solves Hume's problem of induction by rejecting the demand for "conclusive decidability."

**Full title / edition**: *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* (translation of *Logik der Forschung*, Vienna 1934/35 imprint). English edition prepared by the author with Julius and Lan Freed (1959, Hutchinson); Routledge Classics 2002 with new prefaces (1934, 1959), starred footnotes/appendices updating arguments, and references to the *Postscript to the Logic of Scientific Discovery*. ~7450 lines in the ingested .txt.

## Summary

The book is a sustained argument that the logic of scientific *discovery* is the logic of *testing*, not of generating or "justifying" theories from experience. There is no inductive logic that can establish universal laws or theories from singular observation statements. Instead, science proceeds by:

1. Advancing tentative conjectures/hypotheses (often bold, from problems + creative intuition).
2. Deducing testable consequences (especially risky predictions that conflict with background knowledge or rival theories).
3. Testing them against experience via "basic statements" (accepted singular reports).
4. If a prediction fails, the theory is falsified (tentatively); if it survives severe tests, it is *corroborated* ("has proved its mettle") for the time being — but never verified or made probable in the inductivist sense.

This asymmetry (falsification possible, verification impossible for universals) provides a workable **criterion of demarcation**: a system is empirical/scientific if and only if it is falsifiable — i.e., its logical form permits it to be contradicted by possible singular statements of experience. Theories that explain everything (no possible falsifiers) are non-scientific (metaphysical or pseudo-scientific), even if meaningful or heuristically fruitful.

Popper accepts Hume's negative result on induction but bypasses the problem by dropping the positivist requirement that scientific statements be "conclusively decidable" (both verifiable and falsifiable). The empirical method is defined by rules that *maximize exposure to falsification*.

The English edition's prefaces and * notes sharpen the polemic against linguistic philosophy / logical positivism and point forward to later developments (verisimilitude, critical rationalism, the *Postscript*).

## Key Claims and Structure

**Prefaces (1934, 1959, acknowledgments)**: Philosophy's task is contribution to cosmology / understanding the world (including our knowledge of it). All science is cosmology. Rejects "linguistic analysis" or "ordinary language" as the sole or characteristic method of philosophy (or as sufficient for the problems of knowledge growth). Defends the historical + critical analysis of scientific problems and discussions as central to epistemology. "There is no method peculiar to philosophy" — the method of rational discussion (state problem clearly, propose solutions, criticize severely) is common to science and philosophy. Central problem of epistemology: the *growth* of knowledge, best studied via growth of *scientific* knowledge.

**Part I – Introduction to the Logic of Science**

- **1. The Problem of Induction**: Rehearses Hume. Universal statements (laws, theories) cannot be logically derived from singular experiential statements. Any "principle of induction" leads to infinite regress or apriorism. Probabilistic versions fare no better (still require higher-order induction; universal laws receive zero probability on standard measures). "There is no such thing as induction."

- **2. Elimination of Psychologism**: Sharp distinction between *psychology of knowledge* (how ideas occur — creative, "irrational element," intuition; irrelevant to logic) and *logic of knowledge* (logical relations among statements: consistency, derivability, testability). No "logic of discovery" or "rational reconstruction" of the inspirational act. Einstein quote: no logical path to the highly universal laws; reached by intuition.

- **3. Deductive Testing of Theories**: The actual method: conjecture → deduce consequences (via other accepted statements) → compare (consistency, form, with other theories, with experience). Positive test result only temporary support ("proved its mettle" / corroborated); negative result falsifies. No inductive step from "verified predictions" to truth/probability of theory.

- **4. The Problem of Demarcation** ("Kant's problem"): The fundamental problem. Positivist/verifiability/inductivist criteria fail to separate science from metaphysics (they eliminate both, or let metaphysics in). A convention/proposal is needed. The purpose: draw a clear line while allowing theoretical systems of natural science.

- **5–6. Experience as a Method; Falsifiability as a Criterion of Demarcation**: Not verifiability (impossible for theories) but falsifiability. A system is scientific if it is possible to deduce singular statements ("basic statements") that contradict it. Example: "It will rain or not rain tomorrow" is non-empirical (unfalsifiable); "It will rain tomorrow" is empirical. The asymmetry of universal vs. singular statements makes this work via modus tollens. This also dissolves the Humean contradiction: experience decides (negatively) without requiring inductive justification.

- **7–8. The Problem of the Empirical Basis; Scientific Objectivity and Subjective Conviction**: "Basic statements" (singular fact-statements usable as falsifying premises) are not justified by perceptual experience or "protocol sentences" (that would reintroduce psychologism and privileged status for the "given"). They are inter-subjectively testable hypotheses, accepted by *decision* within the scientific community. Objectivity = inter-subjective testability. No ultimate statements in science; testability ad infinitum in principle. Feelings of conviction have no place in justification (only as objects of psychological study).

- Later chapters (9–10 + Part II): Theory of method as conventions/rules whose supreme rule is to ensure testability/falsifiability (no ad hoc protection of theories). Degree of corroboration (survival under severe tests, not probability). Critique of probability as confirmation measure. Simplicity, conventionalism, formalization of probability calculus, etc. Natural laws as "prohibitions."

**Starred additions (English ed.)**: Updates, replies to critics, pointers to *Postscript* (on truth, verisimilitude/"truthlikeness" as aim, corroboration vs. probability), and the 1963 *Conjectures and Refutations* (popular essays expanding the "conjectures and refutations" framing, demarcation lecture, etc.).

## Contributions and Integration with Existing Wiki Content

This source supplies the vault's primary, detailed treatment of 20th-century philosophy of science and the demarcation/growth-of-knowledge problematic. It is the natural capstone/extension of the Early Modern empiricist/rationalist cluster (Hume's negative result on induction is taken as given; Kant's demarcation problem is explicitly named and addressed; Russell's logical analysis and restatement of the "chicken" problem are engaged).

**Explicit agreements / extensions**:
- With [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]]: Accepts the full negative argument (uniformity principle neither demonstrable nor empirically justifiable without circularity). Replaces Hume's "custom" (psychological/naturalistic "sceptical solution") and Russell's a priori inductive principle with a *methodological* solution: science does not need a justifying principle of induction; it operates by deductive criticism and tentative acceptance of the unfalsified.
- With [[Thinkers/Epicurus]] (and ancient empiricism): Notes the "verificationist/falsificationist leanings" — multiple explanations allowed if consistent with sensation; reject only those contradicted. Popper supplies the rigorous logical and methodological development for modern science.
- With [[Thinkers/Kant]]: Demarcation is "Kant's problem" and central; but solution is critical rationalist/empirical (falsifiability + rules of method) rather than synthetic a priori securing of science.
- With [[Thinkers/Bertrand Russell]] (both *History* and *Problems*): Shares piecemeal, logical, anti-dogmatic spirit and the view that philosophy can contribute to (or at least clarify) the growth of knowledge. Popper offers a non-inductivist way out of the Humean/Russellian chicken problem that Russell left partly open (a priori principle). Russell's "No Man's Land" between theology and science resonates with Popper's view of metaphysics as potentially fruitful but non-scientific until falsifiable.

**Contrasts / productive tensions** (flagged, not reconciled):
- Vs. inductivist/probabilist schools (Reichenbach, Carnap, early positivism referenced via Hume/Russell critiques): Probability cannot serve as "degree of confirmation"; universal laws have zero probability; corroboration is qualitatively different (report of past severe tests survived).
- Vs. verificationism / "meaning = verifiability": Falsifiability is for *demarcation of the empirical*, not a criterion of meaning or meaningfulness. Metaphysics can be meaningful and historically important for science.
- Vs. "psychologism" or "given" in experience (lingering in some empiricist and protocol-sentence views): Basic statements are conventional decisions, not incorrigible foundations.
- Vs. pragmatist/process theories of truth (James "corroborate and verify," "truth happens"): Popper's corroboration is survival under attempted refutation within a critical method, not satisfactoriness or leading in experience. (Distinction noted in James pages already using "corroborate.")

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The conventional/decisionist element in accepting basic statements: how "objective" is the empirical basis if acceptance is social? (Popper addresses via intersubjectivity and the supreme methodological rule; later developed in "three worlds" ontology.)
- Does falsificationism understate the role of positive support / heuristics in actual science? **Kuhn's challenge is now primary-sourced** ([[Sources/The Structure of Scientific Revolutions - Kuhn (Chicago, 4th ed.)]]): most science is paradigm-bound normal science, anomalies are not bare falsifiers, and change is revolutionary/conversion-like — see [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]]. (Lakatos, Feyerabend not yet in vault.)
- Relation of corroboration to truth: Popper later introduces verisimilitude (truth-content minus falsity-content) as the aim that increases with corroboration of bold theories. (Referenced in * notes.)
- Tension with "mitigated scepticism" (Hume): Popper is more optimistic about the power of critical method to produce genuine (though fallible) growth; Hume emphasizes the natural limits and the "flames" for much speculation.
