# Neoplatonism - R. T. Wallis (1972)

> R. T. Wallis, *Neoplatonism* (Charles Scribner's Sons, 1972; the standard single-author survey superseding Whittaker). A compact scholarly history of the whole Neoplatonic movement from Plotinus (3rd c.) through Porphyry, Iamblichus, the Athenian School (Syrianus, Proclus, Damascius), to the closing of the Academy (529) and beyond, plus its later influence. Frames Neoplatonism by its three aims and traces how the later school's developments "follow naturally from points made in the *Enneads*."

## Argument Spine

Neoplatonism is a unified movement, not a loose "School of Alexandria," pursuing three interlocking aims: (1) to construct a complete, rationally systematic metaphysics unifying all reality under the transcendent One; (2) to secure the individual soul's salvation — its return and union with the divine; and (3) to defend and systematize the Hellenic religious and philosophical tradition (above all Plato, read together with Aristotle, the Pythagoreans, and the *Chaldaean Oracles*) against Christianity. Plotinus supplies the foundational system — the three Hypostases (One, Intellect, Soul), emanation as undiminished overflow, cosmic sympathy, and the soul's contemplative return — but leaves a set of unresolved problems (catalogued by Wallis on pp. 90–93) that drive all later development: how far the soul descends, whether contemplation alone suffices for union or ritual (theurgy) is needed, how many mediating levels reality contains, and how to reconcile the system with the sacred texts. Porphyry humanizes and popularizes Plotinus while tending to collapse distinctions; Iamblichus reacts by multiplying the hypostases, insisting the soul fully descends, and grounding salvation in theurgy rather than pure thought; the Athenian School (culminating in Proclus) systematizes the whole into a rigorous deductive hierarchy of triads and henads. The movement's ultimate legacy runs through Pseudo-Dionysius, Augustine, the Arabic tradition, and medieval and Renaissance thought.

## Whole-Work Assessment

Wallis gives the vault its first scholarly history of the whole Neoplatonic movement, converting the existing Plotinus cluster (built from the *Enneads* structural reference) into a four-century arc with named phases, mechanisms, and a driving thesis: later Neoplatonic metaphysics is "in many ways merely a series of footnotes to the Enneads," each development answering a problem Plotinus left open (the pp. 90–93 catalogue, now recorded on [[Thinkers/Plotinus]]). The ingest created the movement's hub page ([[Schools/Neoplatonism]]), three thinker pages (Porphyry, Iamblichus, Proclus), four concept pages (theurgy, undescended soul, henads, the monē–proodos–epistrophē triad), and the school's defining internal contradiction (contemplation vs theurgy) — which also reframes Augustine's grace-over-ascent argument as structurally Iamblichean. The Argument Spine written at Step 1 held up against the text; one correction of emphasis: Wallis's closing valuation locates the movement's enduring worth in its analysis of religious experience and consciousness ("paradoxicality is inherent in the nature of conscious experience itself"), not in its metaphysics. The influence chapter wires the classical cluster forward to Augustine, Aquinas, al-Ghazali/Ibn Rushd, Spinoza, and Hegel, and backward links to Lovejoy's Chain of Being are now reciprocal.
