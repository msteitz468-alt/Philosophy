# The Genealogy of Morals - Nietzsche (1887)

> "We are unknown, we knowers, ourselves to ourselves: this has its own good reason. ... Each one is the farthest away from himself."  
> — Preface, §1

Internet Archive text of Friedrich Nietzsche's *On the Genealogy of Morals* (original German *Zur Genealogie der Moral*, 1887). Published as a "polemic" to amplify and complete doctrines sketched in *Beyond Good and Evil* (especially Aphorism 260). Least aphoristic of Nietzsche's major works; noted for analytical power, especially on the ascetic ideal and "sacerdotal psychology." Standard English translation (public domain). Three essays + substantial Preface. Direct continuation and genealogical grounding of the revaluation project begun in BGE.

## Editor's Note (from the edition)

In 1887, with the view of amplifying and completing certain new doctrines which he had merely sketched in *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche published *The Genealogy of Morals*. This work is perhaps the least aphoristic, in form, of all Nietzsche's productions. For analytical power, more especially in those parts where Nietzsche examines the ascetic ideal, *The Genealogy of Morals* is unequalled by any other of his works; and, in the light which it throws upon the attitude of the ecclesiast to the man of resentment and misfortune, it is one of the most valuable contributions to sacerdotal psychology.

## Preface

Nietzsche recounts the personal and intellectual origins of his "thoughts concerning the genealogy of our moral prejudices." Early curiosity (age 13) about the origin of "Good" and "Evil," first expressed clumsily in *Human, All Too Human*. Response to Dr. Paul Rée's *The Origin of the Moral Emotions* (1877, "English" utilitarian/positivist view of altruistic origin via habit and forgetting). Shift from theological "origin of evil" to the *value* of morality and the "unegoistic" instincts (pity, self-denial) that Schopenhauer had deified. Critique of modern pity-morality as "the most sinister symptom of our modern European civilisation" — route to nihilism, a "European Buddhism." Call for a *critique of moral values*: knowledge of the conditions under which they grew, evolved, and distorted (morality as symptom, mask, disease *and* cause, remedy, stimulant). "Grey" authentic historical facts (the "long hieroglyphic script" of the past) over the "blue vacuum of heaven" of English theories. "Rumination" as the forgotten art of reading (be a cow, not a modern man). "We finders of knowledge" must keep silent sufficiently long. Dated Sils-Maria, Upper Engadine, July, 1887.

## First Essay: "Good and Evil," "Good and Bad"

Sustained critique of "English psychologists" (utilitarians, Rée et al.) who locate the origin of "good" in altruistic utility from the standpoint of those *to whom* goodness was shown, followed by forgetting and habit. This is "crass ineptitude" lacking historical sense; the "homestead of the concept 'good'" is wrongly placed.

**Core thesis (pathos of distance)**: The judgment "good" originated among the *good themselves* — the aristocratic, powerful, high-minded — who felt *themselves* good and their actions of the first order, in contrast to the low, vulgar, plebeian. "It was out of this pathos of distance that they first arrogated the right to create values for their own profit, and to coin the names of such values." Etymological evidence across languages (Greek *esthlos*, Latin *bonus* as warrior, "blond" vs "dark" pre-Aryan, etc.) confirms "good" = noble/aristocratic/powerful; "bad" = low/plebeian. Masters' right of giving names extends to language itself as expression of power.

Priestly-aristocratic mode (clean/unclean, introspection, explosive emotionalism, neurasthenia) branches off and intensifies the opposition, eventually producing the "dangerous" sharpening of values. The knightly-aristocratic values (physical cult, war, adventure, joyous action, health) vs. priestly (bad when weak, hate expands into crafty revenge).

**The slave revolt in morals (Jewish/Christian inversion)**: The Jews, "a priestly nation of resentment par excellence," effected the most profound and fateful transvaluation: the aristocratic equation (good = aristocratic = beautiful = happy = loved by the gods) inverted to "the wretched are alone the good; the poor, the weak, the lowly, are alone the good; the suffering... are the only ones who are pious... you aristocrats... are to all eternity the evil..." This "revolt of the slaves in morals" begins when *ressentiment* becomes creative and gives birth to values. Slave morality says "no" from the outset to what is "outside itself"; its action is fundamentally reaction. It requires an external "evil enemy" (the powerful, the aristocrat distorted by the "venomous eye of resentfulness" into the "evil one") to create its "good" (the self as patient, meek, just). Master morality is spontaneous self-affirmation ("we good ones, we beautiful ones...") ; "bad" is a pale late-born foil.

Famous parable: lambs vs. birds of prey. It is no reason for blaming birds of prey for taking lambs. The lambs' setting up of the ideal ("he who is opposite a bird of prey is good") is understandable, but the birds regard it sneeringly. Strength is not an option to be weak; there is no neutral "subject" behind doing ("the action is everything"). The "doer" is a mere appanage invented by popular morality and language tricks (lightning/lightning).

**Rome vs. Judaea**: The symbol of the thousand-year fight. Rome = strong, aristocratic values; Judaea = priestly ressentiment. Judaea victorious via Christianity (the "Redeemer" as bait of the most dangerous revenge), the Reformation, the French Revolution (the "redemption" of humanity progressing, everything "Judaised"). Napoleon as "synthesis of Monster and Superman," last sign-post of the aristocratic ideal. "Beyond Good and Evil — at any rate that is not the same as 'Beyond Good and Bad.'"

## Second Essay: "Guilt," "Bad Conscience," and the Like

**The sovereign individual and the problem of promising**: The breeding of an animal that *can promise* is the paradoxical task nature set itself with man. Requires active forgetfulness (health, psychic order, "tabula rasa of consciousness") *and* a counter-power: memory of the will. The "sovereign individual" — the ripest fruit of the "morality of custom" (*Sittlichkeit der Sitte*) that made man calculable, uniform, disciplined — is the autonomous, "super-moral" being who resembles only himself, has got loose from custom, possesses a "proud consciousness of what has been at last achieved," a "genuine consciousness of power and freedom," and whose dominating instinct is called *conscience*. He honours only those who can promise like sovereigns (sparing with trusts) and has the rod for liars and the heel for "lean and empty jackasses."

**Making memory**: Prehistoric mnemonics through blood, torture, sacrifice, mutilation, cruel rituals. "Something is burnt in so as to remain in his memory: only that which never stops hurting remains in his memory." Ascetic methods for making ideas "fixed" and unforgettable. The severity of penal laws gauges the difficulty of conquering forgetfulness.

**"Ought" from "owe" (Schuld)**: The cardinal moral idea of personal obligation originates in the material idea of debt (buyer/seller, creditor/ower relationship — as old as legal rights, purchase, barter). Punishment developed as retaliation independently of freedom of will. The ower pledges body, life, wife, freedom, even salvation to guarantee repayment; creditor gains right to inflict proportionate pain/torture (exact schedules for limbs in early codes). Compensation = sensation of satisfaction in venting power on the powerless, "the delight 'de faire le mal pour le plaisir de le faire'." The equivalence injury = pain. "The infliction of suffering produces the highest degree of happiness."

**Bad conscience**: *Not* primarily produced by punishment (punishment hardens, numbs, sharpens prudence and cunning, increases fear; genuine remorse extremely rare among wrong-doers; "prisons... are not the soil"; the sight of judicial cruelty prevents the wrong-doer feeling his act intrinsically reprehensible). Spinoza already saw: *morsus conscientiae* = "a sadness accompanied by the recollection of a past event which has turned out contrary to all expectation" (not "I ought not"). Evil-doers felt "here is something which went wrong," not moral guilt. Punishment tames but does not make "better."

**Hypothesis of origin**: Bad conscience as the serious illness man contracted under the stress of the most radical change: imprisonment within the pale of society and peace. The half-animal adapted to war, prowling, adventure suddenly had instincts "switched off." Reduced to thinking, calculating with the "poorest and most erratic organ," their "consciousness." All instincts without outward vent turned inwards — this is the "internalisation" of man; the soul (inner world) burst apart and expanded. The terrible bulwarks of social organisation (punishments) turned "enmity, cruelty, the delight in persecution..." against man himself. "It was this fool, this homesick and desperate prisoner — who invented the 'bad conscience.'" Result: "the suffering of man from the disease called man" — violent break from animal past, declaration of war on the old instincts that had been his power and joy. Yet also "pregnancy": this "wholly active bad conscience" (self-abuse, will for self-torture) has produced "an abundance of novel and amazing beauty and affirmation." Idealism, beauty, even "altruism" as moral value trace to it.

Further development (tribal ancestors as creditors, growing debt to gods with race power, Christian God as record creditor producing record guilt consciousness; eventual atheism as decay of the feeling of owing).

## Third Essay: "What is the Meaning of Ascetic Ideals?"

"What is the meaning of ascetic ideals?" Survey across types:

- Artists: nothing, or too much (late Wagner's homage to chastity in *Parsifal* questioned — parody of the ascetic ideal or serious reversion?).

- Philosophers and scholars: a "flair" and instinct for conditions favourable to advanced intellectualism (asceticism — grim renunciation, chastity, humility, solitude — as condition for highest spirituality and pregnancy of great thought; Schopenhauer example; philosophers as "shadows," "maternal" instinct for the growing work absorbing vitality; not hatred of flesh but will of the dominant instinct).

- Women (at best): additional seductive fascination, "angelhood of a fat, pretty animal."

- Physiological failures: attempt to pose as "too good" for this world, holy form of debauchery, chief weapon vs. pain and ennui.

- Priests: the actual priestly faith, their best engine of power, supreme authority for power.

- Saints: pretext for hibernation, *novissima gloria cupido*, peace in nothingness ("God"), their form of madness.

**Fundamental feature**: "In the very fact that the ascetic ideal has meant so much to man, lies expressed the fundamental feature of man's will, his *horror vacui*: *he needs a goal* — and he will sooner will nothingness than not will at all."

The ideal gives meaning to suffering (especially for the sick/weak); the priest is the true "artist" and "shepherd" of the ascetic ideal, using it for domination. Even the philosopher's apparent "hatred of the senses" is often will to power in disguise (highest intellect requires certain ascetic conditions). The will to power of the weak/sick expresses itself through the ideal (self-denial, self-sacrifice as phases of cruelty turned inward; "the bad conscience... has finally... produced an abundance of novel and amazing beauty").

Detailed treatment of the ideal in artists (Wagner's *Parsifal* as potential self-parody or late nihilistic turn), philosophers (Schopenhauer, the "maternal" instinct, sensuality transfigured not abolished), and especially the priest (the most interesting case of will to power via the ideal).

## Insights + Vault Relevance

- **Genealogy as method**: The vault's core practice (read index first, targeted source reads, cross-references, provenance, contradictions framed as productive tensions, lint for stale claims) is itself genealogical. - **Master vs. slave morality in the vault**: Master: spontaneous self-affirmation, creation of values, "we good ones" (noble, powerful perspectives, will to power as affirmative imposition). Slave: reaction, inversion via ressentiment ("the powerful are evil"), imaginary revenge, "good" as meekness/weakness/suffering. - **Ressentiment**: The creative force of slave morality — the "venomous eye," the "evil enemy" construction, the "no" from weakness. In vault/AI/career: resentment at "the market," "unfair" clients, "biased" models, "the duty" of maintenance, "the boss," "the prompt limits." - **Bad conscience and internalization**: The "serious illness" from caging the free instincts (warrior, prowling, adventure, cruelty-as-joy) inside society/peace/custom. Turned inward: self-torture, the "suffering of man from the disease called man." Yet also the soil of the soul, idealism, beauty, even "altruism." Punishment does *not* primarily create remorse (it hardens, teaches prudence, retards genuine bad conscience). In vault: the "voice" of internalized schema/duty vs. the sovereign individual's proud conscience (memory of will, ability to promise and keep commitments). "Bad conscience" as the modern European inheritance — useful diagnostic for when maintenance feels like self-laceration rather than power.

- **Sovereign individual**: The late, rare fruit — the one who has the *right to make promises*, autonomous ("super-moral"), long unbreakable will, proud consciousness of power/freedom/mastery over self and fate, conscience as the name of that dominating instinct. The ideal (chastity, poverty, humility, self-denial) gives meaning especially to suffering and is the priest's supreme power instrument.
