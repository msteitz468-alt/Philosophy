# What Technology Wants - Kevin Kelly (2010)

> **What Technology Wants** (2010). By Kevin Kelly (b. 1952), co-founder of *Wired*, author of *Out of Control* (1994). Kelly introduces the "technium" as the 7th kingdom of life: the global, massively interconnected system of technology (hardware + culture, art, social institutions, intellectual creations, software, law, philosophical concepts, and the generative impulses of invention). The technium has its own evolutionary dynamics, a measure of autonomy, and inherent "wants" or tendencies (to self-organize into hierarchies, to perpetuate itself, to increase complexity/exotropy, to maximize choices, to converge on recurring forms). Technology coevolves with us; it is an outgrowth of life and self-organization. We should respond to it as we do to nature: sometimes surrender to its lead and abundance, sometimes bend its course to our ends — by listening to its long-term trajectories. The book explores the origins of the technium, its imperatives ("what it wants"), our choices (e.g., Amish hackers as a model of selective, convivial adoption vs. the Unabomber's rejection), and directions (playing the "infinite game" of sustaining the game itself). This is the foundational text for Kelly's later *The Inevitable* (the 12 forces as specific expressions of what the technium wants).

## Nature of the source (read this first)

This is Kelly's major philosophical work on the nature and trajectory of technology, building directly on *Out of Control* (which explored emergent, bottom-up, self-organizing systems in biology, economies, and the early internet). It combines personal narrative (Kelly's years in minimal-tech Asia and Amish country, his role in early online communities like the WELL, Wired), scientific/historical evidence (evolution, exotropy, convergence in biology and tech), and a framework for responding to technological change without naive optimism or Luddite rejection.

The technium is not just "stuff" or specific gadgets; it is a self-reinforcing system with momentum. It has autonomy on a continuum (self-repair in networks, self-organization in traffic flows, "whispering to itself" in unaccounted bits). Its "wants" are unconscious tendencies/leanings/trajectories, not conscious deliberation — analogous to how a bacterium "wants" nutrients or life "wants" eyes (convergent evolution).

Kelly argues that after 10,000 years of slow evolution and 200 years of explosive growth, the technium has enough feedback loops and complexity to exhibit noticeable independence from human intention alone. It obeys laws of mind, life, and self-organization *in addition to* our human minds. Human influence is real but not exclusive or even the strongest force.

The book is structured in four parts:
- Origins: "My Question," inventing ourselves (hunter-gatherers and the burst of tools ~50k years ago), history of the seventh kingdom (technium as extension of life), rise of exotropy (increasing order, information, mind against entropy).
- Imperatives: Deep progress, ordained becoming, convergence (recurring forms in tech mirroring biology), listen to the technology, choosing the inevitable.
- Choices: Critique of the Unabomber (anti-civilization rejection is unappealing and inconsistent; no one wants the tiny smoky shack), lessons from Amish hackers (selective adoption, "owning vs. using," convivial tools that empower community without full dependency), seeking conviviality.
- Directions: Technology’s trajectories (what it wants at cosmic scale), playing the infinite game (from James Carse: play to keep playing/sustaining the game, not to win/finish it).

Kelly draws on process theology, evolution (convergence, exotropy), systems theory, and personal observation. He acknowledges trade-offs, paradoxes, and the "enduring tension" between technology's gifts and demands. The goal is a framework to optimize blessings and minimize costs by aligning with (while sometimes steering) the technium's nature.

This 2010 book is the direct predecessor to *The Inevitable* (2016), where the 12 forces are presented as concrete, overlapping expressions of the technium's wants/biases in the digital age.

## The Technium: Definition and Autonomy

From the raw (Ch. 1 and Ch. 3):

"I call it the technium. The technium extends beyond shiny hardware to include culture, art, social institutions, and intellectual creations of all types. It includes intangibles like software, law, and philosophical concepts. And most important, it includes the generative impulses of our inventions to encourage more tool making, more technology invention, and more self-enhancing connections."

It is the "greater, global, massively interconnected system of technology vibrating around us" — a whole system, not a pile of individual technologies (which can be patented).

Evidence of autonomy (on a continuum, like life/mind): self-repair (communication networks), self-steering (drones), self-reproduction (viruses, though limited), self-improvement (evolutionary algorithms), fractal self-similarity in traffic, bits that "change themselves" unaccounted for by known nodes ("the technium is whispering to itself").

The technium is an outgrowth of the human mind, of life, and of the physical/chemical self-organization that led to life. It shares deep roots with mind and ancient life. It must obey not only human intentions but the laws of mind, life, and self-organization.

## What the Technium Wants (Imperatives/Trajectories)

The technium has its own wants — tendencies, leanings, urges, trajectories (not thoughtful decisions; closer to needs or compulsions, like a sea cucumber seeking a mate or a bacterium drifting toward nutrients).

Explicit from the text:
- "It wants to sort itself out, to self-assemble into hierarchical levels, just as most large, deeply interconnected systems do."
- "The technium also wants what every living system wants: to perpetuate itself, to keep itself going. And as it grows, those inherent wants are gaining in complexity and force."
- Increase the number of choices (von Foerster: "act to increase the number of choices").
- Exotropy: increasing order, information, complexity, "mind" against entropy (the "ordained becoming" or cosmic trajectory that life and technology extend).
- Convergence: recurring forms and solutions (like eyes in biology; inevitable inventions in tech).
- To be ubiquitous (replicate and spread like life, given the chance).
- Deep progress: not just more stuff, but increasing possibilities, options, freedom within constraints.
- To evolve and generate more invention/tool-making/self-enhancing connections.

Later chapters detail trajectories: increasing complexity, diversity, specialization, cooperation, choice, sentience/mind, etc. Technology extends evolution's path toward these.

Kelly: "By placing technology in the context of that evolution, we can see how those macroimperatives play out in our present time. In other words, technology’s inevitable forms coalesce around the dozen or so dynamics common to all exotropic systems, including life itself."

The 12 forces in his later book (*The Inevitable*) are specific, digital-era expressions of these deeper wants of the technium.

## Choices: How We Respond

- Not blind acceptance or total rejection.
- Unabomber (Ted Kaczynski) critique: His diagnosis of technology's problems has some truth (it creates dependencies, "chooses" for us in subtle ways), but his solution (destroy civilization, return to primitive) is unappealing, inconsistent (he used high-tech to spread his message), and nobody wants the "tiny, smoky, dingy, smelly wooden shack."
- Amish hackers as positive model: Not anti-tech, but highly selective. They adopt tools that empower community and "owning vs. using" distinction (e.g., use diesel but not own cars in ways that fragment community; pneumatic shop tools powered by off-grid means; rumspringa to experience outside world before choosing). They ask: Does this tool increase our dependence on the outside world in ways that erode our values? They limit choices to increase other values (community, simplicity, fulfillment). "The Amish are not an anti-technological society; they are a low-technological society."
- Conviviality (Ivan Illich influence): Tools that are accessible, repairable, human-scale, that empower rather than enslave.
- "We can’t demand that technology obey us any more than we can demand that life obey us. Sometimes we should surrender to its lead and bask in its abundance, and sometimes we should try to bend its natural course to meet our own."
- "Our role as humans, at least for the time being, is to coax technology along the paths it naturally wants to go." But we have freedom within the givens (like choosing character within genetic/cultural constraints).

## Directions and the Infinite Game

Technology is playing (and we with it) the "infinite game" (James Carse): play to keep the game going, to sustain play, rather than a finite game with winners/losers/end. The goal is increasing options, possibilities, the perpetuation and expansion of the game of life/mind/choice itself.

Cosmic view: The universe seems biased toward exotropy (order, complexity, mind). "The universe knew we were coming" (Dyson); "starstuff pondering the stars" (Sagan). Technology is the latest phase of this ordained becoming. "What technology wants" is continuous with what life and the cosmos "want": more mind, more choice, more becoming.

Kelly ends with hope in embracing our natures and aligning with the technium's imperative while steering where we can. "We trust in nature, but we hope in technology."

## Key Claims / Positions

- The technium is a real, emergent system with autonomy and its own evolutionary "wants" (tendencies toward hierarchy, self-perpetuation, exotropy/complexity, increasing choices, convergence on forms).
- Technology is coevolving with humanity; we have domesticated it and it has domesticated us (shrinking teeth, etc.). We are symbiotic.
- Hunter-gatherer life had "enough" for survival but not transcendence; technology (starting with language/tools ~50kya) opened greater realms of possibility at a cost (extinctions, violence, new dependencies) but with the gain of civilization and progress.
- Exotropy is the fundamental force: the rise of order, information, and mind in the universe, of which the technium is a continuation.
- We have real but limited choice: within the constraints and trajectories of the technium, we decide how to respond (selective adoption like the Amish, convivial tools, steering rather than stopping).
- The Unabomber's total rejection is neither desirable nor consistent; the Amish selective embrace is a better (though extreme) model for "listening" to technology while prioritizing human values.
- Long-term: play the infinite game — sustain and expand the possibilities of mind, choice, and becoming.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **Autonomy vs. human control**: The technium has momentum and "wants" independent of us, yet we created it and can (to a degree) steer it. Where is the boundary? How much is inevitable vs. chosen?
- **Gifts vs. demands**: Technology gives abundance, options, mind-expansion, but demands dependency, new problems, attention, and sometimes "chooses" paths we might not have (e.g., via lock-in). The tension is "unavoidable" as long as the technium exists.
- **Progress vs. Amish minimalism**: Is "deep progress" (more choices, complexity, mind) always better, or do selective limits (Amish-style) preserve higher human goods (community, fulfillment, less distraction)? Kelly presents the Amish as instructive without endorsing full adoption.
- **With anti-tech views**: The Unabomber correctly identifies some problems (technology as a system that shapes us more than we shape it) but his solution is rejected as unworkable and undesirable.
- **With naive pro-tech**: Not everything the technium "wants" (e.g., maximum ubiquity, certain hierarchies) is automatically good for humans; we must choose convivial expressions.
