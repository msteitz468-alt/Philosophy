# Wittgenstein's Philosophical Investigations: A Critical Guide — Ahmed (2010)
> A scholarly compilation of critical essays analyzing the core themes, arguments, and legacy of Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations*.

## Argument Spine

The volume provides a rigorous, chapter-by-chapter critical evaluation of the central pillars of Wittgenstein's later philosophy. It charts the shift from early referentialism to an anthropocentric metaphysics of human action (Hanna, Luntley). It reassesses his concepts of rules, criteria, and family resemblances by analyzing the Paris standard metre bar (Jacquette), anti-Platonist classifications (Forster), and concept-possession as a potentiality/ability rather than an inner mental state (Glock, McDowell). It engages with contemporary debates in the philosophy of language, arguing against the contextualist reading of Wittgenstein's proper names (Bridges) and evaluating the historic metaphilosophical legacy of the "linguistic turn," contrasting therapeutic quietism with pragmatic social-practice theory (Rorty, Horwich). Finally, it examines the dialogical voices in the private language remarks (Stern), and proposes Wittgensteinian solutions to the epistemology of logic via aspect-seeing (Ahmed) and to the realism of past mental intentions via dispositional realism (Child).

## Whole-Work Assessment

### Theses

- **Shift from Essentialism to Action**: The *Investigations* systematically replaces Tractarian solipsism with an anthropocentric metaphysics of the ordinary, where meaning is use, logic is grammar, and Cartesian minds are replaced by embodied human action (lines 657–675).
- **Ostensive Training (*Abrichtung*) vs. Definition (*Erklärung*)**: Entering language is not a passive empiricist association nor does it presuppose prior linguistic competence. Rather, it relies on pre-conceptual "ostensive training" that tunes the learner's active attention and directedness (lines 1871–1904).
- **The Polarity Principle**: A predicate (such as "X is one metre long") is meaningful only if its negation or complement serves a concrete purpose in a language-game; thus, the Paris standard metre bar cannot be said to be one metre or not one metre long, functioning as a means of representation rather than an object of representation (lines 2199–2213, 2621–2623).
- **Compatibility of Family Resemblance and Rules**: Family resemblances characterize concepts without fixed boundaries, yet remain fully compatible with rule-governedness because grasping a rule is exhibited by obeying or going against it in actual cases, not by possessing a rigid Platonic essence (lines 3195–3197, 3248–3252).
- **Concept Possession as two-way Power**: Possessing a concept is not an occurrent mental or neurological state, but a technique or ability of word-use characterized as a "two-way power" which the agent can choose to exercise or not at will (lines 4112–4134).
- **Critique of Semantic Contextualism**: Appeals to context in Wittgenstein's analysis of names (PI §79) do not support a shifting, context-varying semantic sense. Instead, descriptions are pragmatic tools to resolve specific local doubts of the listener, maintaining the anti-theoretical character of meaning (lines 4549–4554).
- **Pragmatic vs. Therapeutic Wittgenstein**: Rorty distinguishes "therapeutic Wittgensteinians" (who treat philosophy as nonsense to be dissolved quietistically) from "pragmatic Wittgensteinians" (who treat Wittgenstein as replacing representationalism with a positive, social-practice theory of meaning) (lines 5316–5339).
- **Epistemology of Logic via Aspect Perception**: Logical rules are autonomous and not derived from prior semantic grasp. Recognizing the validity of a novel inference schema (N3-rule) is a perceptual achievement analogous to aspect perception (aspect-seeing or gestalt shifts) (lines 7948–7950, 8588–8592).
- **Dispositional Realism of Intention**: Sincere retrospective reports of intention ("I was going to say...") do not recall a conscious experience. The truth-maker for past intentions is the whole complex of abilities, dispositions, and situational connections the agent had at the past time, avoiding both Wright's anti-realism and experiential realism (lines 8856–8858, 8937–8945).

### Key Quotations

- "...a sentence does not have a meaning from which we derive the rules governing its deductive connection with others. Rather, our assigning those rules to it just is our expression of its meaning..." (lines 7948–7950)
- "There is a way of grasping a rule which is not an interpretation, but which is exhibited in what we call 'obeying the rule' and 'going against it' in actual cases." (PI 201; lines 3195–3197)
- "Try not to think of understanding as a ‘mental process’ at all. – For that is the expression that confuses you." (PI 154, lines 6598–6599)
- "The linguistic turn was useful nevertheless, for it turned philosophers’ attention from the topic of experience towards that of linguistic behavior." (lines 5274–5277)
- "This sample is an instrument of the language used in ascriptions of colour. In this language game it is not something that is represented, but is a means of representation." (PI 50; lines 2621–2623)
- "what makes it true that I meant him to put ‘1002’ is the whole complex of dispositions I had at the time..." (lines 8856–8858)

### Method and Scope

The Critical Guide brings together leading analytic and post-analytic philosophers to systematically evaluate the later Wittgenstein's arguments. Using deductive modeling, textual exegesis of source drafts (Nachlass), and critical dialogue, they bridge the historical divide between Wittgensteinian scholars and contemporary philosophy of mind, perception, and logic. The volume serves as a landmark guide for assessing the truth and legacy of the *Philosophical Investigations* half a century after its publication.
