# Sartre — Ruud Welten (2020)
> A compact, chronological intellectual biography (Dutch *Elementaire Deeltjes* series) that reads Sartre's whole oeuvre as one restless, unfinished search organised around a single thesis: consciousness *is not*, and is therefore radically free.

## Argument Spine

Welten presents Sartre not as the author of a finished system but as a "cyclonic" thinker in permanent motion. The through-line of the book is that Sartre takes one idea from Husserl — that consciousness is nothing but intentional directedness — and pushes it to its limit: consciousness is a *nothing* (*néant*), a "hole in being," and therefore the human being *is not* anything fixed but *has to be*. From this single move Welten derives, chapter by chapter, every later development: existence precedes essence, radical freedom and bad faith, the Look of the Other, existential psychoanalysis of writers, engaged literature, and finally the turn from the lone individual of *Being and Nothingness* to the community, history, and revolutionary violence of the *Critique of Dialectical Reason*. The book insists that Sartre's "ethics" is never a doctrine of values (values are arbitrary) but only the refusal to disown one's freedom.

## Whole-Work Assessment

This is the vault's first dedicated Sartre source, and it lands him squarely inside the [[Schools/Phenomenology]] lineage already built around Husserl and [[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]] — while showing how Sartre breaks from both: a "negative phenomenology" of the *néant* rather than Husserl's positive givenness, and an ontology that starts from a *lack* of being rather than Heidegger's Being. It supplies the bridge from phenomenology to French Marxism and post-structuralism (Welten notes Sartre became the "mastodon" the structuralists Lévi-Strauss, Foucault, Althusser defined themselves against). The book's most valuable contribution is its insistence on the *continuity* between early and late Sartre — pour-soi/en-soi reappear as "practical organism"/"practical-inert field" — which resists the standard story of two unrelated Sartres. It opens productive tensions with [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]] (subjective vs. objective dialectic; rejection of natural rights), with Freud (lucidity vs. the unconscious), and with humanism (Sartre as anti-humanist who still titles a lecture *Existentialism is a Humanism*).
