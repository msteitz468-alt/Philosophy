# Aristotle - Modal Logic (Organon, Patterson)

> Scholarly analysis of Aristotle’s modal logic in the *Prior Analytics* (part of the *Organon*), arguing that it is inextricably tied to his essentialist metaphysics. Richard Patterson’s work critiques de dicto/de re interpretations and proposes that Aristotle uses a “modal copula” reflecting two kinds of necessity (weak/strong) grounded in the distinction between essential and accidental properties. Central to scientific demonstration (*apodeixis*).

## Summary

Aristotle’s treatment of modal syllogisms is difficult and often seen as confused by modern readers. Patterson shows it cannot be understood apart from Aristotle’s metaphysics of essence and accident. Modal propositions concern the *kind of connection* (necessary, possible, assertoric) between subject and predicate via a modalized copula, not operators on whole propositions or modifications of predicates alone.

The system supports scientific demonstration, which proceeds from *per se* (essential) predications.

---

## Key Themes and Arguments

### Modal Propositions and the Copula
Aristotle distinguishes:
- Assertoric (plain) propositions
- Necessary
- Possible (one-way and two-way/contingent)

Modality is expressed through the copula (“applies”, “necessarily applies”, “possibly applies”), not sentence-level operators.

### Two Readings of Necessity (Weak vs Strong Copula)
- **Weak**: A predicate necessarily applies to the things that fall under the subject (accidental or extensional necessity, e.g. “Cat necessarily applies to all things on the mat” if they happen to be cats).
- **Strong**: A predicate is essentially connected to the subject term itself (genus-species, per se predication, e.g. “Animal necessarily applies to all humans”).

These arise from the essential/accidental distinction and explain apparent shifts in Aristotle’s text.

### Critique of De Dicto / De Re
Modern frameworks (necessity of propositions vs necessity of properties belonging to things) do not fully capture Aristotle. He attaches modality to the *copula*, expressing how the predicate applies. This unifies the logic with metaphysics rather than treating it as pure formalism.

### Relation to Scientific Demonstration and Essentialism
Modal logic is not independent of ontology. Predicates relate as genus, species, differentia, proprium, or accident. Scientific demonstration relies on *per se* connections. Modal statements reflect these real relations in the world.

Patterson rejects views that see Aristotle as vacillating incoherently or that his logic floats free of his metaphysics.
