# On Liberty - John Stuart Mill (1859)

> **On Liberty**. Dedicated to his wife Harriet Taylor, Mill's essay is the foundational text of modern political liberalism, arguing that individual sovereignty over one's own body and mind must be protected against both state coercion and the stifling social conformity of the "tyranny of the majority."

## Core Thesis: The Harm Principle

Mill's central thesis is that the only justification for society or the state to interfere with the liberty of an individual, against their will, is to prevent harm to others. Over themselves, their own body and mind, the individual is sovereign. Mill defends this principle not as an abstract natural right, but as a utility-maximizing requirement for the intellectual and moral development of humanity.

## Section Summary

### Chapter 1: Introductory
-   **The New Threat to Liberty**: Mill traces the history of liberty from a struggle against tyrannical rulers to a struggle within democratic societies. He warns against the **tyranny of the majority**, which uses social pressure and public opinion to enforce conformity, a force more invasive than political oppression.
-   **The Harm Principle**: Formulates the single, simple principle to govern all societal coercion. Paternalism (coercing someone for their own good) is strictly rejected. See [[Concepts/Harm Principle (Mill)]].

### Chapter 2: Of the Liberty of Thought and Discussion
-   **Defense of Free Speech**: Mill argues that silencing any opinion, even an unpopular or offensive one, is an injury to the human race. He presents three scenarios:
    1.  *The opinion is true*: Silencing it deprives society of truth. To assume an opinion must be silenced because it is false is to assume our own infallibility.
    2.  *The opinion is false*: Silencing it prevents the truth from being challenged. Without debate, true beliefs become dead dogmas rather than living, rational convictions.
    3.  *The opinion is partially true*: Most opinions contain a mixture of truth and error; only free discussion allows us to separate them.

### Chapter 3: Of Individuality, as One of the Elements of Well-Being
-   **Experiments of Living**: Argues that humans should be free to act on their opinions and lead diverse lifestyles, so long as they do not harm others. Mill calls these different ways of living "experiments of living."
-   **Genius and Conformity**: Asserting that conformity crushes creativity, Mill argues that progress depends on eccentrics, geniuses, and innovators who challenge social customs.

### Chapter 4: Of the Limits to the Authority of Society over the Individual
-   **Self-Regarding vs. Other-Regarding**: Distinguishes between actions that affect only the agent (self-regarding) and those that affect others (other-regarding). Society has no right to punish self-regarding vices (like drunkenness or extravagance) unless they violate a specific duty to others (like failing to support one's children).

### Chapter 5: Applications
-   **Practical Cases**: Illustrates how the Harm Principle applies to real situations:
    -   *The Free Market*: Trade is an other-regarding act, so the state can regulate it (e.g., preventing fraud), but free trade is generally more useful.
    -   *Poisons*: The state can require warning labels or registration, but cannot ban poisons, as that would violate the liberty of the buyer.
    -   *Education*: The state should mandate education to prevent harm to the child, but should not run the schools directly, as a state-run education system would enforce ideological conformity.

## Integration and Vault Value

-   **Liberal Political Anchor**: Establishes the core liberal defense of individual autonomy, free speech, and personal diversity in the vault.
-   **Utilitarian Rationale**: Shows how Mill justifies absolute individual rights on utilitarian grounds, arguing that a society that permits freedom of thought and action is ultimately more progressive and happy than a conformist one.
-   **Tension with Absolutism**: Stands in sharp contrast to Thomas Hobbes's argument that the sovereign must control all public speech and opinion to preserve order. See [[Sources/Leviathan - Thomas Hobbes (1651)]].
