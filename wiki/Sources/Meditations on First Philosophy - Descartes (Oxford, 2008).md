# Meditations on First Philosophy - Descartes (Oxford, 2008)

> **Meditations on First Philosophy, in which the existence of God and the distinction of the human soul from the body are demonstrated** (Meditationes de prima philosophia, 1641 Latin; 2nd ed. 1642 with 7th set of objections; French trans. 1647). By René Descartes (1596–1650). Oxford World's Classics edition: translated with introduction and notes by Michael Moriarty (2008). Includes the six Meditations plus substantial selections from the Objections and Replies (full text for Hobbes's Third and Arnauld's Fourth; summaries + key extracts for others). The single most influential text in early modern philosophy; foundational for rationalism, epistemology, and mind-body dualism.

## Summary

Descartes's project is to rebuild knowledge from the ground up on indubitable foundations after realizing how many childhood opinions were doubtful. He adopts a meditative (not scholastic disputation or geometric theorem) form to guide the reader through a personal, step-by-step withdrawal from the senses and preconceptions, using hyperbolic doubt to clear the ground, then reconstructing certainty via the cogito, proofs of God as guarantor of clear and distinct perception, and the real distinction of mind and body. The work aims to provide demonstrative proofs (by natural reason) of God's existence and the soul's real distinction/immortality, both to support faith against unbelievers and to lay metaphysical foundations for the new mechanistic science.

The six Meditations form a single ordered chain (like geometry): each builds only on what has been rigorously established.

- **First Meditation**: Hyperbolic doubt. Senses sometimes deceive → do not fully trust them. Dream argument: no conclusive mark distinguishes waking from sleeping for particular things; even general bodily nature and simple truths of arithmetic/geometry could be illusions if an all-powerful God (or "evil spirit, supremely powerful and cunning") deceives me. Withhold assent from everything dubitable. Purpose: liberate from prejudices and open path for certain knowledge. (The "malicious demon" makes doubt universal and methodical.)

- **Second Meditation**: The cogito and nature of mind. Even under maximal deception, "I am, I exist" is necessarily true whenever I conceive it. The 'I' that exists is a thinking thing (res cogitans): one that doubts, understands, affirms, denies, wills, imagines, and perceives (all as modes of thought). Famous wax example: all sensible qualities (taste, smell, color, shape, hardness, sound) change when heated, yet we affirm it is the same wax. Therefore the wax is known not by the senses or imagination but by the intellect alone grasping extension, flexibility, and mutability. Conclusion: the mind is known more easily and certainly than the body; bodies themselves are perceived by the intellect, not the senses.

- **Third Meditation**: God exists (causal/"trademark" argument). Clear and distinct perceptions are true (provisional rule, secured later). Ideas classified (innate, adventitious, factitious); falsity proper lies in judgements. Causal principle (at least as much reality in cause as effect, formal or eminent) applies to the objective reality of ideas. The idea of God—an infinite, independent, supremely intelligent, powerful, and creative substance—contains more objective reality than any finite cause (including myself) can supply. Hence God exists as its cause. Additional argument from conservation (continuous creation): I cannot conserve myself moment-to-moment; regress ends only at God. The idea is innate, like the craftsman's mark on his work. Since God is perfect, he cannot be a deceiver.

- **Fourth Meditation**: Truth, falsity, and the cause of error. God (perfect, veracious) cannot deceive. I possess intellect (limited, finite) and will (unbounded, infinite in scope—like God's in its essential freedom to affirm/deny or pursue/avoid). Error is a privation (lack of knowledge that ought to be present when the will extends beyond what the intellect clearly and distinctly perceives; "indifference" on unclear matters). Not a positive faculty or something God produced. Proper use of freedom: suspend judgement on whatever is not clearly and distinctly perceived by the intellect. Habit of this (via repeated meditation) prevents error and sin (in the speculative sense). Every clear and distinct perception is true because it derives from God.

- **Fifth Meditation**: Essence of material things + ontological proof of God. Innate ideas of continuous quantity (extension in length/breadth/depth), shapes, numbers, motion, duration, etc., have true and immutable natures/essences independent of my mind or existence (e.g., properties of a triangle can be demonstrated even if no triangle exists anywhere; feels like "remembering"). Ontological argument: the idea of a supremely perfect being includes existence (a perfection); one cannot conceive God without existence any more than a triangle without its properties or a mountain without a valley. Existence is inseparable from God's essence. Even if all prior conclusions were doubted, this (and the earlier causal proof) would still hold with the certainty of mathematical truths. All perfect knowledge (scientia) ultimately depends on knowledge of the true God (non-deceiver): without it, even remembered clear perceptions remain vulnerable to later doubt (dream hypothesis, possible deceptive nature).

- **Sixth Meditation**: Existence of material things + real distinction of mind and body. Imagination (distinct effort, directed toward body) is not essential to mind (pure intellection suffices); its existence is best explained if a body to which mind is applied exists (probable). Sensation is a passive faculty of receiving ideas; requires an active cause. Given God's non-deceptiveness and my God-given strong propensity to believe the ideas come from bodies, bodies must exist (God would be a deceiver otherwise). Real distinction: I have a clear and distinct idea of myself as a thinking, non-extended thing and of body as extended, non-thinking substance; God can produce them separately → they are really distinct substances (mind can exist without body). Yet they are closely conjoined and "fused" into a single entity (not merely pilot in ship): this explains why sensations and appetites are confused modes, not pure intellection, and why pain/pleasure guide the composite toward benefit/harm. Nature (in the sense of the God-established system for the composite) teaches what preserves the whole; secondary qualities (colors, tastes, etc.) are obscure and not to be taken as resembling things in bodies; primary qualities (extension, shape, motion, number) and mathematical objects are clearly and distinctly known. Sensory errors (distance, phantom limbs, childhood habits) corrected by intellect. Many "teachings of nature" are actually unexamined habits.

The Objections and Replies (solicited via Mersenne; 7 sets in 2nd ed.): Caterus (1st: on objective reality, God as causa sui, mind/body); Mersenne circle (2nd: circle problem?, geometrical presentation requested); Hobbes (3rd: materialist challenges, interleaved replies); Arnauld (4th: most penetrating; issues on circle, real distinction, etc.—Descartes thought him best); Gassendi (5th: Epicurean/skeptical; prolix; Descartes sharp); group (6th: dualism, animal souls); Bourdin (7th: Jesuit, longest, focuses on first two Meds and method; Descartes responds rhetorically, defending discovery-process vs. scholastic framework). The Replies clarify key terms (objective vs. formal reality, idea, substance) and defend the order and certainty claims.

## Historical and Philosophical Context

Published 1641 (Latin, for learned) after Discourse on the Method (1637, French, partial version with toned-down skepticism). Descartes (Jesuit-educated at La Flèche, law degree, military/travels, settled in Dutch Republic) sought to ground the new mechanistic corpuscular physics (Le Monde shelved after Galileo's 1633 condemnation) on metaphysics. Solicited objections to preempt criticism and demonstrate rigor. Hoped for Sorbonne endorsement (Letter of Dedication). Responds to revived ancient skepticism (Montaigne's Pyrrhonism via Sextus Empiricus) by using doubt as tool for certainty rather than tranquility. Breaks with scholastic Aristotelianism ("nothing in the intellect not first in the senses"; hylomorphism; final causes/teleology in physics; disputation form) while retaining some vocabulary (substance, clear/distinct). Revives Augustinian interiority for epistemic certainty. Sets stage for rationalism (Spinoza, Leibniz), British empiricism reaction, Kant's synthesis, and modern philosophy of mind/science.

The meditative form ("several months or weeks" per Meditation recommended in Replies) and first-person "I" are deliberate: reader is to meditate along, not merely debate propositions.

## Key Arguments and Tensions

- **Hyperbolic vs. Pyrrhonian doubt**: Descartes uses universal, methodical doubt to reach bedrock certainty (cogito + God guarantor), not suspension/ataraxia. Direct counterpoint to vault's Sextus Empiricus material (equipollence → epoché → ataraxia "by chance").
- **Certainty and God**: Clear and distinct perceptions are true only once God's existence and veracity are established (the "Cartesian circle" objection, raised in 2nd Objections and discussed in Replies).
- **Dualism and union**: Real distinction of substances yet substantial union in the human being (explains sensation as confused). Challenges both Platonic/Plotinian soul-as-prisoner and Aristotelian hylomorphism (soul as form of *this* body).
- **Essences and existence**: Mathematical truths and God's existence have the same status of necessity/innateness. Ontological argument defended against "existence not a predicate" style objections (via Caterus).
- **Error, will, and freedom**: Error is misuse of unbounded will on limited intellect. Strong account of freedom (inclination by clear perception increases freedom).
- **Mechanistic implications**: Only extension and its modes (primary qualities) are clearly known in bodies; secondary qualities subjective/confused. Foundations for corpuscular philosophy and rejection of scholastic qualities.
