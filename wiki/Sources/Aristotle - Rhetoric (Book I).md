# Aristotle - Rhetoric (Book I)

> Rhetoric as the counterpart to dialectic: the faculty of observing in any given case the available means of persuasion. Distinguishes artistic proofs (*logos*, *ethos*, *pathos*) from non-artistic. Central device is the *enthymeme* (rhetorical syllogism from probabilities and signs) and the *example* (rhetorical induction). Three branches of oratory: deliberative (political, future), forensic (legal, past), epideictic (ceremonial, present). Critiques earlier manuals for focusing on emotional manipulation rather than the substance of persuasion. Emphasizes the integrity of the judicial function and the systematic, artistic nature of rhetoric.

## Summary

Rhetoric is an art (*technē*) that can be handled systematically, not merely random practice or emotional trickery. It is the counterpart of dialectic, concerned with things that come within the general ken of people and admit of no definite science. The modes of persuasion (artistic proofs) are the true constituents of the art; everything else (prejudice, pity, anger) is accessory or a perversion of the judge.

---

## Key Ideas

### Rhetoric and Dialectic
Both deal with probable matters open to all. All men use them to some degree; the art inquires into why some succeed by practice and others spontaneously. Current treatises focus on non-essentials (emotional appeals) rather than enthymemes, which are the substance of rhetorical persuasion.

### The Three Artistic Proofs
- **Logos**: the argument itself (logical appeal).
- **Ethos**: the character of the speaker (persuasion through trustworthiness, intelligence, goodwill).
- **Pathos**: putting the audience into a certain frame of mind (emotional appeal, but properly used, not mere manipulation).

Non-artistic proofs (witnesses, contracts, torture, oaths, etc.) are given, not invented by the speaker.

### Enthymeme and Example
The enthymeme is a deductive rhetorical syllogism, usually from probabilities (*eikos*) or signs (*sēmeion* / *tekmerion*). The example is inductive. Rhetoric uses these to deal with the particular and contingent.

### Three Branches of Oratory
- **Deliberative** (symbouleutikon): political advice about future expediency/harm; aims at the advantageous.
- **Forensic** (dikanikon): accusation/defense about past justice/injustice.
- **Epideictic** (epideiktikon): praise/blame about present virtue/vice (ceremonial, display).

Each has its own *topoi* (lines of argument), temporal orientation, and ends.

### Integrity of Judgment
It is not right to pervert the judge by anger, pity, or envy (like warping a carpenter’s rule). The litigant should only prove the facts are so or not; the judge decides importance, justice, etc. Well-governed states (e.g. Areopagus) limit such appeals. Laws should prescribe rules so that personal appeals are unnecessary.

### Critique of Earlier Manuals
They construct only a small portion of the art. They deal with how to put the judge into a certain frame of mind rather than the essential facts and the means of persuasion inherent in the case.
