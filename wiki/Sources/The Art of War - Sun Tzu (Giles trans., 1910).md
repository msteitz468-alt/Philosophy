# The Art of War - Sun Tzu (Giles trans., 1910)

> **The Art of War** (*Sūnzǐ Bīngfǎ*), traditionally attributed to **Sun Tzu** (Sun Wu, fl. c. 544–496 BCE), advisor to King Ho Lu of the state of Wu in the late Spring-and-Autumn / early Warring-States period. "The oldest military treatise in the world." This is the scholarly **Lionel Giles translation (1910)**, "translated from the Chinese with Introduction and Critical Notes," with the voluminous notes of the classical Chinese commentators (Ts'ao Kung/Cao Cao, Tu Mu, Chang Yu, Mei Yao-ch'en, et al.); converted to etext by Bob Sutton. Thirteen short chapters of aphoristic strategy that became foundational not only for war but for statecraft, business, negotiation, sport, and competitive strategy generally.

## Nature of the source

A **primary** classical text — but a *practical* manual, not a philosophical treatise: terse, aphoristic, organized by topic rather than argument. The vault value is (1) a new **strategy / statecraft** domain (its first non-Western classic, and its first dedicated text on conflict, competition, and intelligence); (2) doctrines that map unusually well onto **career/competitive strategy, negotiation, AI/agentic planning, and game theory**; and (3) strong cross-links to the vault's existing **power/war** material (Thucydides), **institutional realism** (the Federalist, Machiavelli as surveyed by Russell), and **strategic mastery** (Kasparov/chess). The Giles edition's commentary is scholarly; the doctrine itself is in the numbered verses, which this summary tracks.

## Author & provenance

- **Sun Tzu (Sun Wu)**: per Ssu-ma Ch'ien's *Shih Chi*, a native of the Ch'i State whose "13 chapters" won him audience with **Ho Lu, King of Wu**. The famous **concubine anecdote** (Sun Tzu drills 180 palace ladies; when the King's two favorite concubines — appointed company commanders — laugh instead of obeying, he has them executed to establish that "the general… is not bound by [the sovereign's] commands" once authority is delegated and orders are clear) dramatizes his core themes: discipline, clarity of command, and the autonomy of the competent general.
- **Authorship/dating** are debated (some scholars place the text in the Warring States period, c. 5th–4th c. BCE, possibly composite); Giles defends a substantially historical Sun Wu.
- **Transmission**: largely unknown in Europe until Amiot's 1782 French version; Giles's 1910 English edition was "the best translation available for 50 years," superseded as a *readable* text by Samuel B. Griffith (1963) but unmatched for scholarly notes.

## The 13 chapters

1. **Laying Plans (計).** War is "a matter of life and death… which can on no account be neglected." The **five constant factors**: (1) the **Moral Law** (*tao* — harmony of people with ruler, "so that they will follow him regardless of their lives"); (2) **Heaven** (weather, seasons, timing); (3) **Earth** (terrain, distance, danger/security); (4) the **Commander** (wisdom, sincerity, benevolence, courage, strictness); (5) **Method and discipline** (organization, logistics, control). The **seven considerations** for forecasting victory (which ruler has the Moral Law, which general more able, etc.). "**All warfare is based on deception**"; appear unable when able, near when far. "The general who wins makes many calculations in his temple ere the battle is fought." See [[Concepts/Calculation and the Five Factors (Sun Tzu)]], [[Concepts/All Warfare Is Based on Deception (Sun Tzu)]].
2. **Waging War (作戰).** The economics of war: protracted campaigns ruin the state ("there is no instance of a country having benefited from prolonged warfare"). Speed over perfection ("though we have heard of stupid haste in war, cleverness has never been seen associated with long delays"); **forage on the enemy** ("one cartload of the enemy's provisions is equivalent to twenty of one's own"). "Let your great object be victory, not lengthy campaigns."
3. **Attack by Stratagem (謀攻).** The most cited doctrine: "to take the enemy's country whole and intact… is better than to shatter and destroy it." "**Supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting.**" The hierarchy of generalship: **balk the enemy's plans** > prevent the junction of his forces > attack his army in the field > **besiege walled cities** (worst). Closes with the maxim: "**If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles.**" See [[Concepts/Winning Without Fighting (Sun Tzu)]], [[Concepts/Know the Enemy and Know Yourself (Sun Tzu)]].
4. **Tactical Dispositions (軍形).** Secure yourself against defeat first; the opportunity of defeating the enemy is provided by the enemy himself. "The good fighters of old first put themselves beyond the possibility of defeat, and then waited for an opportunity." The victorious strategist "**wins his battles by making no mistakes**" — winning where victory is already easy, so his wins bring "neither reputation for wisdom nor credit for courage."
5. **Energy (兵勢 / *shih*).** The combination of **direct (*cheng*) and indirect (*qi*) methods** — "in battle there are not more than two methods of attack… yet these two in combination give rise to an endless series of maneuvers." **Momentum/*shih*** (the onset of troops likened to "the rush of a torrent which will even roll stones along," the strike of a falcon); the good fighter looks to the *combined energy/situation*, not to individuals. See [[Concepts/Shih - Momentum, Formlessness, and Striking Weakness (Sun Tzu)]].
6. **Weak Points and Strong (虛實).** Impose your will: "the clever combatant imposes his will on the enemy, but does not allow the enemy's will to be imposed on him." Be **formless** ("be subtle, even to the point of formlessness… thus you may be the director of the opponent's fate"). "**Military tactics are like unto water**… avoid what is strong and strike at what is weak… Water shapes its course according to the nature of the ground; the soldier works out his victory in relation to the foe whom he is facing." Hence "there are no constant tactics" (formless adaptation).
7. **Maneuvering (軍爭).** The difficulty of turning the devious into the direct. The famous tempo verse: "**Let your rapidity be that of the wind, your compactness that of the forest; in raiding and plundering be like fire, in immovability like a mountain… Let your plans be dark and impenetrable as night, and when you move, fall like a thunderbolt.**" (The "wind/forest/fire/mountain" *fūrinkazan*.)
8. **Variation in Tactics (九變).** The general must adapt to the "five advantages"/contingencies; the **five dangerous faults of a general**: recklessness (→ destruction), cowardice (→ capture), a hasty temper (provoked by insult), a delicacy of honor (sensitive to shame), over-solicitude for his men (→ harassment).
9. **The Army on the March (行軍).** Practical reconnaissance: reading terrain and the **signs of the enemy** (dust, movements of trees, the behavior of birds and men) — an early manual of inference from observable signals.
10. **Terrain (地形).** Six kinds of ground and how to handle each; the general's responsibility. Restates the maxim with cosmic scope: "**If you know the enemy and know yourself, your victory will not stand in doubt; if you know Heaven and know Earth, you may make your victory complete.**" Regard your soldiers "as your children… [and they] will stand by you even unto death."
11. **The Nine Situations (九地).** Nine kinds of strategic ground (dispersive, facile, contentious, open, intersecting highways, serious, difficult, hemmed-in, desperate). Psychology of the army: "throw your soldiers into positions whence there is no escape, and they will prefer death to flight" ("place them in deadly peril, and they will then survive"). The general must be inscrutable, keeping his men "in ignorance of his plans."
12. **The Attack by Fire (火攻).** Five ways of attacking with fire; followed by a caution against acting from passion: "no ruler should put troops into the field merely to gratify his own spleen… Anger may in time change to gladness; vexation may be succeeded by content. But a kingdom that has once been destroyed can never again come into being." (The proto-prudential restraint on war.)
13. **The Use of Spies (用間).** **Foreknowledge** is what enables the wise sovereign to "strike and conquer, and achieve things beyond the reach of ordinary men" — and "**this foreknowledge cannot be elicited from spirits… it cannot be obtained inductively from experience, nor by any deductive calculation. Knowledge of the enemy's dispositions can only be obtained from other men.**" The **five classes of spies**: local, inward, converted, doomed, surviving — to be used together (the "divine manipulation of the threads"). The **converted spy** (the turned enemy agent) is the most valuable. See [[Concepts/Foreknowledge and the Use of Spies (Sun Tzu)]].

## Key Quotes (Giles)

- "All warfare is based on deception."
- "Supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting."
- "If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles. If you know yourself but not the enemy, for every victory gained you will also suffer a defeat. If you know neither the enemy nor yourself, you will succumb in every battle."
- "The good fighters of old first put themselves beyond the possibility of defeat, and then waited for an opportunity of defeating the enemy."
- "Military tactics are like unto water… avoid what is strong and strike at what is weak."
- "Let your rapidity be that of the wind… in immovability like a mountain… when you move, fall like a thunderbolt."
- "The leader of armies is the arbiter of the people's fate."
- "What enables the wise sovereign… to achieve things beyond the reach of ordinary men, is foreknowledge."

## Provenance & Cautions

- **Translation layer**: Giles (1910) is scholarly but Victorian; the etext is a paraphrase-heavy conversion (the converter, Bob Sutton, notes the loss of Chinese characters and "every paraphrase represents a loss"). For precise scholarship, compare Griffith (1963) or the Yinqueshan bamboo-text editions; treat exact wording as Giles-via-Sutton, not the Chinese.
- **Authorship/date** uncertain (possibly composite, Warring States). The historicity of the concubine anecdote is legendary.
- **Domain caution**: a manual of *war and deception*. In the vault it is read for its **transferable strategic logic** (competition, negotiation, planning, intelligence, self-knowledge) and for comparison with the ethics/just-war and power-politics threads — *not* as endorsement of its means. Sun Tzu himself frames war as a last resort, ruinous and not to be entered from passion (Chs. II, XII).

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **Sun Tzu vs. the vault's ethics/just-war cluster**: "all warfare is based on deception" and the instrumental use of spies sit against [[Concepts/Two Cities (Augustine)|Augustine]]'s [[Concepts/Tranquillitas Ordinis - Peace as Order (Augustine)|just-war / peace-as-order]] and Kant's [[Concepts/Categorical Imperative - Universal Law Formulation (Kant)|prohibition on deceit]] / [[Concepts/Formula of Humanity as End in Itself (Kant)|treating persons as mere means]]. Yet Sun Tzu's *preference for bloodless victory* and his restraint-from-passion (Ch. XII) partly converge with prudential and proportionality concerns. Flag the tension.
- **Sun Tzu vs. Thucydides**: both are realists about power and war, but Sun Tzu is *prescriptive* (how to win, ideally without fighting) where Thucydides is *descriptive/tragic* (how power corrupts and revolt follows) — see [[Concepts/Power, Empire, and Revolt (Thucydides)]].
- **"Foreknowledge cannot be obtained inductively from experience"** (Ch. XIII) is a striking near-inversion of the empiricist line ([[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]], [[Concepts/Knowledge by Acquaintance and Knowledge by Description (Russell)]]): Sun Tzu insists strategic knowledge of a specific adversary comes only from *human intelligence* (acquaintance/description of *this* enemy), not from general laws — an interesting convergence with Russell's "every proposition… composed of constituents with which we are acquainted."
- **Determinism of "winning first"** (Ch. IV) vs. the role of chance/momentum (Ch. V *shih*): Sun Tzu holds both — victory is *arranged* in advance by superior calculation/position, yet exploited through dynamic momentum.
