# Plato's Parmenides — Text, Translation & Introductory Essay (Hermann, 2010)

> Plato's *Parmenides* (c. 370–365 BCE), the most notoriously difficult of the dialogues. This edition (Parmenides Publishing, 2010) pairs the Loeb Greek text with a new English translation by **Arnold Hermann** and **Sylvana Chrysakopoulou**, a **Foreword** by Douglas Hedley, and a long **Introductory Essay** by Hermann. The dialogue has two unequal parts: **Part I (126a–137c)** stages the young **Socrates** defending the [[Concepts/Theory of Forms|Theory of Forms]] against the aged **Parmenides of Elea**, who raises a devastating battery of objections (the participation dilemma, the [[Concepts/The Third Man Argument|Third Man regress]], and the "Greatest Difficulty" that Forms would be unknowable to us). **Part II (137c–166c)** is an austere logical "gymnastic": **eight deductions plus a Coda** tracing the consequences of the hypotheses "if one is" and "if one is not" for the One and for the Others. See [[Concepts/Participation Problems and the Critique of the Forms (Plato Parmenides)]] and [[Concepts/The Eight Deductions - Hypotheses about the One (Plato Parmenides)]].

## Argument Spine

Plato puts his own signature doctrine on trial. In Part I, Parmenides shows that the Forms, as young Socrates naively states them, generate contradictions the moment one asks *how* sensible things "partake" of them — whole-or-part, an infinite regress of ever-higher Forms (the Third Man), and a radical **separation** (*chorismos*) so complete that Forms become unknowable to humans and human things unknowable to gods. Yet Parmenides insists the Forms cannot simply be abandoned: without stable intelligible objects "one will destroy the power of discourse" (135b–c). The problem is not the Forms but Socrates' untrained handling of them; the remedy is a rigorous method — examine, for any hypothesis, the consequences **both** "if it is" **and** "if it is not," for the subject in relation to itself and to the others. Part II performs this method on the hypothesis of the **One** itself, and the eight deductions display the pattern: the One taken *itself-by-itself* (absolute, without relation to Being) has **no** predicate at all — it is not even one, cannot be named or known (Argument I); but the One taken *as combined with Being* — a "Unit of Is" — admits **all** predicates and their opposites, and grounds plurality, number, and the whole ontological order (Argument II). Hermann's reading: Being is the "canvas," but **Oneness is the necessary limiter** — nothing can *be* without both. The dialogue is thus not a refutation of the Forms but a demonstration of the training needed to hold them coherently, and the historical seedbed of **negative theology** and the Neoplatonic **One beyond Being**.

## Part I — The Critique of the Forms (126a–137c)

The dialogue is narrated at several removes (a device stressing its distance and difficulty). Zeno reads his book arguing that "if things are many," they must be both like and unlike, which is impossible — a defense of Parmenides' monism by *reductio* of pluralism. Young Socrates counters with the **Theory of Forms**: there is a Form of Likeness itself and of Unlikeness itself; sensible things are both like and unlike merely by *participating* in both, which is unremarkable — the astonishing thing would be to show the **Forms themselves** suffering opposite qualifications.

Parmenides then dismantles the naive theory (Socrates conceding at each step):

- **Extent of the Forms** (130): Socrates confidently posits Forms for Likeness, One, Many, Just, Beautiful, Good — hesitates over Forms of Human, Fire, Water — recoils from Forms of Hair, Mud, Dirt. Parmenides warns this squeamishness is a symptom of youth.
- **The participation dilemma** (131): Does a thing partake of the **whole** Form or a **part**? If the whole, the one Form is present entire in many separate things and so "separate from itself"; if a part, the Form is divisible, and absurdities follow (a thing is *large* by a part of Largeness *smaller* than Largeness itself).
- **The Third Man regress** (132a–b): If a single Form of Largeness is posited over many large things, then Largeness-plus-the-large-things forms a new plurality requiring *another* Largeness, and so on without end — "each of your Forms will no longer be one but unlimited in multitude." See [[Concepts/The Third Man Argument]].
- **Forms as thoughts** (132b–c): Socrates' escape — a Form is just a thought in minds — fails: a thought is *of* something that is, so the Form returns as the object thought.
- **Paradigm / likeness regress** (132d–133a): Forms as *patterns* that things resemble also regresses — if the copy is like the pattern, the pattern is like the copy, requiring a further Form of their common likeness, endlessly.
- **The Greatest Difficulty** (133a–134e): If Forms are truly "themselves by themselves," their relations hold only *among themselves* (Mastery to Slavery, Knowledge to Truth), and things "amongst us" hold only among *themselves*. Then **Knowledge itself** knows only the Forms, so *we* cannot know the Forms; and a god possessing exact Knowledge could not know human affairs. Radical **separation** makes the Forms unknowable and useless — "something even more dreadful."

**The turn** (135–137): Despite all this, Parmenides insists that denying Forms "completely destroys the power of discourse" (135b–c). Socrates' fault is defining Forms "prematurely… before you are properly trained." The required **training** (the *gymnastic*): for any hypothesis, examine the consequences **both** if the thing *is* **and** if it *is not*, in relation to itself and to the others (135e–136c). Part II demonstrates the method on the One.

## Part II — The Eight Deductions on the One (137c–166c)

Parmenides, with young Aristoteles answering, runs the method on "the One." Hermann groups the results by two variables: whether the One **is** or **is not**, and whether it is taken **itself-by-itself** (absolute, no relation — qualifier "neither/nor") or **combined with Being** (a relational "Unit of Is" — qualifier "both"). See the full table in [[Concepts/The Eight Deductions - Hypotheses about the One (Plato Parmenides)]].

- **I — One as Simple** (137c–142a): If the One is *itself-by-itself*, it has **no** property whatever — no parts, shape, place, motion, rest, sameness, difference, likeness, time; it is not even *one*, cannot be named, known, or perceived. (Read by later Platonists as the **One beyond Being**.)
- **II — One as Complex / "Unit of Is"** (142b–155e): If the One *is* (partakes of Being), it is a whole of parts, generates number and unlimited multitude, and admits **all** opposite predicates in different respects — one and many, at rest and in motion, like and unlike, equal and unequal. This "weaving together" of One and Being crowns the ontological hierarchy.
- **Coda / The Instant** (155e–157b): The temporal machinery of change — coming-to-be and ceasing-to-be — requires **the instant** (*to exaiphnes*), a gateway "in no time at all" between motion and rest, into and out of which change passes.
- **III — Others as Complex** (157b–159b): The Others, partaking of the One, become limited unities; they are both limited and unlimited, like and unlike, etc.
- **IV — Others as Simple** (159b–160b): Absent participation in the One, the Others collapse into amorphous masses — neither one nor many, with no qualification at all.
- **V — "One that is not" as Complex** (160b–163b): A One that "is not" but still has reference to Being nonetheless has properties (difference, likeness, being *and* not-being).
- **VI — "One that is not" as Simple** (163b–164b): A One with no reference to Being at all "is not in any state whatsoever."
- **VII — Others without the One (Complex)** (164b–165e): Lacking a real One, the Others only *seem* to have limit, number, and unity — all "apparent," doxastic, deceptive.
- **VIII — Others without the One (Simple)** (165e–166c): Without the One "nothing is." Final line: whether one is or is not, "it and the others both are and are not, and both appear and do not appear to be all things in all ways" (166c).

Hermann's synthesis: **One and Being have a reciprocal relationship** — "Being may be the canvas the participants are painted upon, but Unity is the paint as well as the stroke. Without it, the canvas remains blank."

## Whole-Work Assessment

The *Parmenides* is the vault's pivot between Plato's confident middle-period metaphysics and the self-critical late dialogues. It supplies the **primary text** for objections previously catalogued only from Aristotle's side: the **Third Man** originates here, in Plato's *own* mouth (via Parmenides), decades before the *Metaphysics* — a fact that reframes [[Contradictions/Plato vs Aristotle on the Forms]] as beginning with Platonic self-scrutiny rather than external attack. It furnishes the [[Concepts/The Eight Deductions - Hypotheses about the One (Plato Parmenides)|eight deductions]] whose **first hypothesis** the Neoplatonists ([[Thinkers/Plotinus]], [[Thinkers/Proclus]]) read as disguised theology, extracting the **One beyond Being** and grounding Western **negative theology** — closing the interpretive loop already opened in [[Concepts/The One (To Hen)]] from Wallis's *Neoplatonism*. It connects tightly to the [[Sources/Sophist - Plato]] (the Eleatic "parricide" on Not-Being) and to [[Concepts/Parmenidean Being and the One (Parmenides)]]. Hermann's Unitarian-leaning reading — that Part II *solves* Part I's participation dilemma rather than abandoning the Forms — is filed here without endorsement; the standing tension it raises (does the gymnastic rescue the Forms or merely retire them?) is a live open question.
