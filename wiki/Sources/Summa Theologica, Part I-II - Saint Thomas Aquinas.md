# Summa Theologica, Part I-II - Saint Thomas Aquinas

> The *Prima Secundae* (First Part of the Second Part) of Thomas Aquinas' *Summa Theologica* (c. 1271–1272), translated by the Fathers of the English Dominican Province (Benziger/Gutenberg edition). After treating God and creation (Prima), Aquinas turns to man as the "image of God" endowed with free will and self-movement — the principle of his own actions. This part systematically treats the last end of human life (happiness/beatitude as God), human acts, passions, habits/virtues (acquired and infused), law (eternal, natural, human, divine), and grace. It is the moral/ethical heart of the *Summa*, synthesizing Aristotelian ethics (especially the *Nicomachean Ethics*) with Christian revelation, patristic sources, and natural theology. Central themes: happiness consists in the beatific vision of God; natural law as rational creature's participation in eternal law; grace perfects (does not destroy) nature.

**Source**: `raw/Summa Theologica, Part I-II - Saint Thomas Aquinas.txt` (Project Gutenberg eBook of the complete American edition; ~16,568 lines, structured as Treatises > Questions > Articles in the classic scholastic *quaestio* format: Objections, Sed Contra, Respondeo, Replies to Objections).

This is a major medieval extension of the vault's classical philosophy cluster (Plato, Aristotle, Stoics, Epicurus, Plotinus, Pyrrhonism), moving from pagan antiquity into scholastic synthesis. Augustine is the primary patristic authority (especially the *Confessions* as experiential ground for grace, the divided will, memory, interiority, and the restless heart that finds rest only in God). See new [[Sources/Confessions - Augustine]], [[Thinkers/Augustine]], and the three new Augustine concepts.

## Summary

The *Summa Theologica* is Aquinas' unfinished masterpiece of systematic theology, organized around the *exitus-reditus* (emanation and return) motif: all things proceed from God and return to Him. Part I-II focuses on the *reditus* for rational creatures: how man, created in God's image with intellect and will, attains his last end through free acts ordered to happiness.

**Structure of I-II (QQ. 1-114)**:
- **Treatise on the Last End (QQ. 1-5)**: Every human act is for an end; there is one last end (happiness/beatitude); it is not in created goods (wealth, honor, pleasure, power, etc.) but in the uncreated good, God; happiness in its essence (for man) is an operation of the speculative intellect — the contemplation/vision of the divine essence (beatific vision). In this life imperfect; perfect in the next.
- **Treatise on Human Acts (QQ. 6-21)**: Voluntary acts proceeding from deliberate will; circumstances, end, object specify morality; good/evil acts.
- **Treatise on the Passions (QQ. 22-48)**: Concupiscible and irascible passions (love, desire, joy, hatred, aversion, sorrow; hope, despair, fear, daring, anger); their morality when subject to reason.
- **Treatise on Habits (QQ. 49-89)**: Habits in general; virtues (cardinal and theological) and vices; gifts of the Holy Spirit; beatitudes and fruits.
- **Treatise on Law (QQ. 90-108)**: Essence of law (rule of reason ordered to common good, promulgated by competent authority); kinds: eternal law (God's governance), natural law (participation of eternal law in rational creatures — "the light of natural reason whereby we discern what is good and evil"), human law, divine law (Old and New). This account of natural law and the knowable moral order in creation presupposes real secondary causes and stable natures in things — the exact point debated in the newly ingested [[Sources/Incoherence of the Incoherence - Ibn Rushd]] (Averroes defends Aristotelian natural powers and causation against Ghazali’s occasionalism; see [[Concepts/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation (Ghazali and Ibn Rushd)]] and [[Concepts/Eternity of the World (Ibn Rushd)]]). Aquinas retains the causal realism while completing it theologically.
- **Treatise on Grace (QQ. 109-114)**: Necessity of grace for supernatural end; essence, division (sanctifying, gratuitous), cause, effects (justification, merit), and relation to free will ("grace does not destroy nature but perfects it").

The method is the scholastic *quaestio*: objections from authorities/reason, counter-authority, respondeo (Aquinas' synthesis drawing on Aristotle, Augustine, Scripture, etc.), replies.

## Key Claims and Extracts

**On the Last End (from Q. 1 and Q. 3)**:

- "Every agent, of necessity, acts for an end." (Final cause is first in intention.)
- It is proper to the rational nature (man) to act for an end knowingly and freely, directing himself via will/reason.
- Human acts are specified by their end (morality from the end/intention).
- There is one last end: "it is impossible for one man's will to be directed at the same time to diverse things, as last ends." Infinite regress or plurality contradicts the nature of the perfect good.
- All desire the last end under the aspect of good/happiness, though they disagree on *in what* it consists.
- "Happiness is man's supreme perfection... happiness must consist in man's last act" — an *operation*.
- Not sensitive operation (shared with animals); not in wealth, honor, fame, power, pleasure, or even created goods/virtue alone.
- "Man's last end is the uncreated good, namely, God, Who alone by His infinite goodness can perfectly satisfy man's will."
- In essence: "something created, existing in him" — the attainment/enjoyment of God.
- Specifically (Q. 3, Art. 8): happiness "consists in the sole contemplation of God seen in His Essence" (beatific vision). The intellect's operation uniting man to the Uncreated Good. In this life, imperfect participation; in heaven, perfect and continual.

Prologue: "it remains for us to treat of His image, i.e. man, inasmuch as he too is the principle of his actions, as having free-will and control of his actions."

**On Law (from Q. 90)**:

- "Law is a rule and measure of acts, whereby man is induced to act or is restrained from acting" (from *ligare*, to bind).
- Pertains to reason: "the rule and measure of human acts is the reason, which is the first principle of human acts."
- "Every law is ordained to the common good."
- Made by the whole people or a public personage who has care of the whole people (not private persons for their households — those are ordinances, not laws properly).
- Requires promulgation.
- Later (famous): Natural law is "the light of natural reason, whereby we discern what is good and what is evil" — rational creature's participation in the eternal law (God's providence/reason).

**On Grace**:

- Grace is necessary for man to attain his supernatural last end (vision of God), which exceeds natural powers.
- "Grace does not destroy nature but perfects it" (famous maxim, though the precise phrasing is often attributed to the tradition Aquinas embodies).
- Justification, merit, infusion of virtues, etc.

## Relevance to Vault and Cross-Links

**Extension of the classical philosophy domain into medieval scholasticism**:

- Direct synthesis with Aristotle (already in vault via Nicomachean Ethics Book I, Modal Logic, Rhetoric): Aquinas is the great commentator and completer of Aristotle for a Christian context. The function/ergon argument and eudaimonia are taken up and elevated — natural happiness possible in this life via acquired virtue, but ultimate/perfect happiness (beatitude) is supernatural, in God, requiring grace and the theological virtues (faith, hope, charity) which perfect the natural ones.
- **Eudaimonia vs. Beatitude**: Enriches [[Concepts/Eudaimonia]] (Aristotle: activity of soul according to virtue over complete life in the polis) with Aquinas' teleological completion: the last end is God; happiness is the vision of the divine essence. "We call men happy, but only as men" (imperfect in this life, per Aristotle); perfect happiness promised in heaven "as the angels."
- Ties to prior classical: Plato (Forms/Good as ultimate; philosopher's ascent), Stoics/Epictetus (virtue, role, equanimity — but completed by grace), Epicurus/Lucretius/Ataraxia (tranquility/peace as end — contrasted with beatific vision and infused peace), Pyrrhonism (suspension vs. Aquinas' confident natural theology and demonstration of God's existence/attributes from reason), Plotinus/Neoplatonism (emanation/return, ascent to the One — Aquinas Christianizes the *exitus-reditus* and contemplation).
- **Natural Law**: Major new concept. Eternal law (divine reason governing all) participated in by rational creatures as natural law (synderesis, first principles of practical reason: "good is to be done and pursued, and evil avoided"). Human law derives from it. Provides a bridge from classical natural right (Aristotle) to medieval/ modern ethics and jurisprudence. Relevant to CPCU/insurance (risk, justice, common good), sales playbook (ethical conduct, contracts as human law), personal/career (moral framework for "book of business," leadership).

**Grace perfects nature**:

- Key for vault's "Personal" use case and LLM Wiki pattern: nature (reason, classical philosophy, human effort, deliberate practice from MasterClass) is not abolished but elevated by grace (supernatural aid, theological virtues, the "examined life" completed in divine friendship).
- Contrasts pure naturalism (Epicurus) or duty-alone (Stoics) with a cooperative model (free will + grace).
