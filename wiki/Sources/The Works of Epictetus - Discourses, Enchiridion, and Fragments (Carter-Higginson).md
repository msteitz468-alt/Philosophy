# The Works of Epictetus — Discourses (Four Books), Enchiridion, and Fragments

> The complete extant works of **Epictetus** (c. AD 50–135), the freed-slave Stoic who taught at Nicopolis, as recorded by his student **Arrian**. This edition is the **Elizabeth Carter (1758)** translation as revised by **Thomas Wentworth Higginson** (Boston, 1865). Its bulk and philosophical core is the **Discourses (*Diatribai*) in Four Books** — verbatim classroom conversations, previously represented in the vault only by the compressed *Enchiridion*. The *Enchiridion* and *Fragments* here are already covered by [[Sources/Epictetus - Enchiridion, Fragments, and Golden Sayings]]; **this ingest focuses on the Discourses**, which supply the full argumentative machinery (prohairesis, the discipline of assent, the three *topoi*, freedom, the Cynic ideal, kinship with God) that the *Enchiridion* only summarizes.

## Argument Spine

The single question of the Discourses: **what is genuinely our own?** Answer — only our *prohairesis* (moral purpose / faculty of choice), the power to use impressions (*phantasiai*) rightly. Everything else (body, property, reputation, office, even our own limbs) is "not our own" and therefore indifferent. Philosophy is training (*askēsis*) in three fields (*topoi*): the discipline of **desire** (to want only what accords with nature), of **action/duty** (to act appropriately in our relationships and roles), and of **assent** (to give or withhold judgment correctly). Mastery of these yields *freedom* — the sage is free because nothing not-his-own can hinder his will — and this freedom is simultaneously piety, since it is glad alignment with Zeus/Nature's providential order and recognition of our **kinship with God**. The classroom tone is diatribe: Epictetus rebukes complacent students, mocks mere logic-chopping divorced from conduct, and insists philosophy is measured by how one lives, not what one can prove.

## Whole-Work Assessment

The Discourses convert the vault's Epictetus from a source of memorable maxims (the *Enchiridion*) into a fully-articulated ethical system. The single load-bearing addition is **[[Concepts/Prohairesis (Moral Purpose)|prohairesis]]**: the *Enchiridion*'s practical rule ("distinguish what is up to you") now rests on a claim about the self — the will is the only thing that is "ours," so good, evil, and freedom live nowhere else. Seven concept pages were created to carry the machinery the *Enchiridion* only gestures at: prohairesis, the discipline of assent / use of impressions, preconceptions (*prolēpseis*), kinship with God, the three fields of training, the Cynic ideal, and freedom (*eleutheria*).

Two structural consequences for the vault. **(1)** The Hadot/Holiday page [[Concepts/The Three Disciplines - Perception, Action, and Will (Hadot)]] now has its primary-text anchor in III.2 rather than resting on a modern popularization — the derivation runs Epictetus → Hadot → Holiday, and the vault can show all three links. **(2)** Epictetus is repositioned as the hinge of the vault's Stoic cluster: [[Thinkers/Marcus Aurelius]]'s *Meditations* are visibly Discourses-in-practice (the *hēgemonikon* is prohairesis internalized), and the freedom/"will what happens" doctrine (IV.1) is the direct ancestor of [[Concepts/Amor Fati (Love of Fate)]] — sharpening the standing contrast with [[Thinkers/Nietzsche]], who reads exactly this consent-to-fate as life-denial.

The work opens no new contradiction but deepens an existing one: prohairesis as a genuinely free will inside a providentially determined cosmos is the Stoic compatibilism at the heart of [[Contradictions/Stoic Determinism vs Aristotelian Contingency]], and Hegel's [[Concepts/Master-Slave Dialectic (Hegel)]] critique of "Stoic freedom in chains" now has its clearest target in IV.1. The *Enchiridion* and Fragments in this edition duplicate [[Sources/Epictetus - Enchiridion, Fragments, and Golden Sayings]] and were not re-ingested.
