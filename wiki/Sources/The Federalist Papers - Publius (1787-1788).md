# The Federalist Papers - Publius (1787-1788)

> "It has been frequently remarked that it seems to have been reserved to the people of this country, by their conduct and example, to decide the important question, whether societies of men are really capable or not of establishing good government from reflection and choice, or whether they are forever destined to depend for their political constitutions on accident and force."
> — Federalist No. 1 (Hamilton)

Collection of 85 essays written 1787–1788 by Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and John Jay under the collective pseudonym "Publius" (after the Roman consul Publius Valerius Publicola). Originally published serially in New York newspapers (Independent Journal, New-York Packet, Daily Advertiser) to promote ratification of the proposed U.S. Constitution in New York. Collected in book form (McLean edition) in 1788. This txt is the standard public-domain text of the essays. They remain the most authoritative contemporary exposition and defense of the Constitution's principles.

## Context and Purpose

The essays respond to the weaknesses of the Articles of Confederation (inefficient central government, inability to tax, regulate commerce, raise armies reliably, or resolve interstate disputes). They defend the new Constitution as a "more perfect Union" that is both federal (preserving states) and national (energetic general government). Addressed "To the People of the State of New York." Systematic: utility of union, defects of present system, necessity of energetic government, conformity to republican principles, additional securities for liberty/property, responses to specific objections (standing army, taxation, judiciary, etc.).

Authorship (approximate, per historical scholarship): Hamilton ~51, Madison ~29 (including the most famous on factions and separation of powers), Jay 5 (foreign affairs). All under "PUBLIUS."

## Part 1: The Importance of the Union (Nos. 1–14)

Nos. 1–5 (largely Jay/Hamilton): Union essential for safety against foreign force/influence, peace among states, common defense, commerce, respect abroad. Division into separate confederacies would invite European interference, internal wars, weakness. "A firm Union will be of the utmost moment to the peace and liberty of the States."

Nos. 6–8: Dangers of disunion — history shows republics torn by factions, ambition, commerce rivalries lead to war (Greece, Italy, modern Europe). Union provides barrier against domestic insurrection and foreign arms.

No. 9 (Hamilton): Utility of confederate republic (citing Montesquieu) for extending sphere of popular government while retaining internal advantages of small republics and external force of monarchy. "Enlargement of the orbit" helps control faction. Distinguishes confederacy (states retain separate organization) from consolidation.

No. 14 (Madison): Republic not limited to small territory (contra Montesquieu misapplications); representative principle + federal structure allows large extent. America already practices extended republican government successfully.

## Key Essay: No. 10 (Madison) — Factions

"Among the numerous advantages promised by a well-constructed Union, none deserves to be more accurately developed than its tendency to break and control the violence of faction."

**Definition**: "By a faction, I understand a number of citizens, whether amounting to a majority or a minority of the whole, who are united and actuated by some common impulse of passion, or of interest, adversed to the rights of other citizens, or to the permanent and aggregate interests of the community."

**Causes**: Latent in human nature — fallible reason + self-love; diversity of faculties (property); unequal distribution of property; zeal for opinions (religion, government); attachment to leaders. Most durable source: various and unequal distribution of property (landed, manufacturing, mercantile, moneyed, debtor/creditor interests).

**Cure**: Cannot remove causes without destroying liberty (essential to political life) or forcing uniformity of opinions/passions/interests (impracticable). Relief only in controlling *effects*.

- If minority faction: republican principle (majority vote) defeats it.
- If majority faction: pure democracy offers no cure (common passion felt by majority; no check). Republic (representation + larger sphere) does.

**Republic vs. Democracy**: Delegation to elected representatives refines public views (wisdom + patriotism filter temporary/particular interests). Larger number of citizens + extent of territory: greater variety of parties/interests makes it less probable a majority will have common motive to oppress, or harder for them to concert.

"The influence of factious leaders may kindle a flame within their particular States, but will be unable to spread a general conflagration through the other States. A religious sect may degenerate into a political faction in a part of the Confederacy; but the variety of sects dispersed over the entire face of it must secure the national councils against any danger from that source."

Conclusion: "In the extent and proper structure of the Union, therefore, we behold a republican remedy for the diseases most incident to republican government."

## Nos. 15–22: Insufficiency of the Present Confederation

Critique of Articles: no power to tax or regulate commerce directly; requisitions on states unreliable; no sanction for laws; no national judiciary for uniformity or enforcement; no standing military capacity without state consent; treaties unenforceable; no common treasury or credit. Leads to "imbecility" in government, injustice among states, vulnerability abroad. "Government implies the power of making laws... A mere treaty... is no more than a scrap of paper."

## Nos. 23–36: Necessity of an Energetic Government

The Constitution provides powers (tax, commerce, war, etc.) coextensive with objects of union. Energetic government not incompatible with republican liberty when properly structured (representation, separation, federalism). Response to "standing army" fears: union requires common defense; militia alone insufficient for large-scale or sustained war; Constitution limits (appropriations for army max 2 years).

## Key Essay: No. 51 (Madison) — Separation of Powers and Checks and Balances

"TO WHAT expedient, then, shall we finally resort, for maintaining in practice the necessary partition of power among the several departments, as laid down in the Constitution? The only answer... is, that as all these exterior provisions are found to be inadequate, the defect must be supplied, by so contriving the interior structure of the government as that its several constituent parts may, by their mutual relations, be the means of keeping each other in their proper places."

**Core principle**: "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition. The interest of the man must be connected with the constitutional rights of the place."

Each department should have will of its own, as little dependence as possible on others for appointment/emoluments. But "the great security... consists in giving to those who administer each department the necessary constitutional means and personal motives to resist encroachments of the others."

**Double security in compound republic**: Power first divided between two distinct governments (federal/state), then each subdivided among distinct departments. "Hence a double security arises to the rights of the people. The different governments will control each other, at the same time that each will be controlled by itself."

**Protecting minorities**: Not only guard society against rulers, but "one part of the society against the injustice of the other part." Different interests in different classes. Methods: independent will (hereditary) or multiplicity of interests/sects so majority combination improbable. Federal system + large extent provides the latter. "In a free government the security for civil rights must be the same as that for religious rights. It consists in the one case in the multiplicity of interests, and in the other in the multiplicity of sects."

## Nos. 52–66: The Legislative Branch (House and Senate)

Biennial elections for House (balance dependence on people with stability/experience). Qualifications, apportionment, compensation. Senate: longer term (6 years, staggered) for stability, "cool and deliberate" sense, protection of state sovereignty via equal representation, check on popular passions. Treaty power, appointments (advice/consent), impeachment.

## Nos. 67–77: The Executive

Vesting in single President for energy, accountability, secrecy/dispatch. Election (Electoral College as compromise), term (4 years, reeligible), powers (commander-in-chief, treaties with Senate, appointments with Senate, veto, etc.). "Energy in the Executive is a leading character in the definition of good government." Vigor not incompatible with republican safety when responsibility is secured (impeachment, elections, fixed salary not diminishable).

## Key Essay: No. 78 (Hamilton) — The Judiciary

"We proceed now to an examination of the judiciary department..."

**Least dangerous**: "The Executive... holds the sword... The legislature... commands the purse... The judiciary... has no influence over either the sword or the purse; no direction either of the strength or of the wealth of the society; and can take no active resolution whatever. It may truly be said to have neither FORCE nor WILL, but merely judgment..."

**Permanency in office (good behavior tenure)**: Essential barrier to despotism (monarchy) or legislative encroachments/oppression (republic). "The standard of good behavior for the continuance in office of the judicial magistracy, is certainly one of the most valuable of the modern improvements in the practice of government."

**Judicial review**: In a limited Constitution (with specified exceptions to legislative authority), courts must declare acts contrary to the "manifest tenor of the Constitution" void. "The interpretation of the laws is the proper and peculiar province of the courts. A constitution is, in fact, and must be regarded by the judges, as a fundamental law." Superiority of Constitution (will of the people) over statute (will of agents). Not superiority of judiciary over legislature, but of people over both.

Courts as "bulwarks of a limited Constitution against legislative encroachments" and against "ill humors" of temporary majorities injuring minority rights. Permanent tenure necessary for independence and to attract fit characters (voluminous law requires study; integrity + knowledge rare).

## Nos. 79–83: Judiciary continued

Fixed compensation not diminishable. Jurisdiction (cases arising under Constitution/laws/treaties, controversies involving US, between states, admiralty, etc.). Supreme Court appellate jurisdiction (law and fact, with exceptions). Trial by jury in criminal cases preserved; civil jury left to Congress/legislatures.

## Nos. 84–85: Concluding Remarks; Objections Answered

No bill of rights necessary (Constitution itself is one; enumeration might imply unlisted rights unprotected; state bills already exist; new government limited). "The Constitution itself... is a BILL OF RIGHTS." Supremacy clause, oath, etc. provide security. Miscellaneous objections (titles of nobility, ex post facto, etc.) answered. "A nation, without a national government, is, in my view, an awful spectacle."
