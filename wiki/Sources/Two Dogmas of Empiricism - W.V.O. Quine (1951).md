# Two Dogmas of Empiricism - W.V.O. Quine (1951)

> Modern empiricism has been conditioned in large part by two dogmas. One is a belief in some fundamental cleavage between truths which are analytic, or grounded in meanings independently of matters of fact and truths which are synthetic, or grounded in fact. The other dogma is reductionism: the belief that each meaningful statement is equivalent to some logical construct upon terms which refer to immediate experience. Both dogmas, I shall argue, are ill founded. One effect of abandoning them is... a blurring of the supposed boundary between speculative metaphysics and natural science. Another effect is a shift toward pragmatism. (Quine, opening of "Two Dogmas of Empiricism")

**Full title / edition**: "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" by Willard Van Orman Quine. Originally published in *The Philosophical Review* 60 (1951): 20-43. Reprinted in *From a Logical Point of View* (Harvard University Press, 1953; 2nd rev. ed. 1961) with alterations (esp. §§1,3-4,6). The ingested text includes 1951/1961 variants in parallel tables and endnotes distinguishing versions. ~600 lines of core essay + notes. Foundational critique of logical empiricism (Carnap/Vienna Circle) that helped launch post-positivist philosophy of science and naturalized epistemology.

## Summary

Quine dismantles the two central pillars of the logical-empiricist program: (1) the sharp **analytic/synthetic distinction** (truths of meaning vs. truths of fact), and (2) **reductionism** (the idea that individual statements can be confirmed or infirmed in isolation by reference to immediate experience). The attack proceeds by showing that the notions required to draw these distinctions — meaning, synonymy, analyticity, and statement-by-statement reduction — cannot be clarified without circular appeal to one another or without pragmatic/holistic considerations. The result is **confirmation holism** (the "web of belief"): our statements face the tribunal of experience not individually but as a corporate body (the whole of science). No statement is immune to revision; even logical laws can be revised for pragmatic reasons. Physical objects, numbers, classes, and even the gods of Homer are "cultural posits" or myths whose value lies in expediting our dealings with experience. Ontological questions are on a par with scientific hypotheses; the boundary between metaphysics and natural science blurs. The upshot is a thoroughgoing pragmatism and the program of naturalized epistemology.

## Key Claims / Positions

**The two dogmas and their interdependence**:
- Dogma 1: There is a fundamental cleavage between *analytic* statements (true by virtue of meanings, independently of fact) and *synthetic* statements (grounded in fact).
- Dogma 2 (reductionism): Each meaningful statement is equivalent to a logical construct upon terms referring to immediate experience (radical form) or at least has a unique range of confirming/disconfirming sensory events (attenuated form, still tied to verification theory of meaning).
- The dogmas are at root identical: both rest on the idea that the truth of a statement can be analyzed into a linguistic component and a factual component, with the limiting case (analytic) having a null factual component.

**Critique of meaning, synonymy, and analyticity (Sections I–IV)**:
- Meaning is not naming or reference. Classic examples (Frege's "Evening Star" = "Morning Star" but differ in meaning; Russell's "Scott" and "the author of Waverley"; "9" and "the number of planets") show that co-referential terms can differ in meaning; astronomical observation was needed, not reflection on meanings.
- For general terms/predicates: extension (class of things the term is true of) vs. intension/meaning. "Creature with a heart" and "creature with a kidney" may share extension but differ in meaning.
- Two classes of analytic statements: (1) logical truths ("No unmarried man is married") — true under all reinterpretations of non-logical components, given logical particles. (2) Statements reducible to logical truths by synonym substitution ("No bachelor is married" → by "bachelor" = "unmarried man").
- Definition does not explain synonymy: lexicographers report antecedent usage; explication improves on it while preserving favored contexts; only the extreme conventional introduction of new notation creates synonymy by fiat.
- Interchangeability *salva veritate* (Leibniz) is not a sufficient test for cognitive synonymy in an extensional language (it only guarantees truth of the biconditional, not meaning-based analyticity). In a language with "necessarily" it becomes circular (assumes we already understand analyticity to interpret the adverb).
- Carnap's state-descriptions (exhaustive truth-value assignments to atomic sentences) only reconstruct logical truth (first class), not the second class, because they assume no extralogical synonym-pairs. Thus they fail to capture the analyticity of interest to empiricists.
- Semantical rules fare no better: either they explicitly use the unexplained word "analytic," or they label certain truths as "true by semantical rule" (the label itself is arbitrary and does not illuminate the general notion "analytic for L").

**The verification theory and reductionism (Section V)**:
- Verification theory: the meaning of a statement is the method of its empirical confirmation or infirmation. An analytic statement is the limiting case confirmed no matter what.
- This yields an account of cognitive synonymy (statements synonymous iff same confirmation method) from which analyticity could be recovered.
- But radical reductionism (every meaningful statement translatable into sense-datum language, term-by-term or statement-by-statement) fails. Even Carnap's *Aufbau* (heroic attempt using modern logic) leaves key connectives ("is at") undefined and cannot actually translate physical-object statements into the initial sense-data + logic language. The "is at" connective remains an added undefined element; the "laziest world" canons are heuristics, not reductions.
- The dogma survives in attenuated form: each (synthetic) statement has its own unique range of confirming and infirming sensory events. This is intimately connected with the analytic/synthetic dogma.

**Empiricism without the dogmas (Section VI)**:
- Total science is a "man-made fabric which impinges on experience only along the edges" or "a field of force whose boundary conditions are experience." A conflict at the periphery occasions readjustments in the interior; truth values are redistributed across the web because of logical interconnections. There is much latitude; no particular experiences are linked to particular interior statements.
- It is misleading to speak of the empirical content of an *individual* statement (especially theoretical ones). The unit of empirical significance is the whole of science.
- Any statement can be held true come what may (by drastic adjustments elsewhere); conversely, no statement is immune to revision (even the law of excluded middle has been questioned for quantum mechanics). The distinction between statements held by convention and those held by observation of nature is one of degree, not kind.
- Physical objects are "irreducible posits" — convenient intermediaries, not definable in terms of experience — epistemologically on a par with the gods of Homer (both cultural posits; the physical-object myth has proved more efficacious for managing the flux of experience). Irrational numbers, forces, classes, and abstract mathematical entities are posits of the same kind.
- Ontological questions (e.g., "are there classes?") are on a par with scientific hypotheses: to countenance classes is to quantify over variables that take classes as values. Carnap's attempt to treat ontology as "choice of language form" while keeping scientific hypotheses factual relies on the very analytic/synthetic distinction being rejected.
- The result is a more thorough pragmatism: considerations guiding the warping of our scientific heritage to fit continuing sensory promptings are, where rational, pragmatic (conservatism, simplicity, etc.).

## Contributions and Integration with Existing Wiki Content

This is the single most important primary source for the vault's move beyond logical empiricism into post-positivist epistemology and philosophy of science. It directly attacks the background assumptions (analytic/synthetic + verificationist reductionism) that both the Vienna Circle/Carnap and (in different ways) the early Popper were grappling with. It supplies the "Duhem-Quine thesis" (holism) that later resonates with Kuhnian incommensurability and theory-ladenness.

**Agreements / extensions of existing pages**:
- Directly engages and sharpens [[Concepts/Hume's Fork (Relations of Ideas vs Matters of Fact)]]: Quine attacks the very sharpness and exhaustiveness of the fork that Hume (and later positivists) assumed. The fork is the classical ancestor of the analytic/synthetic dogma.
- Extends the critique in [[Concepts/Problem of Demarcation (Kant and Popper)]]: shows why verificationist criteria (Carnap's successive attempts) could not cleanly separate science from metaphysics without begging questions; metaphysics (as heuristic posits) is continuous with science.
- Strengthens [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]]: once holism is accepted, there is no clean "empirical content" for individual statements or even individual laws; the underdetermination is more radical than Hume's uniformity problem alone.
- Allies with Popper and Kuhn against the positivists: like them, Quine rejects the "given" and cumulative verification; unlike Popper he emphasizes the latitude in revision and the mythical status of all posits (including the objects of science); unlike early verificationism he treats metaphysics as potentially fertile rather than meaningless.
- Engages Russell heavily on meaning/reference (the Scott/Waverley and number examples are Russellian); Quine develops the critique of "meaning" as obscure intermediary while retaining a realist/pragmatist stance on truth and ontology (cf. Russell's correspondence and "cosmic impiety").
- Anticipates themes in Kuhn: meaning change across theory change (incommensurability has a Quinean root in the critique of synonymy and analyticity); theory-laden observation; science as a web rather than a pyramid of reductions.

**Contrasts / productive tensions** (flagged):
- Vs. Carnap / logical empiricism (the explicit target): the two dogmas were the tools used to demarcate meaningful science and to reduce it to experience. Quine shows the tools do not work; the boundary blurs and reduction fails.
- Vs. Popper on testing and holism: Popper wants severe attempted refutations of specific theories; Quine notes that any single contrary experience can be accommodated by adjustments anywhere in the web (auxiliaries, logic, ontology). This is a live tension (Duhem-Quine underdetermination vs. Popperian falsificationism) — noted on the relevant Popper pages and the holism concept.
- Vs. naive realism about posits: physical objects, classes, etc. are on the same footing as Homeric gods — useful myths. (Yet Quine, qua scientist, believes in the former and not the latter; the difference is pragmatic efficacy.)
- With pragmatism (James): Quine ends in a "shift toward pragmatism," but his is a holist, naturalist, realist-leaning pragmatism (truth still constrained by the web's edge with experience; "cosmic impiety" warning from Russell still applies in modified form).

**Vault / AI / personal relevance**:
- The wiki itself is a Quinean web: claims are interconnected; a new raw source (recalcitrant experience) forces redistribution of "truth values" across many pages; no page is immune; revisions are guided by conservatism (preserve existing structure where possible) + simplicity + explanatory power. The schema is a posit that organizes the "experience" of sources.
- Confirmation is holistic: a single source rarely "verifies" or "falsifies" an isolated claim; it is integrated (or resisted) by adjustments across the graph.
- AI/epistemics: neural nets and LLMs are extreme holistic webs; "meaning" is not local definitions but distributed patterns of activation/successful prediction. The critique of reductionism warns against over-hoping for clean, statement-by-statement grounding of model outputs in "ground truth."
- Career/risk: underwriting or strategy "theories" (playbooks, models) are webs; a single bad loss does not falsify the whole framework but prompts distributed re-evaluation. Posits (market assumptions, causal models) should be held pragmatically and revised when they cease to expedite prediction and control.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Quine vs. Popper on the unit of testing and the possibility of localized refutation (holism undercuts the idea of a clean *modus tollens* against a specific theory). Productive tension rather than outright contradiction; Lakatos-style research programmes (not yet in vault) attempt mediation.
- The status of logic and mathematics in the web: Quine allows that even they can be revised (for simplicity), yet in practice they are central and highly protected. How central is "central"?
- Naturalized epistemology: once we abandon the dream of a first philosophy that justifies science from outside, epistemology becomes a chapter of psychology / cognitive science. This is only sketched here; the later essay "Epistemology Naturalized" (1969) develops it. (Future source.)
- Whether the "shift toward pragmatism" fully escapes the problems it diagnoses or merely relocates the normative questions (which adjustments are rational?).
