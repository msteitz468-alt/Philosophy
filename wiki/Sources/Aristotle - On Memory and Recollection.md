# Aristotle - On Memory and Recollection

> Third of the *Parva Naturalia* (*De Memoria et Reminiscentia*, 449b). Memory (*mneme*) is a state grounded in a past-tense presentation — a "picture" or seal-impression left by perception, contemplated *as a likeness* of something absent. Recollection (*anamnesis*) is a distinct, higher achievement: an active, quasi-inferential search through associated "movements," found only in animals capable of deliberation. Founds the classical theory of **associationism** (similarity, contrast, contiguity) later systematized by Hume and the British empiricists.

## Argument Spine

Memory cannot be of the future (mere expectation) or of the present (mere perception); it is essentially of the **past**, and therefore essentially involves the perception of time-elapsed — so only animals that perceive time can remember, and the organ of time-perception (the common sense) is the organ of memory. Memory is grounded in a presentation (*phantasma*) functioning as a "picture": a seal-like impression stamped in the soul by the original perception, which persists after the perceived thing is gone. The puzzle this raises — how contemplating a present impression can count as remembering an absent thing — is solved by the picture/likeness distinction: the very same mnemonic image can be regarded either "in itself" (a mere thought) or "as a likeness of X" (a mnemonic token), and it is the latter mode of regard that constitutes remembering. Recollection is then analyzed as a distinct capacity: not the "recovery" of memory as such but an active search through a network of prior "movements" connected by natural or customary succession, triggered by starting from something similar, contrary, or contiguous to the target, and terminating when the sought movement is reached — "a sort of inference," hence found in man alone among the animals known to Aristotle.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Memory is neither perception nor conception (intellection)** but "a state or affection of one of these, conditioned by lapse of time"; belongs directly to the **primary faculty of sense-perception** (which perceives time), and only incidentally to intellect — hence many non-human animals also have memory.
- **The wax/seal analogy**: perception stamps an impression like a seal on wax; poor memory in the very young (soft, "running water," no impression takes) and the very old (hard, "frayed old walls," impression can't be made) explains age-related memory deficits, as does excessive emotional agitation.
- **The picture/likeness solution** to the puzzle of remembering absent things: a mnemonic presentation, like a painted portrait, is one thing considered two ways — as an object in itself, or as a likeness of its original; regarding it *as* a likeness is what makes the state memory rather than bare thought. Misapplication of this relation (treating a non-likeness as a likeness) explains pathological false-memory (the case of Antipheron of Oreus).
- **Recollection ≠ memory**: good memory and quick recollection are found in different types of people ("slow people have a good memory... the quick-witted are better at recollecting"); recollection presupposes memory but adds an active search.
- **Associative principle of recollection**: search proceeds by moving from a starting-point through movements linked by **similarity, contrast (opposition), or contiguity** — "from milk to white, from white to mist, thence to moist... to Autumn." Ordered material (like geometrical demonstrations) is recollected easily; disordered material is not. Frequently-repeated sequences become as fixed as nature ("custom now assumes the role of Nature").
- **Time-cognition is proportional/analogical**, akin to how magnitude is cognized — genuine remembering requires the "movement" corresponding to the fact and the "movement" corresponding to its temporal position to concur; mismatch produces the mistaken belief that one remembers.
- **Recollection is exclusive to deliberative animals**: "recollection is, as it were, a mode of inference," and only animals capable of deliberation (i.e., man) can investigate in this searching way — sharply distinguishing memory (widely shared) from recollection (uniquely rational).
- Recollection is **corporeal** (a "searching for an image in a corporeal substrate"): failed recollective effort produces physical discomfort (especially in melancholics), and the process, once triggered, runs its course involuntarily — "as those who throw a stone cannot stop it at will."
