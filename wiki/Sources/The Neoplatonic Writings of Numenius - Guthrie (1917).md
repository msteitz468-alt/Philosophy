# The Neoplatonic Writings of Numenius

> The extant fragments and testimonia of Numenius of Apamea (fl. c. 150 AD), the Neopythagorean-Middle Platonist whose three-god theology and matter-as-evil dualism directly shaped [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] — collected and translated by Kenneth Guthrie (1917), with facing Greek/Latin.

## What This Source Is

Numenius left no complete work. What survives is ~70 numbered fragments and testimonia quoted by later authors — chiefly **Eusebius** (*Praeparatio Evangelica*, who preserves the long philosophical fragments of *On the Good* and the whole polemic *On the Divergence of the Academy from Plato*), **Calcidius** (Latin, the dualist matter-doctrine in his *Timaeus* commentary), **Porphyry**, **Proclus**, **Nemesius**, **Origen**, and **Macrobius**. Guthrie gathers them under Numenius's own (largely lost) treatises. This edition prints Guthrie's English against the Greek/Latin but **omits his commentary**; it is a primary-text collection, not an interpretive study — for the modern scholarly frame see [[Sources/Neoplatonism - R. T. Wallis (1972)]].

## Argument Spine

There is no single argument — it is a corpus — but a coherent system recurs across the fragments:
1. **True Being is incorporeal, timeless, and intelligible** (against Stoic corporealism and against locating being in the four elements or in matter). Being neither arose nor perishes; the present-moment "aeon" is its only time.
2. **A graded divine triad**: a First God (the Good, pure uncreating Intellect, "the alone with the alone"), a Second God (the Demiurge/Creator, "double," who orders matter while gazing on it), and a Third God (the created cosmos itself).
3. **Matter is a positive, coeval, malevolent principle** — an unlimited, evil "Indefinite Dyad"; the world is a mixture of the Good (Idea) and the badness of Matter; hence Plato's *two* world-souls, one beneficent, one malign.
4. **The soul is strictly incorporeal and immortal**, wholly one with its divine source, imprisoned in body by desire for matter; two souls (rational/irrational), not two parts.
5. **Method**: recover the true Plato by reading him *through Pythagoras* and cross-checking against the "wise nations" (Brahmins, Hebrews, Magi, Egyptians) allegorically — "What is Plato but Moses speaking Attic Greek?"

## Distinctive Doctrines (what Numenius adds to the vault)

- **The three-god hierarchy** is the clearest pre-Plotinian ancestor of the [[Concepts/The One (To Hen)|One–Intellect–Soul]] hypostases — but with crucial differences: Numenius's First God is *Intellect* (not beyond intellect), the Second is internally "double," and the Third is the *world itself*, not the World-Soul.
- **Matter as absolute evil**, coeval with God — a Gnostic/dualist strain that [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] explicitly *rejected* (matter as privation, not a rival power). See [[Concepts/Matter as a Malevolent Principle (Numenius)]].
- **"All is in all, but in each thing appropriately to its nature"** — the correspondence axiom later transmitted through [[Thinkers/Iamblichus]] and Proclus.
- **Radical syncretism**: Plato = Moses in Greek; truth is one across the wise nations, recoverable allegorically. A key Middle-Platonic bridge between Greek philosophy and Judaism/oriental religion.

## Whole-Work Assessment

For the vault this source **fills the single largest gap in the Neoplatonic genealogy**: it supplies the named Middle-Platonic/Neopythagorean predecessor whom the wiki already referenced (in [[Schools/Neoplatonism]], [[Schools/Neopythagoreanism]], [[Thinkers/Iamblichus]], and the Wallis summary) but never gave a page. It concretizes how the [[Concepts/The One (To Hen)|One]]/[[Concepts/Emanation (Proodos)|emanation]] scheme was assembled out of the *Timaeus* Demiurge, the Pythagorean Monad/Dyad, and *Parmenides* theology *before* Plotinus. It also opens a real doctrinal tension inside Neoplatonism — **matter-as-evil (Numenius) vs matter-as-privation (Plotinus)** — flagged on the relevant pages. Finally, its Academy-polemic gives the vault a hostile Middle-Platonic verdict on the [[Thinkers/Sextus Empiricus|skeptical]] Academy (Arcesilaus, Carneades) as a *betrayal* of Plato.
