# Symposium - Plato

> **The Symposium** (c. 385–370 BCE). By Plato. Translated by Benjamin Jowett. A literary and philosophical masterpiece taking place at a banquet hosted by the dramatist Agathon to celebrate his tragedy victory. The guests, including Socrates, Aristophanes, and Alcibiades, agree to deliver eulogies in praise of **Eros** (Love). The dialogue transitions from physical, social, and mythological views of love to Socrates' metaphysical revelation of the **Ladder of Love**, which ascends from physical attraction to the intellectual contemplation of the **Form of Beauty**.

---

## Speeches on Eros

1. **Phaedrus**: Love is the oldest of the gods and inspires courage and virtue in lovers, making them ashamed to act basely in front of each other.
2. **Pausanias**: Distinguishes between **Common Love** (vulgar attraction to bodies) and **Heavenly Love** (noble attraction to the intellect, character, and virtue).
3. **Eryximachus** (a physician): Generalizes love as a physical force of attraction and harmony in medicine, music, agriculture, and the cosmos (balancing bodily humors).
4. **Aristophanes**: Tells the famous **split-selves myth**: humans were originally double-beings (with eight limbs, four arms/legs, and two faces) who were split in half by Zeus as punishment for hubris. Love is our lifelong pursuit of our "other half" to restore our original wholeness.
5. **Agathon**: Praises Love as young, beautiful, tender, virtuous, and the source of all peace, poetry, and arts.
6. **Socrates**: Refutes Agathon by arguing that Love is not beautiful or good, but rather the *desire* for what is beautiful and good. Since we only desire what we lack, Love itself lacks beauty and goodness. 
   - Socrates recounts what he was taught by the priestess **Diotima of Mantinea**: Love is a great daemon (intermediary) between gods and humans, born of Poverty (*Penia*) and Resource (*Poros*). Love is the desire for the perpetual possession of the good, expressed as "pregnancy in body and soul" and the desire to reproduce in beauty.

---

## Diotima's Ladder of Love (The Ascent)

Socrates outlines the path of initiation into the mystery of Love:
1. **A Single Beautiful Body**: Attracted to physical beauty in one person.
2. **All Beautiful Bodies**: Realizing that beauty in one body is akin to beauty in another; loving physical beauty in general.
3. **Beauty of the Soul**: Valuing beauty of mind and character above physical beauty.
4. **Beauty of Institutions and Laws**: Loving the beauty in social structures, customs, and laws.
5. **Beauty of the Sciences/Knowledge**: Loving the beauty of intellectual and scientific discovery.
6. **The Form of Beauty (Auton to Kalon)**: The sudden revelation of Beauty itself—eternal, pure, unmixed, absolute, and the source of all lesser beauties. See [[Concepts/Ladder of Love and Form of Beauty (Plato Symposium)]].

## The Entrance of Alcibiades
- Alcibiades arrives drunk and delivers a passionate speech praising Socrates rather than Eros. 
- He compares Socrates to the statues of Silenus (rough on the outside, but holding golden images of the gods within). He praises Socrates' absolute self-control, intellectual brilliance, physical endurance in war, and refusal to be seduced, highlighting Socrates as the living embodiment of the philosopher who has ascended Diotima's ladder.

---

## Integration and Vault Value

- **The Ascent to the Forms**: Provides the most vivid, phenomenological description of how a human can ascend from the sensible world to the contemplation of the Forms.
- **Contrast with Materialism**: Directly contrasts Lucretius's materialist deflation of love as a distracting bodily simulacrum in [[Thinkers/Lucretius]] and [[Sources/On the Nature of Things - Lucretius (Books II-III)]].
- **Lovejoy's Plenitude**: Fuses with the *Timaeus* in Arthur O. Lovejoy's analysis of the history of the "Great Chain of Being" ([[Concepts/Great Chain of Being (Lovejoy)]]) as the origin of the dual Platonic view of God: the self-sufficient transcendent Good (Symposium) vs. the generative Demiurge (Timaeus).
