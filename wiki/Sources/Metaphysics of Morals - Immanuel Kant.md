# Metaphysics of Morals - Immanuel Kant

> Nothing can be held to be good without limitation, excepting only a good will. [...] Duty is the necessity of an action from respect for the law. [...] I ought never to conduct myself except so that I could also will that my maxim become a universal law.

This source is an edition of Kant's *Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals* (1785; Grundlegung zur Metaphysik der Sitten), presented in the "Rethinking the Western Tradition" series (Yale University Press, ed. and trans. Allen W. Wood, with essays by J. B. Schneewind, Marcia Baron, Shelly Kagan, and Allen W. Wood). The volume includes a substantial Editor's Preface, A Note on the Translation, the Kant text (Preface + three sections), and four scholarly essays exploring its significance, interpretations, and relation to Kant's later ethical works (Critique of Practical Reason 1788, Metaphysics of Morals 1797).

**Full *Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals* (1785) ingested across three chunks (Editor's apparatus + Kant Preface + all three Sections + summaries of the four appended essays by Schneewind, Baron, Kagan, Wood). Note: the file volume title is *Metaphysics of Morals* but centers on the 1785 preparatory *Groundwork*; the later 1797 *Metaphysics of Morals* (Doctrine of Right + Doctrine of Virtue) is a separate, later work.**

## Edition and Context (from Editor's Preface and Note on Translation)

This is a scholarly edition aimed at presenting Kant's foundational text with accuracy and literalness to allow readers the same interpretive position as German readers of the original. Translator Allen W. Wood prioritizes fidelity to Kant's wording, sentence structure, and terminology over smoothness, preserving ambiguities where present to provoke philosophical reflection. The edition includes Kant's own footnotes and editorial notes on variants (1785 vs 1786).

The Editor's Preface situates the *Groundwork* as Kant's first major work on moral philosophy's foundations, written after the *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781/1787) but before the full development in the *Critique of Practical Reason* and *Metaphysics of Morals*. Kant saw it as laying the ground for a pure (a priori) metaphysics of morals, distinct from empirical anthropology or popular moral philosophy based on happiness or inclination. The *Groundwork* was influential but Kant later clarified and sometimes revised aspects (e.g., in the Third Section on freedom). The volume includes essays that debate its relation to Kant's later works, common interpretations (e.g., of moral motivation, freedom), and its enduring influence on deontological ethics, political philosophy, and religion.

Kant treats the *Groundwork* as successful in its aim (search for and establishment of the supreme principle of morality) but not a complete system. It is "nothing more than the search for and establishment of the supreme principle of morality."

## Kant's Preface

Ancient Greek philosophy divided into physics (nature), ethics (freedom/morals), and logic. Material philosophy is either physics or ethics. Formal is logic.

Philosophy is either empirical (based on experience) or pure (a priori principles). Pure formal is logic; pure material is metaphysics (of nature or of morals).

A metaphysics of morals is necessary: moral laws have absolute necessity and must be grounded a priori in pure reason, not in human nature, happiness, or empirical conditions (otherwise they are mere practical rules, not moral laws). Common idea of duty and moral laws requires this purity; otherwise morals are subject to corruption.

Popular moral philosophy mixes empirical with rational; a true metaphysics of morals must be cleansed of everything empirical to see what pure reason can achieve.

Kant issues this *Groundwork* in advance of a full *Metaphysics of Morals*. It is not yet a critique of pure practical reason (that came later), but preparatory: search for the supreme principle of morality. The method is analytic (from common cognition to the principle) then synthetic (back to common use).

Division:
- First Section: Transition from common rational moral cognition to philosophical moral cognition.
- Second Section: Transition from popular moral philosophy to the metaphysics of morals.
- Third Section: Final step from the metaphysics of morals to the critique of pure practical reason.

## First Section: Transition from Common Rational Moral Cognition to Philosophical Moral Cognition

There is nothing good without limitation except a **good will**. Talents of mind (understanding, wit, judgment), qualities of temperament (courage, resolution), gifts of fortune (power, wealth, honor, health, happiness) are good only if directed by a good will; otherwise they can be evil or harmful. Without good will, they lack inner worth.

The good will is good not through its effects or attainment of ends, but through its willing alone—good in itself. Even if it accomplishes nothing due to fate or nature (but has summoned all means in its control), it "would shine like a jewel for itself, as something that has its full worth in itself." Utility or fruitlessness neither adds nor subtracts from this worth.

Nature's purpose in giving reason and will to a being cannot be happiness (instinct would guide more surely and safely to that end). Reason's true vocation is to produce a will good in itself, which is the highest good and condition for all else (including happiness). A good will may not achieve happiness but has a contentment of its own kind.

To develop the concept of good will (as already in healthy common understanding), consider **duty**, which contains the good will under limitations and hindrances that make it shine brighter by contrast.

Actions contrary to duty are set aside. Actions in conformity with duty but done from inclination (e.g., prudent merchant not overcharging from self-interest, not duty; or sympathetic person helping from natural gratification, not duty) lack true moral worth—the maxim has no moral content.

Examples of actions from duty (when inclination is absent or opposed):
- Preserving one's life from duty when adversities have extinguished the taste for life (not from inclination or fear).
- Beneficence from duty when one's mind is clouded by grief and no sympathy remains (tearing oneself from deadly insensibility).
- Securing one's own happiness from duty (when lack of contentment tempts violation of duties; general inclination to happiness often conflicts with specific ones, but duty requires promoting it from principle, not inclination).

**Propositions**:
1. An action has moral worth only if done from duty (inferred from contrast with inclination).
2. The moral worth lies not in the aim/effect expected, but in the maxim (principle of volition) in accordance with which the action is resolved upon—independent of the actuality of the object.
3. Duty is the necessity of an action from respect for the law. Respect is for the law itself (not objects or inclinations); it is the consciousness of the will's subjection to the law without mediation of other influences. The law is objective; respect is its effect on the subject.

Since every material principle (from inclination) has been withdrawn, the only remaining principle is the universal lawfulness of the action in general: "I ought never to conduct myself except so that I could also will that my maxim become a universal law."

**Example**: In embarrassment, may I make a false promise (intending not to keep it)? Prudence might sometimes favor it, but duty does not. Test: Could I will my maxim ("make an untruthful promise when in embarrassment I cannot otherwise escape") as a universal law? No—such a law would destroy the possibility of promises (no one would believe them). The maxim destroys itself when universalized. Thus, one cannot will it as universal law; it is contrary to duty.

Common human reason has this principle always before its eyes (though not abstractly formulated) and uses it as standard of judgment. It can distinguish good/evil, duty/contrary to duty without philosophy (as Socrates showed by making people aware of their own principle). Philosophy is useful for system and clarity, but not to remove common understanding from its simplicity.

The section establishes that the supreme principle is formal (lawfulness/universalizability), a priori, recognized by common reason, and the ground of the good will's moral worth.

## Second Section: Transition from Popular Moral Philosophy to the Metaphysics of Morals

Kant criticizes "popular moral philosophy" (the dominant Enlightenment approach of his day) for mixing empirical observations, examples, and inclinations with rational elements. Such approaches can never yield pure moral laws with absolute necessity; they produce only contingent rules and prepare the ground for those who deny genuine morality by reducing everything to refined self-love. Experience can never securely show a single case of action from pure duty (covert self-love always possible); examples themselves presuppose prior moral principles to be judged worthy. Moral concepts must originate a priori in pure practical reason and be valid for all rational beings (not merely humans under contingent conditions).

All ideas from the two fountains (sensation/reflection) or custom/sentiment are set aside for the foundations. The will is practical reason: the faculty of acting according to the representation of laws/principles. For imperfect wills (subject to inclinations that may conflict with objective necessity), objective practical principles appear as necessitation = imperatives.

**Hypothetical vs Categorical Imperatives**:
- Hypothetical: represent the practical necessity of an action as *means* to some other end the agent does (or might) will. Conditional. 
  - Problematic/technical (rules of skill/art): end merely possible ("whoever wills the end wills the indispensable means"). Analytic.
  - Assertoric/pragmatic (counsels of prudence): end is happiness (presupposed for all dependent rational beings). But happiness is an indeterminate empirical ideal (no certain, universal means; requires omniscience); these are advisings, not strict commands.
- Categorical: represents the action as objectively necessary *in itself*, without reference to any further end. Unconditional, apodictic practical principle/law. Concerns only the *form* (universal lawfulness) and principle of volition, not matter or consequences. "The categorical imperative... is valid as an apodictically practical principle."

Only the categorical can be a true practical *law* (unconditionally necessary, binding every rational will independently of inclination). All hypothetical are principles of a will good in *some* way (skilled, prudent) but not unconditionally good.

**Derivation of the Categorical Imperative (and system of formulas)**: From the mere concept of a categorical imperative (law + necessity of maxim according with it, with no condition from any object/inclination), the content is known directly: "Act only in accordance with that maxim through which you can at the same time will that it become a universal law." (Or: Act in accordance with maxims that can at the same time have themselves as universal laws of nature.) This is the single categorical imperative; all duties (if any) must be derivable from it. Common reason already uses it (we recognize we make exceptions for ourselves that we cannot will universally). The formula is formal and a priori.

Further formulas (equivalent expressions of the one principle of the absolutely good will):
- **Formula of Humanity (FH)**: "Act so that you use humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, always at the same time as an end, never merely as a means." Ground: rational nature exists as end in itself (absolute worth, not conditioned by inclinations). Persons (rational beings) are objective ends limiting all arbitrary choice; things (non-rational) are relative means. Examples applied: suicide uses self merely as means to escape; lying promise uses other merely as means without the other containing the end.
- **Kingdom (Realm) of Ends**: Every rational being, as end in itself, must regard itself as universally legislative. This yields the idea of a *mundus intelligibilis* — a systematic union ("realm") of rational beings as ends through their own universal legislation (each taking maxims from its own point of view and simultaneously from every other's as co-legislator). "Every rational being must act as if it were through its maxims always a legislative member in a universal realm of ends." Analogy to realm of nature (under external causal laws) but under self-imposed rules. The command remains categorical even if others do not comply ("as if").

**Autonomy vs Heteronomy**: Autonomy of the will (being a law to itself via the suitability of maxims for universal legislation) is the sole principle of morals and the ground of dignity. Heteronomy (law from any external object, inclination, or "alien" representation) yields only conditioned/hypothetical imperatives and cannot ground morality. All prior systems (happiness/pleasure, moral feeling/sympathetic participation, ontological perfection, theological/divine will, etc.) are diagnosed as heteronomous and therefore insufficient. "Wherever an object of the will has to be taken as the ground... the rule is nothing but heteronomy."

The section is analytic (explicates the concept of morality and shows the CI is attached to it). Establishing the synthetic a priori reality of the moral law (how a categorical imperative is possible) requires moving to a critique of pure practical reason (previewed for Third Section).

## Key Insights from Full Groundwork (Chunks 1–2)

- The *Groundwork* seeks the supreme principle of morality in pure reason (a priori), independent of empirical motives, happiness, or consequences. Moral laws have absolute necessity and bind all rational beings.
- Good will is the only unconditional good; its worth is in the principle of volition (from duty/respect for law), not effects.
- The test for duty: universalizability of the maxim (first formulation of the Categorical Imperative). Hypothetical imperatives (if you will the end, will the means) are conditional on inclination; the moral law is categorical (unconditional).
- Full system of formulas (Second Section): Universal Law / Law of Nature; Humanity as End in Itself; Autonomy; Kingdom of Ends — all expressions of the one CI for the absolutely good will.
- Autonomy (self-legislation under universal law) is the supreme principle; all heteronomous principles (empirical or "rational" but material) fail. Common rational moral cognition already contains the principle in practice; philosophy clarifies and secures it against corruption from mixing with empirical elements.
- Editor's apparatus emphasizes that the *Groundwork* is foundational but not Kant's final or complete statement (cf. later works); interpretations should consider the full development. The volume's essays debate motivation, freedom, relation to consequentialism, and what makes ethics distinctively Kantian.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

The *Groundwork* itself notes it is preparatory; Kant's later works (especially *Critique of Practical Reason* and *Metaphysics of Morals*) clarify, develop, or adjust aspects (e.g., role of feeling/reverence/"fact of reason", freedom and its theoretical vs practical status, application to politics/religion/education, full system of duties). Common interpretations (e.g., rigorism, emptiness of the law, motivation solely by duty without inclination, the "as if" status of the intelligible world, relation of formulas) are debated in the volume's essays and scholarship. The Third Section's treatment of freedom leaves the full deduction to the critique of practical reason.

No internal contradictions in the *Groundwork*. Productive tensions with vault sources (as above) enrich the ethics cluster (e.g., duty/autonomy vs eudaimonia/ataraxia/sentiment/experience/conatus/natural law; a priori law vs custom; universal legislation vs deterministic or habituated order). The essays in the volume (summarized below in closing) add further layers without resolving all scholarly disputes.

## Third Section: Transition from the Metaphysics of Morals to the Critique of Pure Practical Reason

The concept of freedom is the key to autonomy of the will. Freedom (quality of causality by which it is effective independently of alien causes) is not lawless; it must be causality according to unchangeable laws of a particular kind (otherwise impossible). Negative freedom (independence of alien causes = not natural necessity/heteronomy of efficient causes) flows into positive: autonomy (law to itself). "A free will and a will under moral laws are the same."

If freedom presupposed, morality + its principle follows from analysis of the concept. But "an absolutely good will is that whose maxim can always contain itself considered as universal law" is *synthetic* a priori; requires a "third" (positive freedom) not found in world of sense. Points to a priori idea.

Freedom must be presupposed for *all* rational beings (morality valid for rational beings as such). Every being that cannot act otherwise than under the idea of freedom is practically free ("just as if" its will were free in itself). We must lend the idea of freedom to every rational being with a will (reason cannot receive steering from elsewhere and still be *its* reason).

**Two standpoints**: Representations given without choice (sense) yield objects only as they affect us (appearances); things in themselves unknown. Distinction passive vs active representations yields world of sense (appearances, varies by sensibility) vs world of understanding (ground, same). Human being knows self via inner sense only as appearance (affected); necessarily assumes ground (I in itself) behind. Counts self in perception/sensibility as in world of sense; in pure activity/reason (immediate, not affection) as in intellectual/intelligible world.

Rational being (intelligence) has two standpoints for laws of its powers/actions: (1) as belonging to world of sense, under natural laws (heteronomy); (2) as belonging to intelligible world, under laws independent of nature, grounded in reason (autonomy). As member of intelligible, cannot think its causality (will) otherwise than under idea of freedom. With freedom, autonomy + universal principle of morality inseparably bound.

The moral 'ought' is the authentic self's (intelligible) necessary volition, experienced as 'ought' only because we also belong to sensible world (inclinations pull). Laws of intelligible apply immediately/categorically to authentic self. "I must regard the laws of the world of understanding for myself as imperatives and the actions that accord with this principle as duties."

Categorical imperatives possible because idea of freedom makes me member of intelligible world; if alone, actions would accord with autonomy; since also sensible, they *ought* to. The 'ought' is synthetic a priori (adding idea of pure will to sensible will).

**Interest and boundary**: Interest in moral law (moral feeling) is *effect* of law on will, not its ground (would be heteronomous). "How pure reason can for itself be practical" (mere principle of universal validity of maxims without material incentive yields incentive/interest) is incomprehensible to human reason; we comprehend only the incomprehensibility. Supreme boundary of moral inquiry: do not look in sensible world for supreme motivation (harmful to morality), nor beat wings in empty transcendent space among figments. Idea of pure world of understanding (whole of intelligences, to which we belong as rational beings, yet also members of sense) is usable/permissible for rational faith; effects lively interest in moral law via splendid ideal of universal realm of ends in themselves.

**Concluding remark**: Speculative reason (nature) leads to absolute necessity of supreme cause; practical (freedom) to absolute necessity of laws of actions of rational being. Reason seeks the unconditioned but can gain no insight into necessity unless grounded on a condition. We do not comprehend the practical unconditioned necessity of the moral imperative, but we do comprehend its incomprehensibility — all that can be fairly required of philosophy striving up to the boundary of human reason.

## The Four Essays (Yale Edition)

**1. J. B. Schneewind, "Why Study Kant’s Ethics?"**: Groundwork worth study for (1) dramatically new vision of morality (autonomy of the will as practical rationality giving itself the law; each rational being equally legislator and subject in moral community; resolves voluntarist servility and intellectualist elitism; simple formula available to all); (2) profound influence on 19th/20th c. moral philosophy and culture; (3) positions still alive in contemporary debates (renewed scholarship shows depth; Kantian views central). Historical revolution: ancient eudaimonia (virtue + happiness inseparable, including good relations); Christianity (imposed law, God as superior or voluntarist tyrant; intellectualist eternal axioms or perfection vs voluntarist arbitrary will; Hume naturalism/sentiment, no special dignity). Kant: morality human creation from rational will; we and God fellow legislators; equal, not servile; autonomy gives dignity without requiring superior minds.

**2. Marcia Baron, "Acting from Duty"**: Defends Kant against Schiller/misreadings. Kant holds moral worth only if from duty (not merely in conformity from inclination); examples chosen to *isolate* the motive (no other incentive available, so must be duty). Does *not* hold that inclination precludes acting from duty or moral worth (difficulty of discernment if inclination present shows compatibility; duty = what fully rational would do, not external imposition or gritting teeth). Acting from duty with inclination is possible and better than without; but the *motive* of duty has special worth (purity, not despising inclination or requiring it be absent). Addresses why "better": not that we should despise friends or help with repugnance; duty tied to rationality, not social expectations; helps clarify when motivation is quintessentially moral (under pressure, no other incentive). Does not flatten all to gritted-teeth duty; inclination can coexist.

**3. Shelly Kagan, "Kantianism for Consequentialists"**: Offers account of Kantian foundations (esp. from Groundwork) that consequentialists may find congenial/usable, while primarily expository for why Kantianism worth taking seriously. Begins with freedom/rationality: theoretical (evaluate/revise beliefs per standards, alter accordingly); practical (evaluate/revise desires/goals/intentions/acts per standards; step back from and reject those that don't make sense — animals intelligent but not rational in this self-evaluative, revisable sense). Rationality = freedom from mere puppeteering by desires. Autonomy and FUL as starting point; argues foundations plausible independently of whether they support deontology or consequentialism (FUL may permit lying when best results, etc.; investigation needed case-by-case; does not trivially entail constraints or options). Encourages consequentialists to engage Kantian foundations directly rather than dismiss.

**4. Allen W. Wood, "What Is Kantian Ethics?"**: Kant treats moral deliberation as species/paradigm of rational deliberation (about ends, not only means; against dogma that practical reasoning is always grounded on desire — some desires we have only because we have independent reasons). Fundamental value: worth/dignity of rational beings as ends in themselves (not isolated individuals but in community of mutual respect). Three features: (1) categorical (not hypothetical) — FUL/FLN (universal validity of maxims as law, form of pure practical reason); (2) taking account of others' standpoint as objective value equal to own — FH (humanity as end in itself); (3) motivates via conception of who we are + incentives of self-worth — FA/FRE (autonomy; realm of ends as ideal community united by freely/rationality chosen common ends on terms respecting each autonomy). Critiques consequentialism/utilitarianism for misunderstanding the nature of human community (treats as if best guided by single prudential reasoner/benevolent despot aggregating will-less subjects; flattens "process values" like promise-keeping/rights into utilities to be weighed; ignores that value of agents + certain communities has first claim on structuring deliberation). Kantian community: rational agreement of distinct/equal persons freely uniting ends on autonomy-respecting terms. Overriding value of rational beings whose rational choice is final authority over what is worth pursuing.
