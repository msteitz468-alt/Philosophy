# The Social Contract - Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1762)

> A landmark work of modern political philosophy, outlining a theory of legitimate political authority based on popular sovereignty and the collective General Will.

## Summary

In *The Social Contract* (*Du contrat social*), [[Thinkers/Jean-Jacques Rousseau|Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] addresses the fundamental problem of political legitimacy: how can individuals unite into a political society without sacrificing their natural freedom? 

Rousseau's answer is the **social contract**, in which each individual alienates all their rights to the community as a whole. By doing so, the citizens form a unified sovereign body that acts in accordance with the **General Will**. Because each citizen is an equal member of the sovereign, obeying the law is simply obeying oneself, thereby preserving individual freedom in a moral and civil form.

---

## Book Structure and Key Arguments

### Book I: Foundations of the Contract
* **The Problem**: *"Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains."* Rousseau seeks to find what can make these chains legitimate.
* **Critique of Force**: Might does not make right. Obedience to force is a matter of necessity, not duty. Legitimate authority must rest on covenants.
* **The Social Covenant**: The pact is formulated as: *"Each of us puts his person and all his power in common under the supreme direction of the general will, and, in our corporate capacity, we receive each member as an indivisible part of the whole."*
* **The Sovereign**: The collective body of citizens. It cannot have interests contrary to its members.
* **Natural vs. Civil Liberty**: In the state of nature, humans have *natural liberty* (unbounded, limited by individual strength). Through the contract, they gain *civil liberty* (limited by the General Will) and *moral liberty* (self-legislation), which elevates humans from beasts to rational beings.

### Book II: Sovereignty and Law
* **Sovereignty is Inalienable and Indivisible**: It cannot be represented by politicians or split into separate executive/legislative branches.
* **The General Will**: The shared interest of the public. Rousseau distinguishes it from the "will of all" (which is merely the sum of private interests).
* **The Legislator**: A rare, extraordinary figure (like Lycurgus or Solon) who designs the fundamental laws of a nation to shape the people's customs and make them capable of self-governance, without holding executive office.

### Book III: Government
* **The Distinction**: Rousseau makes a sharp distinction between:
  1. **The Sovereign**: The legislative body (the citizens), which makes the laws.
  2. **The Government (Prince)**: The executive body (magistrates), which executes the laws.
* **Forms of Government**: Government can take the form of Democracy (suited for small, poor states), Aristocracy (suited for medium, moderate states), or Monarchy (suited for large, wealthy states). Rousseau famously remarks that a true democracy is fit only for "a people of gods," as it is too prone to civil strife.

### Book IV: Civil Religion and Assemblies
* **Citizens' Assemblies**: The citizens must meet regularly in person to vote on laws.
* **Civil Religion**: To secure citizens' loyalty to the state, Rousseau proposes a public "civil religion" with simple dogmas: belief in a benevolent deity, the afterlife, the sanctity of the social contract, and the rejection of religious intolerance.

## Key Tensions / Contradictions

> [!WARNING]
> Rousseau's insistence that citizens can be "forced to be free" by conforming to the General Will stands in sharp tension with John Stuart Mill's defense of individual liberty and the Harm Principle. See [[Concepts/General Will (Rousseau)]] and [[Concepts/Harm Principle (Mill)]].
> Rousseau's peaceful conception of the natural human state stands in direct contrast to Hobbes' war of all against all. See [[Contradictions/Rousseau vs. Hobbes on the State of Nature|Rousseau vs. Hobbes on the State of Nature]].
