# Letters and Sayings of Epicurus - Epicurus

> A 2005 Barnes & Noble edition compilation (translation, introduction, and notes by Odysseus Makridis) of the surviving core texts of Epicurus (341–271 BCE): Letter to Herodotus (physics, epistemology, cosmology), Letter to Pythocles (meteorology and celestial phenomena), Letter to Menoeceus (ethics and how to live well), Principal Doctrines (40 key maxims), Letter to Idomeneus, and short fragments/testimonia. Epicurus revives and refines ancient atomism (Leucippus/Democritus) into a complete materialist, empiricist system aimed at liberating humanity from superstition, fear of gods/death, and unnecessary desires, promising peace of mind (ataraxia) through prudent pleasure, simple living, friendship, and rational understanding of nature.

**Source**: `raw/Letters and Sayings of Epicurus - Epicurus.txt` (full text file of the compilation, including detailed introduction on Epicurus' life, significance, and system; originally published ca. 305 BCE, this edition 2005).

## Summary

Epicurus, an Athenian who founded his school in the Garden in Athens (306 BCE), taught a philosophy of enlightened hedonism grounded in atomist physics and empiricist epistemology. The goal is a happy life of tranquility (absence of mental disturbance/ataraxia and bodily pain/aponia) achieved by understanding the natural world (no need for gods' intervention or fear of afterlife), limiting desires to natural and necessary ones, cultivating friendship, and living "unnoticed" (lathe biosas). His system is anti-teleological, anti-elitist, and contractarian in social theory. Critics (ancient and modern) often caricatured it as vulgar hedonism or atheism, but the texts emphasize prudence, dignity, and mental peace over excess.

The compilation includes Epicurus' own letters (intended as epitomes for memorization) and key sayings, plus modern scholarly introduction and notes.

## Key Sections from the Texts

**Letter to Herodotus** (physics and epistemology):
- Atoms and void as the fundamental realities: infinite atoms of finite types (size, shape, mass), moving in infinite space (the void).
- Worlds are infinite in number, formed and dissolved by accidental collisions of atoms; our world is one among many, not eternal or unique.
- Empiricist epistemology: senses are the criterion of truth; "verificationist" approach — claims must be confirmed by sensation or "clear view"; error comes from adding opinions to sensations.
- Soul is corporeal (fine atoms), mortal (dispersed at death); "death is nothing to us" because when we exist, death does not, and when death exists, we do not. No afterlife torments to fear.
- Swerve (clinamen) of atoms: slight deviation from straight downward fall introduces indeterminacy, allowing for free will (against strict determinism) and enabling atomic collisions for world formation.
- Gods exist (fine atomic beings) but live in perfect bliss, uninvolved in human affairs (intervening would disturb their tranquility; to attribute malice or care to them is impious).

**Letter to Pythocles** (celestial phenomena and methodology):
- Appropriate method for studying meteorology/celestials: multiple possible explanations consistent with phenomena; reject only those contradicted by evidence. Goal is peace of mind, not dogmatic certainty.
- Explains rainbows, lightning, earthquakes, comets, etc., without divine causation or superstition. Celestial bodies (sun, moon, stars) are not divine but natural; their motions and appearances explained materially.
- Warning against method: do not force single explanations or appeal to myth when empirical analogies suffice.

**Letter to Menoeceus** (ethics — how to live well):
- Study philosophy at every age; it is never too early or late for the health of the soul.
- The gods exist (blessed and immortal) but do not meddle in human lives.
- Death is nothing: "when we are, death is not; when death is, we are not."
- Pleasure is the beginning and end of the blessed life, but not vulgar excess: distinguish natural/necessary pleasures (simple food, shelter, friendship) from vain/unnecessary ones. Prudence is the greatest virtue — it teaches what pleasures to pursue/avoid.
- "Live unnoticed" (lathe biosas): avoid public ambition and the turmoil it brings.
- Fate and chance: some things happen by necessity, some by chance, some by our own agency. The wise person attributes responsibility correctly and achieves freedom from fear.
- Principal goal: a life of sober reasoning, free from superstition, enjoying the present with gratitude and without excessive desire for future or fear of death.

**Principal Doctrines** (40 key maxims):
- Condensed ethical and physical principles, e.g.:
  - "A happy and blessed life is impossible without prudence, honor, and justice."
  - "Of the things that wisdom provides for the blessedness of life as a whole, by far the greatest is the possession of friendship."
  - "The just man is most free from trouble; the unjust man is full of the greatest trouble."
  - Physics maxims on atoms, void, multiple worlds, mortality of soul.
  - "Death is nothing to us; for that which is dissolved is without sensation, and that which lacks sensation is nothing to us."

**Fragments and Testimonia**:
- Additional sayings on philosophy's value, sense perception, nature/atoms, soul, gods (exist but aloof), ethics (society as contract for mutual benefit/security, not natural sociability like Aristotle), "pass your life making sure no one notices you."

**Introduction/Context**:
- Epicurus as materialist empiricist opposing Platonic/Aristotelian teleology and popular superstition. Revived atomism (with swerve innovation for free will). Garden as community emphasizing friendship, simple living, mutual aid. Historical influence on Newton (revived void/atoms), Hobbes/Locke (contractarianism), Jefferson, etc. Defended against charges of hedonism, atheism, elitism — his system is accessible, anti-elitist, aimed at universal peace of mind.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **None major within the source**: The system is presented as consistent (physics grounds epistemology grounds ethics). Critics' charges (vulgar hedonism, atheism, secret elitism) are addressed in the introduction as misunderstandings; the texts themselves emphasize prudence, friendship, and tranquility over excess.
- **With prior vault sources**: Contrasts with Platonic teleology/idealism (blind atoms vs. demiurge/Forms), Aristotelian virtue-as-activity (pleasure as natural good vs. eudaimonia through excellence), Stoic active duty/control (withdrawal/"unnoticed" vs. engagement in role, though both seek equanimity). Swerve for free will vs. Stoic acceptance or Platonic necessity. Future syntheses could compare Epicurean contractarianism to sales "alliances," atomism to modern physics/AI emergence, or ataraxia to mindfulness/sleep science in the MasterClass collection.
- **Open**: Limited surviving texts (most lost); reliance on Lucretius and later testimonia for full picture. The "swerve" is ingenious but ad hoc for free will. Ethical hedonism risks the naturalistic fallacy (as noted in intro). How does "live unnoticed" reconcile with Epicurus' own influential school/Garden community?
