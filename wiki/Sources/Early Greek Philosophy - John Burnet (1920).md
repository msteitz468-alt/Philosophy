# Early Greek Philosophy - John Burnet (1920)

> **Early Greek Philosophy** (3rd edition, 1920). By John Burnet (1863–1928). The classic and still-standard scholarly reference for the fragments and doxography of the Pre-Socratic thinkers. Burnet provides English translations of the surviving fragments alongside exhaustive philological and historical commentary. He argues that Greek philosophy (science/cosmology) was an independent, rational invention of the Ionian and mainland Greek colonies, free from mythological dogmatism and eastern theological influence, aimed at identifying the primary substance (*arche*) of the cosmos.

## Core Thesis: The Birth of Science and Cosmology

Burnet's primary contention is that **philosophy and science were born together in Greece** as a purely rational attempt to understand the natural world (cosmology) without appealing to mythological personification. 

- **Rejection of Eastern Origin**: Burnet rejects the contemporary theory that Greek philosophy was merely borrowed from Egypt or Babylon. While Greeks borrowed practical geometry (Egypt) and astronomical observations (Babylon), they transformed these into a systematic, deductive, and theoretical science.
- **The Search for the *Arche***: The early thinkers sought to identify the primary substance (*arche*)—the underlying, indestructible material that persists through all changes. 
- **The Problem of Change**: The history of Pre-Socratic thought is driven by a logical dialectic:
  1. *The Milesians* identify a single primary material and assume it changes.
  2. *Parmenides* argues logically that if Being is, it cannot change (since change requires non-being, which is impossible).
  3. *The Pluralists* (Empedocles, Anaxagoras) and *Atomists* (Democritus) attempt to rescue the appearance of change by proposing that while the fundamental building blocks of reality (elements/atoms) are changeless and eternal, their combination and separation create the illusion of generation and decay.

## Chapter Summary

### Introduction
- The secular character of early Greek thought. 
- Definition of *Physis* (nature) as the primary substance.
- The shift from theogonies (Hesiod) to cosmologies.

### Chapter I: The Milesian School
- **Thales of Miletus**: Predicted the solar eclipse of 585 BC. Declared **water** to be the *arche*.
- **Anaximander of Miletus**: Introduced the **Apeiron** (the Boundless/Infinite) as the arche. Postulated that the earth floats unsupported in space due to equilibrium. First to draw a map of the world.
- **Anaximenes of Miletus**: Identified **air** (*aer*) as the arche. Explained all physical variety through the mechanical processes of **condensation** and **rarefaction**.

### Chapter II: Science and Religion
- **Pythagoras of Samos**: Founded a religious-philosophical society in Croton. Introduced the doctrine of the **metempsychosis** (transmigration of souls) and the purification of the soul through science and music. Held that **numbers** are the ultimate reality/essence of all things.

### Chapter III: Herakleitos of Ephesos (Heraclitus)
- The philosopher of change: **all is flux** (*panta rhei*), symbolized by **fire**. 
- The **Logos**: The rational pattern or law governing all transformation.
- The **unity of opposites**: Strife and tension are necessary for harmony.

### Chapter IV: Parmenides of Elea
- The founder of the Eleatic school. Wrote a poem in two parts: *The Way of Truth* and *The Way of Belief*.
- **Strict Monism**: Being is; Non-Being is not. What is not cannot be conceived or spoken of. 
- Since change and motion would require empty space (Non-Being) or generation (from nothing), they are logical contradictions and therefore illusions of the senses.

### Chapter V: Empedokles of Akragas
- Pluralist reconciliation. Postulated **four eternal roots** (fire, air, water, earth) instead of one arche.
- Change is explained by the mechanical mixing and separating of these roots under the influence of two cosmic forces: **Love** (unifying force) and **Strife** (separating force).

### Chapter VI: Anaxagoras of Klazomenai
- Brought philosophy to Athens.
- Postulated that matter is infinitely divisible: **"portions of everything are in everything."**
- **Nous** (Cosmic Mind): A separate, pure substance that initiates the physical vortex (*perichoresis*) that separates the elements and forms the cosmos.

### Chapter VIII: Zeno of Elea (Young of the Eleatic School)
- Student of Parmenides. Devised **dialectical paradoxes** (Achilles and the Tortoise, the Arrow, the Stadium, the Dichotomy) to show that the hypotheses of multiplicity and motion lead to infinite regress and self-contradiction, thereby defending Parmenides' monism.

### Chapter IX: Leucippus and Democritus (The Atomists)
- **Atomism**: The ultimate synthesis. The universe consists of **atoms** (indivisible, solid particles of varying shapes) and **the void** (empty space / Non-Being).
- Reconciled Eleatic logic with common sense by asserting that Non-Being (the void) exists as a physical reality.
- All change is simply the collision, interlocking, and separation of atoms moving deterministically through the void.

## Integration and Vault Value

This text serves as the primary historical anchor for the vault's **Philosophy of Science** and **History of Ideas** legs:
- **Critical Tradition**: Directly supports Karl Popper's view in [[Sources/Conjectures and Refutations - Karl Popper]] that the Milesians invented the tradition of critical rationalism (conjecturing bold ideas and inviting pupils to critique them).
- **The Roots of Metaphysics**: Connects directly to Plato's *Parmenides* (which critiques the Forms using Eleatic arguments) and *Timaeus* (which adapts Pythagorean geometry and Empedoclean elements for its cosmology).
- **Materialist Counter-thread**: Roots the atomism of Epicurus and Lucretius ([[Thinkers/Epicurus]], [[Thinkers/Lucretius]], [[Sources/Letters and Sayings of Epicurus - Epicurus]]) in the primary formulations of Leucippus and Democritus.
