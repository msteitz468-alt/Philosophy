# The Communist Manifesto - Marx and Engels (1848)

> **Manifesto of the Communist Party** (*Manifest der Kommunistischen Partei*). Commissioned by the Communist League and written by Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels in London just before the revolutions of 1848, this pamphlet is the most influential political document in modern history, presenting a sweeping materialist theory of historical change, a critique of capitalist relations, and a call to global proletarian revolution.

## Core Thesis: History as Class Struggle

Marx and Engels argue that the history of all human society is the history of **Class Struggle**. Under modern capitalism, society has split into two polarized, mutually antagonistic classes: the **Bourgeoisie** (who own the means of production) and the **Proletariat** (wage-laborers who must sell their labor power to survive). The manifesto asserts that capitalism is inherently unstable, prone to crises of overproduction, and will inevitably be overthrown by the organized proletariat, leading to a classless, communist society.

## Section Summary

### Chapter 1: Bourgeois and Proletarians
-   **The Rise of Capitalism**: Traces the transition from feudalism to modern capitalism, driven by navigation, colonization, and steam-powered industry.
-   **The Revolutionary Role of the Bourgeoisie**: The bourgeoisie has dissolved feudal ties and replaced them with naked self-interest and "callous cash payment." It has globalized markets and rapidly developed the forces of production.
-   **The Grave-Diggers of Capitalism**: In developing these productive forces, the bourgeoisie inevitably produces its own "grave-diggers": the proletariat. Machine labor dehumanizes and exploits the worker, reducing wages and driving workers to organize. The struggle moves from isolated local strikes to a unified national class struggle.

### Chapter 2: Proletarians and Communists
-   **The Communist Program**: Communists represent the interest of the proletariat as a whole. The immediate aim is the formation of the proletariat into a class, the overthrow of bourgeois supremacy, and the conquest of political power.
-   **Abolition of Bourgeois Property**: Rebuts charges that communism seeks to destroy personal property. Marx explains that they seek to abolish *bourgeois private property*—private ownership of the means of production, which is based on the exploitation of labor.
-   **Immediate Reforms**: Lists ten measures to be implemented upon the proletarian conquest of power, including:
    1.  Abolition of private property in land.
    2.  A heavy progressive income tax.
    3.  Abolition of all rights of inheritance.
    4.  Centralization of credit, communication, and transport in the hands of the state.
    5.  Free public education for all children.

### Chapter 3: Socialist and Communist Literature
-   **Critique of Rivals**: Classifies and critiques other socialist movements:
    -   *Reactionary Socialism*: Feudal or petty-bourgeois thinkers who want to return to pre-industrial relations.
    -   *Conservative/Bourgeois Socialism*: Philanthropists who want to reform the abuses of capitalism to preserve bourgeois society without the revolutionary proletariat.
    -   *Critical-Utopian Socialism*: Thinkers (like Saint-Simon, Fourier, Owen) who draft blueprints for ideal societies but fail to recognize class struggle and the material forces of history.

### Chapter 4: Position of the Communists in Relation to the Various Existing Opposition Parties
-   **Revolutionary Alliance**: Declares that communists fight for the immediate interests of the working class but always represent the future of the movement. They support every revolutionary movement against the existing social and political order.
-   **The Call to Arms**: Concludes with the famous rallying cry: "The proletarians have nothing to lose but their chains. They have a world to win. Working men of all countries, unite!"
