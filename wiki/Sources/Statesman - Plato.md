# Statesman - Plato

> **The Statesman** (c. 360 BCE). By Plato. Translated by Benjamin Jowett. A late, highly analytical dialogue featuring the **Eleatic Stranger** and Young Socrates (with Socrates and Theodorus listening). The dialogue seeks to define the **statesman** (*politikos*) and isolate the true nature of political science. It utilizes the **Method of Division**, introduces the **Myth of the Cosmic Cycles** (the age of Cronus vs. the age of Zeus) to correct a premature definition, and delivers Plato's mature, realistic political theory: that the **Rule of Law** is a "second-best" necessity because the ideal ruler (the wise statesman with the royal art) is rarely found.

---

## Defining the Statesman and the Shepherd Analogy

- The interlocutors start by defining the statesman using the Method of Division, classifying him as a **shepherd of the human flock** (a provider of collective nurture for two-legged, featherless herds).
- The Eleatic Stranger rejects this definition as too broad: unlike a literal shepherd (who is of a different species than his sheep and provides all their food, shelter, and medical care), the statesman is of the same species as his subjects. Merchants, doctors, and farmers all compete with the statesman to be the "nurturer" of humans.
- The statesman must be defined not as a nurturer, but as a **weaver** who coordinates the different skills and temperaments of the citizens.

---

## The Myth of the Cosmic Cycles (The Reversal of Time)

To correct the "shepherd" error, the Eleatic Stranger recounts a major cosmic myth:
- **The Age of Cronus**: The god himself guides the rotation of the cosmos. During this era, time runs backward: humans are born from the earth as old men and grow progressively younger, food grows spontaneously without agriculture, and the god acts as a direct shepherd to humanity. There are no states, laws, or families.
- **The Age of Zeus**: The god releases his control, and the cosmos, being physical, begins to rotate in the opposite direction under its own momentum. Time runs forward: humans must reproduce, build shelters, cultivate crops, and govern themselves. 
- **The Error**: The shepherd definition was only true during the Age of Cronus, when a god ruled directly. In the current Age of Zeus, humans must govern other humans. The statesman is a director, not a divine shepherd.

---

## The Royal Art of Statesmanship and the Rule of Law

The Eleatic Stranger outlines Plato's mature political philosophy:
- **The Royal Art (*Basilike Techne*)**: True government is a science/art, not a matter of wealth, numbers, or consent. The true statesman is a wise ruler who possesses this royal art. 
- **Above the Law**: Just as a doctor is permitted to change his prescription for the patient's health, the wise statesman should be permitted to act **above the written law**, adjusting his decrees to the unique circumstances of each citizen. Written laws are too rigid and cannot capture the complexity of human life.
- **The Reality of Law (The Second-Best)**: However, because a perfectly wise statesman is rarely found, cities must rely on the **Rule of Law** as a necessary safeguard. 
  - If a city has no wise ruler, an unyielding, strict adherence to written laws is the only way to prevent absolute tyranny and corruption.
  - Written laws are the accumulated, tested wisdom of past generations, and violating them without the royal art leads directly to disaster.

---

## The Statesman as the Weaver
- The final definition is established: the statesman is a **weaver** (*politike symploke*). 
- His royal art is to weave the warp and weft of the city, bringing together the two opposing human temperaments: the **courageous/aggressive** and the **moderate/gentle**. Through education and marriage laws, the statesman weaves these temperaments into a unified, harmonious state, preventing the brave from dragging the city into war and the moderate from rendering it helpless.

## Integration and Vault Value

- **Evolution of Plato's Politics**: Represents the transitional step between the radical, utopian philosopher-kings of the *Republic* ([[Sources/The Republic - Plato (Cambridge, 2000)]]) and the highly detailed, practical legislation of the *Laws* ([[Sources/Classical Philosophy - Stoics, Additional Plato Dialogues, and Notes 2026]]).
- **Downstream Influence**: The classification of the six regimes (Monarchy/Aristocracy/Constitutional Democracy vs. Tyranny/Oligarchy/Extreme Democracy) directly influenced Aristotle's *Politics*.
