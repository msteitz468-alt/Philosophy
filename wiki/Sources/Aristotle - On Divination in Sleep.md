# Aristotle - On Divination in Sleep

> Fourth and shortest of the *Parva Naturalia* (*De Divinatione per Somnum*, 462b). Neither dismisses nor endorses prophetic dreams wholesale. Rejects the idea that dreams are sent by a god (they occur in "inferior" persons and even in lower animals) and rejects Democritus's theory of "images" and "emanations." Instead offers a fully naturalistic account: most apparently prophetic dreams are **coincidence**; some are **causes** or **tokens** of bodily and future events because sleep's quiet amplifies faint internal "beginnings" that are otherwise swamped by waking activity.

## Argument Spine

Aristotle frames the options exhaustively: a prophetic dream must be either a **cause**, a **token (sign)**, or a mere **coincidence** of the event it seems to predict — and coincidences, by definition, follow no universal rule, so most "fulfilled" dreams (especially of remote, extravagant, or agency-independent events like sea-battles) are simply coincidences, no more remarkable than a chance verbal remark that turns out true. But not all: because daytime perception is dominated by strong waking movements, small internal bodily disturbances (the true "beginnings" of disease or affect) are imperceptible by day and vivid by night — hence dreams can be genuine **tokens** of bodily conditions (a physician's-eye argument, echoing scientific medicine's attention to dreams), and, more speculatively, can be **causes** of subsequent waking action, since a nocturnal image can "pave the way" for a daytime action just as daytime pursuits pave the way for a corresponding dream. Since animals other than man also dream, and since foresight in dreams disproportionately afflicts "commonplace" and even deranged persons rather than the wise, a divine sender is ruled out; dreams have a "divine aspect" only in the loose sense that nature itself is divinely ordered, not divine.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **The threefold taxonomy — cause / token / coincidence** — supplies a general logical framework for evaluating any purported predictive sign, illustrated by careful examples (moon as cause of eclipse; entering shadow as token of eclipse; a walk coinciding with an eclipse as mere coincidence).
- **Rejection of divine sending**: if God sent prophetic dreams, they would come to the wise, not to "commonplace persons," and would occur equally by day; instead foresight clusters in the mentally "vacant" or deranged, whose minds offer no resistance to faint alien movements — a naturalistic, almost psychiatric explanation.
- **Critique of Democritus**: Democritus's theory that dreams are caused by physical "images"/"emanations" streaming from external objects is rejected as unexplained in its own right, though Aristotle borrows and adapts the underlying physics (propagating motion in air, as in *On Dreams*) to explain why such movements would be more perceptible at night.
- **Signal amplification at night**: the treatise's central mechanistic insight — thunder mistaken for faint ringing, honey for a drop of phlegm, walking through fire for slight local warmth — "the beginnings of all events are small," and sleep's quiet makes small beginnings (of disease, of intended action) disproportionately vivid, explaining both medically useful dream-signs and the general unreliability of "prophecy."
- **Familiar-persons effect**: friends dream of one another more accurately because of habitual mutual attentiveness/solicitude — an early sociological/attentional explanation for a folk phenomenon, not a mystical one.
- **The skilled interpreter is the one who discerns resemblances** in blurred, distorted presentations — dream interpretation likened to reading distorted reflections in disturbed water, reinforcing that "prophecy" is pattern-matching under noise, not supernatural insight.
- Explicitly frames the whole *Parva Naturalia* sleep/dream inquiry as now complete: "the questions... as to the nature of sleep and the dream... have been considered."

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Aristotle's admission that dreams can function as **causes** of future waking action sits in tension with his own insistence elsewhere (Metaphysics, Physics) that chance/spontaneity is not a genuine per se cause — flagged as a minor internal nuance rather than a major contradiction, since Aristotle explicitly restricts the causal role to a narrow class of dreams (those "paving the way" for actions the dreamer himself performs), consistent with efficient causation via psychological priming rather than supernatural prediction.
