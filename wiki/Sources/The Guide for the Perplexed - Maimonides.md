# The Guide for the Perplexed - Maimonides

> **Moreh Nevukhim / Dalalat al-Ha'irin** (c. 1190), by Moses Maimonides (Rambam, 1138–1204), trans. M. Friedländer (2nd ed., 1904). The supreme work of medieval Jewish rationalism: a deliberately esoteric reconciliation of the Hebrew Bible with Aristotelian philosophy, written for the believer "perplexed" by the apparent conflict between Scripture and reason. It sits in the same Aristotle-vs-revelation arena as the vault's [[Sources/The Incoherence of the Philosophers - Al-Ghazali|Ghazali]]–[[Sources/Incoherence of the Incoherence - Ibn Rushd|Averroes]] cluster, but threads a third path — accepting far more of Aristotle than Ghazali while refusing the philosophers' eternal world.

## Argument Spine

The "perplexed" reader accepts both the Law and demonstrative science and is tormented by their literal conflict. Maimonides' cure has two prongs: (1) **linguistic** — most apparent conflicts are dissolved by recognizing that scriptural terms applied to God (place, sitting, seeing, even "existence") are **homonyms** to be read figuratively, never literally; and (2) **metaphysical** — a rigorous **negative theology** in which no positive attribute can be predicated of God's essence (only attributes of *action*, or negations), securing absolute divine unity and incorporeality. On this basis he critiques the **Kalam** (Ash'arite/Mu'tazilite atomism and occasionalism) as building physics to fit theology, yet *also* breaks with the Aristotelians on the decisive point: the world's **eternity is not demonstrated**, and creation *ex nihilo*, though likewise not strictly provable, is the better-supported and scripturally required view. The later Parts naturalize **prophecy** (an overflow through the imaginative and rational faculties), restrict **providence** and **evil** (evil as privation; providence graded by intellectual perfection; Job as a parable about this), and give **rational reasons for the commandments** (ta'amei ha-mitzvot, often as historically-situated opposition to Sabean idolatry), culminating in intellectual love of God as human perfection.

## Whole-Work Assessment

The *Guide* supplies the **third corner** of the medieval Aristotle-vs-revelation triangle the vault already held in Arabic ([[Thinkers/Al-Ghazali]] vs. [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]) — and it is the corner that most reshapes the existing cluster, because Maimonides lands on *different* sides of the two contradiction pages I built for the Ghazali ingest. On **causation** he is an ally of Averroes and Aristotle against Ghazali: *Guide* I.71–76 reconstructs the Kalam atomism/occasionalism and rejects it as physics-bent-to-dogma, so the vault's [[Contradictions/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation]] now reads as Ghazali-alone vs. (Averroes + Maimonides + Aquinas). On **creation** he opens a genuine third position in [[Contradictions/Temporal Creation vs Eternal World]] — eternity *undemonstrated* (not, as Ghazali, disproved), creation accepted on prophecy plus a design argument — which is precisely the verdict [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]] later adopts, making Maimonides the documented conduit ("Rabbi Moses") for that move into scholasticism.

Beyond the cluster, the work adds a rigorous **negative theology** that the vault lacked as a primary statement: the via negativa and attributes-of-action doctrine that Aquinas softens into analogy and that [[Sources/Ethics - Spinoza (Cambridge, Kisner ed. 2018)|Spinoza]] (raised on the *Guide*) inverts into immanent *Deus sive Natura*. It deepens three further threads — the **privation theory of evil** (shared with [[Thinkers/Augustine]] and [[Thinkers/Plotinus]]), the **intellectualist eudaimonia** (knowledge of God as the highest of four perfections, crowned by *imitatio Dei*), and a **functional/historical theory of religious law** (the Sabean account of sacrifice) that prefigures later anthropology of religion.
