## Core Content

### Timaeus
A long, near-monologue by the character Timaeus (Locrian statesman/astronomer/philosopher) giving Plato's most elaborate account of the origins and structure of the cosmos and humanity.

- **Philosophical preamble**: Distinction between eternal Being (grasped by intellect/reason) and Becoming (perceptible, opinion). The universe is a generated image of an eternal model; the creator (demiurge = "craftsman") is good, non-jealous, and imposes order on pre-existing chaos because order is better.
- **Work of Reason (teleology)**: The demiurge constructs the world as a living intelligent being. World-soul, regular circular motions of stars/planets, time as "moving image of eternity". Astral gods. Human souls created from same ingredients as world-soul, placed in bodies as vehicles; reincarnation for disorderly souls. Emphasis on mathematics/geometry: elements composed of two types of right triangles; regular polyhedra (Platonic solids implied) for fire/air/water/earth.
- **Work of Necessity (the Receptacle)**: "Receptacle" (chora) as space/womb/nurse in which becoming occurs; chaotic motions before ordering. Construction of elements from triangles, their interactions, sensible qualities, pleasure/pain.
- **Cooperation of Reason and Necessity**: Anatomy and physiology of human body (head houses rational soul, etc.), senses (esp. sight and hearing for philosophy), digestion, respiration, diseases (imbalance), plants, women and animals as reincarnations of failed men. Health via imitating cosmic order (astronomy, harmony of body/soul).
- **"Likely account" (eikos logos)**: Explicitly presented as probable story, not literal truth, because the subject is the generated world.

**Key Themes**: Teleological explanation (why best, not just how/mechanism); mathematics as key to understanding nature; microcosm/macrocosm (human as small cosmos); aspiration to become like god by ordering one's soul after the heavens.

### Critias (unfinished)
Direct sequel. After Timaeus finishes, Critias (elderly Athenian) tells the Atlantis story in detail, which he had only summarized in Timaeus intro.

- **Frame**: Solon (lawgiver) visited Egypt ~590s BC, learned from priests at Sais of records of ancient events ~9000 years before (i.e. ~9600 BC). Ancient Athens (ideal-like constitution) led alliance that defeated aggressive Atlantis; then cataclysm (earthquake/flood) destroyed both the Athenian force and sank Atlantis "in a single day and night".
- **Description of Atlantis** (the part that exists): Large island "larger than Libya and Asia combined" beyond Pillars of Heracles (Strait of Gibraltar). Concentric rings of land and sea around central acropolis/island; huge harbors, canals, bridges, walls with orichalcum (mythic metal), temple of Poseidon and Cleito (enormous, with golden statues, bulls), royal palace, plain with irrigation, 10 kings with specific laws, bull-hunt and ritual sacrifice every 5-6 years, decline into hubris leading to war.
- **Breaks off** abruptly mid-sentence while Zeus is about to speak/punish the Atlanteans. No full account of the war or sinking in the surviving text. No Hermocrates dialogue ever written.

**Note on historicity**: Modern consensus (per intro): Plato's invention, sole ancient literary source for the legend. No geological or archaeological support for literal sunken civilization at the described location/time. Possible political allegory (Atlantis ~ imperial Athens or Persia; ancient Athens ~ idealized early Sparta or ancestral constitution). Inspired endless later speculation, pseudo-history, and fiction.

## Significance and Influence

- **Cosmology & Science**: Most influential ancient text on teleological/natural theology views of the universe. Shaped Neoplatonism (Plotinus etc.), early Christian thought (demiurge assimilated to creator God; Timaeus 28c "maker and father" echoed in theology), Renaissance science/philosophy (Kepler, Galileo references to "book of nature" written in math/geometry). Aristotle both used and critiqued it.
- **Atlantis**: Origin of the enduring myth. Whether read as allegory, moral tale (hubris punished, importance of good constitution), or "lost civilization" seed, it has no independent ancient sources.
- **Relation to other Plato**: Dramatic sequel to (or parallel with) Republic — Socrates' ideal city "brought to life" via the Atlantis tale as historical exemplar. Timaeus' human body/soul account continues themes from Phaedo, Republic (tripartite soul echoes), etc. Late-period work (c. 360s BC?).

## Key Excerpts / Memorable Ideas (paraphrased from text)

- Demiurge: "He was good, and nothing good is ever characterized by mean-spiritedness... he led it from chaos to order, which he regarded as in all ways better."
- Receptacle and triangles: The "third kind" (besides model and copy) is the receptacle; elements built from scalene and isosceles right triangles.
- Human task: "We should... bring the wanderings of our mind into order to match the unwandering motions of the heavens" and "be as much like god as possible."
- Atlantis scale and splendor described in detail before the text (and island) "disappears."
