# On the Soul (De Anima, Aristotle)

## Argument Spine
The soul (*psychē*) is not a separate substance inhabiting a body but the **form/essence of a living body** — "the first actuality of a natural body which has life potentially," definitionally inseparable from a body of the right kind, except possibly for intellect (*nous*). Aristotle builds a hierarchy of psychic capacities — nutritive (all living things), perceptive and locomotive (animals), intellective (humans) — each higher capacity presupposing but not reducible to the lower, and analyzes each faculty (perception via the senses, imagination, desire, and thought) as the *actualization of a potentiality*, continuous with his general hylomorphic metaphysics.

## Whole-Work Assessment

*De Anima* completes the vault's Aristotle metaphysics with its most consequential special-case application: hylomorphism applied to living things, yielding a soul that is form-not-substance and therefore neither separable *simpliciter* (as Plato held) nor eliminable (as the atomists held). The Argument Spine held: Book I's demolition of predecessor theories (self-motion, harmony, self-moving number, elemental composition) clears the ground precisely for Book II's actuality-of-a-natural-body definition, and Book III's perception→imagination→intellect→desire sequence is a genuinely cumulative architecture, not a loose collection of topics. The work's single most productive tension is the one it opens rather than closes: the active/passive intellect distinction (Book III, ch. 5) declares mind "alone immortal and eternal" without settling whether this intellect is individual or universal, a crack later exploited in opposite directions by [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]] (unicity of intellect, shared across humanity) and [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]] (individually subsistent, grounding personal immortality) — both already in the vault, now with their common textual root established. The extant text is confirmed incomplete, breaking off mid-sentence in Book III, ch. 13.
