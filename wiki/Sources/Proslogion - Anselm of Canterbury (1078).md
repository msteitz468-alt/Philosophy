# Proslogion - Anselm of Canterbury (1078)

> A theological meditation by [[Thinkers/Anselm of Canterbury|Anselm of Canterbury]] containing the first formulation of the ontological argument for the existence of God.

## Summary

The *Proslogion* (originally titled *Fides quaerens intellectum* or *Faith Seeking Understanding*) is written in the form of an address or prayer to God. Having written the *Monologion*, which relied on multiple, complex a posteriori proofs, Anselm sought a single, self-contained a priori proof that would demonstrate God's existence and perfections. 

He found this proof in what is now called the **ontological argument** (in Chapters 2 and 3), defining God as **"that than which nothing greater can be conceived"** (*aliquid quo nihil maius cogitari possit*). The rest of the work deduces God's attributes (justice, mercy, eternity, simplicity) from this supreme definition.

---

## Key Chapters and Arguments

### Preface: The Project
Anselm explains his search for a single, self-sufficient argument (*unum argumentum*) that would prove God's existence and attributes. He operates under the principle that faith is the prerequisite for rational understanding: *"For I do not seek to understand in order that I may believe, but I believe in order that I may understand."*

### Chapter 1: The Awakening of the Mind
A reflective prayer lamenting human separation from God since the Fall, urging the soul to seek God's light: *"Teach me to seek Thee, and reveal Thyself to me when I seek Thee."*

### Chapter 2: That God Truly Exists (The Ontological Proof)
Anselm introduces the definition of God: *that than which nothing greater can be conceived*. 
* Even the atheist (the "fool" of Psalm 14:1) understands the concept, meaning it exists in his understanding (*in intellectu*).
* It is greater to exist in reality (*in re*) than in the understanding alone.
* If that than which nothing greater can be conceived existed only in the understanding, we could conceive of a being who also exists in reality, which would be greater.
* This leads to a contradiction (a being greater than that than which nothing greater can be conceived).
* Therefore, God must exist in reality.

### Chapter 3: That God Cannot Be Conceived Not to Exist
Anselm argues that God's existence is necessary, not contingent. A being that cannot be conceived not to exist is greater than a being that can be conceived not to exist. Therefore, God exists in such a way that He cannot even be conceived not to exist.

### Chapter 4: How the Fool Said in His Heart What Cannot Be Conceived
Anselm resolves how the "fool" can deny God if God's existence is logically necessary. He distinguishes between:
1. **Thinking of the words** representing a concept (which allows denying God).
2. **Understanding the actual nature** of the concept itself (which makes denying God impossible, as the contradiction is recognized).

### Chapters 5–26: The Deduction of God's Attributes
Using the *unum argumentum*, Anselm deduces the traditional divine attributes:
* God must be all-powerful, merciful, passionless, simple (not composed of parts), eternal, and the supreme good, because possessing these qualities is greater than lacking them.
* He addresses theological paradoxes, such as how a passionless God can feel mercy (God is merciful in his effects on us, but not in his own immutable nature).

## Key Tensions / Contradictions

* The ontological argument represents the ultimate realization of a priori deduction, but it is countered by the epistemological critiques of Gaunilo (the Lost Island), Kant (existence is not a predicate), and Hume (denial of existence is never a contradiction). See [[Arguments/Ontological Argument|Ontological Argument]].
