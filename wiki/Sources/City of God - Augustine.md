# City of God — Augustine

> *De Civitate Dei contra Paganos* (c. 413–426 CE). Augustine's longest work: a 22-book defense of Christianity against pagan charges that Rome's Christianization caused its fall, and a comprehensive philosophy of history built around the contrast between two cities — the City of God (love of God to contempt of self) and the earthly city (love of self to contempt of God).

**Edition**: Penguin Classics, Henry Bettenson translation (1972), Introduction by John O'Meara (1984). ~2.9MB, 33,000 lines.

---

## Background and Occasion

- **Trigger**: Alaric's Visigoths sacked Rome in August 410 CE — the first foreign sack in 800 years. Pagan critics blamed Rome's Christian emperors for abandoning the old gods who had protected the city.
- **Addressee**: Marcellinus, a Christian imperial official who relayed the pagan charge plus philosophical objections (miracles of Apollonius vs. Christ; why did God change from OT to NT sacrifice; did pacifist Christianity weaken the Empire?).
- Written over ~13 years (413–426); structured response to both immediate crisis and the deepest questions of history, politics, and eschatology.
- **Relationship to the Confessions**: The *Confessions* is Augustine's personal pilgrimage; the *City of God* applies that same drama of grace, fall, and return to all of human history. "The *City of God* is the application of the *Confessions* to the history of mankind." (O'Meara)

---

## Structure: The Three Foci (Rome, Greece, Jerusalem)

Augustine himself says the work revolves around three foci:

| Part | Books | Topic |
|------|-------|--------|
| Against pagan Rome | I–V | Gods failed to protect Rome; pagan religion corrupted morals; Roman virtue was Providence's instrument, not the gods' gift |
| Against Greek philosophy | VI–X | Platonists are "near-Christians" but worship demons; Porphyry's failed search for a universal way; Christ as the one Mediator and Priest |
| Scripture and the two cities | XI–XXII | Origin, development, and destiny of the two cities — from creation/angels to resurrection and the eternal Sabbath |

**Five volumes** (Augustine's own division, Letter to Firmus):
- Vol. 1 (Bks I–V): Against those who think pagan worship leads to earthly happiness
- Vol. 2 (Bks VI–X): Against those who think pagan worship leads to eternal happiness
- Vol. 3 (Bks XI–XIV): Origin of the two cities
- Vol. 4 (Bks XV–XVIII): Development/history of the two cities
- Vol. 5 (Bks XIX–XXII): Ends/destinies of the two cities

---

## Part One: Against Pagan Rome (Books I–V)

**Book I**: The sack of Rome contextualized. The barbarians (Gothic Christians under Alaric) spared pagan Romans who sheltered in Christian basilicas — mercy unparalleled in pagan war history. Augustine contrasts this with Troy's fall, where Juno's sanctuary offered no protection. Christians who suffered shared in trials befalling both good and bad (Providence uses adversity). Pagans who now blaspheme Christ were saved by sheltering under his name. Opening thesis: God's providence works through even disasters; the earthly city's lust for domination is itself ruled by what it lusts for.

**Book II**: Pagan gods had no moral teaching. Their rites demanded obscenity (theater, temple); Sallust's account shows Roman moral decay long predated Christianity. First appearance of Scipio's definition of a republic (requires justice). Cicero's judgment: Rome never was a true republic because it lacked true justice.

**Book III**: The gods failed to protect Troy (mother of Rome) or prevent Rome's long list of pre-Christian catastrophes. Early Roman moral firmness under Numa — later religious depravity.

**Book IV**: Multiplicity and futility of Roman gods. Praise of Varro's scholarly taxonomy (three theologies: mythical, civil, natural). The proliferation of petty gods (every function has its own deity) reveals the system's absurdity.

**Book V**: Astrology is false; God is not Fate. Roman success due to Roman virtue — love of honor, glory, and civic devotion. God rewarded these temporal virtues with temporal empire; they had their reward already. God uses Rome providentially. Model Christian emperors: Constantine (spread Christianity) and Theodosius (penance, just legislation). Christians must serve God not for temporal reward but eternal.

## Part Two: Against Greek Philosophy (Books VI–X)

**Books VI–VII**: Varro's "select gods" collapse on inspection — Jupiter cannot be supreme if all nature is god; identification of gods with natural forces is inconsistent. Even natural theology can't yield eternal life.

**Book VIII**: "Natural theology" — a brief history of Greek philosophy leading to the Platonists, who are "raised above the rest by the glorious reputation they so thoroughly deserve." Platonists acknowledge an immaterial, immutable supreme Good from which the soul receives illumination and bliss. They are "near-Christians." But they fell into demonology — Apuleius: demons are intermediate beings with immortal bodies, rational souls, and *passions like men*. Augustine demolishes this: passions make demons unfit as mediators between mortal-wretched man and immortal-blessed God. They cannot give what they themselves lack.

**Book IX**: The Neoplatonist demon-theory fails on its own terms. The true Mediator must be both mortal (to share our condition) and immortal (to reach God) — only Christ satisfies this.

**Book X** — *Key philosophical summit*:
- **Latreia** (Greek: true worship/service due only to God) — Augustine's extended discussion of what worship means. Only God is to be worshipped with sacrifice; no angel or demon may claim it.
- **Plotinus on illumination**: the Soul of the Universe receives its blessedness from the same light (God) as we do; Plotinus agrees the rational soul cannot be light to itself. This agrees with John 1:9 ("the true light that illuminates every man"). Augustine endorses Plotinus here.
- **True sacrifice**: not animal offerings but the whole redeemed community offered to God through the one Priest (Christ). "The sacrifice of Christians is: 'many, making up one body in Christ.' This is the sacrifice which the Church continually celebrates in the sacrament of the altar." True sacrifice = any act designed to unite us to God in holy fellowship.
- **Porphyry and the Universal Way**: Porphyry (232–305 CE), the Neoplatonist and "fiercest enemy of Christians," searched for a *via universalis* (universal road of soul liberation) and admitted he had not found it. He came closest — his oracles acknowledged one great God; he saw the destination — but rejected Christ (the humble Way) out of pride. "Some people can see the land of peace from a wooded hilltop but cannot find the road to it… Others follow the high road to that land of peace." The Neoplatonists see the goal; they lack the road. Christ is the way because he is both God (the goal) and man (the road). Porphyry's failure is the failure of pride (the demons' sin) vs. humility (Christ's).

## Part Three: Origin of the Two Cities (Books XI–XIV)

**Book XI**:
- Creation: the world was made *with* time, not *in* time; no "before" creation in the temporal sense. God's eternal plan undergoes no change.
- The six days of creation as angelic knowledge: each day = a mode of angelic cognition (knowing things in God's wisdom vs. in themselves = day vs. evening knowledge).
- Angels: created as light; those who turned from God became darkness (evil = privation of the good).
- The Trinity: God is simple — in Him, being and attribute are identical; Father, Son, Spirit are one substance, three persons, each fully God.

**Book XII**:
- Evil has no positive nature: "there cannot exist a nature in which there is no good… but there can exist a nature in which there is no evil." Even the Devil's nature is good insofar as it is a nature; his evil is his defection.
- The cyclic theory of history (Platonic/Stoic eternal return) is false: time is linear and creation is unique.
- Creation took place a finite time ago; the world is not eternal.

**Book XIII**:
- Death entered through sin (the Fall). Adam and Eve were created immortal *conditionally* (if they obeyed); their sin introduced physical and spiritual death.
- The body is not evil in itself; resurrection of the flesh is the Christian hope.

**Book XIV** — *The Two Cities Defined*:
- The carnal life is not merely bodily pleasure but living "by the standard of man" rather than "by the standard of God" — pride, envy, and enmity are "works of the flesh" though fleshly in no bodily sense (even the Devil, who has no flesh, sins through pride).
- The will determines the quality of all emotions: desire, joy, fear, grief are good or evil depending on what the will is attached to.
- Sin = falsehood: "man wills happiness by means that produce misery." The only path to true well-being is God, not self.
- The Devil's sin: not flesh, but living "by his own standard" — lying, pride, the lust to be self-sufficient. This is the earthly city's root.
- **The Two Cities**: "There is one city of men who choose to live by the standard of the flesh, another of those who choose to live by the standard of the spirit." → "Some live by man's standard, others by God's." The two cities are interwoven throughout history; separated only at the Last Judgment.
- Love is the differentiating principle: "Two cities have been formed by two loves: the earthly city by love of self to the contempt of God; the Heavenly City by love of God to the contempt of self." (Bk XIV.28 — the locus classicus, not quoted verbatim in the Bettenson but present in spirit throughout.)
- The Stoic ideal of the passionless sage is rejected: Christ himself wept; the blessed will love and fear appropriately in eternity. Apathy is not virtue.

## Part Four: Development of the Two Cities (Books XV–XVIII)

**Book XV**: Abel = first citizen of the City of God (pilgrim; no earthly city); Cain = earthly city (first murderer; builder of the first city). This sets the pattern: the earthly city builds, rules, fights for worldly domination; the City of God lives as pilgrim, using earthly goods without worshipping them. Patriarchs, Noah, the Ark.

**Book XVI**: From Noah to Abraham. The genealogies track the two cities through history; the covenant with Abraham inaugurates the explicit sacred history of the City of God.

**Book XVII**: Prophecy as the backbone of sacred history — Samuel, David, Solomon, the prophets all point forward to Christ and the Church. The Psalms especially.

**Book XVIII**: The parallel histories of the earthly city (Assyria, Rome) and City of God (Israel, Church) from Abraham to Christ. The Church contains a mixed body — predestined saints and reprobate — like the dragnet that gathers all fish until the shore. Job as example of a non-Jew in the City of God. Two sorts of men in the Church will be separated only at the Last Judgment.

## Part Five: Ends of the Two Cities (Books XIX–XXII)

### Book XIX — The Supreme Good is Peace

**The philosophical survey**: Augustine surveys Varro's taxonomy of 288 possible philosophical sects (4 desiderata × 3 rankings × 2 social orientations × 2 epistemologies × 2 lifestyles × 3 life-types), all finally reducible to 3 genuine positions on the supreme good (desiderata subordinated to virtue; virtue subordinated to desiderata; both desired equally). Augustine follows Varro's preference for the Old Academy (primary natural goods + virtue, social life) — but then radically criticizes it.

**Christian answer**: The supreme good is *eternal life* (not natural happiness); the supreme evil is *eternal death*. All philosophical attempts to locate happiness in this life are defeated by life's ills: sickness, madness, torture, war, the corruption of friends, the betrayal of justice, the misery of judging. The Stoic "wise man" who must kill himself to escape unbearable ills proves that life is miserable, not happy. "We are saved in hope; we do not yet possess salvation in present reality."

**Peace as the Final End**: Peace is the greatest single theme of Book XIX:
- "Even wars are waged with peace as their object."
- All creatures seek peace: even robbers maintain peace within their gang; even the monstrous Cacus desired bodily repose.
- **The definition**: *"The peace of the body is a tempering of the component parts in duly ordered proportion… the peace of the Heavenly City is a perfectly ordered and perfectly harmonious fellowship in the enjoyment of God and a mutual fellowship in God; the peace of the whole universe is the tranquillity of order — and order is the arrangement of things equal and unequal in a pattern which assigns to each its proper position."*
- **Tranquillitas ordinis** — peace as the tranquility of order — is Augustine's most precise definition. It encompasses every level: body, soul, household, city, world, cosmos, and the heavenly community.
- The Supreme Good = "peace in life everlasting" or "life everlasting in peace."
- The earthly city and the City of God share temporal peace and earthly goods; the pilgrims of the City of God use temporal peace (obey laws, raise families, support order) while not confusing it with ultimate peace.

**On the state and justice**: A just republic requires true justice, which requires the worship of the true God. Rome never had true justice (never worshipped the true God). Augustine redefines the republic (following Scipio via Cicero) as a "congregation of men associated by a common acknowledgment of right and by a community of interests" — and notes that by this definition Rome *was* a republic, though an unjust one. The truly just republic exists only in the City of God.

**Just war**: Wars can be just but are always tragic. "The wise man will wage just wars. But if he remembers that he is a human being, he will rather lament the fact that he is faced with the necessity of waging just wars." Injustice on the opposing side forces the just man's hand; both the injustice and the necessity are to be mourned.

### Book XX — The Last Judgment

- Extensive scriptural commentary on the judgment: Daniel, Revelation, Paul's letters.
- The wicked and good will be separated; the saints reign with Christ (millennium = the present age of the Church, not a literal future 1000 years — Augustine's anti-millenarianism).
- "God will be all in all" (1 Cor. 15:28) — the eschatological formula that recurs throughout Books XX–XXII.

### Book XXI — Eternal Punishment

- How can finite sins deserve eternal punishment? Because sin against an infinite God is of infinite gravity.
- The fire of Gehenna is real; the spiritual "pain" of loss is even worse than physical fire.
- Augustine allows that some may be purified after death (purgatorial fire) but not all; the hardened wicked suffer eternally.
- Careful refutation of universalist and annihilationist positions.

### Book XXII — Resurrection and the Eternal Sabbath

**Resurrection of the body**: The philosophical objection (Cicero: "Nature does not allow what comes from earth to dwell in heaven") is refuted: if an immaterial soul can be linked to an earthly body — which it is, in our present existence — then there is no absurdity in an earthly body being elevated to a heavenly state by God's will.

**The three incredibilities**: (1) Christ rose in the flesh and ascended. (2) The whole world came to believe this incredible event. (3) A few uneducated fishermen persuaded all of humanity. The second and third incredibilities are *observed facts*; denying the first is the most irrational position.

**Ongoing miracles**: Augustine documents dozens of contemporary miracles at Hippo and surrounding churches (healing of fistulas, cancers, paralysis, demon-possession, and the dead raised) through the relics of St. Stephen and other martyrs. He initiated a system of written testimonies (over 70 documented at Hippo alone within 2 years). These prove God's power continues; they are less well-known only because they lack the publicity infrastructure of the apostolic age.

**The resurrection body**: Spiritual (pneumatikon) but still bodily; all defects healed; wounds of martyrs glorious, not ugly; the resurrected body perfectly subject to the spirit. No more conflict of flesh and spirit.

**The vision of God**: The blessed will see God directly — not through images, dreams, or spiritual visions, but by direct intellectual sight. Whether they will see God with the bodily eyes is debated; Augustine leans toward thinking the glorified eyes will see God through the glorified body, but the intellectual/spiritual vision is primary. Elisha saw his absent servant by the spirit, not by bodily sight; the saints will have an immeasurably greater gift.

**The eternal Sabbath**: History has six ages (Adam–Noah–Abraham–David–Babylon–Christ; then the seventh age = eternal Sabbath). Augustine numbers the ages carefully: the current Church age is the sixth; the seventh day of rest (God's Sabbath) will be without evening. Final words of the entire work: *"There we shall rest and see; we shall see and love; we shall love and we shall praise. Behold what will be, in the end, without end."*

## Key Theological Claims

1. **Two cities by two loves**: earthly city (love of self to contempt of God) vs. City of God (love of God to contempt of self). Interwoven in history, separated at Last Judgment.
2. **Pax = tranquillitas ordinis**: all peace is ordered peace; the City of God's peace is perfectly ordered fellowship in God; earthly peace is useful but not ultimate.
3. **Christ as the universal Way**: fulfills Porphyry's search; the Neoplatonists saw the destination but lacked the road; humility (Christ's way) vs. pride (philosophy's way) is the key difference.
4. **Latreia/true sacrifice**: due only to God; the whole redeemed community is the universal sacrifice through the one Priest.
5. **Providence over Rome**: God used Roman virtue and Roman empire instrumentally; Rome's true fulfillment is in becoming part of the City of God.
6. **Grace and predestination**: God brings good out of evil (fallen angels, fallen Adam); the elect are gathered by unmerited grace; the numbers of the redeemed may exceed the fallen angels.
7. **Evil as privation**: not a positive nature but the turning of a creature from the Good; even the Devil's nature is good insofar as it exists.
8. **Bodily resurrection**: real, bodily, perfected; the glorified body is "spiritual" in Paul's sense (fully subject to spirit) but truly physical.
9. **Vision of God** as the Final End: "God will be all in all"; eternal Sabbath; rest, sight, love, praise — without end.

## Key Texts and Quotations

- "God resists the proud, but gives grace to the humble." (Preface — the book's keynote)
- "I know how great is the effort needed to convince the proud of the power and excellence of humility." (Preface)
- "Remove justice, and what are kingdoms but gangs of criminals on a large scale?" (Bk IV.4)
- On the two cities: two loves; "The city of man seeks the praise of men; the other City of God, as shown by its conscience, counts its saints' greatest glory in God." (throughout Bk XIV)
- "As I said, there is one road, and one only, well secured against all possibility of going astray; and this road is provided by one who is himself both God and man. As God, he is the goal; as man, he is the way." (Bk XI.2)
- "Peace is so great a good that even in relation to the affairs of earth and of our mortal state no word ever falls more gratefully upon the ear, nothing is desired with greater longing." (Bk XIX.11)
- *Pax omnium rerum tranquillitas ordinis* — "The peace of all things is the tranquillity of order." (Bk XIX.13)
- "We are saved in hope. But when hope is seen fulfilled it is hope no longer." (Bk XIX.4, quoting Romans 8)
- "There we shall rest and see; we shall see and love; we shall love and we shall praise. Behold what will be, in the end, without end." (Bk XXII.30)

## Relationships to Vault

**Extends and deepens [[Thinkers/Augustine]]** significantly (previously based on *Confessions* only):
- *City of God* is the *Confessions* scaled to all of history; same themes (pilgrimage, grace, two loves, peace/rest) elaborated on the civilizational canvas.
- Major new contributions: the two-cities political theology, peace as tranquillitas ordinis, just war, the universal Way, the extended engagement with Rome and Platonism, the philosophy of history, eschatology, resurrection, vision of God.

**To [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] and [[Concepts/The One (To Hen)]] / [[Concepts/Return of the Soul (Epistrophe)]]**:
- Augustine praises Plotinus (and cites him directly in Book X on illumination) as closest to Christian truth among pagans.
- But: Neoplatonists lack the road (Christ/Mediator); their pride prevents acceptance of the humble Way; their demonology substitutes demons for the one true Mediator.
- *City of God* is a sustained argument that Plotinus's destination (vision of the One) is real but the path (philosophical contemplation alone) is insufficient; the *via universalis* is Christ.

**To [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]**:
- *City of God* is a primary source for Aquinas: two-city framework, natural law as basis of just governance, peace, the common good, grace perfecting nature, Providence.
- Aquinas systematizes what Augustine articulated narratively/polemically.

**To [[Concepts/Eudaimonia]]**:
- Book XIX is Augustine's direct, extended engagement with the philosophical debate on the supreme good (Varro's taxonomy, Old Academy, Stoics, Epicureans).
- He subordinates all natural happiness to eternal life; eudaimonia is real but cannot be attained in this life and is not the final end — beatific union with God is.

**To [[Concepts/Ataraxia]] / [[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]]**:
- Augustine argues all philosophical tranquility (Epicurean, Stoic, Pyrrhonian) fails in the face of life's actual evils.
- True peace requires not suspension of judgment but grace-enabled transformation and eschatological hope.

**To [[Thinkers/Plato]] / [[Sources/The Republic - Plato (Cambridge, 2000)]]**:
- Explicit comparison throughout: Augustine's two-city framework vs. Plato's ideal republic; philosopher-kings vs. Christian emperors; justice vs. true justice (worship of God required); the Cave allegory resonates with the earthly city's ignorance.
- Augustine calls the City of God a "Charter of Christendom" analogous to Plato's *Republic* but based on Scripture and grace rather than reason alone.

**To [[Thinkers/Thucydides]] / [[Concepts/Power, Empire, and Revolt (Thucydides)]]**:
- Both analyze the dynamics of empire, war, and justice with clear eyes. Augustine's "even just wars are to be lamented" parallels Thucydidean realism about power's costs.
- Augustine goes beyond Thucydides: Providence governs history; earthly justice is real but incomplete; only the City of God achieves true justice.

**To [[Thinkers/Sextus Empiricus]] / [[Concepts/Equipollence (Isosthenia) and the Modes]]**:
- Augustine's Book X engagement with Porphyry is in some ways a response to the Pyrrhonian challenge: Augustine offers a *via* (the road of faith and Christ) precisely where the skeptic can only suspend. The three philosophies (Dogmatist, Academic, Skeptic) are all superseded by the one who is both Truth and Way.

**To personal/career (Mack Steitz)**:
- "Pilgrimage" as organizing metaphor for career transition; using earthly goods (job, salary, title) without making them ultimate ends.
- Augustine's "just war" framework for competitive/adversarial business situations: fight when necessary; always lament the necessity; seek genuine peace.
- The two-cities framework for organizational life: where does the firm's/team's ultimate loyalty lie?

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Augustine's doctrine of **predestination** is present but not the focus of the *City of God* (the Pelagian controversy sharpened it in his later *anti-Pelagian* works). The vault should note that his late doctrine of double predestination / irresistible grace was controversial and not fully received by the Church.
- **Purgatory**: Augustine allows some post-death purification for lesser sins but is not systematic; Aquinas and later tradition develop this more fully.
- **Millennium**: Augustine's **amillennialism** (the 1000 years = the current age of the Church) is the dominant Catholic and Reformed reading; futurist/dispensationalist readings differ sharply.
- **Just war**: Augustine laid the foundation (just cause, right intention, proper authority); Aquinas and later Scholastics developed the formal criteria. Vault currently lacks a dedicated just war concept page.
- **Open**: Fuller treatment of Books XV–XVIII (prophecy and sacred history) would enrich the vault's biblical/theological thread. Also the explicit anti-heretical contexts (Donatists, Pelagians, Manichees) are mentioned but not detailed.
