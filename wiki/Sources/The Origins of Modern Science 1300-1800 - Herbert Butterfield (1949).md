# The Origins of Modern Science 1300-1800 - Herbert Butterfield (1949)

> Herbert Butterfield's influential 1949 book (based on 1948 Cambridge lectures; revised ed.) argues that the "Scientific Revolution" — associated especially with the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries but with roots reaching back to the fourteenth — was the most important event in the history of Western civilisation since the rise of Christianity. It "outshines everything since the rise of Christianity and reduces the Renaissance and Reformation to the rank of mere episodes"; it changed "the character of men’s habitual mental operations", overthrew the authority of both the Middle Ages and the ancient world (especially Aristotelian physics), transformed the diagram of the physical universe and the texture of human life, and marks the real origin of both the modern world and the modern mentality. Our usual periodisation of European history has therefore become "an anachronism and an encumbrance."

## Nature of the source (read this first)

This is a foundational work in the historiography of science, written by a general historian (Butterfield, Master of Peterhouse, Professor of Modern History at Cambridge; also author of the separate *The Whig Interpretation of History*). It is not a comprehensive chronicle or biographical dictionary but a set of lectures that seeks the "lines of strategic change" and puts the "microscope upon those moments that seem pivotal". 

Key methodological points stressed by Butterfield:
- Avoid reducing history of science to straight lines from one "great figure" to another or a mere catalogue of "anticipations" of modern truths.
- Study the "misfires and the mistaken hypotheses", the "particular intellectual hurdles that seemed insurmountable", courses of development that ran into "blind alleys" but still had effects.
- Always have a picture of the *older systems* being overthrown (not just the new doctrines we now regard as right).
- The revolution involved not only solving problems but altering "mentality" or "thinking cap" in the process.

The text is a digitised/OCR version of the book (with page markers and some extraction artifacts); it covers the period 1300–1800 with emphasis on the 16th–17th centuries. Chapters include treatment of Copernicus (his conservatism as well as his contribution), the study of the heart up to Harvey, Bacon and Descartes, the effect of the revolution on non-scientific fields, the place of the revolution in the history of Western civilisation, and the "postponed" revolution in chemistry.

## Butterfield's central thesis

- The Scientific Revolution was not merely an accumulation of discoveries or the triumph of a "scientific method" in the abstract. It was a profound transformation in the way people *thought* about the world — a change in "the very texture of human life itself" and in "men’s habitual mental operations even in the conduct of the non-material sciences".
- It ended the long authority of ancient (especially Aristotelian) and medieval (scholastic) frameworks in natural knowledge.
- Because of its scope and transforming power, it (rather than the Renaissance or Reformation) is the decisive break that creates "modern" civilisation and mentality. "Since that revolution overturned the authority in science not only of the middle ages but of the ancient world... it looms so large as the real origin both of the modern world and of the modern mentality..."
- By the latter part of the seventeenth century the changes become "congested"; a new chapter opens with political stability after mid-century upheavals, the rise of middle-class intellectual power (especially in England, Holland, and Huguenot/exile circles), and the shift of the "centre of gravity" of civilisation from the Mediterranean (which had held leadership for thousands of years) northward to the Atlantic seaboard and Protestant regions.
- Science did not merely appear as one factor among others; from the first it "consciously assumed a directing role" and began "to take control of the other factors" — analogous to the presiding role Christianity had played in the Middle Ages.
- The revolution was a "creative product of the West", depending on a complicated set of conditions existing only in western Europe and on a certain "dynamic quality" in its life and history. The ancient world and the Byzantine Empire (despite continuity of classical tradition) did not produce anything equivalent.
- Chemistry was "postponed" relative to astronomy, mechanics, and physiology; the phlogiston theory and its overthrow (Lavoisier etc.) illustrate how different fields had different intellectual hurdles and tempos.
- The older systems (Ptolemaic astronomy, Aristotelian physics and biology, Galenic medicine, etc.) must be understood on their own terms; progress is visible only when one sees what had to be unlearned or reconfigured.

Butterfield repeatedly warns against anachronistic or purely "whiggish" readings (though the overall narrative has a strong sense of the emergence of modernity). He stresses the need for the historian to recover the "diagram" of the older world-picture that was being replaced.

## Key Claims / Positions

- The Scientific Revolution "changed the character of men’s habitual mental operations" and "the whole diagram of the physical universe".
- It is the event that makes our conventional ancient/medieval/modern periodisation misleading.
- The revolution was not inevitable from ancient Greek science alone; specific Western conditions (including the recovery and critical engagement with antiquity, technological developments, religious and political contexts, and a dynamic quality in Western history) were required.
- By the late 17th century science begins to preside over and direct other aspects of civilisation (technology, religion, historiography, political thought, the very idea of progress).
- The "place" of the revolution is as the decisive origin of modern Western civilisation and the modern mentality; when "Western civilisation" is later exported (e.g. to Japan), what is primarily meant is "the science, the modes of thought and all that apparatus of civilisation which were beginning to change the face of the West in the latter half of the seventeenth century."
- Historiographical implication: proper history of science must attend to the internal intellectual struggles, the persistence of older frameworks even in "revolutionary" figures (e.g. Copernicus' conservatism), the role of "misfires", and the way textbooks later render revolutions invisible by rewriting the past as continuous accumulation.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **With later historiography (especially Kuhn)**: Butterfield presents the Scientific Revolution as a relatively sharp and fundamental break in mentality that creates modernity. Kuhn's *Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (see [[Sources/The Structure of Scientific Revolutions - Kuhn (Chicago, 4th ed.)]] and [[Concepts/Scientific Revolutions and Paradigm Shifts (Kuhn)]]) offers a major revision: most science is "normal science" (puzzle-solving within a paradigm), not revolutionary; revolutions are community conversions/gestalt switches rather than simple triumph of better evidence or a single "change in thinking cap"; incommensurability means rival paradigms "talk through each other" and do not simply accumulate or correct one another in a linear way. The older "Scientific Revolution" narrative (Butterfield, Koyré and others) is one of the images Kuhn is complicating. Hacking's introductory essay to the 4th ed. of Kuhn situates this historiographical shift.
- **"Whig" tendencies**: Although Butterfield famously criticised Whig history elsewhere, this work has been read as presenting science as the triumphant emergence of modernity out of medieval darkness. Later historians (including some internal to the vault's Phil of Science cluster) push back against overly progressive or present-centred reconstructions.
- **Scope and unity**: Was there one "Scientific Revolution" or multiple, field-specific transformations with different chronologies (chemistry "postponed")? Butterfield acknowledges variation in tempo while insisting on an overall transformation in outlook.
- **Relation to pre-modern worldviews**: The book implicitly describes the overthrow of the kind of hierarchical, teleological, continuous cosmos surveyed in [[Sources/The Great Chain of Being - Arthur O. Lovejoy (1936)]]; the "diagram" being replaced included elements of the Chain/Aristotelian-Ptolemaic synthesis.

Minor tensions are best flagged via cross-links to Kuhn, Hacking, Popper (the image of science as potentially permanent revolution by falsification), and Feyerabend. No dedicated Contradictions/ page required at present.
