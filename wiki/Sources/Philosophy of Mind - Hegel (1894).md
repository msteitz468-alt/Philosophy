# Philosophy of Mind - Hegel (1894)

> **Hegel's Philosophy of Mind** (translated by William Wallace). Published originally as Part III of his *Encyclopaedia of the Philosophical Sciences* (1817). It represents the culmination of his philosophical system, detailing how *Geist* (Spirit or Mind) frees itself from the constraints of nature and progresses through individual psychology and social institutions to absolute self-knowledge.

## Core Thesis: The Unfolding of Mind

Hegel rejects the idea that the mind is a static container of thoughts. Instead, **Mind is activity**—the process of self-actualization. Mind develops through three systematic stages:

1.  **Subjective Mind**: The mind in relation to itself (sensory soul, individual consciousness, and cognitive/volitional psychology).
2.  **Objective Mind**: The mind realized externally in social institutions (law, morality, family, civil society, and the state).
3.  **Absolute Mind**: The mind returning to itself in absolute self-conscious freedom (through Art, Religion, and Philosophy).

## Section Summary

### I. Subjective Mind (Individual Psychology)
The mind developing from unconscious biological roots to conscious rational will:
-   **Anthropology (The Soul)**: The mind as it is bound up with nature and the body. Hegel discusses temperaments, racial characters, sleeping/waking, habits, and the immediate feeling soul. Habit is characterized as the mechanism of bodily liberation (making actions automatic so the mind can focus on higher things).
-   **Phenomenology (Consciousness)**: The mind in relation to an external object. Hegel summarizes the stages of the *Phenomenology of Spirit*: Consciousness (perception), Self-Consciousness (desire and recognition), and Reason.
-   **Psychology (The Mind Proper)**: The mind as active intelligence and will.
    *   *Theoretical Mind*: The progression from sensation, attention, and memory to imagination and pure thought.
    *   *Practical Mind*: The development of impulses, choices, and happiness, culminating in the **Free Will** (the unity of theoretical intelligence and practical action).

### II. Objective Mind (Social Ethics / Sittlichkeit)
The free will realizing itself externally in the social world. True freedom requires shared social structures:
-   **Abstract Right**: Property rights, contracts, and the restoration of right through the punishment of crime.
-   **Morality**: The internal, subjective conscience. The individual recognizes moral duties but suffers from the division between "ought" and "is" (Kant's ethics).
-   **Ethical Life (*Sittlichkeit*)**: The concrete realization of freedom in institutional duties:
    *   *The Family*: Natural, emotional unity based on love.
    *   *Civil Society*: The system of economic needs and labor where individuals pursue their private interests (anticipating capitalism).
    *   *The State*: The ultimate ethical unity where individual liberty is harmonized with the laws of the community.

### III. Absolute Mind (Absolute Self-Knowledge)
Mind completely liberated, contemplating its own eternal conceptual nature:
-   **Art**: The Absolute represented in sensuous, physical form (architecture, sculpture, painting, music, and poetry).
-   **Revealed Religion**: The Absolute represented in pictorial, narrative thought (symbols, myths, and doctrines, particularly the Christian incarnation representing the unity of the infinite and finite).
-   **Philosophy**: The highest stage. Mind conceptualizes its own development in pure, systematic thought. Philosophy is the self-consciousness of Geist, where thought comprehends the entire system of reality.

## Integration and Vault Value

-   **Systemic Architecture**: Serves as the primary source for [[Concepts/Geist (Absolute Spirit)]] and coordinates the divisions of his social philosophy.
-   **Philosophy of Mind Anchor**: Rejects both Descartes' substance dualism ([[Concepts/Res Cogitans and Res Extensa (Descartes)]]) and modern reductionist materialism (such as Dennett's Multiple Drafts or Minsky's Society of Mind), presenting mind as an emergent, intersubjective, and historical process.
