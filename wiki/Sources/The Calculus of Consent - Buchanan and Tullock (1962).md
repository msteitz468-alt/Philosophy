# The Calculus of Consent — Buchanan and Tullock (1962)
> The foundational text of public choice theory and constitutional political economy, applying economic analysis to the design of democratic constitutions.

## Argument Spine

Buchanan and Tullock apply economic methodology (rational choice and methodological individualism) to analyze constitutional design. They model politics not as a quest for some organic "public good," but as a process of exchange ("politics-as-exchange"). They propose that at the "constitutional stage," individuals choose rules of collective decision-making under a "veil of uncertainty" regarding their future positions. They formulate the expected costs model of constitutions: an individual seeks to minimize the sum of "external costs" (imposed by hostile collective decisions) and "decision-making costs" (transaction costs of reaching agreement). While unanimity is the only rule that guarantees Pareto-efficient outcomes, high transaction costs lead rational individuals to agree to less-than-unanimous voting rules (like qualified majority or simple majority) for day-to-day legislation, paired with constitutional checks like bicameralism to protect individuals from exploitation.

## Whole-Work Assessment

### Theses
- **Methodological Individualism**: Collective choices must be resolved into the choice calculus of individual decision-makers; the state is not an organic entity but an artifact designed by men (lines 688–689, 1041).
- **Politics as Exchange**: Political activity is a positive-sum system of cooperative exchange where individuals trade consent and resources for mutual benefits, rejecting zero-sum views of political power (lines 1332–1348).
- **The Expected Costs Model**: Constitutional design is a rational trade-off aimed at minimizing the sum of expected external costs (harm from collective choices) and decision-making costs (transaction costs of agreement) (lines 1987–1999).
- **Unique Status of Unanimity**: The rule of unanimity is the unique voting rule that reduces expected external costs to zero, guaranteeing Pareto-efficient collective outcomes (lines 2935–2938, 4196–4197).
- **Optimal Less-than-Unanimous Rules**: Rational individuals constitutionalize less-than-unanimous voting thresholds (such as qualified or simple majorities) solely to avoid the exponential transaction and holdout costs of unanimity (lines 3705–3715).
- **Efficiency of Vote-Trading**: Under perfect vote-trading (explicit or implicit logrolling), any voting rule can reach Pareto-optimal allocations by allowing voters to express preference intensity (lines 6771–6775).
- **Majoritarian Waste Limit**: Simple majority rule logrolling over localized projects financed by general taxes leads to Pigovian overinvestment and public budget overexpansion, with the waste limit bounded by the $M/N$ ratio (lines 5165–5167, 7795–7797).
- **Constitutional Checkreins**: Structural checkreins (like bicameralism with diverse representation bases) reduce expected external costs without incurring the high decision costs of qualified majority rules (lines 8262–8264).
- **Pressure Group Dynamics**: Organizing pressure groups is a rational response to expected differential advantages from the public budget, and their growth is a direct function of the expansion and lack of generality of government (lines 9946–9949).

### Key Quotations
- "Collective action must be, under our postulate, composed of individual actions." (lines 688–689)
- "Agreement among all individuals in the group upon the change becomes the only real measure of 'improvement' that may be accomplished through change." (lines 805–807)
- "The reaching of unanimous agreement is the only possible test for improvement in the restricted Pareto sense..." (lines 4196–4197)
- "the two-house legislature may produce results ranging from those equivalent to simple majority voting in a single house to those equivalent to the operation of the unanimity rule in a single house." (lines 8262–8264)
- "The distinction between the power of taking action and of blocking action proposed by others is an essential one; it represents the difference between the power to impose external costs on others and the power to prevent external costs from being imposed." (lines 9046–9050)
- "the purpose or aim of the checks and controls provided by the political constitution should be that of making it 'the interest, even of bad men, to act for the public good.'" (lines 10891–10894, quoting Hume)

### Method and Scope
Buchanan and Tullock's work pioneered the integration of microeconomics and political theory. Rather than offering historical narratives, they build deductive models using individual utility-maximization, expected cost curves, and cooperative n-person game theory. The analysis spans direct and representative democracy, legislative structures (unicameral vs. bicameral), and pressure group dynamics. It serves as a rigorous, value-free framework to evaluate political institutions based on human behavior as it is.
