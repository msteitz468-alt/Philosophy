# Language, Truth and Logic — A.J. Ayer (1936)

> The founding English-language manifesto of logical positivism: Ayer synthesizes the Vienna Circle's verification principle, Russell's logical analysis, and Hume's empiricism into a radical program that eliminates metaphysics as literally meaningless, reduces all a priori truth to tautology, and deflates ethics to expression of feeling.

## Bibliographic Details

- **Author**: Alfred Jules Ayer (1910–1989); wrote the book at age 25, published 1936 (Victor Gollancz); second edition with new Preface/Appendix 1946; Penguin edition 2001 with introduction by Ben Rogers.
- **Context**: Ayer had spent four months in Vienna in late 1932, attending meetings of the Vienna Circle (Schlick, Carnap, Neurath). He returned to Oxford intent on introducing logical positivism to British philosophy, which was then dominated by Hegelians and Aristotelians.
- **Influence**: Sold 100,000+ copies in Britain and 300,000+ in the US by the 1970s; became a staple of undergraduate philosophy courses; widely described as a "modern classic."
- **Caution**: The most polemical and youthfully overconfident expression of logical empiricism; Ayer himself later qualified several positions in the 1946 Appendix and subsequent work.

## Chapter Summaries

### Chapter 1 — The Elimination of Metaphysics

Ayer's central move: we should not attack metaphysical claims by demanding to see their premises (the rationalist can always appeal to intellectual intuition), but by showing that their *sentences fail to conform to the conditions under which language can be literally significant*.

**The verification principle** (weak form): a sentence is factually significant iff the person who utters it knows what observations would lead them to accept or reject it. Any putative statement that is consistent with *every* possible experience is, unless a tautology, a *pseudo-proposition* — emotionally significant perhaps, but not literally significant.

- **Strong vs. weak verification**: Strong (conclusive verifiability) condemns general scientific laws as nonsense — unacceptable. Ayer adopts the *weak* sense: possible experience must be *relevant* to truth or falsehood.
- **Definition of metaphysics**: a sentence that purports to express a genuine proposition but expresses neither a tautology nor an empirical hypothesis. Being metaphysical means being *literally senseless*, not false.
- **Sources of metaphysics**: mostly linguistic confusions — the grammar of a language misleads us into positing "substance" (because we use nouns), "Being" (because existence appears as a predicate, though it is not), subsistent entities for fictitious terms, etc. Heidegger on "Nothing" is the extreme case.
- **Metaphysics and poetry**: metaphysicians don't intend to write nonsense (unlike deliberate artists using empty language for aesthetic effect); they produce it through logical errors or grammar-worship.

### Chapter 2 — The Function of Philosophy

Philosophy's function is *wholly critical and analytic* — not to provide speculative first principles or a bird's-eye view of reality, but to clarify propositions, exhibit logical relationships, and provide definitions.

- **Against Descartes's system-building**: no first principle can serve as a foundation. The cogito fails as a base because "I exist" does not follow from "there is a thought now"; and from tautologies only further tautologies can be deduced.
- **The problem of induction is a fictitious problem**: the only justification for inductive procedure is its success in practice; demanding a logical guarantee is asking for the impossible. Rationality consists in being guided by past experience — no further justification is needed or available. (This is Ayer's *dissolution* of Hume's problem, distinct from Popper's *bypass*.)
- **Historical claim**: Locke, Berkeley, and Hume were primarily analysts, not metaphysicians. Hume's "commit it to the flames" is "a rhetorical version of our own thesis."

### Chapter 3 — The Nature of Philosophical Analysis

Philosophy provides *definitions in use* (not dictionary synonyms). A symbol is defined in use when we show how sentences containing it can be *translated* into equivalent sentences without it — Russell's theory of descriptions is the paradigm case.

- **Logical constructions**: material things are logical constructions out of sense-contents. This is a linguistic claim: all sentences about material things are translatable (in principle) into sentences about sense-contents. Material things are not fictitious; "logical construction" does not mean "made up."
- **Phenomenalism outlined**: two sense-contents belong to the same material thing iff they are related by resemblance and continuity in appropriate respects; a material thing can exist unperceived because its existence consists in the *hypothetical* occurrence of certain sense-contents (if conditions were met).
- **Philosophy vs. empirical/metaphysical inquiry**: philosophical propositions are linguistic propositions — they express the formal consequences of definitions, not facts about the world or anything metaphysical.

### Chapter 4 — The A Priori

How does an empiricist account for the *necessity and certainty* of logic and mathematics?

- **Rejection of Mill**: mathematical propositions are *not* inductive generalizations. Their necessity is absolute, not just very-probable.
- **All a priori = analytic = tautological**: "a proposition is analytic when its validity depends solely on the definitions of the symbols it contains." Analytic propositions say nothing about the world; they are true in virtue of linguistic conventions. They can still *surprise* us because our calculating powers are limited — the complexity of a tautological derivation may hide the result.
- **Emendation of Kant**: Kant's distinction between analytic and synthetic is confused (using a psychological criterion in one case, logical in another). Properly understood, all propositions that appear necessarily true (including 7+5=12, geometric theorems) are analytic. Kant's synthetic a priori is an illusion produced by a faulty criterion.
- **Geometry**: axioms are simply definitions; theorems are their logical consequences. Geometry is not "about" physical space — whether a geometry applies to physical space is an empirical question.
- **Consequence**: empiricism is vindicated; rationalism is defeated. There are no a priori truths about matters of fact.

### Chapter 5 — Truth and Probability

- **Redundancy theory of truth**: "'p is true' says no more than p." The phrase "is true" is logically superfluous; it functions as an assertion sign. The "problem of truth" (what is truth?) reduces to a pseudo-question.
- **No certain empirical propositions**: not even propositions about immediate sense-contents are certain. There are no "ostensive propositions" that record experience without classifying it — all description goes beyond what is immediately given.
- **Holism of hypothesis-testing**: observations never confirm or refute a single hypothesis but always a *system* of hypotheses. One can preserve any hypothesis by adjusting other commitments ("the facts of experience can never compel us to abandon a hypothesis").
- **Rationality and probability**: rationality = using an accredited self-consistent procedure to form beliefs; probability = degree of confidence it is rational to hold, given that procedure. No absolute standard.

### Chapter 6 — Critique of Ethics and Theology

**The emotive theory of ethics**: normative ethical symbols are *pseudo-concepts*. When I say "You acted wrongly in stealing," I say nothing more than "You stole that money" — the "wrongly" adds no factual content; it expresses my moral disapproval, like a tone of horror or exclamation marks. Ethical judgments are neither true nor false; they cannot be disputed as propositions, only as facts.

- **Against subjectivism**: Ayer differs from orthodox subjectivism (which claims moral statements assert "I approve of x") because he holds they *express* feelings, not *assert* them.
- **Against intuitionism and naturalism (utilitarianism, subjectivism)**: moral judgments are not equivalent to any factual claims; they are "unverifiable" in the relevant sense.
- **What passes for ethical argument**: actually disputants are arguing about *facts* — motives, consequences, circumstances, general causal principles. When facts are settled but attitudes remain divided, argument ends. Ethics as science = psychology/sociology of moral habits.
- **Aesthetics**: same analysis applies; aesthetic judgments express and arouse feeling; aesthetic criticism communicates emotion rather than knowledge.
- **Theology**: "there is a transcendent God" is a metaphysical utterance — neither true nor false. Neither atheism nor agnosticism is correct either; both presuppose that the question is genuine. Mystical experience is not cognitive; it tells us about the mystic's mental state, not the world.

### Chapter 7 — The Self and the Common World

- **No certain empirical basis**: no "ostensive propositions" exist; all empirical propositions are hypotheses.
- **Sense-contents are neutral**: neither mental nor physical. The mental/physical distinction applies only to *logical constructions* out of sense-contents.
- **The self as logical construction**: personal identity = bodily identity (defined by resemblance and continuity of organic sense-contents). The substantive ego is unverifiable and metaphysical. "Hume showed… he always stumbled on some particular perception" — Ayer's definition solves Hume's problem by grounding personal identity in bodily continuity.
- **Other minds**: not solipsism — others are defined in terms of the (hypothetical) occurrence of appropriate sense-contents, just as material things are. Mutual understanding = harmony of linguistic behavior.

### Chapter 8 — Solutions of Outstanding Philosophical Disputes

Ayer attempts to dissolve the three great disputes (rationalism/empiricism, realism/idealism, monism/pluralism) by separating their metaphysical (senseless), logical (answerable analytically), and empirical (answerable by experience) components.

- **Rationalism vs. empiricism**: rationalists right that there are necessary truths; Ayer's empiricists right that these are tautologies and contain no factual knowledge. Both the supersensible world claim (metaphysical = senseless) and the synthetic a priori (logically refuted by Ayer's account) are wrong.
- **Realism vs. idealism**: Berkeley's dictum "esse est percipi" holds for sense-contents (they cannot occur without being experienced) but not for material things (which are constituted partly by *possible* sense-contents — permanent possibilities of sensation in Mill's phrase). A material thing can exist unperceived without contradiction.
- **Monism vs. pluralism**: the monist's "all properties are internal" confuses analytic (defining) properties with synthetic relational facts. Causality is not a logical relation; the empirical evidence supports limited, not universal, causal interconnection. Philosophy should develop into the **logic of science** — not speculative but clarificatory.

## The 1946 Appendix (Preface to Second Edition)

Ayer's honest self-revision on four points:

1. **The verification principle**: the original weak formulation ("some possible sense-experience relevant") is too liberal — it allows literally anything to be meaningful. He proposes a revised formulation distinguishing *directly verifiable* (an observation-statement, or entails one with the help of premises) and *indirectly verifiable* (entails directly verifiable statements via analytic bridge-premises). Still imperfect.
2. **The a priori**: analytic propositions are not empirical propositions *about* language, nor are they linguistic rules (which are arbitrary; analytic propositions are necessary). They *presuppose* linguistic rules but are not identical with them.
3. **Emotive theory**: should have included a richer account of how ethical language arouses feeling and can serve as command; recognizes moral arguments often involve factual classification of actions under a type.
4. **Other minds**: reverts toward a "behaviouristic" interpretation; the argument from analogy remains vulnerable on phenomenalist grounds.

## Key Doctrines (Summary)

| Doctrine | Ayer's Position |
|---|---|
| **Verification principle** | Statement is meaningful iff analytic or (weakly) verifiable — i.e., possible experience is relevant |
| **Metaphysics** | Literally senseless (neither true nor false), not merely false or speculative |
| **A priori** | All necessary truths are analytic tautologies; no synthetic a priori exists |
| **Ethics/aesthetics** | Normative judgments express feeling, not propositions; neither true nor false (emotivism) |
| **Truth** | "p is true" = p (redundancy theory); no substantial theory of truth needed |
| **Philosophy** | Wholly analytic/logical activity; provides definitions in use; becomes logic of science |
| **Induction** | A "fictitious problem"; pragmatic success is the only available and sufficient justification |
| **Material things** | Logical constructions out of sense-contents (phenomenalism) |
| **The self** | Logical construction out of sense-experiences; personal identity = bodily identity |
| **God/theology** | "There is a transcendent God" is metaphysical = meaningless; neither atheism nor agnosticism |

## Relations to the Vault

### Direct lineage from Hume
Ayer calls Hume "always his master." Hume's Fork (relations of ideas vs. matters of fact) is the direct ancestor of Ayer's analytic/empirical division; Hume's "flames" conclusion (*commit it to the flames* — metaphysics = sophistry and illusion) is "a rhetorical version" of Ayer's verification principle. See [[Concepts/Hume's Fork (Relations of Ideas vs Matters of Fact)]]. Ayer explicitly inherits [[Concepts/Impressions and Ideas - Copy Principle (Hume)]] (meaning-test), the [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)|dissolves rather than solves Hume's induction problem]], [[Concepts/Necessary Connexion and the Two Definitions of Cause (Hume)|Hume's view of causation]], and [[Concepts/Mitigated Scepticism (Hume)|mitigated scepticism]] (the "flames" criterion).

### From the Vienna Circle (Carnap / Schlick)
Ayer credits [[Thinkers/Carnap]] as his primary Vienna Circle influence. The *Aufbau* is the most ambitious prior attempt to reduce physical-object language to sense-data. Ayer adopts the verification criterion while weakening the strong form of conclusive verifiability. Quine's "Two Dogmas" attacks precisely this Vienna Circle inheritance: see [[Concepts/Reductionism and Verification Theory of Meaning (Quine)]] and [[Concepts/Analytic-Synthetic Distinction (Quine)]].

### From Russell and Wittgenstein
Russell's theory of descriptions supplies the paradigm for "definitions in use." Ayer adopts Russell's program of philosophical analysis as logical-linguistic clarification (see [[Concepts/Philosophy of Logical Analysis (Russell)]]). Wittgenstein's *Tractatus* supplies the background for treating logic as tautological and for the idea that philosophy draws limits of language. Ayer also inherits [[Concepts/Sense-Data vs. Physical Objects (Russell)|Russell's sense-data framework]].

### Vs. Kant
Ayer directly attacks Kant's synthetic a priori: geometry and arithmetic are not synthetic; they are analytic tautologies. The transcendental aesthetic (space as form of outer intuition, time as form of inner intuition) is therefore unneeded. Ayer also rejects Kant's indirect route to the limits of the understanding ("a matter of fact"), in favor of making metaphysical meaninglessness *a matter of logic*. See [[Thinkers/Kant]], [[Concepts/Autonomy and Heteronomy of the Will (Kant)]].

### Vs. Quine (the decisive internal critique)
Quine's "Two Dogmas" (1951) is a direct attack on the two foundations of Ayer's program: (1) the analytic/synthetic distinction (which Ayer relies on to divide tautologies from empirical hypotheses), and (2) the verification theory of meaning (which Ayer uses to condemn metaphysics). If Quine is right, the clean division Ayer draws cannot be maintained and verification is never statement-by-statement. See [[Concepts/Analytic-Synthetic Distinction (Quine)]], [[Concepts/Reductionism and Verification Theory of Meaning (Quine)]], [[Concepts/Confirmation Holism (Duhem-Quine Thesis)]].

### Vs. Popper
Both reject metaphysics and address the problem of induction. But Popper says the induction problem is genuine and must be *bypassed* (via falsifiability); Ayer says it is *fictitious* (a pragmatic dissolution). Popper uses falsifiability as a criterion of *demarcation* (not meaning); Ayer uses verifiability as a criterion of *meaning*. Popper explicitly distinguished his position from logical positivism on this point. See [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)]], [[Concepts/Problem of Demarcation (Kant and Popper)]].

### Vs. Russell on ethics/truth
Russell is a realist about truth (correspondence theory), which Ayer deflates with the redundancy theory. Russell believes moral philosophy can still be pursued rationally (even if not as a priori); Ayer makes moral judgments factually empty. See [[Concepts/Correspondence Theory of Truth (Russell)]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Ayer's verification principle is self-defeating on its own terms: is the principle itself analytic, or is it an empirical hypothesis? Ayer in the Appendix acknowledges it should be read as a "definition" — a methodological choice, not a fact. Quine pushes this further: see [[Concepts/Reductionism and Verification Theory of Meaning (Quine)]].
- > [!warning] The 1946 revised criterion (directly/indirectly verifiable) is still insufficient — Ayer admits it and subsequent analysts have pressed the point. The verification principle has never been given a fully satisfactory formulation.
- > [!warning] Ayer's emotive theory leaves no room for moral reasoning or moral progress — a counterintuitive result he partially addresses in the Appendix but never fully resolves.
