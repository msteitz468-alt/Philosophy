## Summary

Book I establishes that there is a chief human good (eudaimonia/happiness) that is complete and self-sufficient. It is an active life of the soul in accordance with reason and virtue. Political expertise is sovereign because it directs the other sciences and aims at the good of the city (greater and more godlike than for one person). The inquiry is practical: its end is doing, not just knowing.

---

## Key Sections and Ideas

### Teleological Framework and the Master Science
Every expertise, inquiry, action, and undertaking seeks some good. Ends are either activities or products. Controlling capacities have more desirable ends. Political expertise is the most architectonic: it determines which sciences are needed in the city, what people should learn, and legislates on what to do and abstain from. Its end contains the ends of the others and is the human good (for the city even more than for the individual).

### Method and Audience
Precision appropriate to the subject (ethics involves variation and convention; do not demand mathematical demonstration from oratory or vice versa). The young (or immature in character) are not suitable audience because inexperienced in the actions of life and led by emotion; the end is action, not knowledge. Requires prior good upbringing to grasp the “that” (the facts/principles).

### The Good and Happiness: Common Views and the Function Argument
Most people (and intellectuals) call the highest good “happiness” (living well and doing well). Vulgar identify it with pleasure (life of consumption/grazing cattle, like Sardanapallus). People of quality with honor (political life), but honor seems too external and dependent on others. Excellence/virtue is better but can be inactive or accompanied by misfortune.

The function (*ergon*) argument: just as the good for a flute-player, sculptor, etc. lies in performing their characteristic activity well, so for the human being the good lies in the characteristic activity of the soul in accordance with reason (or the best and most complete virtue) over a complete life. This is eudaimonia.

Happiness must be complete (lacking nothing) and self-sufficient.

External goods (prosperity, friends) are instruments or ornaments to excellent activity; a completely wretched person cannot be called happy.

### Three Lives
- Life of pleasure (slavish).
- Life of politics/honor (more superficial; excellence itself seems incomplete without activity).
- Life of reflection/theōria (to be investigated later).
- Life of money-making (compelled by need, instrumental).
