# The Conscious Mind — David J. Chalmers (1996)
> Chalmers's landmark case that phenomenal consciousness fails to supervene logically on the physical, so materialism is false — and his constructive program of "naturalistic dualism": a nonreductive theory of consciousness built on psychophysical laws, coherence with awareness, organizational invariance, and a double-aspect theory of information.

## Argument Spine
Consciousness (phenomenal experience, "what it is like") must be taken seriously as a datum. Chalmers distinguishes the phenomenal from the psychological concepts of mind, then argues via zombies, inverted qualia, epistemic asymmetry, Mary, and the failure of analysis that consciousness does not supervene logically on physical facts — so no reductive explanation can succeed and materialism is false. In its place he offers *naturalistic dualism*: experience is a fundamental feature governed by psychophysical laws. Candidate laws: the coherence of consciousness with awareness (functional access), the principle of organizational invariance (fading/dancing qualia arguments), and, speculatively, a double-aspect theory of information (information spaces realized both physically and phenomenally, flirting with panpsychism). Applications: strong AI is defended (machine consciousness is possible in principle) and quantum mechanics gets an Everett-style interpretation.

## Whole-Work Assessment

The vault's philosophy-of-mind axis now has both poles at full primary-source strength: Dennett's *Consciousness Explained* (1991) and its direct answer, *The Conscious Mind* (1996). The book supplies the canonical statements of the hard problem, the zombie/knowledge arguments, and the supervenience framework that all the vault's later consciousness material presupposes (Clark's deflationism, Searle's biological naturalism, the machine-consciousness pages). Its constructive half is what makes it more than a critique: coherence with awareness, organizational invariance, and the double-aspect information prototheory are the 1996 ancestors of *Reality+*'s it-from-bit and simulation-realism apparatus. Notably, this dualist delivers the vault's strongest *defense* of strong AI and machine consciousness — cutting across the Searle/Hofstadter contradiction on independent grounds. New contradictions sharpened rather than opened: Chalmers vs Dennett (now documented from both sides with each's reply to the other), Chalmers vs Searle (agreeing on irreducibility, splitting on dualism and AI).
