# The Inevitable - Kevin Kelly (2016)

> **The Inevitable: Understanding the 12 Technological Forces That Will Shape Our Future** (2016). By Kevin Kelly (b. 1952), co-founder of *Wired* magazine, author of *Out of Control* (1994) and *What Technology Wants* (2010). Kelly identifies 12 overlapping, codependent "inevitable" technological forces—rooted in the physics and mathematics of bits, networks, and computation—that will shape the next 30 years. These are not specific predictions or predestined details but strong aggregate biases/tendencies ("metatrends") that tilt culture toward continuous processes (present participles/verbs in -ing form). "Inevitable" here means momentum from underlying conditions that are still active; banning or resisting usually backfires. Embrace with "eyes wide open" to civilize particulars, maximize benefits, and manage harms. The future is "protopia"—incrementally better each day through constant becoming, not utopia or dystopia. We are at the "Beginning" of wiring up a planetary "holos" (superorganism/global mind of humans + machines).

## Nature of the source (read this first)

This is an optimistic but grounded futurist/techno-philosopher text by a long-time observer and participant in digital culture (Wired, Whole Earth Review, early online communities). Kelly draws on decades of watching technology unfold to argue that certain broad trajectories are "baked into" the nature of digital technology (cheap ubiquitous copying, networking, intelligence, etc.) independent of culture or politics in their general contours. Specifics (brands, exact products, etiquette) are decided by fads, commerce, and human choice and remain unpredictable. The book is structured as 12 chapters, one per force, with the forces presented as highly overlapping and mutually accelerating. The intro defines "inevitable" carefully (bias/momentum, not strict determinism or free-will denial) and advocates "vigilant, eyes-wide-open embrace" over prohibition. The final chapter ("Beginning") frames the current era as the awakening of a planetary-scale collaborative interface ("holos")—7+ billion human minds + silicon "neurons"—that is already the most reliable artifact humans have built.

The text is a digitized version of the 2016 Viking/Penguin book. It continues themes from Kelly's earlier work (the "technium" as technology's own evolutionary "wants" or biases, extending life and mind).

## The 12 Inevitable Forces (present participles of continuous action)

Kelly sorts emerging technological change into 12 verbs-in-progress. They are not discrete but overlapping and codependent:

- **Becoming**: Everything is in flux; products become services and processes; constant upgrading and maintenance (even of "intangible" bits/code/websites); everyone is a "perpetual newbie"; existence is chiefly maintenance. Shift from fixed nouns to fluid verbs. Protopia: incremental improvement amid new problems created by yesterday's solutions.

- **Cognifying**: Cheap, ubiquitous intelligence (AI) added to everything, like electrification or industrialization before it. AI as a "dumb" but tireless worker that gets smarter with use; applied to all jobs, objects, and processes. Not just robots replacing humans but humans + AI creating new value. The "manufacturing cost" of answers/cognition drops toward zero.

- **Flowing**: Bits and data in real-time streams rather than fixed stocks; on-demand access, real-time everything (news, entertainment, logistics). Copies are free and ubiquitous; the value shifts to flows, updates, and live interaction. "Streaming" becomes the default mode.

- **Screening**: The world mediated and experienced through screens/interfaces; reading becomes "screening" (scanning, filtering, interacting with dynamic content). Everything gets a screen layer; physical objects gain digital overlays and behaviors.

- **Accessing**: Ownership gives way to access/rental/on-demand (Uber, Netflix, cloud computing, Spotify). "Using" without possessing. Platforms and services replace products; access to the best version trumps ownership of a fixed one.

- **Sharing**: Peer-to-peer collaboration, platforms, and commons at unprecedented scale (Wikipedia, open source, Airbnb, ride-sharing). "Collaborative consumption" and "platform cooperativism." Value in what is shared and co-created rather than hoarded.

- **Filtering**: Curation, personalization, and recommendation to navigate abundance (search, feeds, AI curators). The problem of too much is solved by smart filtering; attention becomes the scarce resource managed by algorithms and human taste.

- **Remixing**: User-generated content, mashups, combinatorial creativity, endless derivatives. Everything is raw material for further remixing; "rip, mix, and burn" scaled to culture, code, and biology. Originality lies in novel combinations.

- **Interacting**: Immersive, real-time, bidirectional interfaces (VR/AR, games, voice, haptics). The screen becomes a portal for interaction rather than passive consumption; simulation and "being there" at scale.

- **Tracking**: Quantified self, total (but also sous)veillance, data exhaust, sensors everywhere. Everything and everyone is measured, logged, and modeled in real time. Privacy shifts; new social contracts around data.

- **Questioning**: AI shifts from search (answers) to oracles that answer *and* question back; Socratic dialogue with machines. Good questions become more valuable than cheap answers (Picasso: "Computers are useless. They only give you answers."). The frontier moves to asking better questions.

- **Beginning**: AI/robots destroy some jobs but create more new ones and new categories of work faster than they eliminate; a new era of entrepreneurship, prosumers, and "making." The holos begins; we are at the start of wiring up planetary intelligence. Endless newness rather than end of work.

These forces are "trajectories, not destinies." They are rooted in the biases of bits, networks, and computation (cheap copies, instant communication, scalable intelligence) and will persist for decades even as specifics change.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Technology has inherent biases (from physics/math) that favor certain aggregate outcomes (e.g., digital favors cheap ubiquitous duplication and networking). Culture can retard or accelerate expression but cannot easily reverse the general direction.
- "Inevitable" = strong momentum from active underlying conditions; prohibition usually backfires and creates worse problems (example: music industry vs. copying).
- Embrace + manage: "A vigilant, eyes-wide-open embrace works much better." Regulate particulars (Uber-like services) but do not attempt to prohibit the decentralization or other forces.
- Protopia over utopia/dystopia: Incremental, messy improvement ("a little better each day") that also creates new problems. The benefits compound over time into civilization but are rarely cinematic.
- Shift from products to processes, nouns to verbs, ownership to access, stocks to flows, answers to questions.
- We are perpetual newbies; constant upgrading is hygiene. The holos (global mind of humans + machines) is already running and reliable; we are inside its "Beginning."
- Questions will be the last (and highest) human job; good questions generate new territories, reframe answers, and seed innovation. The answer machine makes questions more valuable.
- Optimism grounded in history: despite disruptions (job loss, inequality, confusion), the net direction has been positive protopian progress since the Enlightenment/scientific method. Dystopias are unsustainable or not as lawless as portrayed; greed and organization reassert order.

Kelly positions this as continuation of his "technium" thesis: technology as an extension of life with its own evolutionary dynamics and "wants."

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **With control/alignment concerns (Bostrom, Wiener)**: If these forces are strongly biased and "inevitable" in aggregate, how do we steer the particulars (esp. cognifying/AI) to avoid existential or social harms? "Embrace" must include serious management; banning fails, but naive optimism also fails. Cross to [[Sources/Superintelligence - Nick Bostrom (2014)]] (orthogonality, instrumental convergence as risks within the inevitable) and [[Sources/The Human Use of Human Beings - Norbert Wiener (1950)]] (early warning on machines displacing judgment and the need for "know-what").
- **With limits/hype critiques (Mitchell, Hofstadter)**: Cognifying and the other forces sound like strong AI/singularity narratives that Mitchell (Hofstadter collaborator) critiques as overhyped. DL succeeds at pattern matching but lacks common sense/analogy/robust understanding; scaling may not deliver the full "cognifying." The 2014 Google meeting "terror" (Kurzweil + DeepMind) is the optimistic pole Kelly represents. Cross to [[Sources/Artificial Intelligence - Melanie Mitchell (2019)]] and [[Sources/Gödel, Escher, Bach - Douglas Hofstadter (1979)]].
- **With Kurzweil-style exponential optimism**: Kelly is more process-oriented and "protopian" (incremental, with new problems) than rupture/singularity 2045; shares the embrace of acceleration but emphasizes the mess and the value of questions over godlike answers. Cross to [[Sources/The Singularity Is Near - Ray Kurzweil (2005)]] (GNR, law of accelerating returns) and the 2026 AI capability sources (Grok/Claude/Gemini as partial realizations of cognifying/filtering/etc.).
- **With pre-modern or static views**: The forces accelerate the "becoming" that displaces fixed hierarchies/diagrams (see [[Sources/The Great Chain of Being - Arthur O. Lovejoy (1936)]] and classical/medieval sources). Technology as humanity's accelerant of flux.
- **Social/ethical**: Massive tracking, sharing, remixing, and cognifying raise questions of privacy, ownership, labor, inequality, and what it means to be human. Kelly acknowledges heartbreak and the need for new social contracts but argues the forces cannot be stopped—only shaped.
