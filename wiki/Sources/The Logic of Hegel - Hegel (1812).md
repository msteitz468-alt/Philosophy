# The Logic of Hegel - Hegel (1812)

> **The Logic of Hegel** (also known as the *Lesser Logic*, translated by William Wallace). Originally published as Part I of his *Encyclopaedia of the Philosophical Sciences* (1817), representing a condensed version of his massive *Science of Logic* (1812–1816). Hegel outlines the systematic development of pure conceptual thought, demonstrating that logic is identical to metaphysics: the categories of thought are the very categories of reality.

## Core Thesis: Logic as Metaphysics

Hegel rejects the traditional view that logic is merely a formal tool for organizing thoughts separate from things. Instead, **logic is the structure of the universe itself**.

-   **The Identity of Thought and Being**: Rational concepts do not merely represent the world; they constitute it. Reality is the self-unfolding of the "Logical Idea."
-   **Dialectical Progression**: The categories of metaphysics develop logically through three subdivisions:
    1.  *Being*: The immediate, external character of reality (as it is on the surface).
    2.  *Essence*: The reflective, internal ground of reality (the hidden laws, forces, and causes beneath the surface).
    3.  *The Notion / Concept*: The organic unity of Being and Essence, where reality is understood as a self-determining, conscious whole.

## Section Summary

### Preliminary Notion and Attitudes to Objectivity
-   **Definition of Logic**: The science of the pure Idea.
-   **Three Positions of Thought**:
    1.  *Dogmatism / Metaphysics (Descartes/Spinoza)*: Believes that reason can grasp the truth of things directly via abstract concepts.
    2.  *Empiricism and Critical Philosophy (Locke/Hume/Kant)*: Limits knowledge to experience and appearances, declaring the ultimate ground of reality unknowable.
    3.  *Immediate Knowledge (Jacobi)*: Relies on subjective feeling and faith to grasp the Absolute directly, bypassing conceptual thought.
-   Hegel argues that all three attitudes are one-sided; logic must combine conceptual rigor with organic development.

### I. The Doctrine of Being (Immediate Reality)
The category of reality as it immediately presents itself to observation:
-   **Quality**:
    *   *Being / Nothing / Becoming*: Pure Being (empty immediacy) is identical to Nothing; their dialectical transition is **Becoming**. See [[Concepts/Dialectical Method (Hegel)]].
    *   *Being-there (Dasein)*: Determinate being defined by limit and quality.
*   **Quantity**: Pure quantity (space/number) defined by size, which moves from continuous magnitude to discrete units (**Quantum**).
*   **Measure**: The qualitative quantity—the point where a quantitative change alters the quality of a thing (e.g., heating water to $100^\circ\text{C}$ quantitatively causes a qualitative change into steam).

### II. The Doctrine of Essence (Reflected Reality)
The category of reality seeking its own internal ground, showing that things exist in relation to their opposite:
-   **Essence as Ground of Existence**:
    *   The "Three Laws of Thought" are shown to be dynamic, not static: Identity requires Difference, and Difference drives toward **Contradiction**, which is the ground of all existence.
-   **Appearance**: Essence must manifest itself. The inner ground (essence) and the outer expression (appearance) are unified in Form, Matter, and Content.
-   **Actuality**: The complete unity of inner and outer. Hegel analyzes:
    *   *Substance and Accident* (Spinoza's monism)
    *   *Cause and Effect* (Hume's problem of causality)
    *   *Reciprocity (Interaction)*: The realization that cause and effect are mutually determining, leading to the concept of freedom and the Concept.

### III. The Doctrine of the Notion / Concept (Self-Conscious Reality)
The category of reality understood as an organic, self-determining whole:
-   **The Subjective Notion**: The traditional logical forms (Notion, Judgment, Syllogism) reinterpreted as active stages of developing concrete thought.
-   **The Object**: The objective world organized through:
    *   *Mechanism* (blind physical laws, e.g. Democritus)
    *   *Chemism* (chemical affinity and attraction)
    *   *Teleology* (purposive organization, e.g. Aristotle).
-   **The Idea**: The absolute unity of Subject and Object:
    *   *Life* (the organic body)
    *   *Cognition* (the pursuit of Truth and Goodness)
    *   *The Absolute Idea*: Pure self-conscious thought contemplating its own rational system.
