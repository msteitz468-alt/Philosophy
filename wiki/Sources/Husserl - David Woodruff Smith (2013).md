# Husserl (David Woodruff Smith, 2013)
> Second-edition monograph introduction to Edmund Husserl's complete philosophical system — phenomenology in interdependence with logic, ontology, epistemology, and ethics.

## Argument Spine

Husserl's philosophy is not phenomenology standing alone but a tightly interdependent system in which logic (pure theory of meaning), formal and material ontology, transcendental phenomenology (science of intentional consciousness), epistemology, and ethics all mutually support one another. Smith's thesis is that only by seeing phenomenology within this system — especially its dependence on formal ontology and its ground in the concept of "dependence" — can one understand Husserl's project or evaluate his legacy for contemporary philosophy of mind.

## Whole-Work Assessment

Smith's *Husserl* (2013) is the most systematic English-language account of Husserl's philosophy as a unified system rather than as phenomenology alone. Its central contribution to the vault is threefold: (1) it establishes [[Thinkers/Edmund Husserl]] as the vault's primary philosopher of mind and consciousness for the phenomenological tradition, completing the picture begun with Heidegger, Sartre, and Merleau-Ponty references; (2) it supplies the formal-ontological framework (Part, Whole, Dependence, Moment) that bridges Husserl to contemporary debates on the hard problem of consciousness and to the AI/cognitive science leg (linking to Dennett, Turing, and Searle); (3) it grounds the anti-naturalism of phenomenology in a way that creates a productive contradiction with the vault's naturalist sources (Dennett, Hofstadter, Shannon, Turing). The book deepens and substantially revises the Phenomenology school page, which previously had Husserl only at second-hand through Heidegger and Sartre. Nine concepts created (intentionality/noema, phenomenological reduction, formal/material ontology, essences/eidetic variation, transcendental ego/constitution, intersubjectivity/life-world, evidence/truth, pure logic/meaning, formal/material ethics), one contradiction (vs. naturalism), one new thinker (Franz Brentano), and cross-updates to Heidegger, Sartre, Aristotle, Kant, and Descartes.
