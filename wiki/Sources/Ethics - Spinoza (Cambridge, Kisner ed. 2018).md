# Ethics - Spinoza (Cambridge, Kisner ed. 2018)

> **Ethics Proved in Geometrical Order** (*Ethica ordine geometrico demonstrata*) by Benedict de Spinoza (Baruch Espinoza, 1632–1677). Cambridge Texts in the History of Philosophy edition, edited by Matthew J. Kisner, translated by Michael Silverthorne and Matthew J. Kisner (2018). The central work of Spinoza’s philosophy: a complete system (God/Nature, mind, emotions, bondage, freedom) demonstrated *more geometrico* from definitions and axioms. Posthumously published 1677 after Spinoza’s death; the only work of his published under his name in his lifetime was the commentary on Descartes’ *Principles*.

## Summary

Spinoza’s project (sketched in the earlier *Treatise on the Emendation of the Intellect*) is to find the true good and highest happiness through knowledge of the union of the mind with the whole of nature, and to derive from this the right conduct and plan of life. The *Ethics* delivers this as an integrated metaphysical and ethical system proved with Euclidean rigor.

The work is divided into five parts whose structure mirrors the movement from metaphysics to ethics:

**Part I: Of God**  
Definitions establish the basic vocabulary: cause of itself, finite, substance (in itself and conceived through itself), attribute (what the intellect perceives as constituting the essence of substance), mode (affections of substance), God (absolutely infinite substance consisting of infinite attributes), free, eternity.  
Axioms include the key rationalist principle (1a4): cognition of an effect depends upon cognition of its cause and involves it (conceptual dependence mirrors causal dependence).  
The propositions build to substance monism: there is only one substance (God or Nature, *Deus sive Natura*). Everything that is, is in God and cannot be or be conceived without God (1p15). God is *natura naturans* (nature naturing/active) when conceived as the attributes that cause all things, and *natura naturata* (nature natured) when conceived as the modes that follow from God. All things follow from God’s essence with the same necessity as properties follow from the definition of a triangle (causal determinism/necessitarianism). No purposes or final causes in nature/God (Appendix). Extended substance and thinking substance are attributes (or affections of attributes) of the one substance.

**Part II: Of the Nature and Origin of the Mind**  
Continues from 1p16: infinite things in infinite ways follow from God’s essence; here only those leading to knowledge of the human mind and its highest blessedness.  
Thought and Extension are attributes of God (God is a thinking thing and an extended thing). Parallelism (2p7): “The order and connection of ideas is the same as the order and connection of things.” The mode of extension (a body) and the idea of that mode (its mind) are one and the same thing expressed under different attributes. The mind is the idea of the body. Adequate vs. inadequate ideas; three kinds of cognition/knowledge: imagination (inadequate, from senses, source of falsity), reason (adequate, from common notions), and *scientia intuitiva* (intuitive knowledge of particulars as following from God’s essence—the highest). God’s power of thought equals his power of action.

**Part III: Of the Origin and Nature of the Emotions**  
Treat human emotions and actions geometrically, as part of nature following universal laws (no “empire within an empire”). Preface criticizes those who deplore or ridicule human nature instead of understanding it; also critiques Descartes’ view of the mind’s absolute power over the body.  
Conatus (3p6–7): Each thing, as far as it can by its own power, strives to persevere in its being. This striving is the actual essence of the thing.  
Affect/emotion: affections of the body by which its power of action is increased or diminished, together with the ideas of those affections. Active affects (actions) arise from adequate ideas (we act); passive affects/passions arise from inadequate ideas (we are acted on). Joy (increase in power), sadness (decrease), desire. All ideas have some power to affirm their content.

**Part IV: Of Human Servitude, or the Powers of the Affects**  
Good and evil are not intrinsic to things but relative to what helps or hinders our endeavor/power (4pref, 4p8). Virtue = power (4def8): acting from one’s own nature/adequate ideas. Bondage = being determined by external causes/passions. The “free person” (hypothetical ideal) is led only by reason, thinks of death least of all, is faithful, grateful, etc. Highest good = understanding of God (4p28). Blessedness begins to appear as the joy that accompanies the highest increase in power through knowledge.

**Part V: Of the Power of the Intellect, or of Human Freedom**  
The power of the mind over the affects consists in the knowledge of God/Nature (adequate ideas, especially the third kind). Intellectual love of God (*amor Dei intellectualis*): the joy that accompanies the intuitive knowledge of God as the cause of our joy, accompanied by the idea of God. This is eternal (insofar as the mind conceives things under a species of eternity); it is our highest blessedness and happiness. Freedom is acting from the necessity of one’s own nature (adequate ideas) rather than external compulsion. The more we understand God/Nature, the less we are subject to passions and the more we act freely and virtuously. “All things excellent are as difficult as they are rare” (5p42s).

The geometrical method is not merely expository: because conceptual relations are coextensive with causal relations in nature, deducing properties from definitions and axioms reveals the actual order of things.

## Historical and Philosophical Context

Spinoza (born Baruch, later Benedict) was excommunicated (*herem*) from the Portuguese Jewish community in Amsterdam in 1656 for his views (denial of personal immortality, etc.). He earned his living as a lens grinder, associated with Collegiants and freethinkers, studied Descartes (published a commentary on the *Principles*—his only work under his own name during his lifetime), and developed a radical naturalism. The *Ethics* was completed ~1675 but withdrawn from publication due to opposition (including from authorities); it appeared posthumously in 1677 along with other works.

It radicalizes and corrects Cartesianism: mechanistic science is retained and extended to mind and ethics, but dualism is rejected in favor of monism; God is not a transcendent creator with free will and purposes but identical with Nature (immanent cause); there is no separate realm of grace or supernatural. It also engages (and often opposes) Aristotelian/scholastic categories while using some of their vocabulary (substance, attribute, mode, essence). The work was (and is) highly controversial for its pantheism/panentheism, determinism, denial of free will in the libertarian sense, and secular ethics grounded in the understanding of nature rather than divine command.

Spinoza’s other major works include the *Theological-Political Treatise* (published anonymously 1670; biblical criticism, defense of freedom of thought and speech, separation of philosophy and theology) and the unfinished *Political Treatise*.

## Key Arguments and Tensions

- **Substance monism vs. pluralism/dualism**: Only one substance (God/Nature). Finite things are modes, not substances. Thought and Extension are parallel attributes of the same substance (not two substances interacting, contra Descartes). “Whatever is, is in God.”
- **Rationalism and parallelism**: The order of ideas = order of things (1a4 + 2p7). We can know the natures of things by analyzing the logical consequences of their essences/definitions. This justifies the geometrical method applied to metaphysics and ethics.
- **Conatus and naturalism**: Striving to persevere is the actual essence of every thing. Emotions are natural phenomena (increases/decreases in power of acting) explicable by the same laws as everything else. No teleology in nature (Appendix to Part I).
- **Freedom and determinism**: Compatibilist. Freedom = self-determination (being the adequate cause of one’s actions via adequate ideas), not absence of determination. God/Nature acts from the necessity of its essence. Human freedom increases with knowledge.
- **Ethics as knowledge**: Virtue, perfection, and blessedness consist in increasing one’s power through adequate ideas, especially intuitive knowledge of God/Nature (*scientia intuitiva*). The intellectual love of God is eternal and is our highest happiness. A secular, naturalistic ethics.
- **Three kinds of knowledge**: Imagination (inadequate, passive, source of bondage); Reason (adequate, common notions); Intuition (highest, direct grasp of particulars sub specie aeternitatis).

Tensions with predecessors (explored in vault cross-references): vs. Descartes’ dualism and free will; vs. Aristotelian teleology and separate substances; vs. traditional theism (personal God, creation ex nihilo, miracles, divine purposes/commands); vs. Stoic and Epicurean ethics (while sharing naturalism and emphasis on understanding); deep affinities with certain strands of medieval Jewish and Islamic rationalism (Maimonides) but radicalized.

  - Aristotle: shares substance/essence/attribute language and demonstration, but rejects multiple substances, hylomorphism (modes inhere in one substance), and final causes/teleology in favor of strict immanent necessity.
