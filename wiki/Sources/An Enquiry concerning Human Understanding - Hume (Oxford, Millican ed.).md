# An Enquiry concerning Human Understanding - Hume (Oxford, Millican ed.)

> **An Enquiry concerning Human Understanding** (1748; originally *Philosophical Essays concerning Human Understanding*, retitled 1758). By David Hume (1711–76). Oxford World's Classics edition: edited with introduction and notes by Peter Millican (2007), with appendices (the 1740 *Abstract* of the *Treatise*, "Of the Immortality of the Soul," excerpts from the *Dialogues concerning Natural Religion*, selected letters, and "My Own Life"). Hume's recasting in essay form of the epistemology of Book I of *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40), and "the definitive statement of his mature theoretical philosophy." The single most influential statement of empiricist epistemology; source of the problem of induction, the regularity theory of causation, compatibilism, and the argument against miracles.

## Summary

The *Enquiry* is a connected chain of twelve short sections that builds a "science of human nature" (*moral philosophy* in the old sense) on strictly empirical foundations, then turns that science back on itself to mark the narrow bounds of human reason. Hume's project is descriptive and naturalistic: not to legislate how the mind *ought* to reason a priori, but to observe, by the "experimental method," how it actually does — and to find that our most fundamental operations (causal inference, belief, the expectation of regularity) rest not on reason but on **custom** (habit) and feeling.

The structure moves from the theory of ideas, through the demolition of rationalist accounts of causation and induction, to a "sceptical solution" grounding inference in instinct, and finally to applications (necessity and liberty, animal reason, miracles, providence) and a concluding statement of **mitigated scepticism**.

- **Section I — Of the Different Species of Philosophy**: Two kinds of moral philosophy: the "easy and obvious" (man as active being; virtue painted in amiable colours) and the "accurate and abstruse" (man as reasonable being; the anatomist of the mind). Defends the abstruse kind as subservient to the easy, and as the only "catholic remedy" against superstition. Announces the program: an "exact analysis of the powers and capacity" of the understanding ("mental geography"), in hopes of also reaching its "secret springs and principles" (a Newton of the mind).

- **Section II — Of the Origin of Ideas**: All perceptions divide into **impressions** (lively: sensations, passions, emotions) and **ideas** (faint copies in thought/memory/imagination), distinguished by *force and vivacity*. The **Copy Principle**: every simple idea is a copy of a prior simple impression; the mind's creativity is only compounding/transposing/augmenting/diminishing materials given by the senses. Test for meaning: "from what impression is that supposed idea derived?" — if none, the term is empty. The one "contradictory phaenomenon": the **missing shade of blue** (a person could supply an unseen shade), which Hume admits but dismisses as too singular to overturn the maxim.

- **Section III — Of the Association of Ideas**: Three principles of connexion among ideas: **Resemblance, Contiguity** (in time or place), and **Cause or Effect**. A gentle "gravitational" psychology of thought.

- **Section IV — Sceptical Doubts concerning the Operations of the Understanding**: Introduces **Hume's Fork**: all objects of reason are either *Relations of Ideas* (a priori, intuitive/demonstrative, their contraries imply contradiction — geometry, algebra, arithmetic) or *Matters of Fact* (whose contrary is always possible and conceivable). All reasoning about matters of fact (beyond present sense/memory) rests on **cause and effect**; knowledge of cause and effect comes *entirely from experience*, never a priori (Adam could not infer drowning from water's fluidity; one billiard ball's motion is a wholly distinct event from another's). Part II poses the **problem of induction**: all factual inference presupposes that *the future will resemble the past* ("the course of nature continues uniformly the same"), but this Uniformity Principle can be supported neither by demonstration (its denial implies no contradiction) nor by experience (that would be circular, presupposing the very point at issue). Hence inductive inference is *not founded on reasoning or any process of the understanding*.

- **Section V — Sceptical Solution of these Doubts**: The doubt is theoretical only; "Nature will always maintain her rights." If inference is not made by reason, it is made by **Custom or Habit**: after repeated constant conjunction, the mind is *determined* by habit to pass from the impression of one object to a lively idea of its usual attendant. Custom is "the great guide of human life." Part II analyses **belief** as not a separate idea but a *manner of conception* — a "more vivid, lively, forcible, firm, steady conception" — a *feeling* that cannot be summoned at will. A present impression enlivens the associated idea (a "pre-established harmony" between the course of nature and the succession of our ideas), secured by instinct rather than the slow, fallible deductions of reason.

- **Section VI — Of Probability**: There is no chance in the world, only our ignorance. Probability of *chances* (dice) and of *causes* (mixed evidence) both reduce to custom: the mind transfers past frequencies to the future, weighting belief in proportion to the observed superiority of instances.

- **Section VII — Of the Idea of Necessary Connexion**: The hardest idea (power, force, energy, necessary connexion) submitted to the Copy Principle: *find its impression.* It is not found in external objects (we see only one event follow another, never the tie); not in the will's command over the body (we learn its limits and the muscle/nerve mediation only by experience); not in the will's command over ideas. Critiques **occasionalism** (the Malebranchean "God is the only real cause") as "fairy land" — too bold, and resting on the same ignorance it exploits. The idea's true source: after *constant conjunction*, the mind feels a **customary transition** from one object to its attendant; *this internal impression of determination* is the origin of the idea of necessity. Hence the famous **two definitions of cause**: (1) *an object followed by another, where all objects similar to the first are followed by objects similar to the second* (constant conjunction; with a counterfactual gloss — "where, if the first had not been, the second never had existed"); (2) *an object followed by another, whose appearance always conveys the thought to that other* (the mind's determination). "All events seem entirely loose and separate… They seem conjoined, but never connected."

- **Section VIII — Of Liberty and Necessity**: A "reconciling project" (compatibilism). The dispute is merely verbal. **Necessity** in matter is nothing but constant conjunction + the mind's inference; and *the same two circumstances are universally observed in human action* (the uniformity of human nature across ages and nations; the historian's, politician's, and judge's reliance on it; "natural" and "moral" evidence form one chain — the prisoner foresees death from the gaoler's constancy as from the axe). So all mankind have always tacitly held the doctrine of necessity as applied to the will. **Liberty** is not absence of causation (that would be chance) but *the power of acting or not acting according to the determinations of the will* — "hypothetical liberty," belonging to everyone not in chains. Both doctrines are *essential to morality*: reward/punishment, praise/blame presuppose that actions flow from durable character (necessity), and attach only to actions that do so flow (liberty). Closes by conceding the hard theological problem (if God is the ultimate cause of a necessary chain, He is author of sin) as a mystery beyond reason's province.

- **Section IX — Of the Reason of Animals**: All factual reasoning is analogical. Animals plainly learn from experience and infer effects from causes; they cannot do so by abstract argument (which escapes even philosophers in action). Therefore the inferential mechanism is **custom/instinct**, common to beasts, infants, and philosophers alike — confirming Section V. "Experimental reasoning itself… is nothing but a species of instinct."

- **Section X — Of Miracles**: "A wise man proportions his belief to the evidence." Testimony's authority rests entirely on experienced conformity of reports to facts. A **miracle is a violation of the laws of nature**, against which there is "a firm and unalterable experience" — "as entire as any argument from experience can possibly be." The maxim (Part I): *no testimony suffices to establish a miracle unless its falsehood would be more miraculous than the fact it endeavours to establish.* Part II: as a matter of fact, no miracle has ever met this bar — owing to (1) want of witnesses of sufficient number, integrity, education, and public exposure; (2) the human propensity to wonder and surprise; (3) miracle reports abounding chiefly among "ignorant and barbarous nations"; (4) the mutual destruction of the rival miracles of competing religions. Examines and dismisses even the best-attested cases (Vespasian, the Jansenist Abbé Pâris, Cardinal de Retz). Limits the claim to *religious* miracles offered as foundations of a system; closes with the ironic fideist coda: "Our most holy religion is founded on Faith, not on reason," and whoever defends it by reason exposes it to a trial it cannot endure.

- **Section XI — Of a Particular Providence and of a Future State**: A dialogue: a friend, speaking as "Epicurus" before the Athenians, defends the political harmlessness of denying providence and an afterlife by attacking the **design argument**. The rule: *the cause must be proportioned to the effect; we may ascribe to a cause only what is exactly sufficient to produce the observed effect, and may not then "return back" from the inferred cause to infer further effects.* From a finite, imperfect, mixed world one cannot infer an infinitely perfect, just deity, nor a future state of rewards/punishments not visible in the present. The religious hypothesis is therefore "useless" — it can yield no new fact beyond the phenomena themselves.

- **Section XII — Of the Academical or Sceptical Philosophy**: Distinguishes (a) *antecedent* scepticism (the Cartesian universal doubt of faculties) — incurable if taken absolutely, useful only in a moderate, "begin with self-evident principles, advance by sure steps" form; from (b) *consequent* scepticism arising after inquiry. Surveys sceptical arguments against the senses (the representative theory: we know only perceptions, never their connexion to external objects; secondary *and* primary qualities collapse into the mind) and against reason (infinite divisibility paradoxes). Then **Pyrrhonism** (excessive scepticism): its "great subverter" is *action and common life* — its doubts "vanish like smoke" before "the first and most trivial event in life"; "Nature is always too strong for principle." The lasting fruit is **mitigated (Academical) scepticism**: (1) a durable *modesty and diffidence* curing dogmatism; and (2) the **limitation of inquiry** to subjects "best adapted to the narrow capacity of human understanding" — common life and the proper objects of knowledge. The closing **fork**: only *quantity and number* (demonstration) and *matters of fact* (experience) are proper objects of inquiry. Hence the incendiary conclusion: take any volume of "divinity or school metaphysics" — *"Does it contain any abstract reasoning concerning quantity or number? No. Does it contain any experimental reasoning concerning matter of fact and existence? No. Commit it then to the flames: for it can contain nothing but sophistry and illusion."*

## Historical and Philosophical Context

Hume (b. Edinburgh) published *A Treatise of Human Nature* anonymously at age 28; it "fell dead-born from the press." Convinced the failure was one of *manner* rather than matter, he recast Book I's theoretical philosophy as the *Enquiry* (1748), shorter, more elegant, more pointed, and now openly carrying the irreligious applications (miracles, providence) he had prudently suppressed in the *Treatise*. The *Enquiry* is the work Hume wished to be judged by ("the following Pieces may alone be regarded as containing my philosophical sentiments and principles"). Millican's edition foregrounds the *Enquiry* (not the *Treatise*) as the mature statement.

Intellectually, Hume stands downstream of **Locke** (the theory of ideas, anti-innatism, experience as the source of all materials — Section II is "derivative from Locke") and **Berkeley** (immaterialism, the critique of abstraction invoked in Section XII), and is the culmination of British **empiricism**. He radicalizes it: where Locke and Berkeley retained substance, real powers, or God to stabilize the system, Hume follows the empiricist principle to its sceptical and naturalistic conclusion — there is no impression of power, of the self as substance, or of necessary connexion in objects. He is the central figure of the **Scottish Enlightenment** (with Adam Smith, Thomas Reid as critic, etc.), and the proximate cause of **Kant's** "Copernican revolution": Kant credited Hume with interrupting his "dogmatic slumber" on the question of causation (now fully primary-sourced via [[Sources/Metaphysics of Morals - Immanuel Kant]] / [[Thinkers/Kant]] + the complete *Groundwork*). Kant responds with a pure a priori metaphysics of morals: Second Section delivers the detailed diagnosis of all prior ethics (including Humean sentiment/custom/utility) as heteronomous (law from "alien" objects or feelings, yielding only conditional hypothetical imperatives) and establishes autonomy of the will (self-legislation under universalizable maxims) as the sole supreme principle, plus the system of CI formulas (universal law, Formula of Humanity as end in itself, Kingdom of Ends); Third Section grounds it in freedom as the key to autonomy (positive concept: causality under self-given laws), resolves the freedom vs. natural necessity dialectic via two standpoints (sensible/appearance under custom/natural law vs. intelligible/thing in itself under moral law, "as if" member of the intelligible world), and notes the incomprehensibility of how pure reason can motivate without sensible interest. The volume's essays (Schneewind on the historical autonomy revolution vs. voluntarist/intellectualist/naturalist; Baron clarifying acting from duty; Kagan exploring foundations for consequentialists; Wood on the three features and community of ends) further illuminate the response. This completes the vault's Early Modern ethics arc with the rationalist/deontological pole.

Against the rationalists already in the vault: Hume's empiricism is the direct antithesis of **Descartes** (no innate ideas; clear-and-distinct conceivability proves *possibility*, never *existence*; the *cogito* yields no impression of an enduring self) and of **Spinoza/Leibniz** necessitarianism (the Principle of Sufficient Reason and a priori insight into causes are exactly what Hume denies we possess). He engages the **occasionalists** (Malebranche) and, through them, the same causation debate the vault holds via **Ghazali and Ibn Rushd**: Hume agrees with the occasionalists *that* no necessary connexion is observed in bodies, but rejects their theological remedy and relocates "necessity" into the observer's mind.

## Structure and Edition

- Section I–XII (the text proper, 1748 final form).
- Hume's own Endnotes (lettered).
- **Appendix I**: *An Abstract of A Treatise of Human Nature* (1740) — Hume's own anonymous summary, the clearest short statement of the induction/causation argument.
- **Appendix II**: "Of the Immortality of the Soul" (suppressed essay).
- **Appendix III**: excerpts from the *Dialogues concerning Natural Religion* (the fuller treatment of the design argument).
- **Appendix IV**: excerpts from Hume's Letters.
- **Appendix V**: "My Own Life" (the brief, serene autobiography written months before his death).

Millican's introduction is a leading scholarly treatment: it defends the *Enquiry*'s priority, tracks each section's argument, and addresses interpretive controversies (e.g., whether Hume is a sceptic, a naturalist, or a "sceptical realist" about causation — the "New Hume" debate).

## Key Arguments and Tensions

- **Empiricism vs. rationalism**: the Copy Principle and Hume's Fork are anti-Cartesian engines. Conceivability gives modal/logical possibility, never matters of fact; only experience reaches existence. Direct counterpoint to [[Concepts/Clear and Distinct Perception (Descartes)]], [[Arguments/Ontological Argument|Ontological Argument]], and Spinoza's [[Concepts/Geometrical Method - Rationalism (Spinoza)]].
- **Scepticism about reason that ends in naturalism**: the *negative* argument (induction unfounded by reason) is followed by a *positive* psychology (custom, belief as vivacity, instinct). Hume is not a paralyzed Pyrrhonist; he is a naturalist who explains the very beliefs reason cannot justify.
- **Three positions on scepticism in the vault**: Pyrrhonian suspension → ataraxia ([[Thinkers/Sextus Empiricus]]); Cartesian hyperbolic doubt → certainty ([[Concepts/Method of Hyperbolic Doubt (Descartes)]]); Humean **mitigated scepticism** → modesty + limitation to common life and the two proper objects of inquiry. Section XII explicitly names and positions against both Pyrrho and "Des Cartes."
- **Compatibilism**: redefines necessity (as constant conjunction + inference) and liberty (as unconstrained action per the will) so that determinism and moral responsibility are not merely compatible but mutually required. Contrast with Spinoza's harder necessitarianism (freedom as adequate causation, no contingency) and Stoic fate/assent.
- **The regularity theory and its tension**: the two definitions of cause are admitted to be "drawn from circumstances foreign to the cause." Whether Hume *denies* real powers (regularity theory) or merely denies we can *know/represent* them (the "New Hume," sceptical realism) is the central interpretive crux.
- **Religion**: miracles (epistemic, Section X), providence/design (metaphysical, Section XI), immortality (Appendix II), and natural religion (Appendix III) form a sustained, ironic, deniable assault — always couched in protestations of fideism.

## Relevance to the Vault and Cross-References

The empiricist hinge of the Early Modern cluster, and the most disruptive epistemology in the vault. Where [[Thinkers/Descartes]] sought certainty and [[Thinkers/Spinoza]] sought a complete deductive system, Hume shows the *limits* of both — and replaces foundations with **custom, feeling, and instinct**.

- **Classical / Early-Modern cluster**:
  - Vs. **Descartes**: no innate ideas (Copy Principle vs. trademark/innatism); conceivability proves possibility not existence (undercuts the God proofs); the self yields no impression (deflates the *cogito* as substance). Hume *uses* Descartes' "clear and distinct conceivability" but only to establish that the contrary of any matter of fact is *possible*.
  - Vs. **Spinoza**: denies the a priori intelligibility of nature and the Principle of Sufficient Reason; "all events seem entirely loose and separate." Spinoza's necessity is in the things; Hume's is in the mind.
  - Vs. **Sextus Empiricus**: shares the diagnosis (no rational ground for inference) but rejects suspension; "Nature is always too strong for principle." Mitigated scepticism = Academical, not Pyrrhonian. See [[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]], [[Concepts/Equipollence (Isosthenia) and the Modes]].
  - Vs. **Epicurus / Lucretius**: Section XI ventriloquizes Epicurus against providence; Hume's naturalism, anti-superstition, and "proportion belief to evidence" are deeply Epicurean in spirit (cf. [[Concepts/Epicurean Atomism (Swerve)]], [[Concepts/Ataraxia]]), though Hume is sceptical where Epicurus was dogmatic about atoms.
  - Vs. **Aristotle / Aquinas**: dissolves the four causes and "real natures"; Section XI is a frontal attack on the design argument (cf. Aquinas' Fifth Way) using the proportionality rule. Engages the same causation question as [[Concepts/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation (Ghazali and Ibn Rushd)]] — Hume sides with neither real powers nor occasional causes, but with constant conjunction + mental habit.
  - Vs. **Stoics**: Section V opens by warning that philosophy (like Epictetus' Stoicism) can become "a more refined system of selfishness"; Hume's compatibilism contrasts with Stoic fate + assent ([[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)]]).
  - Vs. **Kant** (full *Groundwork* now primary-sourced): Hume grounds morality in sentiment, custom/habit as "the great guide of life," and utility (mitigated scepticism + naturalism); Kant's a priori autonomy, categorical (vs. hypothetical) imperatives, heteronomy critique (explicitly encompassing sentiment/custom as yielding only conditioned principles), Formula of Humanity, Kingdom of Ends, freedom via two standpoints ("as if" intelligible self), and incomprehensibility of pure practical motivation directly respond to and tension with Hume's entire project. "Dogmatic slumber" awakening now has its full textual response in the vault (see [[Sources/Metaphysics of Morals - Immanuel Kant]], [[Thinkers/Kant]], and Kantian concepts incl. Autonomy and Heteronomy, Two Standpoints, Kingdom of Ends). Productive synthesis: Hume supplies the sharpest empiricist limits and naturalistic psychology; Kant supplies the rational guardrails and "constitutional" form without which custom alone cannot secure unconditional duty or dignity. Essays add historical framing (autonomy as revolution giving equal moral status to all rational beings) and contemporary bridges (Kantian foundations potentially usable even by consequentialists).
- **AI / Agentic / Vault meta**:
  - The **problem of induction** is the deepest statement of the limits of learning from data: every generalization from past instances presupposes an unprovable uniformity. Directly relevant to model generalization, distribution shift, and the epistemic status of any pattern induced from a finite corpus.
  - **Belief as vividness / custom as inference** anticipates connectionist and predictive-processing pictures of cognition: inference as a trained, sub-symbolic transition strengthened by frequency, not a rule applied by reason. "Experimental reasoning is a species of instinct."
  - **"Proportion your belief to the evidence"** and the testimony calculus (Section X) are a near-Bayesian standard for weighing reports — a rubric for source reliability, provenance, and the vault's own contradiction-flagging.
  - **Hume's Fork** and the "commit it to the flames" test are a sharp meaning/verification filter — a precursor to logical-positivist criteria and a useful "lint" heuristic (does a claim reduce to relations of ideas or to matters of fact?).
  - **Mitigated scepticism** = the ideal maintainer's temperament: durable modesty + limitation of inquiry to what the method can actually reach (common life, the index, the sources), leaving "the origin of worlds" to poets and priests.
- **Personal / Career**: proportioning belief to evidence in underwriting and risk assessment (CPCU); the testimony calculus for evaluating claims and sources; the uniformity-of-human-nature thesis as the working premise of sales, negotiation, and judgment of character (cf. [[Concepts/Persuasion & Sales Science]]); custom/habit as the real engine of expertise (cf. [[Concepts/Expertise & Deliberate Practice]]).

**Contradictions / Open Questions**:
- **"New Hume" debate**: regularity theory (no real powers) vs. sceptical realism (real powers exist but are unknowable/unrepresentable). The two definitions of cause and talk of "secret powers" cut both ways.
- The **status of the missing shade of blue**: a genuine counterexample to the Copy Principle that Hume waves away — does it undermine the central tool?
- Whether the **"sceptical solution"** truly answers the doubt or merely *describes* the belief reason cannot justify (does naturalism dissolve or dodge the normative question?).
- **Self-application**: if all factual reasoning is custom and possibly "fallacious and deceitful," what is the epistemic status of Hume's own science of human nature? (The Section XII tension between excessive and mitigated scepticism.)
- The **miracle argument**: does Hume beg the question by building "uniform experience against" the miraculous into the definition of a law of nature? (The classic objection.)
