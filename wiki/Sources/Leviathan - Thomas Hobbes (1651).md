# Leviathan - Thomas Hobbes (1651)

> **Leviathan or The Matter, Forme and Power of a Common-Wealth Ecclesiasticall and Civil**. Written during the English Civil War, Hobbes's masterwork establishes the foundation of modern social contract theory, arguing that the only way to escape the brutal chaos of the State of Nature is through absolute, undivided political sovereignty.

## Core Thesis: The Necessity of Absolute Sovereignty

Hobbes's central argument is that human beings, in their natural state without a governing authority, exist in a state of war where everyone is set against everyone else. Because human life in this state is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short," rational self-interest drives individuals to enter a **Social Contract**. They collectively agree to surrender their individual rights and power to an absolute sovereign (the "Leviathan") in exchange for peace, order, and physical security. To maintain this security, the sovereign must hold absolute, indivisible, and permanent power, and subjects retain no right of rebellion.

## Section Summary

### Part I: Of Man
-   **Materialist Psychology**: Hobbes begins with a mechanistic analysis of human beings, asserting that all thought, memory, and emotion are patterns of matter in motion (sense perceptions).
-   **Desire and Felicity**: Humans are driven by desires; "felicity" is the continual progress of desire from one object to another. There is no ultimate good (*summum bonum*) or ultimate rest, only a perpetual, restless desire for power after power that ceases only in death.
-   **The State of Nature**: Because humans are roughly equal in physical and mental capacity, and all seek the same scarce resources, they live in constant fear and competition. This creates a state of war where there is no law, no justice, and no industry. See [[Concepts/State of Nature (Hobbes)]].
-   **Laws of Nature**: Reason discovers certain rules of self-preservation (the Laws of Nature), the first of which is to seek peace, and the second to defend oneself by all means. The third is that covenants must be performed.

### Part II: Of Commonwealth
-   **The Social Contract**: To make the Laws of Nature binding, a common power must be created. This is achieved by every individual transferring their right of self-governance to a sovereign person or assembly. See [[Concepts/Social Contract (Hobbes)]].
-   **Absolute Sovereignty**: The sovereign is not a party to the contract, so they cannot breach it. The sovereign has absolute control over the military, the judiciary, legislation, and public speech. Divisible power is rejected because "a kingdom divided in itself cannot stand."
-   **Forms of Commonwealth**: Evaluates Monarchy, Aristocracy, and Democracy. Hobbes strongly favors monarchy because a single ruler's private interest is closely aligned with the public interest, preventing factionalism.

### Part III: Of a Christian Commonwealth
-   **Subordination of Church to State**: Hobbes argues that there is no independent spiritual power. The sovereign is the supreme head of both the state and the church (Erastianism). 
-   **Scriptural Interpretation**: Analyzes scripture to show that the Kingdom of God is not of this world, and that Christians are commanded to obey their civil rulers in all outward actions, even when those rulers are non-Christian.

### Part IV: Of the Kingdom of Darkness
-   **Critique of False Religion**: Denounces the Roman Catholic Church, spiritualist sects, and pagan philosophy for fabricating false doctrines (such as purgatory, demonology, and the immortality of the soul separate from the body) to subvert civil authority and seize political power.

## Integration and Vault Value

-   **Social Contract Paradigm**: Serves as the starting point of early modern political contract theory, providing a stark materialist contrast to John Locke's rights-based constitutionalism. See [[Contradictions/Hobbesian Absolutism vs. Lockean Constitutionalism]].
-   **Epistemological Mechanism**: Connects to the vault's materialist philosophy, anticipating modern behaviorism and physicalist theories of mind.
-   **Critique of Idealism**: Rejects scholastic teleology and the Aristotelian view of human beings as naturally social or political animals (*zoon politikon*), asserting instead that society is an artificial construct built to escape fear.
