# Aristotle (Pseudo) - Economics

> The *Oikonomikos* (1343a), traditionally transmitted with the Aristotelian corpus but generally attributed by modern scholarship to a student of Aristotle or to Theophrastus. Three short books: Book I, household management proper (*oikonomia* in its original sense — governing the *oikos*, not "economics" in the modern sense), on the relations of husband/wife, master/slave, and the acquisition and use of property; Book II, a catalogue of historical revenue-raising stratagems used by kings, governors, cities, and individuals in financial straits; Book III (extant only in Latin translation), on the virtues proper to husband and wife.

## Argument Spine

Household management (*oikonomia*) is prior to and the component part of statecraft (*politikē*): a nation is an assemblage of households sufficient for a civilized life, so the art that creates and orders the household is older than the art that orders the nation. The household has two components — persons and property. Governing the human component begins with the natural, most fundamental relations: husband and wife (procreation and the "happy," not merely bare, existence distinctive of human as opposed to animal pairing) and master and slave (property management extended to human chattels, requiring a mean between harsh cruelty and permissive insolence). Governing the material component requires the fourfold competence of acquiring, preserving, improving, and using property, with natural (agricultural) acquisition ranked above extractive (mining) and far above coercive (war) or merely consensual-but-unproductive (trade, wage-labor) modes. Book II generalizes this household-management framework upward to four types of political administration (royal, satrapal, republican, private) and their four to six revenue streams, illustrated by a long catalogue of historical fiscal expedients — many involving deception, forced loans, currency debasement, and religious pretext — offered as practically useful precedent rather than moral exemplars. Book III (via the Latin) closes by specifying the complementary virtues of a good wife (household governance, chastity, endurance of adversity, deference in external affairs) and a good husband (fidelity, self-restraint, honorable treatment, provision of a household "philia" analogous to the concord Homer praises in Odysseus and Penelope).

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Household management is prior to and continuous with statecraft**, differing chiefly in that a household has "a single ruler" while a nation's government "is in many hands"; both nonetheless cover "acquiring" and "conducting the affairs of" their respective communities.
- **Natural gender complementarity, not sameness**: man and woman are assigned by "Divine Providence" and differing natural faculties to complementary tasks (man for external defense/acquisition, woman for internal safekeeping/handicraft) "though contributing to the same end" — human pairing aims at a "happy existence," not mere procreation as among lower animals.
- **Slave management as a mean**: slaves should be given "work, and a sufficiency of food," neither food-without-labor-and-discipline (breeds insolence) nor labor-and-discipline-without-food (breeds resentment and weakness); the prospect of eventual freedom is endorsed as both "just and expedient" motivation.
- **Fourfold competence of household property**: acquisition, preservation, improvement, and use — acquisition without preservation is "baling with a colander"; productive property should exceed unproductive, and total ruin should never be risked at once.
- **Ranking of modes of acquiring wealth**: agriculture (most honest, "not derived from other men," and character-forming) > extraction (mining) > trade/wage-labor (consensual but from others) > war (coercive) — an implicit ethical hierarchy of economic activity that anticipates *Politics* Book I's distinction of natural vs. unnatural (chrematistic) wealth-getting.
- **Personal supervision principle**: "no one takes the same care of another's property as of his own," so the master/mistress should personally oversee their domain rather than delegate entirely — the Persian and Laconian systems commended for close accounting.
- **Book II's revenue catalogue** (Cypselus, Dionysius of Syracuse, Mausolus, Timotheus, Chabrias, Cleomenes, and many others) documents recurring fiscal techniques: forced "loans" never repaid, currency debasement/reissue, religious pretext for confiscation (temple ornaments, false oracles), manipulated price controls, tax farming, and manufactured emergencies — presented value-neutrally as precedent, not endorsed morally, illustrating the gap between the household-management ethics of Book I and the realpolitik of state finance.
- **Book III's marital ethic**: the good wife governs the interior household, defers to her husband in external affairs, and above all endures adversity with loyalty (Penelope/Alcestis exempla vs. Clytemnestra/Agamemnon as counter-exempla); the good husband approaches his wife with self-restraint and "reverent fear" (modeled on Homeric Helen-to-Priam and Odysseus-to-Nausicaa), and marital *homonoia* ("unity of mind... for righteous ends") is presented, via Homer, as the household's highest good — the domestic analogue of civic concord.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The gap between Book I's ethically-framed household management (mean-structured, virtue-oriented, echoing the *Nicomachean Ethics*') and Book II's amoral catalogue of state fiscal deception is left unresolved by the text itself — flagged as an internal tension rather than a contradiction with other wiki pages, since the work itself seems to separate household ethics from raw statecraft technique without reconciling them.
- The naturalized gender-complementarity argument here should be read alongside, and is consistent with, the *Politics*' treatment of household rule (see [[Concepts/Classification of Constitutions (Aristotle)]] and the *Politics* Book I household chapters, not yet separately ingested as a concept page) — no direct contradiction, but a thinner and more prescriptive treatment than the vault currently has cross-linked.
