# Incoherence of the Incoherence - Ibn Rushd

> **Tahafut al-Tahafut** (The Incoherence of the Incoherence) by Averroes (Ibn Rushd, 1126–1198 CE): the last great philosopher of Islamic Spain (al-Andalus, Cordoba). A point-by-point defense of Aristotelian philosophy (as transmitted via Farabi and Avicenna, with Neoplatonic coloring) against Al-Ghazali’s *Tahafut al-Falasifa* (Incoherence of the Philosophers). Editor/translator Simon van den Bergh’s introduction provides essential historical framing of the transmission of Greek thought into Islam and the clash between *falasifa* (philosophers) and *mutakallimun* (theologians).

## Summary

Ghazali (d. 1111), building on Ash‘arite occasionalist theology (itself influenced by Stoic sensationalism, nominalism, and skepticism), attacks the philosophers on ~20 points, focusing on the eternity of the world (the longest discussion), God’s nature/attributes/knowledge/creation, and in the “natural sciences” section: denial of necessary causal connections in the world, the soul’s spirituality and immortality, and bodily resurrection. Averroes, the most scrupulous and literal commentator on Aristotle in the Islamic tradition, follows Ghazali’s arguments almost point-for-point and refutes them in defense of demonstrative philosophy, real essences/natures, secondary causation, and the eternity of the cosmos as the proper Aristotelian position.

The work is not a simple “philosophy vs. faith” tract. Averroes repeatedly affirms that religious law (Sharia) and the prophetic message are necessary for the virtue and happiness of the masses, that philosophers must not publicly undermine the principles of religion (which function as a political art blending reason and inspiration), and that certain religious truths (e.g., resurrection in some form) are to be accepted even if their full causes are beyond ordinary demonstration. He accuses Ghazali of sophistry, equivocation (especially on “eternal will”), inconsistency, and weakening the very intellect and science whose possibility the theologians claim to defend.

## Historical and Philosophical Context (from the Introduction)

- **Transmission**: Sudden, violent impact of Hellenism on early Islam (7th c. conquests). Major translation movement 750–850 CE (often via Christian Syrians). Aristotle (“the master of those who know”) studied through Neoplatonic commentators; Plotinus seen as harmonizing Plato and Aristotle.
- **Theologians**: Mu‘tazilites (rational, optimistic, Stoic-influenced *lumen naturale*, best of all possible worlds). Ash‘arites (more skeptical; right/wrong not applicable to God in the same way; occasionalist leanings, denial of real secondary causation; strong influence on Ghazali). Ghazali (like Augustine) moved through skepticism to mystical certitude via grace, yet remained a powerful dogmatist and critic of philosophy’s pretensions to mathematical-like certainty in metaphysics.
- **The Philosophers**: Followers of Aristotle via Avicenna and Farabi, with emanationist (Plotinian) overlay on creation. Key flashpoints: eternity vs. temporal creation; real vs. occasionalist causation; God’s knowledge (universals/self vs. particulars); nature of the soul and its survival.
- **Averroes’ Stance**: Last major Islamic philosopher; better known in Latin Europe (“The Commentator”) than in the East (few Arabic works survived there). Immense later influence on medieval/Renaissance thought, Latin Averroism, and the Paris condemnations (1270/1277). Aquinas engages him constantly—often correcting his (or Avicenna’s) Aristotle on the soul/intellect while using the commentaries.

Ghazali’s book is unsystematic and repetitive; Averroes mirrors the structure while providing clearer philosophical analysis.

## Structure

**Preface + Long Introduction** (historical framing, summary of the debate, editor’s analysis of Aristotle’s tensions, Ghazali’s methods, and the equivocal nature of key terms like “will” and “eternal”).

**Metaphysics / Divine Science** (~16 Discussions):
- 1. Eternity of the World (4 proofs from philosophers; Ghazali’s refutations via “eternal will” and infinite series arguments; Averroes defends no absolute beginning, critiques equivocation on will, notes Aristotle’s own antinomies).
- 2–16. Incorruptibility of world/time/motion; God as agent/creator (metaphors?); proof of a creator; God’s unity; denial of attributes; simplicity of the First; incorporeality; knowledge of other things/universals vs. particulars/self; heaven as animal/moved by aim; souls of the heavens observing particulars.

**Natural Sciences** (4 Discussions):
- 1. **Denial of logical necessity between cause and effect** (the most philosophically explosive for the vault): Ghazali’s classic occasionalism—fire does not burn cotton “by nature”; God creates the blackness, separation, burning at each instant. Observation yields only concomitance, not causation. All “causes” are really God acting (possibly via angels). Miracles pose no problem because there are no fixed natures.
  - Averroes: Sophistry that destroys intellect, logic, essences, definitions, science, and the very possibility of knowledge. Things have real specific natures from which acts follow (fire burns by warmth/ form). Four Aristotelian causes are necessary. “Habit” is either nature in things or in our judgments. Philosophers posit a First immaterial Agent whose act sustains, but secondary causes are real and observable. External impediments can prevent usual action without denying the power. Miracles possible through special divine alteration of dispositions (as in talc example or rapid transformations), not by abolishing causation altogether. Religious principles are to be conceded; philosophers do not publicly dispute miracles.
- 2. Impotence to prove the soul is a spiritual substance subsisting by itself (not body, not in body, like God/angels).
- 3. Refutation of philosophers’ proof for immortality of the soul.
- 4. Philosophers’ denial of bodily resurrection (Averroes clarifies philosophers affirm some form of afterlife/resurrection as necessary for moral order and mass virtue; they see religious laws as political arts from reason + inspiration; detailed discussion of simulacra vs. identical bodies, matter receiving forms, etc.).

**The End**: Averroes on the three famous points of heresy Ghazali leveled (eternity, God’s knowledge of particulars, resurrection): the philosophers’ actual nuanced positions do not match the accusations exactly. On resurrection (Fourth Discussion of natural sciences): it “is not found in any of the older philosophers, although resurrection has been mentioned in different religions for at least a thousand years...” Philosophers regard the doctrine as most important because “it is conducive to an order amongst men on which man’s being, as man, depends and through which he can attain the greatest happiness proper to him...” “In short, the philosophers believe that religious laws are necessary political arts, the principles of which are taken from natural reason and inspiration, especially in what is common to all religions...” All religions agree on an afterlife (differing in description), on God’s knowledge/attributes/acts, and on acts conducive to bliss. The learned must not publicly object to general religious principles; one must choose the best religion of one’s period. On the soul’s survival (Third Discussion): nuanced engagement with Avicenna on individuation by bodies (one single soul for all leads to impossibilities like shared knowledge); creative souls/potencies in subtle heavenly warmth (from heavenly bodies) or elements for generation/spontaneous generation; Plato’s argument from the soul forming/creating the body (hence separate from it). Averroes breaks off the inquiry, asks pardon for the discussion, and notes the obligation to seek truth for those entitled to it (“one in a thousand” per Galen). Translator note: some later critiques of the translation’s handling of Ghazali (e.g., Marmura edition).

## Key Arguments and Tensions

- **Eternity vs. Creation**: Aristotle (first to affirm eternal ordered cosmos) + Plotinian eternal emanation vs. temporal creation ex nihilo by undetermined divine will. Averroes defends no “new decision” in the eternal; critiques both sides’ handling of potentiality/will.
- **Causation / Occasionalism**: The central vault-relevant debate. Ghazali/Ash‘arites: only God causes; things have no inherent powers. Averroes: denial of observed efficient causes = denial of intellect and being itself. Aligns with Aristotle’s hylomorphism and four causes.
- **God’s Knowledge and Will**: Philosophers often limit to universals or self-knowledge to preserve immutability/necessity; theologians demand full particular knowledge and arbitrary will. Averroes nuances.
- **Soul, Intellect, Resurrection**: Engages Avicenna heavily on individuation. Defends forms of survival and the necessity of religion for virtue, while protecting demonstrative philosophy.
- **Faith and Reason / Elites and Masses**: Philosophy for the few who can handle demonstration; religion (with its images and laws) for universal moral formation. The learned inherit the prophets’ wisdom but must preserve the principles.

## Relevance to the Vault and Cross-References

This is the **primary Islamic source** grounding and extending the vault’s Aristotle thread and the critiques already noted in Aquinas pages. It supplies the “other side” of the debate that Aquinas is refuting when he insists (against Averroes) that the intellectual soul is the substantial form of *this particular body*, that each person has their own active and possible intellect (so that “Socrates understands”), and that personal immortality and moral responsibility are preserved.

- **Aristotle entity and sources**: Direct defense and literal commentary on the Stagirite in the Islamic context; transmission history complements the Organon/Ethics/Rhetoric material.
- **Thomas Aquinas / Summa sources**: Explicitly anti-Averroist on hylomorphism, unicity of intellect, and individuation (see Q. 76 in Part I source summary). The *Tahafut* shows *why* those positions were live and dangerous in the 13th c. Latin reception of Aristotle-via-Averroes. Enriches “grace does not destroy nature but perfects it” and natural law (which presuppose real secondary causes and knowable essences/natures in things).
- **Classical cluster**: Bridges Greek (Aristotle, Plato’s Timaeus demiurge/creation, Plotinus emanation) → Islamic philosophy/theology → Latin scholasticism (Aquinas as “baptizer” of Aristotle). Contrasts with Augustine’s *creatio ex nihilo* + time as distentio, Stoic/Epicurean materialism, Pyrrhonian skepticism about causes, and Lucretius’ mortalism.
- **Causation, Natural Law, Last End**: Occasionalism is the radical alternative to the Aristotelian-Aquinian view of natures, secondary causation, and rational participation in eternal law. Directly relevant to [[Concepts/Natural Law (Aquinas)]], [[Concepts/Beatific Vision - Last End (Aquinas)]], [[Concepts/Eudaimonia]] (natural happiness via excellent activity of soul presupposes real causal powers and stable natures).
- **Epistemology and Method**: Averroes’ defense of essences, definitions, and causes as conditions of intellect/logic/science is a powerful ancient statement of why denying real causation collapses knowledge—useful parallel for reasoning scaffolds, agentic reliability, and the wiki’s own “opposition before synthesis” (cf. Sextus).
- **Faith/Reason and Elite/Mass**: Strong model for the vault’s own pattern (GROK.md schema for disciplined maintenance by the “few”; Obsidian/wiki as the persistent artifact for the user). Philosophy as necessary but not sufficient for the whole; religion/law as the universal practical art.

**Contradictions / Open Questions**:
- Averroes acknowledges real tensions in Aristotle (eternity + finite causes; God as self-contemplating yet prime mover; potentiality vs. Megarian denial).
- Ghazali’s own mysticism vs. dogmatism creates inconsistencies that Averroes exploits.
- How far does Averroes himself hold “two truths” (philosophical vs. religious)? He insists on public conformity and that the religious principles are true, while allowing deeper interpretation for the qualified.
- Bodily vs. spiritual resurrection: philosophers affirm a form that serves moral ends; exact mechanism left partly open.
- Relation to vault’s other “third ways” (Pyrrhonian epoché, Epicurean withdrawal, Stoic control, Augustinian grace): here the debate is between demonstrative naturalism (with First Cause) and radical divine voluntarism/occasionalism.
