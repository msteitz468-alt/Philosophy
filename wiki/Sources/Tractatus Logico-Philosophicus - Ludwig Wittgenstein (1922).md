# Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus - Ludwig Wittgenstein (1922)

> A foundational text of 20th-century analytic philosophy and logical atomism, outlining the strict logical limits of language, representation, and thought.

## Summary

The *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* is a short, dense, and highly structured work consisting of seven numbered main propositions, with sub-propositions branching out (e.g., 1.1, 1.11, 1.12) to elaborate on details. 

Written by [[Thinkers/Ludwig Wittgenstein|Ludwig Wittgenstein]] during World War I, the work aims to determine the relationship between language and reality, and to define the boundary between what can be logically said with sense and what must remain unsayable. Wittgenstein presents the **Picture Theory of Language**, arguing that propositions are logical pictures of facts, and famously concludes that the most important aspects of human existence (ethics, aesthetics, meaning) lie outside the limits of logical language and can only be *shown*, not *said*.

---

## The Seven Main Propositions

1. **"The world is all that is the case."**
   - The world is the totality of facts, not of things. Facts exist in logical space.
2. **"What is the case—a fact—is the existence of states of affairs."**
   - A state of affairs (*Sachverhalt*) is a combination of simple, indestructible objects. Objects are simple and fit together in logical structures.
3. **"A logical picture of facts is a thought."**
   - The totality of true thoughts is a picture of the world.
4. **"A thought is a proposition with a sense."**
   - Propositions are expressions of thoughts. An elementary proposition is a combination of names that represent objects. Propositions must share the same **logical form** as the facts they represent.
5. **"A proposition is a truth-function of elementary propositions."**
   - All complex propositions can be built from simple ones using logical operators (truth-functions, truth tables). Logic is tautological; its propositions (e.g., p or not-p) say nothing about the world.
6. **"The general form of a truth-function is: $[\bar{p}, \bar{\xi}, N(\bar{\xi})]$."**
   - The general form of a proposition. Wittgenstein transitions to the limits of the world: *"The limits of my language mean the limits of my world."* The subject does not belong to the world but is a limit of the world.
   - Ethics, aesthetics, and religion cannot be put into words: *"It is clear that ethics cannot be put into words. Ethics is transcendental. (Ethics and aesthetics are one.)"*
   - The meaning of life lies outside the world of facts.
7. **"What we cannot speak about we must pass over in silence."**
   - The famous closing maxim. Language must fall silent when attempting to express transcendent truths.

## Key Concepts and Tensions

### 1. Picture Theory of Language
Language represents the world by sharing its logical form. Names stand for objects, and their arrangement pictures a possible combination of those objects. See [[Concepts/Picture Theory of Language (Wittgenstein)|Picture Theory of Language (Wittgenstein)]].

### 2. Saying vs. Showing
Logical form itself cannot be pictured in language; it can only be shown. Similarly, the deepest truths of value (ethics, aesthetics, the meaning of life) cannot be represented in logical propositions. They show themselves in the world but are unsayable.

### 3. The Self-Application Paradox
Wittgenstein admits that his own philosophical propositions in the *Tractatus* violate his semantic rules. They are not empirical facts or logical tautologies, and are therefore nonsensical. They are a ladder that the reader must climb and then discard:
> *"My propositions clarify in this way: he who understands me finally recognizes them as senseless, when he has climbed out through them, on them, over them. (He must so to speak throw away the ladder, after he has climbed up on it.)"*
