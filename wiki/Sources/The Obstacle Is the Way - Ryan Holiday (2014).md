# The Obstacle Is the Way — Ryan Holiday (2014)

> *The Obstacle Is the Way: The Timeless Art of Turning Trials Into Triumph* (Portfolio/Penguin, 2014). A modern popularization of Roman Stoicism as a practical "operating system" for adversity, built around a single sentence of Marcus Aurelius: *"The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way."* Holiday distills Stoic ethics into three sequential disciplines — **Perception, Action, and Will** — illustrated almost entirely through short historical vignettes (Rockefeller, Grant, Edison, Demosthenes, Lincoln, Roosevelt, Stockdale, Amelia Earhart, Steve Jobs, and dozens more) rather than through textual exegesis. It is deliberately not a scholarly study; Holiday credits Pierre Hadot's reading of Stoicism as *philosophy as a way of life* and points readers back to the primary sources.

**Author**: Ryan Holiday (b. 1987), American author and marketer, popularizer of Stoicism (see [[Thinkers/Ryan Holiday]]). Introduced to Stoicism via Gregory Hays's translation of the *Meditations*; student of Robert Greene.

---

## Argument Spine

Nothing that happens to us is, in itself, good or bad — only our judgment makes it so (the Stoic doctrine of impressions). Therefore *every* obstacle contains, for the person who perceives and acts rightly, the raw material of an advantage: at minimum the chance to practice a virtue. Overcoming adversity is a repeatable three-part discipline: **see clearly** (Perception), **act correctly and creatively** (Action), and **endure and accept what cannot be changed** (Will). The three feed into one another and must be practiced until they become "muscle memory." The book's method — teaching philosophy through the actions of "doers" rather than lecturers — is itself a Stoic thesis: philosophy is something you *do*, not something you talk about.

The three-part structure was suggested to Holiday by editor Shawn Coyne and maps directly onto Epictetus's **three *topoi*** (fields of study — desire/aversion, impulse to act, assent) as reconstructed by Hadot in *The Inner Citadel*.

## Part I — Perception (Discipline of the Mind)

How we see events; the source of strength or weakness. Learn to strip away subjective judgment and see things "as they truly are — neither good nor bad."

- **Recognize your power** — Rubin "Hurricane" Carter, Nelson Mandela: even in prison the mind, beliefs, and reactions remain one's own. *"Nothing either good or bad, but thinking makes it so"* (Shakespeare) restates the Stoic doctrine of impressions.
- **Steady your nerves / control your emotions** — Grant under fire; NASA training panic out of astronauts. The Greek term **apatheia** — freedom from disturbing passions (not absence of feeling). *"If an emotion can't change the condition you're dealing with, it is likely an unhelpful emotion."*
- **Practice objectivity** — Musashi's *observing eye* (sees what is there) vs *perceiving eye* (adds fear and story). The Stoic exercise of **Contemptuous Expressions** — Marcus describing roast meat as "a dead animal," fine wine as "old fermented grapes" — to strip the ornament from impressions. Advising others is easy because we lack the "baggage"; treat your own situation as if it were someone else's.
- **Alter your perspective** — Pericles and the eclipse; the added "I" ("*I* am harmed by this") is what magnifies the obstacle. Two senses of perspective: *context* and *framing*.
- **Is it up to you?** — the **Dichotomy of Control**: *ta eph'hēmin, ta ouk eph'hēmin* (what is up to us, what is not). The Serenity Prayer is a Christianized descendant. See [[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)]].
- **Live in the present moment; Think differently** — Steve Jobs's "reality distortion field"; perceptions influence (do not determine) reality.
- **Finding the opportunity** — Eisenhower and the Battle of the Bulge: the enemy's thrust carried "its own destruction within itself." **Adversarial / post-traumatic growth**: "That which does not kill me makes me stronger" treated as empirical fact, not cliché.

## Part II — Action (Discipline of the Body)

Not any action but *directed* action — deliberate, bold, persistent, creative.

- **Get moving / practice persistence / iterate** — Demosthenes ("Action, action, action!"); Grant at Vicksburg; Edison testing 6,000 filaments. **Persistence** as energy directed at one problem until it breaks; the Silicon Valley "Minimum Viable Product" and *"failure is a feature"* as iteration. Epictetus's *"persist and resist."*
- **Follow the process** — Nick Saban's "The Process": ignore the prize, finish the task in front of you; break the mammoth into component parts.
- **Do your job, do it right** — *Quidvis recte factum quamvis humile praeclarum* ("whatever is rightly done, however humble, is noble"). Frankl: the world asks you the meaning of life and your actions answer.
- **What's right is what works** — Samuel Zemurray, pragmatism as flexibility; the Stoic reminder *"Don't go expecting Plato's Republic."*
- **In praise of the flank attack** — Liddell Hart's *Strategy*: only 6 of 280 campaigns won by direct attack; the "line of least expectation." Kierkegaard's *indirect communication* as an intellectual flank attack.
- **Use obstacles against themselves** — Gandhi, MLK ("physical force with soul force"); Alexander tiring out Bucephalus; nonaction as action.
- **Channel your energy; seize the offensive; prepare for none of it to work** — Arthur Ashe ("physically loose and mentally tight"); Obama's "A More Perfect Union" speech; Rahm Emanuel's "never let a serious crisis go to waste." Closes by conceding that right action can still fail — which is the bridge to Will.

## Part III — Will (Discipline of the Heart/Soul)

Internal power, the "final trump card" for when agency has all but disappeared. Holiday insists true will is "quiet humility, resilience, and flexibility" — closer to *surrender* than to the "will to win."

- **Build your inner citadel** — Theodore Roosevelt remaking his asthmatic body; the Stoic **Inner Citadel** (Hadot's phrase) as a fortress that must be *built* in good times to be relied on in bad. See [[Concepts/The Inner Citadel (Hadot)]].
- **Anticipation (thinking negatively)** — the **premortem** = the Stoic ***premeditatio malorum*** (premeditation of evils); Seneca rehearsing shipwreck; managing expectations. See [[Concepts/Premeditatio Malorum (Premeditation of Evils)]].
- **The art of acquiescence** — accepting what is not up to us (Jefferson's stammer channeled into writing; Deo volente, "the answer is in the lap of the gods"). *Amor fati*'s precondition: "nature, in order to be commanded, must be obeyed" (Bacon).
- **Love everything that happens: Amor Fati** — Edison watching his factory burn ("we've just got rid of a lot of rubbish"); Jack Johnson's smile. Nietzsche's formula for greatness: not merely to bear the necessary but to *love* it. See [[Concepts/Amor Fati (Love of Fate)]].
- **Perseverance** — Odysseus; *Sitzfleisch*; Churchill's "KBO — Keep Buggering On." Persistence (an action) vs perseverance (the long game of will).
- **Something bigger than yourself** — Stockdale and McCain as POWs, "Unity over Self"; stop putting the "I" in front of events.
- **Meditate on your mortality** — Montaigne's riding accident; ***memento mori***; "the diagnosis is terminal for all of us." See [[Concepts/Memento Mori (Stoic Practice)]].
- **Prepare to start again** — "Behind mountains are more mountains"; Elysium is a myth; life is a series of fortified lines to break through.

**Final Thoughts / Postscript** — Marcus and the revolt of Avidius Cassius (forgiveness as the last available virtue). The Postscript ("You're Now a Philosopher") lists the lineage of Stoic practitioners of action (Cato, Frederick the Great, Washington, Jefferson, Toussaint Louverture, J.S. Mill, T. Roosevelt, Stockdale) and insists, with Hadot, that philosophy was "never what happened in the classroom" but "a set of lessons from the battlefield of life."

## Key Quotations

- *"The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way."* — Marcus Aurelius (the book's seed text, *Meditations* 5.20)
- *"Objective judgment... Unselfish action... Willing acceptance of all external events. That's all you need."* — Marcus Aurelius (epigraph to the method)
- *"It's simple, it's just not easy."* — Holiday's refrain
- *"Not: This is not so bad. But: I can make this good."*
- *"See things for what they are. Do what we can. Endure and bear what we must."*
- Taleb's definition Holiday adopts: a Stoic *"transforms fear into prudence, pain into transformation, mistakes into initiation, and desire into undertaking."*

## Whole-Work Assessment

For the vault, this source is the first **21st-century revival of practical Stoicism** and the first to make explicit the **three-discipline architecture** (Hadot's reading of Epictetus's *topoi*) that the vault had previously captured only piecemeal (dichotomy of control, ruling faculty, view from above, every act as if the last). It contributes four genuinely new concept pages the classical Stoic sources had left as gaps — [[Concepts/Amor Fati (Love of Fate)]], [[Concepts/Premeditatio Malorum (Premeditation of Evils)]], [[Concepts/Memento Mori (Stoic Practice)]], and [[Concepts/The Inner Citadel (Hadot)]] — plus the framework page [[Concepts/The Three Disciplines - Perception, Action, and Will (Hadot)]] and the book's signature move [[Concepts/Turning Obstacles Upside Down (Holiday)]]. It deepens the Roman Stoic triad ([[Thinkers/Epictetus]], [[Thinkers/Seneca]], [[Thinkers/Marcus Aurelius]]) with their modern reception, and links Stoic *amor fati* forward to [[Thinkers/Nietzsche]]. Its limitations are worth flagging: it is a motivational/popular work that reads virtue instrumentally (obstacles as "fuel" for ambition and success), softening the Stoic claim that virtue is the *only* good — a tension Nietzsche's own critique of Stoicism (see [[Sources/Beyond Good and Evil - Nietzsche (AmazonClassics Edition)]]) anticipates. It over-narrativizes history (the vignettes are selective and occasionally embellished, as Holiday half-concedes about Washington). As a source it is best treated as reception/application, not primary Stoic doctrine.

## Relationships to Vault

- **To the Roman Stoic triad** — supplies modern reception for [[Thinkers/Marcus Aurelius]] (the 5.20 "obstacle" passage; Hadot's Inner Citadel reading; Bill Clinton/Wen Jiabao/Ferriss as modern readers), [[Thinkers/Epictetus]] (Stockdale's *Courage Under Fire*; the three *topoi* as the book's spine), and [[Thinkers/Seneca]] (*premeditatio malorum*, "rehearse death daily").
- **To [[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)]]** — the whole of Part I is an application; *ta eph'hēmin* and the Serenity Prayer.
- **To [[Concepts/View from Above and Cosmic Perspective (Marcus Aurelius)]]**, [[Concepts/Every Act as if It Were the Last (Marcus Aurelius)]], [[Concepts/Ruling Faculty and the Daemon (Marcus Aurelius)]] — the mortality, presence, and inner-citadel chapters restate these directly.
- **To [[Concepts/Ataraxia]]** — Holiday's *apatheia* / equanimity is the Stoic branch of the three Hellenistic tranquillities.
- **To [[Thinkers/Nietzsche]]** — *amor fati* is Nietzsche's phrase; Holiday yokes it to Stoic acceptance (a synthesis Nietzsche himself would have contested, given his critique of Stoic "living according to Nature").
- **To personal/career ([[Mack Steitz]])** — the book's explicit self-help register targets exactly the vault owner's career-transition use case: focus on the controllable, treat setbacks as training, *premeditatio malorum* for planning, perseverance over persistence.
