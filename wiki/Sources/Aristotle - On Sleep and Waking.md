# Aristotle - On Sleep and Waking

> The first of the short *Parva Naturalia* treatises (*De Somno et Vigilia*, 453b). Asks what sleep and waking are, to which part of soul or body they belong, and why all animals are subject to both. Answers: sleep is a periodic, necessary "tying up" (privation) of the **primary/common sense-organ**, caused physiologically by the upward evaporation of digested nutriment and its cooling reflux around the heart.

## Argument Spine

Waking and sleep are contrary affections of one and the same faculty — sense-perception — so whatever underlies waking underlies sleep. Since perception cannot belong to soul or body alone (it is an actuality of soul-through-body), sleep is not an attribute of plants (no perception) nor of anything perpetually awake or asleep (every organ that functions must also rest). The affection belongs specifically to the **primary/common sensory organ** (located, in blooded animals, at the heart) rather than to the special senses individually — proven by the fact that all senses fail together in sleep, which would be inexplicable if sleep struck each sense separately. The efficient/material cause is physiological: food's evaporation rises with the body's heat to the head, cools, and refluxes downward, overpowering the primary sense-organ; digestion's completion and the sorting of pure from turbid blood around the heart causes waking. Final cause: sleep conserves the animal, since no natural power can be exercised without interruption.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Sleep = privation of waking**, both affections of the same subject (contraries share a substrate), and both are affections of *sense-perception*, hence impossible for plants (which lack perception) — thus restricted to animals.
- **All animals sleep** (argued from the definition of animal as perceiving being); testaceous/insect sleep is inferred by the same reasoning even where direct observation is inconclusive.
- The affection resides in the **common/primary sense-organ** (identified with touch's associated organ, seated at the heart), not in each special sense separately — this is why swooning/asphyxia (impotence of a particular or all senses from external causes) differs from true sleep, which arises internally from nutritive evaporation.
- **Physiological mechanism**: warm vaporous matter from digested food rises to the head, cools, and sinks back, overpowering the heart-region sense organ — "like a tide-race in a narrow strait." Explains post-meal drowsiness, infant/dwarf/large-head susceptibility to sleep, wine and narcotics (poppy, mandragora, darnel) as sleep-inducers, and epilepsy's kinship to sleep (both are seizures of the same organ).
- Sleep's **final cause** is conservation: no animal capacity can function without limit without loss of power, so periodic rest is necessary; but waking (exercise of sense/thought) remains the animal's higher end.
- Brief anticipation of dreaming: a dream is "in a certain way a sense-impression"; sleepers who move and act do so "not without a phantasm."
