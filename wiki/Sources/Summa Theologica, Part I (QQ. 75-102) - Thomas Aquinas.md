# Summa Theologica, Part I (QQ. 75–102) — Thomas Aquinas

> The *Prima Pars*, Questions 75–102: the complete **Treatise on Man** from the First Part of Aquinas' *Summa Theologica* (c. 1265–1274). Dominican Province translation (English), Second and Revised Edition, London, 1932. Imprimatur Westminster. This is Part I of the Summa — not to be confused with the already-ingested Part I-II (*Prima Secundae*). Part I-II covers moral theology (last end, acts, law, grace); this Part I covers metaphysical anthropology: what man *is*, how his soul and body are united, how he knows, wills, and chooses, and what his original state was. The scholastic *quaestio* format throughout: Objections, Sed Contra, Respondeo (I answer that), Replies to Objections.

**Source file**: `raw/Thomas Aquinas - Summa Theologica.txt` (~851KB, ~16,849 lines). Augustine is the primary patristic source (Trinitarian analogy of memory/intellect/will as *imago Dei*; interiority; grace themes). See new [[Sources/Confessions - Augustine]] and [[Thinkers/Augustine]].

**Scope**: QQ. 75–102. This is Part I only — the Five Ways (QQ. 2-3), the Trinity (QQ. 27-43), and other divine matters are in a different volume not yet in the vault.

## PASS 1 — QQ. 75-76: The Essence of the Soul and Its Union with the Body

*The philosophical foundation of the entire Treatise on Man. Aquinas establishes what the soul is (Q. 75) and how it is united to the body (Q. 76). These two questions together constitute the definitive medieval synthesis of Aristotelian hylomorphism with Christian immortality doctrine.*

### Q. 75 — The Essence of the Soul (7 Articles)

**Structure**: Seven articles asking: Is the soul a body? Is it subsistent? Are brute-animal souls subsistent? Is the soul the man? Is it composed of matter and form? Is it incorruptible? Is it of the same species as an angel?

#### Art. 1 — The Soul Is Not a Body
The soul is the *first principle of life* in living things. Life appears through knowledge and movement. The ancient materialists (Democritus, Empedocles) said the soul must be a body because only bodies are real. Aquinas refutes this: to be a principle of life does not belong to a *body as such* (otherwise every body would be alive). A body becomes a living thing by receiving a principle which is its act. "Therefore the soul, which is the first principle of life, is not a body, but the **act of a body**; thus heat, which is the principle of calefaction, is not a body, but an act of a body."

The soul moves the body not as body moves body (by contact of quantity) but by contact of *power* — incorporeal causation of a corporeal effect.

#### Art. 2 — The Human Soul Is Subsistent (Has Per Se Existence)
The intellectual principle — what we call the mind or intellect — is *incorporeal and subsistent*: it can operate independently of any bodily organ. Proof: the intellect can know *all* corporeal things. If it were itself a body (or the act of a corporeal organ), its own determinate nature would obstruct knowledge of other natures — as a fever-bitter tongue cannot taste sweetness. Since the intellect knows universally, it must be free of any particularizing material nature. "The intellectual principle which we call the mind or the intellect has an **operation per se** apart from the body. Now only that which subsists can have an operation per se."

The body is necessary not as the *organ* of understanding but on the side of the *object*: phantasms (sense images) are to the intellect as colours are to vision. This dependence doesn't make the intellect non-subsistent any more than an animal's need for external objects makes it non-subsistent.

#### Art. 3 — Brute-Animal Souls Are Not Subsistent
Unlike the human intellectual soul, animal sensitive souls have *no per se operation*: sensation always involves bodily change (the pupil affected by colour; strong stimuli damage the sense). Therefore brute souls have no per se existence — they perish with the body. Man is different from beasts in species, not merely degree.

#### Art. 4 — Man Is Soul and Body, Not Soul Alone
Against Plato's view that man is essentially a soul *using* a body: sensation is not the operation of the soul alone (Art. 3 established this). Since man senses, and sensing requires body, man is not soul alone but "something composed of soul and body." Socrates is this particular composite, not a soul in a garment. However, the "inward man" (2 Cor. 4:16) refers to the intellectual part as the governing principle.

#### Art. 5 — The Soul Has No Matter
The soul is not composed of matter and form (contra various opponents). Two proofs:
1. The soul is the *form* of a body. A form, as such, is an act. Pure potentiality (matter) cannot be part of an act — they are opposites.
2. The intellectual soul knows things *absolutely* (as universal forms, not individualized). If it had matter, received forms would be individualized (as in the senses, where matter individualizes). An immaterial knower of universals cannot be materially composed.

Angels and separated intellects are also immaterial — but they are composed of *form and participated existence* (esse), not form and matter. God alone is pure act with no composition whatsoever. The soul's potentiality is *receptive potentiality for intelligible species*, entirely different from primary matter's potentiality.

#### Art. 6 — The Human Soul Is Incorruptible (Immortal)
The central immortality argument:
- A substance can only be corrupted *per se* (by its own dissolution) or *accidentally* (by the dissolution of something else it depends on).
- Brute souls are incorruptible only accidentally — they perish when the composite perishes because they have no per se existence.
- The human soul, being subsistent, can only perish per se.
- But a subsistent form cannot be separated from itself (existence belongs to form by virtue of itself; a form cannot lose its own formness).
- Furthermore: corruption requires contrariety. The intellect receives contraries without suffering from them (knowing hot/cold destroys neither the knowledge nor the knower). No contrariety in the intellect → incorruptible.
- Natural sign: the intellect apprehends existence "absolutely and for all time" — not merely *here and now* as the senses do. Everything with intellect naturally desires *always to exist*. A natural desire cannot be in vain. Therefore every intellectual substance is incorruptible.

After death, the soul will have "another mode of understanding, similar to other substances separated from bodies" (anticipating Q. 89).

#### Art. 7 — The Soul Is Not of the Same Species as an Angel
Angels and human souls are different in species. Angels are pure subsistent forms — no matter at all. Each angel is therefore its own species (no matter to individualize multiple instances of one form). Human souls, though immaterial in operation, are *ordered to bodies* as forms. Multiple human souls of one species are possible because the body (signate matter) individuates them. Soul and angel have different cognitive natures: angels know by simple, direct intuition; humans must abstract from phantasms.

### Q. 76 — The Union of Soul and Body (8 Articles)

**Structure**: Eight articles on how the soul is the body's form; whether intellect is one for all men (Averroism); whether there are multiple souls in man; whether there is more than one substantial form; the qualities needed in the animated body; the mode of union; whether the soul is in each part of the body.

#### Art. 1 — The Intellectual Principle Is the Body's Form (Hylomorphism)
The decisive article. Aquinas argues that *Socrates understands* — understanding is properly attributed to *this man*, not to a separate intellect. Socrates understands (a) as a whole, (b) by a part, or (c) accidentally. (c) is false (understanding is essential to man). (a) would make Socrates a pure soul (Plato's view — already refuted). Therefore (b): the intellect is a *part* of Socrates, united to his body.

Three alternatives tried and rejected:
1. **Averroes**: intellect united to body through the intelligible species (shared double-subject in intellect and phantasm). Fails: if my phantasm produces my understanding, that understanding is still the intellect's act, not mine — as the wall seen is not said to see. (Primary text of the opponent: [[Sources/Incoherence of the Incoherence - Ibn Rushd]] — Averroes’ defense of Aristotelian positions on the soul/intellect against Ghazali supplies the exact views Aquinas is correcting here and in Art. 2.)
2. **The motor view**: intellect united to body as motor to moved. Fails: the act of understanding is *immanent* (remains in the agent); it doesn't pass out like heat passing from fire to iron. Also: a man would then be a tool of his own intellect.
3. **The correct view**: "There remains therefore no other explanation than that given by Aristotle — namely, that **this particular man understands because the intellectual principle is his form**."

The soul is therefore *forma corporis* — the substantial form of the human body — while also having an intellectual power that exceeds what any corporeal organ can do. "The nobler a form is, the more it rises above corporeal matter, the less it is merged in matter... the human soul is the highest and noblest of forms."

#### Art. 2 — Each Person Has Their Own Intellect (Anti-Averroism)
Against the claim that there is one intellect for all humanity (Averroes): It is "absolutely impossible for one intellect to belong to all men." If intellect were one:
- Socrates and Plato would be one man — differing only accidentally (like a man in a tunic vs. a man in a cloak).
- There would be one "understander" for all men — contradicting immediate conscious experience ("each one is conscious that it is himself who understands").
- The distinction of my intellectual act from yours cannot be explained by diversity of phantasms alone, because the intelligible species (abstracted from phantasms) is numerically one within a single intellect regardless of how many phantasms produced it.

Each person has their own intellect, individuated through their soul's union with *this particular body*.

#### Art. 3 — One Soul with Multiple Powers (Not Multiple Souls)
Against Plato's view that nutritive, sensitive, and rational souls are distinct substances in distinct organs (liver, heart, brain): Aquinas argues that man has *one soul* with multiple powers. Key evidence: in animals that can be divided (certain worms), each divided part shows sensation and appetite — proving the powers are not anchored to specific organs by separate souls but radiate from one principle.

The one rational soul in man *contains virtually* (eminently) the sensitive and vegetative powers: "the soul is one in essence, but has many powers." The intellect subsists; the sensitive and vegetative powers operate *through* the body as instrument.

#### Art. 8 — The Soul Is Wholly in Each Part of the Body
The soul is not distributed by quantity (as if a part of the soul is in each part of the body). The soul is in the body as form — and form is related to matter not by spatial extent but by act. The *whole* soul is in *each* part of the body, as the whole form of a thing is in each part — just as the whole nature of fire is in each part of fire, and the soul of an animal is in each part (as shown by division experiments).

However, the soul's powers are not all equally present in every part: sight is in the eye, hearing in the ear. The soul is "whole in each part" by virtue of its essence, but different parts participate in different powers.

## PASS 2 — QQ. 77-83: Powers of the Soul — Vegetative, Sensitive, Intellectual, Appetitive; Sensuality; Will; Free Choice

*The systematic taxonomy of human psychological powers, establishing what the soul can do and how all these powers are ordered. Philosophically culminates in the doctrine of free choice as intellectual appetite — the faculty that makes moral life possible.*

### Q. 77 — Powers of the Soul in General (8 Articles)

**Soul's essence ≠ its powers**: Unlike God, whose essence is His own being and His being is His understanding (*esse = intelligere*), created souls have an essence that is *not* their act of operation. Powers flow from the essence as properties flow from a substance, but are distinct from it. In God alone is the intellect identical with the divine essence.

**Powers distinguished by objects**: Soul's powers are distinguished generically by their objects. The higher a power, the more universal its object: vegetative (only own body), sensitive (all sensible bodies), intellectual (universal being as such).

**Hierarchy of powers**:
- Vegetative (lowest): operates through a corporeal organ *by* corporeal quality (heat digests food)
- Sensitive: operates through a corporeal organ but *not by* corporeal quality (the eye is changed by colour, but colour does not see — the organ receives the form without the matter)
- Intellectual (highest): operates *without* a corporeal organ entirely

**Powers after death**: Vegetative and sensitive powers do not remain in the separated soul (they require the body as organ). Intellectual powers — memory, intellect, will — remain.

---

### Q. 78 — Specific Powers: Five Genera and Their Parts (4 Articles)

**Five genera of soul's powers**: vegetative, sensitive, appetitive, locomotive, intellectual.

**Vegetative soul** — three powers:
- *Nutritive*: preserves the body in being
- *Augmentative*: brings the body to due quantity (growth)
- *Generative*: noblest — produces another like the organism itself (the only vegetative power whose effect reaches outside the organism)

**Five exterior senses**: distinguished by the kind of *spiritual immutation* involved (the form received in the organ without matter). Sight is noblest — requires no physical contact, only light as medium. Touch is most material — requires contact and receives physical qualities (hot/cold/wet/dry).

**Four interior senses**:
1. *Common sense*: unifies and integrates the reports of the five exterior senses; judges between sense objects ("this white thing is sweet")
2. *Imagination* (*phantasia*): stores images without the presence of the object; the storehouse of sense forms
3. *Estimative power* (*vis aestimativa*): perceives what is beneficial or harmful without deliberation — e.g., the sheep perceives the wolf as enemy, not through any sensible quality, but through natural instinct. In man, this is replaced by the **cogitative power** (also called *particular reason*): it can compare individual intentions and operate under the guidance of universal reason
4. *Memory*: stores past sense experience with the quality of "pastness" attached. Includes *reminiscence* — deliberate recall by searching through stored associations (a quasi-syllogistic process)

### Q. 79 — Intellectual Powers (13 Articles)

**Intellect as power, not essence**: Only in God is the act of understanding (*intelligere*) identical with being (*esse*). In all created intellects, the intellect is a power distinct from the essence — because they are not always actually understanding.

**Passive/Possible Intellect**: The human intellect is at first *in potentiality* to all intelligible objects, "like a clean tablet on which nothing is written" (*tabula rasa*). Understanding is a kind of "being passive" in the third sense: receiving act without losing anything — passing from potentiality to actuality. The human intellect is the *lowest* in the order of intelligence (below angels, infinitely below God) — most in potentiality, requiring the most process to reach act.

**Active Intellect**: Because sensible forms in matter are not actually intelligible (particulars, materialized), something must make them intelligible — must *abstract* the universal intelligible species from the material phantasms. This is the *active intellect*: a power within the human soul (not a separate divine intellect, contra some interpretations). "As in every nature, so in the soul is there something by which it becomes all things, and something by which it makes all things." The active intellect illuminates the phantasm and abstracts the species.

**One active intellect per person**: The active intellect is not one shared intellect for all humanity (contra Averroes again). Each soul has its own active intellect.

**Memory in the intellectual part**: Distinct from sensitive memory. The intellect retains intelligible species *habitually* between acts of understanding — this habitual possession is intellectual memory. Reminiscence in the intellect: actively searching through the logical/associative order of concepts to recover what was known.

**Reason vs. Intellect**: Not two distinct powers, but two aspects of the same power. *Intellect* grasps first principles immediately and without movement. *Reason* moves discursively from premises to conclusions. "To reason is related to the intellect as to move is to be at rest, or to acquire to have." Man reasons because his intellect is not strong enough to grasp all truth at once (unlike angels, who know by simple intuition).

**Speculative and Practical Intellect**: Same power; different ends. Speculative intellect: directed to truth for its own sake. Practical intellect: directed to truth ordered to action.

**Synderesis**: The natural, indemonstrable habit of the *first principles of practical reason* — "good is to be done and pursued; evil is to be avoided." Synderesis is infallible in its first principles (cannot err about the universal precepts of natural law). It is the innate seed of moral knowledge. Links directly to [[Concepts/Natural Law (Aquinas)]]: synderesis is the *subjective* side — the rational creature's inner participation in eternal law.

**Conscience**: Not a power but an *act* — the application of moral knowledge to a particular action. Conscience *binds* (commands or prohibits), *witnesses* (testifies to past acts), and *accuses or excuses* (judges past acts). One can act against conscience (invincible ignorance, passion overriding judgment) but this does not eliminate obligation.

---

### Q. 80-81 — Appetitive Powers and Sensuality

**Appetite as a distinct power**: Cognitive powers receive the *form* of the desired thing (a likeness in the soul). But appetite tends toward the thing *itself* in its actual existence. Different directions → different power.

Two kinds of appetite:
- **Sensitive appetite** (sensuality): follows sensitive apprehension; divided into *concupiscible* and *irascible*
- **Will** (intellectual appetite): follows intellectual apprehension

**Concupiscible and Irascible**:
- *Concupiscible*: simple inclination toward the suitable; flight from the harmful (love, desire, joy / hatred, aversion, sorrow)
- *Irascible*: the "champion" of concupiscible — overcomes obstacles and resists what impedes the suitable or inflicts harm. Its object is the *arduous* good (difficult to attain or protect). Passions: hope, despair, fear, daring, anger.
- Irascible rises from concupiscible passions and terminates in them: anger rises from sadness; satisfaction terminates in joy.

**Sensuality obeys reason**: Not by despotic power (as the soul rules the body — limbs cannot resist) but by *political power* — like a king ruling free subjects who retain some autonomy. Man can be moved by imagination and sense against what reason judges; but he can also redirect these movements through reason. In man, the cogitative power (particular reason) stands between universal reason and the sensitive appetite — mediating moral perception.

---

### Q. 82-83 — Will and Free Choice

**Q. 82 — The Will**

The will is the *rational/intellectual appetite*: it follows upon the presentation of the good by the intellect.

**What the will desires necessarily**: The will necessarily (by natural necessity, not coercion) desires *happiness* — the last end — because it is the universal perfect good. One cannot will unhappiness. But the will does NOT necessarily desire any particular means or any finite good — because reason can consider these under the aspect of their privations and limitations. Only when the intellect is *clearly presented with the universal perfect good* (God as seen in Himself) would the will be determined with necessity (the beatific vision removes choice in the Scholastic tradition).

**Will vs. intellect — which is higher?**
- With respect to *God*: the will is nobler — love of God attains God as He really is; the intellect only attains God's likeness in the soul. "To love God is better than to know God."
- With respect to *lower created things*: the intellect is nobler — to know a stone is better than to love it (knowledge raises the thing to the level of the soul; love lowers the soul to the level of the thing).

**Will moves intellect, and intellect moves will**: Both move each other but in different orders. The intellect presents the good to the will (final cause). The will then directs the intellect to actually consider this or that object (efficient cause). This is the Scholastic resolution of the intellectualism/voluntarism debate.

---

**Q. 83 — Free Choice (Liberum Arbitrium)**

**Man has free will**: Without it, "counsels, exhortations, commands, prohibitions, rewards and punishments would be in vain." Free will is the *precondition of moral life*.

**What makes free choice possible**:
- Stone moves by physical necessity.
- Brute animals act from judgment, but *not free* judgment — the sheep flees the wolf by natural instinct, not deliberation.
- Man acts from judgment, but his judgment is *free* because it proceeds from *reason*: reason in contingent matters "may follow opposite courses" — the same facts can be judged differently from different angles (as in dialectic, both sides can be argued). Particular operations are contingent → reason is not fixed → man can go either way → freedom.

**Free choice is an appetitive power** — specifically the will as exercising choice. Choice requires both deliberation (cognitive) and acceptance of that deliberation (appetitive). The appetitive element is principal: "choice is the desire of those things which are in us" (Aristotle). Free choice = desire proceeding from counsel = *intellectual appetite*.

**Free choice and God's causation**: God moves the will from within as first cause. This does not destroy freedom because "just as by moving natural causes He does not prevent their acts being natural, so by moving voluntary causes He does not deprive their actions of being voluntary." God is the cause even of the will's freedom — He operates in each thing according to its own nature.

**Sin and freedom**: Sin does not destroy *natural* freedom (freedom from external coercion). It impairs *freedom from fault and unhappiness* — the full integration of will with reason and grace (treated in Part I-II, QQ. 85 seq.).

## PASS 3 — QQ. 84-86: How the Soul Knows Corporeal Things — Abstraction, Intelligible Species, and the Limits of Human Knowledge

*The Scholastic theory of abstraction and intellectual knowledge: how the human intellect genuinely knows the material world, the role of phantasms, the levels of abstraction, and the intellect's limits (singulars, infinites, futures).*

### Q. 84 — Through What Does the Soul Know Bodies? (8 Articles)

**The historical survey**: Aquinas frames the question through the history of philosophy. The early materialists (Heraclitus, Empedocles) held only bodies are real and that the soul must be corporeal to know bodies. This collapses intellect into sense. Plato, wishing to save certainty of knowledge despite the flux of bodies, posited separate immaterial Forms — but then natural science (of moving, material things) becomes impossible, and the Forms, differing in essence from sensible things, cannot help us judge *those* things.

**The correct answer — abstraction**:
"The received is in the receiver according to the mode of the receiver." The eye receives colour without receiving gold (the form of colour without the matter of the coloured thing). The intellect likewise receives the form of material things without their matter — and further, without the *individuating* material conditions. Hence: "Through the intellect the soul knows bodies by a knowledge which is **immaterial, universal, and necessary**."

**Knowledge is in inverse ratio to materiality**:
- Plants: receive forms only materially → no knowledge at all
- Senses: receive forms without matter but *under material individual conditions* (the pupil receives colour of this apple) → knowledge of particulars only
- Intellect: receives forms abstracted from individual material conditions → universal knowledge

**The soul cannot know bodies through its own essence**: Only God, whose Essence virtually contains all things as their first cause, can know all things through His essence. The human soul's essence is finite and determinate — it cannot contain all things.

**Not through innate species**: The soul begins as a *tabula rasa*. The slave-boy in the Meno can answer mathematical questions because he grasps *first principles* (general demonstrations from self-evident starting points) — not because he has specific innate knowledge of geometry.

**Must turn to phantasms**: Even after abstracting intelligible species, "the intellect needs to turn to the phantasms in which it understands the species" (De Anima III.7). Phantasm is to intellect as colour is to sight. This explains why brain injury or mental distraction impedes intellectual operation — it disrupts the phantasms that the intellect needs as objects, not as instruments, of understanding.

---

### Q. 85 — Mode and Order of Understanding (8 Articles)

**The process of abstraction**: The active intellect illuminates the phantasm and produces in the passive intellect an *intelligible species* — a formal likeness of the thing considered *without* its individual material conditions. This species is not moved from the phantasm like a body from one place to another; rather, the active intellect's power causes the passive intellect to receive an immaterial representation.

**Two kinds of abstraction** (critical distinction):
1. **Compositional/divisive abstraction**: affirming that two things exist separately (e.g., "whiteness exists apart from bodies"). This is *false* in the case of things not actually separate.
2. **Simple consideration**: attending to one aspect without considering another (e.g., considering the nature of white without considering the apple). This is *not false* — it makes no claim about separate existence; it simply focuses on one aspect.

Plato confused these: because the intellect grasps forms *universally* and *immaterially*, he concluded the forms *exist* universally and immaterially (separate Forms). This does not follow. The form is in the thing materially; it is in the intellect immaterially. Different modes of existence in different subjects.

**Three grades of abstraction** (Aquinas' philosophy of science):

| Abstraction Level | Science | What Is Abstracted | What Is Not |
|---|---|---|---|
| From *individual* sensible matter (this flesh, these bones) | Natural philosophy | Individual conditions | Common sensible matter (flesh/bone as such) |
| From *common* sensible matter (hot/cold, wet/dry) | Mathematics | Sensible qualities | Common intelligible matter (substance as subject to quantity) |
| From *all* matter including intelligible | Metaphysics | All material conditions | Nothing — considers being, unity, act, potency as such |

This is the basis for the distinct sciences: natural philosophy studies mobile material things (cannot abstract from matter); mathematics abstracts from sensible but not from intelligible matter; metaphysics and theology abstract from all matter.

**The intelligible species is NOT what is understood — it is the means of understanding**:
If we knew only our own mental impressions (species), then: (a) science would be about ideas in the soul, not about real things, (b) every opinion would be equally "true" (Protagoras: "whatever seems, is true"), since the faculty would judge only its own impression. Rather: the species is the *form by which* the intellect understands; the real thing outside the soul is *what* is understood. The intellect reaches through the species to the extra-mental reality.

**The intellect knows universals directly; singulars indirectly**: Since the principle of singularity is individual matter, and abstraction removes individual matter, the intellect directly grasps the universal. It grasps singulars only *by reflection* — by turning back to the phantasms in which the universal species was grasped (explaining how we can say "Socrates is a man" — universal predicate applied to singular by reflexion through phantasm).

**The intellect cannot actually know the infinite**: The infinite in material things exists only potentially (always one more). The intellect, too, knows the infinite only potentially — it can always understand more, but it cannot simultaneously actually know infinite things (which would require an infinite number of species simultaneously). It can know infinite things *potentially and successively*, never all-at-once.

**Contingent things**: Known as contingent (singular) by sense indirectly; their necessary elements (what-follows-from-form) are known by intellect directly.

**Future things**: Cannot be known in themselves except by God (to whom all time is simultaneously present). Known by us only through causal principles (with certainty if necessary connection; with probability if contingent).

---

### Q. 86 — What the Intellect Knows in Material Things

Summary of key limits:
- **Singulars**: Known indirectly by intellect (reflection to phantasm), directly by sense
- **Infinite**: Only potentially known; never actually or habitually in full
- **Contingent**: Universal-necessary aspects known by intellect; the singular contingent event by sense
- **Future**: Not knowable in itself; known through causes (necessary or probable) or divine revelation

## PASS 4 — QQ. 87-89: Self-Knowledge, Knowledge of Immaterial Things, and the Separated Soul

*How the soul knows itself, its habits, and its acts; why it cannot naturally know angels directly in this life; and how the separated soul knows after death — the pivot point where hylomorphism meets immortality.*

### Q. 87 — How the Soul Knows Itself (4 Articles)

**Does the soul know itself by its own essence?** No — unlike God (whose essence = pure act = perfectly intelligible) or angels (whose essence is act in the intelligible order, so they know themselves by their essence), the human soul's essence is *potential* with respect to intelligible things (tabula rasa). It becomes known as it becomes actual.

"The intellect knows itself not by its essence, but **by its act**."

Two modes of self-knowledge:
1. **Singular/existential**: Socrates perceives that he understands → he perceives that he has an intellect. This is immediate and infallible.
2. **Universal/essential**: Careful inquiry into what the soul is — its nature, what distinguishes it from other things. This can err and requires disciplined investigation. Many have been wrong about the soul's nature (materialists, dualists, etc.).

**The soul knows its own habits** by their acts (since habits are between pure power and pure act; only the actual is knowable). You know you have the habit of faith because you know you believe; you know you have courage because you act courageously.

**The intellect knows its own act of understanding**: "The ultimate perfection of the intellect consists in its own operation; for this is not an act tending to something else... but it remains in the agent as its perfection and act." Understanding is the intellect's own immanent act — it understands that it understands. This is reflexive and immediate.

**The intellect knows acts of will**: By knowing that good is presented to the will, and that the will is moved by what the intellect presents.

---

### Q. 88 — How the Soul Knows What Is Above It (Immaterial Substances/Angels) (4 Articles)

**In this life, the human intellect cannot directly/naturally know angels or the divine essence**: Its proper object is "the quiddity of material things" — forms abstracted from sensible phantasms. Immaterial substances (angels) cannot be represented adequately by any species abstracted from matter, because they are of an entirely different order of being.

We know immaterial things in this life only:
- **Via material effects** (knowing God exists from creation's order; knowing angels from Scripture and effects)
- **Analogically** (knowing what they are *not*, or what they are *by comparison* to material things)
- **By their influence** (the light of the active intellect, ultimately from God, illuminates our understanding — but we do not see God directly through this)

Plato's error: if separate Forms were the direct objects of intellect, natural science would be impossible (knowledge of motion and matter excluded), and knowing separate Forms would not give knowledge of their sensible instances (since they differ essentially).

**The hierarchy of intellectual light**: God knows by His one essence. Superior angels know through fewer, more universal species (deep but abstract). Inferior angels through more, less universal species. The human soul holds the *lowest place* among intellectual substances — it requires the most concrete, particular, sensible starting points for its (weakest) intellectual power. This is why union with the body is fitting for the soul: not a prison (Plato) but an appropriate means of knowledge for its grade of intellect.

---

### Q. 89 — Knowledge of the Separated Soul (8 Articles)

**Can the separated soul understand anything?** Yes — by a different mode.

The crucial resolution: "The soul has one mode of being when in the body, and another when apart from it, its nature remaining always the same." The soul's *nature* doesn't change at death — only its *mode of existence* (and therefore its mode of operation) changes. As fire's nature (to rise) remains the same whether it is in its proper place or displaced, the soul's rational nature remains whether embodied or separated.

**Mode of understanding in embodied state**: By turning to phantasms in corporeal organs.

**Mode of understanding in separated state**: By turning to "simply intelligible objects" — by *divinely participated species*, similar to how angels understand, but at a lesser degree. This is not supernatural grace but the natural mode of a separated intellectual substance.

Why was the soul given a body at all, if separated understanding is more like the angelic mode?
- The soul is the *lowest* intellectual nature. If it received angelic-style species in life (few, highly universal), it would understand only confusedly — like giving Leibnizian calculus to a child. The concrete, particular sensory grounding allows the soul to build proper and adequate knowledge of its natural objects, thing by thing.
- "It was for the soul's good that it was united to a body, and that it understands by turning to the phantasms."

**What the separated soul knows**:
- Other separated souls: *perfectly* (same mode of being)
- Angels: *imperfectly* (their mode exceeds the soul's natural capacity)
- Natural things: *confusedly* (by the divinely infused participated species — general and less distinct than embodied knowledge through phantasms)
- Individual singulars: only insofar as specific divinely infused species are provided (saints may know particulars by grace/glory; naturally limited)

**Habits of knowledge acquired in life**: Generally *persist* in the separated soul — the intelligible species stored habitually in the possible intellect survive (the intellect itself is incorruptible). The soul retains what it learned. However, *exercise* of these habits (actual understanding) requires a different mode after death.

**Local distance**: Does not impede the separated soul's knowledge, since it does not understand through corporeal organs requiring physical proximity.

## PASS 5 — QQ. 90-93: Production of Man — Soul, Body, Woman, and the Image of God

*The theological anthropology of origins: how man's soul and body were made, why woman was made from man, and the deepest question — in what sense man is the "image of God" and what that means for knowledge of ourselves and of God.*

### Q. 90 — Production of Man's Soul (4 Articles)

**Soul is not of God's substance**: Soul is in potentiality (can potentially understand all things, but does not always actually understand); it acquires knowledge from external things; it has various distinct powers. God's nature is pure act, self-sufficient, receives from nothing, admits of no internal diversity. These are flatly incompatible — hence the soul cannot be of divine substance. (This refutes Manichaean and Neoplatonic panpsychism that views souls as fragments of the divine light.)

**Soul is made by creation**: The soul is a subsistent form (established in Q. 75). Subsistent forms cannot be produced from pre-existing matter: not from corporeal matter (this would make it a corporeal thing) nor from spiritual matter (this would be transmutation of one spiritual substance into another — and there is no spiritual matter of that kind). The soul must be produced from nothing — i.e., *by creation alone*. This is uniquely God's act: every secondary cause presupposes something given by a prior cause and works by *changing* it; only the first cause can produce without presupposing anything.

**Not made by angels**: For the same reason — only God can create; angels can only change pre-existing matter.

**Not made before the body**: The soul, as form of the body, has its natural perfection only united to its proper matter. God created things in their perfect natural state. Creating the soul before the body (Origen, and tentatively Augustine) would mean creating an incomplete thing (the form without its proper complementary matter). The soul's separation from the body after death is *secondary* — due to the defect (death) — not the primary natural state.

---

### Q. 91 — Production of the First Man's Body (4 Articles)

**From the slime of the earth**: The human body was formed by God from earth — consistent with Genesis and with the human body's constitution (water, minerals, etc.). This ground-material is fitting: it shows man's connection to the whole material creation; it grounds humility; it recalls mortality ("dust to dust," Gen. 3:19). The body was well-disposed at creation — properly proportioned for the soul's governance, though not incorruptible by nature (incorruptibility would come from grace, as Q. 97 treats).

**Formed by God immediately**: As with the soul, the body of the first man was not produced through natural generation but by God's direct creative act from earth — since natural generation presupposes an already-existing member of the species.

---

### Q. 92 — Production of Woman (4 Articles)

**Why woman was made**: As a helper in generation. For other works, another man is a more efficient helper; but generation requires sexual differentiation. Among perfect animals: in plants, active and passive generative powers are in one organism; in perfect animals, active = male, passive = female; in man, who is further ordered to intellectual life (the noblest vital operation), the distinction is even more complete, and the two live together for life (not just for the moment of coition) — hence domestic community.

**Why from man** (four reasons):
1. To give Adam a certain dignity as *principle of the whole human race* (as God is principle of all) — "God made the whole human race from one" (Acts 17:26)
2. So man would love woman more, knowing she is of his own substance ("bone of my bone")
3. Because man and woman unite not only for generation but for domestic life, in which the man is the head (Aristotle: the human couple is a lifelong domestic partnership)
4. Sacramental signification: the Church takes her origin from Christ, as woman from man

**From the rib** (two reasons):
1. Signifies social union: not from head (she would rule), not from feet (he would despise her as slave), but from side — as social companion/partner
2. Sacramental: from Christ's side flowed blood and water — the Church's sacramental foundation

**Reply to "defective male" (Aristotle)**: Woman is "defective" only regarding the individual procreative act (the male seed tends toward producing its own likeness = male). But regarding *human nature as species*, woman is not defective — the general intention of nature (directed by God as universal Author) includes both sexes for generation. Individual defect vs. species-level intention.

**Two kinds of subjection**: Servile subjection (superior uses subject for *own* benefit) = a result of sin. Economic/civil subjection (superior governs for *subject's* benefit) = existed before sin. In innocence, woman would have been subject to man's reason for the order of domestic life — not as slave but as rational partner in common life.

---

### Q. 93 — Image of God (Imago Dei) — 9 Articles

**The richest question in QQ. 75-102.**

**What an image is**: An image requires (1) likeness, and (2) that the likeness is *derived from* (copied from) the original — not merely accidental resemblance. (Two identical eggs are not images of each other.) An image does not require equality — it can be imperfect. Man is "to the image" of God (*ad imaginem*) — a likeness in approach, not in full equality.

**Only intellectual creatures are properly "to the image of God"**: Things are like God: first by *existing* (all creatures), second by *living* (plants, animals), third by *knowing/understanding* (intellectual creatures). The image-likeness must be in the most specific difference. Since God knows Himself and loves Himself, the image is principally in the capacity for *self-knowing and self-loving* — which only intellectual creatures have. Irrational creatures have generic or accidental likeness, not species-proper image.

**Three degrees of the image in man**:
1. **Image of creation** (*imago creationis*): the soul's *natural aptitude* to know and love God — present in all human beings, simply by having an intellectual nature
2. **Image of re-creation/grace** (*imago recreationis*): actually and habitually knowing and loving God, though imperfectly — present only in the *just* (those in sanctifying grace)
3. **Image of likeness/glory** (*imago similitudinis*): knowing and loving God perfectly — present only in the *blessed* in heaven

This three-level structure directly connects to the three states of man: before grace (natural), with grace (supernatural in this life), and in glory (supernatural in heaven). The image is always there in potency (level 1) but increasingly realized (levels 2-3).

**The image is in the mind, not the body**: Body can be like God only in a generic way (as a being). The image is specifically in the intellectual soul — memory, intellect, will — which can reflect the Trinity.

**Augustine's Trinitarian image** (Art. 5-8): The soul's faculties of *memory* (retaining God), *understanding* (knowing God), and *will* (loving God) constitute a created analogy of the Trinity (Father/Son/Holy Spirit). This is the famous Augustinian psychological analogy (from *De Trinitate*). Man's mind images the Trinity because God is most perfectly known by understanding Himself and loving Himself — and the human mind, in its most elevated operations, moves in a parallel structure. This image does not perfectly represent the Trinity (we believe the Trinity; we see the mental trinity); it is an *imperfect* copy at distance.

**Image vs. likeness**: Image = likeness derived from an original. Likeness = general resemblance. Every image involves likeness; not every likeness involves image. The term *likeness* (similitudo) sometimes refers to grace-level resemblance ("be holy as I am holy"), while *image* refers to the natural intellectual structure.

**Angels are more to the image than man**: In their intellectual nature (more perfect intellect, always in act, no need for phantasms). But man has unique image-aspects (proceeds from man as God from God; whole soul in whole body as God is in the world). Primarily, angels are more perfectly the image of God; in certain accidental respects, man has unique resemblances.

## PASS 6 — QQ. 94-102: State of the First Man — Original Justice, Gifts, Mastership, and Paradise

*The theological portrait of unfallen man: what Adam knew, how his will was ordered, what gifts he had, how the race would have continued, and where he lived. This is the "before the fall" anthropology that grounds Aquinas' account of original sin and grace in Part I-II.*

### Q. 94 — First Man's Intellect (4 Articles)

**Did Adam see God's essence?** No. Proof: anyone who sees God's essence cannot willingly turn from God — because God's essence = beatitude itself, and the will necessarily and irresistibly desires beatitude. Since Adam sinned, he did not see God's essence. His knowledge was intermediate: *superior to ours* (who are distracted by sense, clouded by passion) but *below the beatific vision*. He knew God through intelligible effects more clearly and steadily than we can, because the lower powers did not disturb his intellectual operation.

**Did he know angels through their essence?** No — the soul's proper connatural mode of knowing is through phantasms (as established in QQ. 84-89), and this was true of Adam also. He could not transcend his natural cognitive mode. But he had clearer knowledge of intelligible things generally (less noise from passions and sensory distraction) and thus better knowledge of angels than we have, analogously.

**Did he know all things?** By divinely infused intelligible species — yes, for all things naturally knowable and all things necessary for governing human life and directing it to its supernatural end. Not: hidden individual facts (the number of pebbles in a stream), other men's secret thoughts, future contingent events — these are not needed for governance and cannot be reached by natural knowledge.

Adam would have advanced in knowledge — not in *number* of things known, but in *mode* (from speculative to experiential). Regarding supernatural truths: further revelation would have given more. This advancement of knowledge is not comparable to advancement in merit (unlike merit, knowledge can be shared from one person to another).

**Could he be deceived?** Not in his practical reason's judgment about things ordered to his end — this was perfectly ordered by grace. But the senses themselves could report as they do now (a distant object looks small) — this is a *natural* effect of physical laws, not sinful deception, and would have existed in innocence.

---

### Q. 95 — First Man's Will: Original Justice and Grace (4 Articles)

**Man was created in grace (original justice)**: The key argument: the body's subjection to soul and the lower powers' subjection to reason in the state of innocence was *not* natural (otherwise it would have remained after sin, as natural gifts remain even in demons). Since this ordered subjection was greater than natural, it required a supernatural cause — sanctifying grace. Original justice = this ordered subjection of all lower to higher, grounded in grace.

Three-level ordering in the state of innocence:
1. Reason subject to God (by grace)
2. Lower powers subject to reason (because of #1)
3. Body subject to soul (because of #1-2)

Loss of grace at the Fall broke the first ordering, which immediately dissolved the second and third — explaining why after sin "the flesh lusteth against the spirit" (Gal. 5:17).

**Passions in the state of innocence**: Present but perfectly ordered by reason. Passions directed to present good (love, joy) and future good (hope, desire-not-yet-obtained) *existed*. Passions directed to present evil (fear, sorrow, immoderate concupiscence) *did not* — because no evil was present or imminent, and no good was lacking. Moral virtue does not eliminate passions but *regulates* them (the temperate person desires as they should, not that they don't desire).

**Adam had all the virtues**: Rectitude = reason subject to God, lower powers to reason. Virtues = perfections directing reason to God and regulating lower powers. Therefore rectitude entails all virtues:
- Virtues implying no imperfection (charity, justice): existed in act and habit
- Virtues implying imperfection compatible with the state (faith, hope): existed in act and habit
- Virtues implying imperfection incompatible with the state (penance for sin, mercy for others' wretchedness): existed as habits only, not in act (since no sin, no wretchedness)

---

### Q. 96 — First Man's Mastership (4 Articles)

In the state of innocence:
- **Mastery over animals and material things**: yes — consistent with the natural order (man's intellect is the highest created form; animals and plants are ordered to serve rational creatures)
- **No servile dominion over other men**: one man is not by nature a slave to another. Difference of talents → different roles without servile subjection. Post-sin slavery = a punishment for sin and a remedy against worse disorder (preventing greater evils by constraining disordered men).
- **Economic/civil governance existed**: for the common good, the wiser direct others — but as *stewardship*, not ownership. Social life would have existed; hierarchy of roles without oppression.

---

### Q. 97 — Preservation of the Individual: Immortality (4 Articles)

**Man in innocence was immortal** — not because the body had a naturally incorruptible constitution (it was formed of contrary elements as now) but because the soul had a *supernatural gift* that preserved the body from corruption and dissolution. This is a *donum supernaturale* (supernatural gift) added to nature.

**The body was passible externally but not in ways that would lead to dissolution**: External agents could affect the body (not absolutely impassible), but the soul's governing power prevented any such affection from causing death or fundamental corruption.

**Man sustained by food**: Normal vegetative operations continued; the body required nourishment as now. But the digestive waste and natural aging process were continuously counteracted by the supernatural gift.

**The Tree of Life**: A real, material tree whose fruit had the power — naturally or supernaturally? — to preserve bodily life. Aquinas follows the view that the fruit had a natural power that prevented the consumption of vital humidity, thereby delaying the body's natural tendency toward dissolution. It was not a magical object but a natural remedy for natural decay, which however required both God's institution and man's use to function as a life-preserver.

---

### QQ. 98-101 — Preservation of the Species: Generation and Offspring

**Generation in innocence would have occurred** — through sexual union, but without lust or the disordering of reason by passion. Sexual desire would have been subject to reason's command (as the hand obeys to take or not take an object). Generation was ordered by reason like all other natural acts — pleasurable but not overwhelming reason.

**Offspring in innocence**:
- Born as infants (not as fully-formed adults) — the body's nature requires growth from small beginnings
- But with faster development toward reason's governance: less helplessness than now, given that the body was entirely subject to soul
- Born with original justice (grace) — because original justice would have been transmitted with human nature through generation (as a kind of "natal gift" of the species)
- Passions in offspring would be subject to reason from the start; concupiscence would not exist as a disordering force

**Children in paradise** would have been educated and brought to the perfection of knowledge and virtue through human teaching — demonstrating that even the ideal state involved genuine human community, teaching, and temporal development.

---

### Q. 102 — Paradise (4 Articles)

**Paradise is a real, corporeal place** — situated in the east, as Genesis says. Augustine's view: both corporeal and spiritual (has both a literal and a spiritual dimension). Aquinas holds it is literal (real geography) as the foundation for any spiritual interpretation.

**Why in the east**: The east is the "right hand of the heavens" (Aristotle) — the nobler direction from which heavenly motion proceeds. Fittingly, the most excellent earthly abode for the highest earthly creature is in the noblest earthly region.

**Why suited for man**: Even temperature (protecting against external corruption of the body); beautiful and delightful; equipped with food (for preservation of individual) including the Tree of Life; commanding location for governance of creation.

**Man was placed in paradise**, not created there — placed after being created (implied by Gen. 2: "God placed man in Paradise to cultivate and guard it"). His work there was not idle leisure but real tending — though without toil or pain.

**Why man could be expelled**: Despite all gifts, the state of innocence was *viator* (wayfarer) status — merit-earning, not yet confirmed in glory. The beatific vision and full confirmation awaited the completion of probation. Paradise was a beginning, not an end.
