# The Myth of the Rational Voter: Why Democracies Choose Bad Policies
> Democracies fail not because voters are ignorant but because they are *irrational* — they hold systematically biased beliefs about economics and vote on them, making bad policy a rational response to irrational demand.

## Argument Spine
Caplan's central thesis: the standard defense of democracy rests on the "Miracle of Aggregation" — that random voter errors cancel out, leaving a rational collective result. This miracle requires that errors be *unsystematic*. Caplan demonstrates that voters instead hold *systematically biased* economic beliefs (anti-market, anti-foreign, make-work, and pessimistic biases), that these biases are not driven by ignorance but by *rational irrationality* (believing false things is costless when your vote doesn't determine outcomes), and that politicians supply the policies voters irrationally demand. The solution is not better voter education but reducing the scope of decisions subject to majority rule, and expanding the role of markets and expert-constrained institutions.
