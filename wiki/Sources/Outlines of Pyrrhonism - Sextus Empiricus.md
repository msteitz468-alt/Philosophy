# Outlines of Pyrrhonism - Sextus Empiricus

> The classic exposition of Pyrrhonian Skepticism by Sextus Empiricus (fl. late 2nd–early 3rd century CE), in Benson Mates' translation (Oxford 1996). Outlines the "Skeptic Way" as the third philosophy: Dogmatists (e.g. Aristotle, Epicurus, Stoics — claim to have found truth), Academics (assert it cannot be apprehended), Skeptics (continue to search). Core practice: oppose any claim with an equal and opposing one (equipollence/isosthenia) to reach epoché (suspension of judgment) and thereby ataraxia (tranquility). Detailed 10 Modes (Aenesidemus), 5 Modes (Agrippa), slogans, and specific critiques of logic, physics (god, cause, body, elements), and ethics. Explicitly positions itself against the "Dogmatists" already extensively covered in the vault.

## Summary

Sextus provides a systematic "outline account" (not dogma) of how the Skeptic lives and thinks: by holding to appearances for daily action (criterion = phantasia/appearance; 4-fold ordinary life: nature, compulsion of pathé, laws/customs, instruction), while suspending assent on all non-evident matters. The result of equipollent opposition is not paralysis but the "shadow" of ataraxia following epoché, plus moderate pathé for the unavoidable. The Skeptic reports what appears "like a chronicler," without firm belief even in the skeptical slogans (they are self-refuting or non-assertoric). 

Book I: general account — nomenclature (Zetetic, Ephectic, Aporetic, Pyrrhonean), definition via opposition of phenomena/noumena, origins (hope of ataraxia + practice of equal opposition), does not dogmatize, has a "system" only as a way of life producing epoché, criterion of action, goal of ataraxia, the modes, slogans, and differences from other philosophies.

Book II: specific account — objections to the parts of "philosophy," especially the criterion of truth (by whom, by means of which, according to which), signs, proof, truth itself, leading to regress, circularity, hypothesis problems.

Book III: physics and ethics — productive sources (esp. god as cause: disagreements on substance/form/location/providence make inconceivable; if cares for world then impious implications), cause in general (relative to effect; circularity in conceiving cause before/after effect), material sources (total disagreement among all prior thinkers, including Democritus/Epicurus atoms), bodies (definitions via extension or acting/pat lead to aporiai on points, lines, surfaces, touch), and further topics in the full text.

This is the "anti-Dogmatist" counterweight and completer to the vault's rich coverage of Plato, Aristotle, Stoics, Epicurus, Plotinus — the third of the three philosophies that Sextus himself names using the exact schools dominating the classical cluster.

## Key Claims and Structure (extracted from text)

**The Three Philosophies** (opening of Book I):

- Dogmatists (properly so called): followers of Aristotle and Epicurus, the Stoics, and certain others — "think that they have found it [the truth]."
- Academics (Cleitomachus and Carneades, and other Academics): "have asserted that it cannot be apprehended."
- Skeptics: "continue to search."

**Definition of the Skeptic Way**:

"A disposition to oppose phenomena and noumena to one another in any way whatever, with the result that, owing to the equipollence among the things and statements thus opposed, we are brought first to epoché and then to ataraxia."

**Epoché and Ataraxia**:

Epoché: "a state of the intellect on account of which we neither deny nor affirm anything."

Ataraxia: "an untroubled and tranquil condition of the soul."

Famous Apelles anecdote: the painter, unable to depict the horse's froth, threw his sponge in frustration — and it produced exactly the effect sought. So too the Skeptics, unable to resolve the anomaly, suspended judgment — and ataraxia followed "as a shadow follows the body." Goal re belief is ataraxia; for unavoidable things, moderate pathé (ordinary people suffer the pathos + the additional belief that it is "by nature" bad).

**The Skeptic Does Not Dogmatize**:

Assents only to pathé forced by phantasia (e.g. "I seem to be hot"). Does not assent to non-evident. Even the slogans are not propounded as certain; they are self-applicable ("Nothing more" applies to itself) or reports of current pathos "like a chronicler."

**Criterion of the Skeptic Way**:

The appearance (phantasia). Fourfold ordinary regimen of life (followed without belief): (1) guidance of nature (sensation/thought), (2) compulsion of the pathé (hunger/thirst), (3) handing down of laws and customs (piety good, impiety bad), (4) instruction in arts and crafts.

**The Ten Modes** (Aenesidemus; detailed with examples):

1. Variety of animals (different origins, body structures, sense organs, pleasures/aversions — e.g. dung beetles love dung, humans find it repugnant; even the dog has internal "dialectic," justice, art of hunting, and better "apprehensive phantasia" in some cases per Chrysippus/Homer's Argus).

2. Differences among human beings (body: Scythian vs Indian, humors, tolerances like old Attic woman drinking 30 drams hemlock; soul: different choices, poets on different delights).

3. Differences in constitution of the sense organs (eye vs touch for paintings; honey pleasant to tongue but not eye; apple appears smooth/fragrant/sweet/yellow — but may have more qualities or only one appearing differently).

4. Circumstances (natural/unnatural condition, awake/sleep, age, motion/rest, liking/disliking, hunger/satiety, drunk/sober, predispositions, courageous/fearful, distressed/cheerful — e.g. same food pleasant to hungry, unpleasant to sated; same things shameful sober, not when drunk).

5. Positions, distances, locations (stoa tapers from end, boat small far away; lamplight dim in sun bright in dark; oar broken in water straight out; egg soft in bird hard in air).

6. Admixtures (nothing affects us by itself but with something — complexion in warm vs cold air; spices more pungent in bath/sun).

7. Quantity and constitution of external objects (e.g. moderate wine strengthens, excess maddens; small quantity of food nourishes, large disturbs).

8. Relativity (everything in relation to something — the judging subject or things observed together; "everything appears in relation to something").

9. Frequency or infrequency (sun more marvelous to those seeing it rarely; earthquakes to those unaccustomed).

10. Ways of life, customs, laws, mythic beliefs, dogmatic opinions (huge variation in what is held good/bad, pious/impious, beautiful/ugly by different peoples and schools).

Superordinate: modes based on what does the judging / what is judged / both, all reducing to relativity as most generic.

**The Five Modes** (later Skeptics):

Disagreement (unresolvable impasse in life and philosophy), infinite regress, relativity, hypothesis (undemonstrated starting point), circularity (reciprocal dependence). Any matter of inquiry (sense or thought object) falls under them → epoché. Used together with the ten against Dogmatist precipitancy.

**Skeptic Slogans** (expressive of temper of mind, not firm assertions):

"Not more" (or "nothing more this than that" — equipollence), "Non-assertion" (aphasia: avoidance of affirmation or denial in the wider sense), "Perhaps / It is possible / Maybe" (and their negatives), "I withhold assent," "I determine nothing," "Everything is indeterminate," "Everything is non-apprehensible," "I am non-apprehensive," "To every argument an equal argument is opposed."

They are imprecise, self-referential, or reports of pathos; not dogmas.

**Differences from Other Philosophies** (explicitly engages the vault's schools):

- Heraclitean: flux vs. our report of what appears.
- Democritean: atoms/void as non-evident "truth" vs. our suspension on non-evident.
- Cyrenaic: pleasure/pain as criterion (subjective) vs. our broader appearances + ordinary life.
- Protagorean: "man is measure" as dogma vs. our non-assertoric reporting.
- Academic: they *assert* non-apprehensibility (a negative dogma); we continue searching without asserting even that.

Book II/III apply the method to destroy the Dogmatists' criterion, signs, proof, god, cause, elements, bodies, etc., via the same equipollence and regress arguments. E.g., god: no agreed conception (corporeal/incorporeal? anthropomorphic? inside/outside cosmos?); if provident then cause of evils or partial/weak/malevolent (impious); cannot be proved without circularity/regress or making the non-evident pre-evident. Cause: relative, circular to conceive (effect needs cause known first and vice versa); cannot exist before, at same time, or after effect properly. Material sources: endless disagreement (water, air, fire, earth, atoms [Democritus/Epicurus named], numbers, homogeneous things, etc.) → no agreed elements. 

## Relevance to Vault and Cross-Links

- **The "Third Philosophy" naming the Dogmatists**: Sextus uses precisely the philosophers dominating the vault (followers of Aristotle and Epicurus, the Stoics) as the paradigmatic examples of those who claim to have found the truth.
