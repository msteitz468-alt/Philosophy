# Beyond Good and Evil - Nietzsche (AmazonClassics Edition)

> Supposing that Truth is a woman—what then? ... It amounted to the very inversion of truth, and the denial of the perspective—the fundamental condition—of life, to speak of Spirit and the Good as Plato spoke of them...

AmazonClassics Edition (Seattle; public domain translation, standard English rendering of Nietzsche's 1886 text). *Jenseits von Gut und Böse: Vorspiel einer Philosophie der Zukunft*. Part of Nietzsche's project of revaluation of all values, following *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* and preceding *On the Genealogy of Morals*. Aphoristic style, 9 chapters + preface + interlude epigrams. No scholarly apparatus in this edition; straightforward presentation of the text.

## Preface

Nietzsche opens with the provocative image: supposing Truth is a woman, then all dogmatist philosophers have been clumsy and unseemly in their addresses to her. Dogma now stands discouraged; all dogmatizing in philosophy may have been only noble puerilism. The basis of imposing philosophical edifices has been popular superstition (soul-superstition, subject- and ego-superstition), play upon words, deception on the part of grammar, or audacious generalization of very human facts. The philosophy of the dogmatists was a promise, like astrology; we owe to it the grand style of architecture. To inscribe themselves on humanity, great things first wander as caricatures: dogmatic philosophy (Vedanta in Asia, Platonism in Europe). 

The worst, most tiresome, and most dangerous error hitherto has been the dogmatist error—Plato’s invention of Pure Spirit and the Good in Itself. The struggle against Plato (or, for the “people,” against the ecclesiastical oppression of Christianity, which is Platonism for the “people”) produced in Europe a magnificent tension of soul, as had not existed previously. With such a tensely strained bow one can now aim at the furthest goals. Europe feels this tension as distress and has twice tried to unbend the bow: by Jesuitism, and by democratic enlightenment (liberty of the press, newspaper-reading). But we, who are neither Jesuits, nor democrats, nor even sufficiently Germans—we good Europeans, and free, very free spirits—we have it still, all the distress of spirit and all the tension of its bow! And perhaps also the arrow, the duty, and, who knows? the goal to aim at...

Dated Sils Maria Upper Engadine, June, 1885.

## Chapter 1: Prejudices of Philosophers

The chapter is a sustained attack on the fundamental prejudices underlying all previous philosophy (especially metaphysics and dogmatism).

Key aphorisms and arguments (paraphrased with key quotes):

1. The "Will to Truth" has led philosophers to many hazardous enterprises. But why want truth rather than untruth, uncertainty, or ignorance? The problem of the *value* of truth arises. Who is the Oedipus here? The Sphinx? We may be the first to discern and risk raising it. There is risk—perhaps no greater risk.

2. The typical prejudice of metaphysicians of all times: the belief in antitheses of values. They assume things of the highest value must have an origin of their own, in the lap of Being, the intransitory, the concealed God, the “Thing-in-itself”—not in this transitory, seductive, illusory, paltry world of delusion and cupidity. But perhaps the value of the "good and respected things" consists precisely in their being insidiously related, knotted, and crocheted to the "evil" ones—perhaps even essentially identical with them. "Perhaps!" But who wishes to concern himself with such dangerous “Perhapses”! This investigation awaits a new order of philosophers—philosophers of the dangerous “Perhaps” in every sense. Nietzsche sees such new philosophers beginning to appear.

3. The greater part of conscious thinking (even philosophical) must be counted among the instinctive functions. One must learn anew about "innateness." Behind all logic and its seeming sovereignty are valuations—physiological demands for the maintenance of a definite mode of life. Valuations such as "the certain is worth more than the uncertain," "illusion is less valuable than 'truth'" may be only superficial, special kinds of niaiserie necessary for beings like ourselves. Supposing man is not just the "measure of things."

4. The falseness of an opinion is not for us any objection to it. The question is how far an opinion is life-furthering, life-preserving, species-preserving, perhaps species-rearing. We are fundamentally inclined to maintain that the falsest opinions (including the synthetic judgments a priori) are the most indispensable to us; without recognition of logical fictions, without constant counterfeiting of the world by means of numbers, man could not live. To recognize untruth as a condition of life: that is certainly to impugn the traditional ideas of value in a dangerous manner, and a philosophy which ventures to do so has thereby alone placed itself *beyond good and evil*.

5. What causes philosophers to be regarded half-distrustfully and half-mockingly is not their innocence or mistakes, but that there is not enough honest dealing with them. They pose as though their real opinions had been discovered through the self-evolving of a cold, pure, divinely indifferent dialectic, whereas in fact a prejudiced proposition (generally their heart’s desire abstracted and refined) is defended with arguments sought out after the event. They are advocates who do not wish to be regarded as such. The spectacle of the Tartuffery of old Kant, equally stiff and decent, with which he entices us into the dialectic by-ways that lead (more correctly mislead) to his “categorical imperative”—makes us fastidious ones smile. Or the hocus-pocus in mathematical form by which Spinoza clad his philosophy in mail and mask (the “love of his wisdom”) in order to strike terror into the heart of the assailant—how much personal timidity and vulnerability does this masquerade of a sickly recluse betray!

6. It has gradually become clear what every great philosophy up till now has consisted of—namely, the confession of its originator, and a species of involuntary and unconscious auto-biography; and that the moral (or immoral) purpose in every philosophy has constituted the true vital germ out of which the entire plant has always grown. To understand how the abstrusest metaphysical assertions have been arrived at, ask: “What morality do they (or does he) aim at?” The fundamental impulses of man have all practiced philosophy; each has attempted to look upon itself as the ultimate end and legitimate lord over the other impulses. In the philosopher there is absolutely nothing impersonal; his morality furnishes a decided testimony as to who he is.

7. How malicious philosophers can be! Epicurus called the Platonists "Dionysiokolakes" (flatterers of Dionysius/tyrants’ accessories and lick-spittles; also "they are all actors, there is nothing genuine about them"). The malignant reproach Epicurus cast upon Plato: annoyed by the grandiose manner and mise en scène style of which Plato and his scholars were masters. Greece took a hundred years to find out who the garden-god Epicurus really was.

8. There is a point in every philosophy at which the “conviction” of the philosopher appears on the scene. (Mystery reference to "the spirit of truth" or similar.)

9. You desire to live “according to Nature”? Oh, you noble Stoics, what fraud of words! Imagine a being like Nature, boundlessly extravagant, indifferent, without purpose, pity, or justice, at once fruitful and barren and uncertain. To live is not endeavoring to be otherwise than this Nature? Living is valuing, preferring, being unjust, being limited, endeavoring to be different. The imperative “living according to Nature” means the same as “living according to life”—how could you do differently? But you want something quite the contrary: you wish to dictate your morals and ideals to Nature herself, to incorporate them therein; you insist that it shall be Nature “according to the Stoa.” This is an old and everlasting story: as soon as a philosophy begins to believe in itself, it always creates the world in its own image. Philosophy is this tyrannical impulse itself, the most spiritual Will to Power, the will to “creation of the world,” the will to the causa prima.

(Continuing aphorisms in the chapter develop the critique: philosophers as actors and self-deluders; the "conviction" appearing; the will to power as fundamental; explicit attacks on the "Tartuffery of old Kant"; Spinoza's geometrical method as mask; the belief in antitheses as the fundamental prejudice; the unconscious autobiography; etc.)

The chapter establishes Nietzsche's method of genealogy and suspicion: question the "will to truth," the belief in value antitheses, the dogmatists' claims to purity and disinterestedness. All previous philosophy (Platonism, Christianity as its popular form, Stoicism, Kantianism, Spinozism) is revealed as prejudiced, value-laden, often life-denying in its pursuit of "the Truth," "the Good in Itself," "living according to Nature," or the categorical imperative. The new philosophers will be "philosophers of the dangerous 'Perhaps'" who recognize untruth as a condition of life and place themselves beyond good and evil.

## Insights + Vault Relevance

- Nietzsche's radical suspicion: all philosophy (including the "pure" a priori of Kant or the geometrical of Spinoza) is "confession" and "involuntary and unconscious auto-biography" driven by the philosopher's deepest (often moral or immoral) impulses and physiological demands. The "will to truth" itself must be questioned for its value; falseness of an opinion is no objection if it is life-furthering. "To recognize untruth as a condition of life" places one "beyond good and evil."

- Direct, primary-sourced critiques of the vault's core figures: Plato's invention of Pure Spirit and the Good in Itself as the worst dogmatist error (Christianity = Platonism for the "people"); Stoics' "living according to Nature" as fraud and self-tyranny (they want to tyrannize Nature in their own image); "the Tartuffery of old Kant" with the categorical imperative (stiff, decent enticement into dialectic by-ways); Spinoza's "hocus-pocus in mathematical form" and "love of his wisdom" as mask for personal timidity and vulnerability (clad in mail to strike terror).

- "Will to Power" introduced as the most spiritual, the fundamental impulse behind even the will to truth and the "creation of the world" (causa prima). Philosophy as tyrannical impulse to impose one's image on the world.

- Perspectivism and "frog perspectives": valuations and "truths" are provisional perspectives, often made "from below" or from some corner. No absolute antitheses of values; higher value may lie in what was previously called "evil" (pretense, will to delusion, selfishness, cupidity).

## Contradictions / Open Questions

Nietzsche positions himself against the entire tradition the vault has built (Plato's Forms/Good, Aristotle's eudaimonia/function, Stoic control/acceptance of Nature, Spinoza's conatus/monism/geometrical truth, Kant's good will/duty/categorical/a priori, Hume's custom/sentiment as guide). Productive tensions: will to power vs. conatus (both "will" but one creative/tyrannical, one striving to persevere); perspectivism vs. a priori or mitigated scepticism; beyond good and evil vs. duty or ataraxia or beatific vision. No unresolved contradictions in the text itself—Nietzsche embraces the "dangerous Perhaps" and the risk. The vault can hold the tension as enrichment (genealogy + critique + affirmation).

The text itself is the "prelude to a philosophy of the future."
