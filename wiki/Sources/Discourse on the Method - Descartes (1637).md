# Discourse on the Method (1637)
> Descartes's intellectual autobiography and the first public statement of his philosophy — written in French for the unprejudiced general reader — presenting the method's four rules, a provisional morality, the *cogito*, a sketch of the God-proofs and mind-body distinction, and a mechanistic account of nature, body, and animals, with the program of making mankind "the lords and possessors of nature."

## Bibliographic Note

*Discours de la méthode pour bien conduire sa raison, et chercher la vérité dans les sciences*, first published anonymously in 1637 (Leiden) as the preface to three scientific essays (the *Dioptrics*, *Meteorics*, and *Geometry* — not included in this edition). Written in French rather than Latin deliberately, so that "those who make use of their unprejudiced natural reason will be better judges of my opinions than those who give heed to the writings of the ancients only." This edition: Duke Classics (2012), the classic public-domain English translation, six Parts + prefatory note.

Chronologically the *Discourse* (1637) **precedes** the *Meditations* (1641) — it is the earlier, more accessible, autobiographical presentation of the same foundational project the [[Sources/Meditations on First Philosophy - Descartes (Oxford, 2008)]] later developed with full metaphysical rigor and the Objections/Replies apparatus.

## Argument Spine

Reason ("good sense") is equal in all; what matters is the *method* by which it is applied. Having found the traditional learning uncertain, Descartes resolves to rebuild knowledge on foundations wholly his own. He distills a four-rule method (from mathematics), adopts a provisional morality so as to live well while doubting, then applies universal doubt until he reaches the one indubitable point — *je pense, donc je suis* (cogito ergo sum) — from which he derives the existence of God (as a more perfect being whose idea he could not have produced) and the real distinction of soul from body. He then sketches a purely mechanistic physics and physiology (a "new world," the circulation of the blood, the body as a machine made by God), argues that beasts and even man-imitating machines lack reason and language, and concludes with the practical program of a useful science that will make us masters of nature — and his reasons for publishing (and withholding) his work.

## Part-by-Part Summary

**Part I — Against the learning of the schools.** Good sense is equally distributed; error comes from misapplying reason. Descartes reviews his Jesuit education (languages, history, eloquence, poetry, mathematics, theology, philosophy, the "false sciences") and finds only mathematics certain and everything grounded in scholastic philosophy in dispute. He abandons book-learning for "the great book of the world" (travel, experience), then resolves to study himself.

**Part II — The four precepts of the method.** In a "stove-heated room" in Germany (winter 1619) he reflects that works of a single designer are more perfect than those patched together by many hands (the architect/city analogy) — and resolves to rebuild his *own* opinions on a single plan (explicitly *not* to reform the state or the sciences at large). Rejecting the excesses of logic (syllogism), ancient analysis, and modern algebra, he adopts **four rules**: (1) accept nothing not clearly and distinctly known (avoid precipitancy and prejudice); (2) divide each difficulty into as many parts as needed; (3) proceed in order from the simplest to the most complex; (4) make enumerations complete enough to omit nothing. Modeled on the "long chains of reasonings" of the geometers. → [[Concepts/The Four Precepts of the Method (Descartes)]]

**Part III — The provisional (provisory) morality.** Since one cannot suspend action while rebuilding belief, Descartes adopts a temporary code of three-or-four maxims: (1) obey the laws, customs, and religion of his country, following the most moderate opinions of the most judicious; (2) be firm and resolute in action once a course is chosen, like a traveler lost in a forest walking straight; (3) master himself rather than fortune, and change his desires rather than the order of the world — since nothing is wholly in our power except our own thoughts (an explicitly Stoic maxim). He resolves to devote his life to cultivating reason. → [[Concepts/Provisional Morality (Descartes)]]

**Part IV — Metaphysics: cogito, God, soul.** Applying hyperbolic doubt (senses deceive; reasoning errs; waking is indistinguishable from dreaming), he finds that while he tries to think everything false, "it was absolutely necessary that I, who thus thought, should be somewhat": **"I think, therefore I am" (COGITO ERGO SUM)** — the first principle of his philosophy. The 'I' is a substance whose whole essence is thinking, entirely distinct from body. From "I think, therefore I am" he extracts the general rule that whatever we conceive very clearly and distinctly is true. From the fact that he doubts (an imperfection), he infers the idea of a more perfect being that he could not have produced from himself — hence **God exists**. A version of the **ontological argument** follows (existence is contained in the idea of a perfect being as the equality of its angles is in the idea of a triangle). God's veracity is the ultimate guarantor that clear and distinct ideas are true; without God, even geometry could be doubted.

**Part V — Mechanistic physics and the animal-machine.** He summarizes his suppressed treatise (*Le Monde*): a "new world" whose chaos, given only the laws of nature God established, would arrange itself into heavens, stars, earth, and living bodies. He gives a mechanistic account of the **circulation of the blood** and the motion of the heart (crediting the "physician of England," Harvey, while differing on the mechanism — heat rather than the heart's muscular pump). The human body is "a machine made by the hands of God." He then argues that even a machine perfectly imitating an animal, or man, could be exposed by **two tests**: (1) it could never use *language* — arrange words to reply appositely to any meaning; (2) it could not act from *universal reason* applicable to every contingency (organs need a particular arrangement for each action). By these same tests beasts differ from men: brutes have *no reason at all* (the *bête-machine* thesis); the rational soul cannot be educed from matter and must be specially created and closely united to the body (not "a pilot in a ship"). → [[Concepts/The Two Tests and the Animal-Machine (Descartes)]]

**Part VI — The program of a useful science, and why publish.** A "practical philosophy" replacing the "speculative philosophy taught in the schools" can make us "lords and possessors of nature" — especially for the preservation of health (medicine). He explains why he suppressed *Le Monde* (fear of controversy after Galileo's 1633 condemnation), his qualified defense of publishing to enlist collaborators for experiments, the necessity of experiment as knowledge advances, his low regard for scholastic disputation and the followers of Aristotle, and his choice to write in French. → mechanistic **mastery of nature** program.

## Whole-Work Assessment

The *Discourse* is the vault's second Descartes primary source and the chronologically prior one: it shows the *Meditations*' metaphysics in embryo but adds three things the *Meditations* lack. First, the **explicit four-rule method** and the **provisional morality** — the latter one of the clearest bridges in the vault between rationalism and Stoicism (the third maxim restates the [[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)]] almost verbatim). Second, the **mechanistic-science program** ("lords and possessors of nature") that connects Descartes to Bacon's project and to the philosophy-of-science leg. Third, and most valuable to the vault's AI & Cognitive Science leg, the **two tests and the animal-machine**: Descartes's language test and universal-reason test are the direct ancestors of the [[Concepts/Imitation Game (Turing)]] — Turing's behavioral criterion answers, three centuries later, precisely the question Descartes raised of whether a machine's outputs could be distinguished from a rational soul's. The work sharpens rather than opens contradictions: it reinforces the standing [[Thinkers/Descartes]] ↔ [[Thinkers/Alan Turing]] tension over whether thought requires a non-mechanical *res cogitans*, and the Cartesian innatism vs. Lockean empiricism contradiction.
