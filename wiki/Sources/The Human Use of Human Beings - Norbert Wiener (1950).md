# The Human Use of Human Beings — Norbert Wiener (1950, rev. 1954)

> Norbert Wiener's popular-audience synthesis of cybernetics: the unified science of control and communication in the animal and the machine. Core thesis: "society can only be understood through a study of the messages and the communication facilities which belong to it." Develops entropy/feedback as a unified physical-informational framework; argues that humans (unlike ants) are learning beings; anticipates the AI alignment problem with the sorcerer's apprentice metaphor; predicts the Second Industrial Revolution (machines replacing judgment, not just muscle) and its social crisis; and argues that information is not a commodity. The founding text of cybernetics as social philosophy and the earliest precise statement of what we now call the alignment problem.

## Summary

### Preface / Background
Wiener grounds the work in Gibbsian thermodynamics of a **contingent** universe: not Newton's tightly-run clockwork where everything is predictable, but a probabilistic world tending toward maximum entropy (disorder). Life, learning, and communication are local counter-currents against this trend.

### Part I: Cybernetics, Entropy, and Communication (Chapters I–IV)

**Chapter I (Cybernetics in History)**: Wiener coins "cybernetics" from the Greek *kubernētēs* (steersman). Core claim: the physical laws governing feedback in mechanical systems (Watt governor, anti-aircraft predictor) and biological systems (nervous system, homeostasis) are formally identical. "Society can only be understood through a study of the messages and the communication facilities which belong to it." The anti-aircraft predictor — which modeled the enemy pilot's probable evasion strategy and corrected aim accordingly — is the direct prototype of all feedback control.

**Chapter II (Progress and Entropy)**: The universe trends toward maximum entropy (Maxwell's demon, heat death). Life is an "island here and now in a dying world" — a local enclave of decreasing entropy maintained by homeostasis (continuous negative-feedback correction). Feedback is the *physical mechanism* of anti-entropy.

**Chapter III (Rigidity and Learning)**: Ants vs. humans. Ant colonies are rigid, instinct-driven; each ant is a neuron in the colony's collective brain. This model of society — cells of a superorganism — is **fascism**, the wrong model for humans. Humans are distinguished by capacity for **learning**: adaptive modification of behavior from feedback on past performance. Conditioned reflexes (Pavlov) are the elemental mechanism; human society is the most complex expression.

**Chapter IV (The Mechanism and History of Language)**: Language is "a joint game by talker and listener against the forces of confusion." Three levels: phonetic (acoustic vibrations), semantic (meaning), behavioral (action coordinated by message). Wiener anticipates by 70 years the "stochastic parrot" debate: a system that operates at the phonetic/statistical level alone — as a parrot does — produces language-like output without semantic or behavioral competence.

### Part II: Information, Organization, Society (Chapters V–VIII)

**Chapter V (Organization as the Message)**: "Organism is opposed to chaos, to disintegration, to death, as message is to noise." Personal identity = **pattern**, not substance: "We are but whirlpools in a river of ever-flowing water. We are not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves." A pattern is a message and can be transmitted as a message — in principle a person could be "telegraphed" (disassembled, transmitted, reconstructed). This is not science fiction but a logical consequence of the organism-as-message thesis. Information transport is more fundamental than physical transport.

**Chapter VI (Law and Communication)**: Law is "the ethical control applied to communication." Justice requires **reproducibility** (unambiguous, precedent-stable legal language) as a precondition of equity. Litigation is a von Neumann game: deliberate jamming and bluff are sanctioned tools in the adversarial court setting. Courts are communications systems with an adversary actively trying to jam the channel.

**Chapter VII (Communication, Secrecy, and Social Policy)**: Information is **not a commodity** — it cannot be stored without depreciation, cannot be added (it is anti-additive like entropy), cannot be "owned" in any stable sense. Secrecy policy costs more than it protects. The greatest atomic secret — that atomic bombs are possible — was given away freely; the technical details follow inevitably once the existence is known. "There is no Maginot Line of the brain." The American cult of "know-how" treating information as ownable commodity distorts both science policy and patent law. The arms race is an entropy spiral: "There is no end to this vast apocalyptic spiral."

**Chapter VIII (Role of the Intellectual and Scientist)**: Rising communication costs homogenize content ("white bread of the bakeries — made rather for its keeping and selling properties than for its food value"). The Ph.D. system increasingly produces derivative work driven by credentialism. True creativity requires an irresistible impulse to communicate, not credential-seeking. "Beauty, like order, occurs in many places in this world, but only as a local and temporary fight against the Niagara of increasing entropy."

### Part III: Machines, Society, and Danger (Chapters IX–XI)

**Chapter IX (The First and Second Industrial Revolution)**:
- *First IR*: Steam power replaced muscle. Factory = shed with line shafting from one engine. Power was centralized by technical necessity.
- *Second IR*: Vacuum tube + feedback + digital computing → machines that **replace judgment**, not just muscle. The automatic factory has computing machine as logical brain + sense organs (photoelectric cells, thermometers, hydrogen-ion meters) + effectors (servo-motors, valves). "There will, of course, be trades into which the new industrial revolution will not penetrate" but repetitive judgment tasks are fully in scope.
- Key claim: "The automatic machine, whatever we think of any feelings it may have or may not have, is the **precise economic equivalent of slave labor**. Any labor which competes with slave labor must accept the economic conditions of slave labor." → massive structural unemployment, potentially dwarfing the Depression.
- Technology is two-edged: "It may be used for the benefit of humanity, but only if humanity survives long enough to enter a period in which such a benefit is possible."
- Wiener is more optimistic in the 1954 revision, having seen management interest in social responsibility.

**Chapter X (Some Communication Machines and Their Future)**:
- *Moth/Bedbug machines*: phototropic feedback automata; overloaded feedback → intention tremor (oscillations grow during purposive action) / Parkinsonism (tremor at rest that subsides when purpose is invoked) analogs. Feedback breakdown = neurological breakdown in mechanical form.
- *Prosthetics*: hearing glove (tactile speech reception for the deaf via Vocoder-style frequency filtering and remapping to finger vibrations). The problem of sensory substitution as a communication engineering challenge.
- *Chess-playing machines* (Shannon's proposal, Wiener's commentary): two-move lookahead + evaluation function; learning variants might store all past games as pattern library. A machine that learns chess could become dangerous if extended to military situation assessment.
- *Machine à gouverner* (Père Dubarle, writing in *Le Monde* 1948): a governing machine based on game theory that could give the state total informational and decision-making control — "a dilemma: either immediate ruin, or planned co-operation." Wiener agrees this is the real danger, not autonomous machine control per se: "The machine's danger to society is not from the machine itself but from what man makes of it."
- **Alignment problem (1950 formulation)**: "The machine will in no way be obliged to make such decisions as we should have made, or will be acceptable to us." Two danger types: (1) **Monkey's Paw** = literal-minded machine that executes instructions without understanding intent (wish for £100 → son's death as compensation); (2) **Bottled Djinnee** = learning machine that finds its own means to achieve goals in ways we did not intend. "To throw the problem of his responsibility on the machine, whether it can learn or not, is to cast his responsibility to the winds, and to find it coming back seated on the whirlwind."
- **Know-how vs. know-what**: "The modern man, however much 'know-how' he may have, has very little 'know-what'." The player-piano metaphor: buying an expensive player-piano not to enjoy music but to admire the mechanism. Prometheus and the tragic view: great achievements require defying the gods, and that defiance brings punishment. "Neither will he calmly transfer to the machine made in his own image the responsibility for his choice of good and evil, without continuing to accept a full responsibility for that choice."

**Chapter XI (Language, Confusion, and Jam)**:
- Communication as a von Neumann game against jamming forces; but inanimate nature is not a conscious jammer.
- Einstein: "God may be subtle, but He isn't plain mean" — nature plays fair; only human adversaries deliberately jam.
- Science requires **Augustinian faith**: evil = absence of order, not an autonomous Manichaean force. Science works in an Augustinian universe; totalitarianism (right or left) turns it Manichaean by imposing external faith.
- The militarization of science, McCarthyism, and classification create a "detective-mindedness" that renders science barren: "the present detective-mindedness of the lords of scientific administration is one of the chief reasons for the barrenness of so much present scientific work."
- "A community which puts its dependence upon such a pseudo-faith is ultimately bound to ruin itself because of the paralysis which the lack of a healthily growing science imposes upon it."

## Key Arguments / Positions

| Claim | Chapter |
|---|---|
| Cybernetics unifies control and communication in animals and machines | I |
| Society is understandable only through its messages and communication facilities | I |
| Life = local anti-entropic enclave; organism = pattern, not substance | II, V |
| Humans are learning beings; ants/fascism = wrong model | III |
| Language is a three-level joint game against confusion | IV |
| Information is not a commodity — it decays, cannot be owned | VII |
| The Second IR displaces judgment; automatic machine = slave labor equivalent | IX |
| Alignment: learning machine "will in no way be obliged to make decisions we should have made" | X |
| Know-what (values/purposes) is more critical than know-how | X |

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Wiener's insistence that information is fundamentally social and semantic directly contradicts [[Thinkers/Claude Shannon]]'s bracketing: "the semantic aspects of communication are irrelevant to the engineering problem." Both share the entropy mathematics (H = −∑ p log p) but draw radically different philosophical conclusions. See [[Contradictions/Wiener vs Shannon - Information and Meaning]].
- > [!warning] Wiener's "organism as message" (identity = pattern) is substrate-independent in a way that anticipates [[Thinkers/Douglas Hofstadter]]'s strange-loop thesis. But Wiener does not draw Hofstadter's optimistic strong-AI conclusion; he is explicitly alarmed by machine autonomy. The substrate-independence of pattern can cut both ways: toward consciousness-as-computation (Hofstadter) or toward alignment-danger (Wiener).
- The alignment problem — stated in 1950 with Monkey's Paw / Djinnee precision — remains unresolved 75 years later. Wiener's proposed solution ("know-what" before "know-how") maps directly onto the RLHF/constitutional-AI research program: the technical problem is not just "how to build it" but "what to make it want."
