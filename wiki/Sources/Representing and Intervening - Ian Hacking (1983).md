# Representing and Intervening - Ian Hacking (1983)

> Ian Hacking's 1983 book that reframes the realism debate: instead of arguing over whether theories truly represent the world ("representing"), focus on what experimenters actually *do* with postulated entities when they intervene in the world to manipulate other things ("intervening"). The emblematic claim: "If you can spray them, then they are real." Distinguishes **realism about entities** (the objects exist and have causal powers we can use) from **realism about theories** (theories aim at and sometimes approach the truth). Argues that experiment has a life of its own, often independent of high theory, and that the working experimenter is committed to a robust entity realism.

**Full title**: *Representing and Intervening: Introductory Topics in the Philosophy of Natural Science*, Ian Hacking (Cambridge University Press, 1983). Two parts: Part A "Representing" (survey and critique of theory-realism debates) and Part B "Intervening" (positive account of experimental practice). Written from Stanford intro course; acknowledges parallel work by Nancy Cartwright (*How the Laws of Physics Lie*).

## Summary

Hacking opens by separating rationality questions (largely set aside here) from realism questions. The post-Kuhn "crisis of rationality" (normal science, revolutions, incommensurability) had made theory-comparison and truth-aims look shaky; Hacking treats this as one driver of anti-realism about representations and turns instead to what experimenters actually manipulate.

**Part A (Representing)** surveys the field and finds the representation-level debates inconclusive:
- Realism about theories (aim at truth, sometimes succeed) vs. anti-realism (theories are instruments, useful fictions).
- Realism about entities (theoretical objects really exist) vs. anti (useful intellectual fictions).
- Positivism (Comte, Mach, van Fraassen): anti both; only observational propositions believable; suspicious of causes, explanations, theoretical entities.
- Pragmatism (Peirce's long-run community agreement; James/Dewey/Rorty more present comfort; Putnam's shifts toward internal realism).
- Incommensurability (Kuhn, Feyerabend): topic, dissociation, and especially meaning-incommensurability undermine literal comparison and translatability, feeding irrealism.
- Reference (Putnam's causal-historical account tries to secure stable reference across theory change, with mixed success on terms like "electron").
- Lakatos's methodology of scientific research programmes: presented as rationality story but functions as a surrogate for truth — objectivity without requiring correspondence or convergence on a single true theory (hard core protected by protective belt; progress vs. degeneration judged retrospectively).

A "Break" chapter offers an anthropological parable (cave-dwellers to Hertz) showing why representation/reality debates are perennial and inconclusive; humans are "homo depictor" — representers. Hence the pivot: stop trying to settle realism at the level of pictures and theories; look at intervention and use.

**Part B (Intervening)** develops the positive thesis through detailed historical and contemporary examples (optics, thermodynamics, solid-state, radio astronomy, microscopes, electron guns, etc.):
- No universal answer to "which comes first, theory or experiment?" — it varies by science and stage; many experiments are curiosity-driven or technology-driven.
- Observation is over-rated as a foundation and is a *skill*, not bare seeing or always theory-loaded (contra strong Hanson/Kuhn/Feyerabend readings). There are pre-theoretical observations; we "observe" via instruments whose theory we may not fully command; multiple independent physical systems (different microscopes) converging on the same image gives confidence.
- Experiments often *create* phenomena that did not stably exist in nature before (improve apparatus until the effect is regular and repeatable); "repeating" is usually refining.
- Measurement serves many roles beyond theory-testing (pure determination of constants; Kuhn's unexpected functional roles in normal science).
- Bacon's experimental natural history provides an early taxonomy and already saw that crucial experiments are rarely decisive in the way later methodologists hoped; auxiliary hypotheses cannot always be piled on indefinitely.
- The capstone: while the speculator, calculator, or model-builder can remain anti-realist about theories, "the experimenter must be a realist" about the entities used as tools and probes. We count as real what we can use to intervene to affect something else (or what affects us). "It is not thinking about the world but changing it that in the end must make us scientific realists."

Natural science since the seventeenth century is the interlocking of representing and intervening; philosophy of science had neglected the intervening half.

## Key Claims / Positions

**Two realisms (entities vs. theories)**: One can (and Hacking does) defend robust realism about the entities experimenters use while remaining cautious or anti-realist about the truth of overarching theories. "Realism about theories says they aim at the truth, and sometimes get close to it. Realism about entities says that the objects mentioned in theories should really exist."

**The spray argument (entity realism via use)**: In the Stanford experiment hunting fractional charges (quarks) on a superconducting niobium ball, experimenters "spray it with positrons to increase the charge or with electrons to decrease the charge." What convinced Hacking was not the quarks (still controversial, possibly measuring something else) but that electrons/positrons were stable, understood tools whose causal powers were reliably deployed to do something else. "So far as I'm concerned, if you can spray them then they are real." Long chains of theoretical assumptions are accepted in practice because the entities function as instruments.

**Experiment independent**: Detailed cases show experiment/technology can lead, lag, or run in parallel with theory; "happy meetings" occur but there is no single logic. Microscopes of radically different physical kinds (light properties, acoustic) produce agreeing images — we believe what we see because of the convergence, not because we fully trust one underlying theory.

**Creation of phenomena**: "Many experiments create phenomena that did not hitherto exist in a pure state in the universe." Talk of "repeating" misleads; one improves the apparatus and conditions until the phenomenon can be produced regularly and studied.

**Against strong theory-loading of all observation**: While granting much observation is skill- and expectation-influenced, Hacking documents cases of observation without (or prior to) the relevant theory, and the practical independence of instrumental observation.

**Engagements with vault sources**:
- Kuhn: incommensurability and "different worlds" threaten realism at the level of representation and theory comparison; Hacking treats this as a reason to change the question to intervention.
- Feyerabend: shares skepticism about universal method and "law-and-order" pictures; Hacking's detailed experimental histories supply concrete counterweight to "anything goes" without restoring a single method.
- Lakatos: research programmes offer a way to retain scientific objectivity and a form of realism without a correspondence theory of truth or cumulative approach to it (Hacking's Ch. 8 "A surrogate for truth").
- Positivism (Ayer, earlier Comte/Mach/van Fraassen): the anti-entity, anti-cause, anti-explanation stance is directly challenged by experimental practice.
- Pragmatism/Putnam: discussed as anti-realist options at the representation level.
- Parallel (not ingested): Nancy Cartwright — both downplay full theory truth while defending selected theoretical/phenomenological entities (different routes: she via models/laws that "lie"; he via what experimenters actually spray and trap).

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] The representation-level realism debates (theory truth, reference, incommensurability) remain inconclusive on Hacking's telling; his intervention criterion grounds entity realism without fully resolving them. See [[Concepts/Incommensurability (Kuhn)]] and [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]] (Hacking supplies an experimentalist complement rather than another voice in the pure rationality debate).
- Does successful intervention commit us only to the causal powers we use, or to the full theoretical description of the entity?
- How far does the "experimenter must be realist" thesis extend beyond physics (Hacking's main examples) into biology, social science, etc.?
- Relation to Cartwright: convergent but distinct anti-theory + pro-entity positions; no direct contradiction yet.
