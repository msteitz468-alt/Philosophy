# On the Universe (Pseudo-Aristotle, De Mundo)

> **Spurious**: not a genuine work of Aristotle. Traced by Wilhelm Capelle to the lost works of the Stoic Poseidonius (1st c. BC), addressed to an "Alexander" (probably not Alexander the Great) and likely composed 1st–2nd c. AD. Not to be confused with the genuine *On the Heavens*.

## Argument Spine
The *Universe* (*kosmos*) is a single, harmoniously ordered whole composed of five nested elements (earth, water, air, fire, ether), whose apparent conflict of contrary qualities (hot/cold, wet/dry) is in fact the very source of its permanence — as in a well-run city or a chorus, unity emerges from managed plurality, not from sameness. This ordered whole is sustained not by a hands-on, everywhere-active deity but by a single, remote, effortless divine cause whose power is transmitted through a graduated chain — like a Persian Great King ruling through satraps, a puppeteer through one string, or a chorus-leader through one signal — reaching from the heavens down to the sublunary world of change and decay.

## Whole-Work Assessment

This short apocryphal treatise is philosophically interesting precisely because it is *not* Aristotle: it presents a Stoic-flavored theology of an effortless, remote, chain-transmitting cosmic governor — closer to Stoic providence and to the later Neoplatonic emanation scheme than to Aristotle's own [[Concepts/The Unmoved Mover (Aristotle)]], which neither creates nor administers the world and knows no particulars. Filing it under the Aristotle hub with an explicit spurious-authorship flag preserves the historical record of how ancient and medieval readers actually received "Aristotelian" cosmology (this treatise circulated as genuine for centuries) while keeping the vault's Aristotle material accurate. Its unity-from-contraries argument and city/chorus/king analogies for divine governance are its most durable philosophical contributions, and its five-element cosmology (elevating ether above the sublunary four) matches material already in [[Sources/Aristotle - Metaphysics]] and [[Sources/The Enneads - Plotinus]].
