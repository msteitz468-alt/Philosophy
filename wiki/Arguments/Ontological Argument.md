# Ontological Argument

> An a priori argument for the existence of God that proceeds from the definition or concept of God to the necessity of His actual existence.

## Summary

The ontological argument is a classic metaphysical proof that attempts to demonstrate God's existence using pure reason, independent of empirical observation (unlike the cosmological or teleological arguments). First systematically formulated by [[Thinkers/Anselm of Canterbury|Anselm of Canterbury]] in 1078, and later independently formulated in a different style by [[Thinkers/Descartes|René Descartes]] in 1641, the argument argues that existence is a necessary property of the supreme concept of God. 

It has been one of the most debated arguments in the history of philosophy, drawing famous counter-arguments from Anselm's contemporary Gaunilo, and later from [[Thinkers/Kant|Immanuel Kant]] and [[Thinkers/Hume|David Hume]], who challenged its logical and semantic premises.

---

## Anselm's Formulation (Proslogion Chapters 2 & 3)

In the *Proslogion*, Anselm defines God as:
> **"That than which nothing greater can be conceived"** (*aliquid quo nihil maius cogitari possit*).

Anselm's proof operates on a contrast between two types of existence:
1. **Existence in the understanding** (*in intellectu*): The concept exists as a thought (e.g., a painter's conception of a painting before it is painted).
2. **Existence in reality** (*in re*): The object exists in the actual world.

### The Argument (Chapter 2)
1. Even the "Fool" (atheist) understands the concept of "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" when he hears it. Thus, it exists in his understanding.
2. It is greater to exist both in reality and in the understanding than to exist in the understanding alone.
3. If "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" existed *only* in the understanding, we could conceive of a being that also existed in reality, which would be greater.
4. But this would mean we could conceive of a being greater than "that than which nothing greater can be conceived"—a logical contradiction.
5. Therefore, "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" must exist in reality.

### Modal Necessity (Chapter 3)
Anselm extends the argument to show that God exists so truly that He cannot even be conceived not to exist. A being that cannot be conceived not to exist is greater than a being that can be conceived not to exist. Therefore, God possesses necessary existence (*necesse esse*), whereas all other things exist contingently.

## Descartes' Formulation (Fifth Meditation)

In the *Fifth Meditation*, Descartes approaches the proof through his system of clear and distinct ideas, treating existence as an inseparable property of the essence of a supremely perfect being.

1. Descartes finds in himself the idea of a supremely perfect being.
2. Existence is a perfection (lacking existence would be a defect).
3. Therefore, existence is inseparable from the essence of a supremely perfect being.
4. **Mathematical Analogy**: Just as the property of having its internal angles equal to two right angles is inseparable from the essence of a triangle, or a valley is inseparable from a mountain, existence is inseparable from the essence of God. One cannot clearly and distinctly conceive a God who lacks existence any more than a mountain without a valley.
5. Therefore, God exists.

Descartes replies to the objection that while he must think of a mountain with a valley, this doesn't make the mountain exist. He clarifies that his thought does not impose necessity on reality; rather, the necessity of the thing itself (God's existence) determines his thought. He is not free to think of a supremely perfect being without existence, unlike a winged horse where he can arbitrarily join separate concepts.

## Historical Critiques

The ontological argument has faced three classic objections:

### 1. Gaunilo's "Lost Island" Objection
Anselm's contemporary, the monk Gaunilo of Marmoutiers, argued that if Anselm's logic were sound, one could prove the existence of a perfect "Lost Island" that is superior to all other lands. Since it is greater for this island to exist in reality than merely in the mind, it must exist.
* **Anselm's Reply**: The argument cannot be applied to contingent, finite entities like islands. The concept of "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" uniquely involves necessary existence and has no upper limit of perfection, unlike an island which can always be conceived with more trees, rivers, or riches.

### 2. Kant's "Existence is Not a Real Predicate" Critique
In the *Critique of Pure Reason*, Immanuel Kant argued that **existence is not a real or determining predicate**. 
* A real predicate is a term that adds something to the description of a concept (e.g., "the coin is round, gold, and heavy").
* Saying "the coin exists" or "is" does not add any new properties to the concept of the coin; it merely posits the subject itself in the actual world with all its existing predicates. 
* A concept of 100 real thalers contains no more conceptual predicates than 100 possible thalers. Therefore, we cannot deduce existence from the mere definition of a concept.

### 3. Hume's Fork Critique
David Hume, in *An Enquiry concerning Human Understanding*, targets all a priori claims of existence:
* By [[Concepts/Hume's Fork (Relations of Ideas vs Matters of Fact)|Hume's Fork]], all propositions are either *relations of ideas* (a priori, logical, whose denial is a contradiction) or *matters of fact* (contingent, verifiable only by experience).
* "The non-existence of any being is as clear and distinct an idea as its existence."
* Because we can conceive the non-existence of God without logical contradiction, God's existence cannot be a relation of ideas demonstrated a priori.
