# Zombie Argument (Chalmers)
> A phenomenal zombie — physically and functionally identical to you, but with no experience, "all dark inside" — is conceptually coherent; therefore consciousness does not logically supervene on the physical, and reductive explanation must fail.

## Summary

*The Conscious Mind* ch. 3 marshals five converging arguments against the logical supervenience of consciousness:

1. **Zombies**: a physical duplicate without experience is conceivable — "There is nothing it is like to be a zombie." Mere conceptual coherence (not empirical possibility) defeats logical supervenience; the burden lies on those alleging a hidden contradiction. Bolstered by nonstandard realizations (Block's China-nation, silicon isomorphs).
2. **Inverted spectrum**: a replica with red/blue-inverted qualia is coherent; even the *character* of experience is unexplained.
3. **Epistemic asymmetry**: knowledge of consciousness derives only from the first-person case. There is a problem of other minds, but no problem of "other lives" or "other economies" — those supervene logically.
4. **Knowledge argument**: Jackson's Mary (with Nagel's bat) — complete physical knowledge does not yield knowledge of what it is like to see red.
5. **Absence of analysis**: any a priori entailment would need a functional analysis of consciousness, and all "fail miserably" — "One might as well define 'world peace' as 'a ham sandwich.'"

Result: Levine's **explanatory gap**. The vitalism analogy fails as a rejoinder: vitalist doubts concerned *functions* and dissolved when the functions were explained; the consciousness problem survives complete functional explanation. Surveyed reductive programs all explain awareness, not experience: Baars's global workspace, Dennett's models, Crick & Koch's 40-Hz binding, Edelman's re-entrant circuits, Penrose–Hameroff quantum microtubules ("from structure and dynamics, we can only get more structure and dynamics"), and evolutionary accounts ("evolution cannot distinguish between me and my zombie twin").

The argument's modal machinery (primary intensions, conceivability→possibility) and the a posteriori-necessity objections are handled in [[Concepts/Logical Supervenience and Reductive Explanation (Chalmers)]] and [[Concepts/Naturalistic Dualism (Chalmers)]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] [[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]] ([[Concepts/Qualia Disqualified (Dennett)]]) denies zombies are coherently conceivable — anyone who conceives one has failed to imagine the full functional story. See [[Contradictions/Dennett vs Nagel - Multiple Drafts vs Subjective Experience]].
- > [!warning] The argument's success saddles Chalmers with the [[Concepts/Paradox of Phenomenal Judgment (Chalmers)]]: his zombie twin writes the same book for the same functional reasons.
