# Occasionalism vs Natural Causation

> When fire meets cotton, does the fire genuinely *cause* the burning by its own nature, or is God the sole real cause, the fire being merely the "occasion"?

## The Tension

A dispute about whether causal power resides in created things at all. It is simultaneously theological (God's omnipotence vs. secondary causes) and epistemological (what licenses our belief in necessary connection between cause and effect).

## Position A — Occasionalism (al-Ghazali)

- In *The Incoherence of the Philosophers* (Seventeenth Discussion), al-Ghazali argues there is no necessary connection between what we call cause and effect; the "connection" is only habitual conjunction. God directly creates each event; the fire is merely the occasion on which God creates the burning — "as for fire, which is inanimate, it has no action."
- This protects divine omnipotence and the possibility of miracles, and denies the philosophers' claim that the world runs by autonomous natures.
- His primary text is now ingested ([[Sources/The Incoherence of the Philosophers - Al-Ghazali]]; held by [[Thinkers/Al-Ghazali]]) — no longer reconstructed only through Averroes' rebuttal. The text also shows al-Ghazali floating a *second*, conciliatory theory (things have real dispositions, but God can suspend them for miracles); Marmura and the sequel *Al-Iqtisad* identify strict occasionalism as the view he actually holds. Full treatment: [[Concepts/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation (Ghazali and Ibn Rushd)]].

## Position B — Natural (secondary) causation (Ibn Rushd / Averroes)

- In *The Incoherence of the Incoherence*, Ibn Rushd defends real causal powers in things: to deny that fire burns by its own nature is to deny knowledge itself, since to know a thing is to know its causes and proper effects.
- God works *through* a stable order of secondary causes; denying natures makes science and even definition impossible.
- Held in: [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]. See [[Sources/Incoherence of the Incoherence - Ibn Rushd]].

## A Third Medieval Voice — Maimonides (with Position B)

- In *Guide* I.71–76, Maimonides reconstructs and **rejects** the Kalam atomism and occasionalism that Ghazali defends, siding firmly with real natures and secondary causation. His objection is methodological: the theologians "first considered what must be the properties of the things which should yield proof for... a certain creed" — bending physics to dogma — whereas "our opinions must be adapted to the existing properties [of things]." See [[Concepts/Critique of the Kalam (Maimonides)]].
- This makes the medieval line-up concrete: **Ghazali alone** defends occasionalism; **Averroes, Maimonides, and (later) Aquinas** all defend real causal natures, each for his own reasons. Held in: [[Thinkers/Maimonides]]; source [[Sources/The Guide for the Perplexed - Maimonides]].

## A Later Twist — Hume

- Hume agrees with Ghazali *descriptively* — we never observe necessary connection, only constant conjunction ([[Concepts/Necessary Connexion and the Two Definitions of Cause (Hume)]]) — but draws a naturalist rather than theological conclusion: the necessity is a projection of [[Concepts/Custom and Belief (Hume)|custom]], not an act of God. This reframes the same datum yet again.

## Attempted Resolutions

- No reconciliation in the ingested material; the three readings (divine sole-causation, real secondary causes, psychological projection) remain rival interpretations of the *same* observation that effects do not display visible necessity.
