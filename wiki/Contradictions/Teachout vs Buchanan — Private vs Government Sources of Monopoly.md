# Teachout vs Buchanan — Private vs Government Sources of Monopoly
> Teachout and Buchanan both oppose monopoly concentration, but diagnose its source in opposed ways: Buchanan attributes it to government intervention and regulatory capture; Teachout attributes it to deregulation ideology and abandonment of antitrust enforcement.

## Position 1: Teachout — Monopoly is Produced by Deregulation

[[Thinkers/Zephyr Teachout]] argues that the wave of monopoly concentration dominating the 21st-century U.S. economy is a direct product of the *retreat* of government antitrust enforcement. The Chicago School revolution (Robert Bork, *The Antitrust Paradox*, 1978) changed the legal and intellectual framework, Reagan's DOJ stopped enforcing merger law, and judges appointed by Reagan gutted antitrust statutes. Without active government enforcement, market power naturally concentrates — strong companies buy competitors, create choke points, and use network effects and predatory pricing to achieve monopoly. The solution is *more* government: reinvigorated FTC and DOJ enforcement, new legislation, state AG investigations, structural breakups.

Implicit in Teachout's argument: markets left to themselves tend toward monopoly, not competition. Competition must be actively maintained by government; it is not the natural equilibrium.

## Position 2: Buchanan — Monopoly is Produced by Government (Regulatory Capture)

[[Thinkers/James M. Buchanan]]'s public choice theory ([[Concepts/Constitutional Political Economy (Buchanan-Tullock)]]) argues that government intervention in markets systematically tends to serve concentrated private interests. Regulation creates *rent-seeking* opportunities: firms that would face competition in free markets instead lobby for regulations, licensing requirements, patents, and subsidies that entrench their position. The regulatory agency, captured by the regulated industry (regulatory capture), becomes a tool of monopoly protection rather than a check on it. On this account, the FDA, FTC, and DOJ are *not* the solution — they are part of the problem, because concentrated industries will always have more incentive to influence them than diffuse publics have to resist.

## Where They Agree

Both Teachout and Buchanan are opposed to monopoly concentration, and both recognize that the consumer welfare standard is inadequate (though for different reasons). Both see concentrated power as threatening democratic self-governance. Both are suspicious of concentrated power — they disagree only on whether that power resides primarily in government or in private corporations.

## The Crux: Which Came First?

The historical argument is key. Teachout: antitrust was actively enforced from the 1890s to the 1970s, and markets were more competitive; deregulation under Reagan produced the current concentration. Buchanan: active antitrust enforcement *itself* was a form of intervention that inevitably got captured and manipulated. "Public utility" designations, for instance, can be used to entrench incumbents.

The empirical question: did the 1981 pivot *increase* monopoly (Teachout) or was the pre-1981 system itself producing regulatory-capture monopolies (Buchanan)?

## A Possible Synthesis (Not Endorsed by Either)

Teachout's focus is on the *structural* anti-monopoly tools (divestiture, merger blocking) that directly reduce concentration without creating ongoing regulatory relationships. If the solution is "break up Amazon into five companies" rather than "have the FTC regulate Amazon's pricing," the regulatory capture problem is less severe — there is no ongoing regulatory relationship to capture. Buchanan's concern applies more to behavioral regulation than to structural remedies. Neither thinker develops this synthesis explicitly.
