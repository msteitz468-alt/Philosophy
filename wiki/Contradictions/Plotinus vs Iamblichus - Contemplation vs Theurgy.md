# Plotinus vs Iamblichus: Contemplation vs Theurgy

> Can the soul return to the divine by philosophy alone, or does salvation require ritual action and divine grace? The defining internal dispute of Neoplatonism.

## Position 1 — Plotinus (and, qualified, Porphyry): contemplation suffices

Because a level of every soul never descends from the Intelligible world ([[Concepts/The Undescended Soul (Plotinus vs Iamblichus)]]), the return is an awakening achieved through virtue, dialectic, and contemplation; Porphyry reports Plotinus attained union four times (*V. Pl.* 23). Magic and ritual operate only through cosmic sympathy, touching body and lower soul; the philosopher transcends them. [[Thinkers/Porphyry]] (*Letter to Anebo*, *De Regressu Animae*) allows theurgy at most a purification of the lower soul's pneumatic vehicle — it "cannot affect the higher souls of the gods" and is unnecessary for the philosopher. Held by: [[Thinkers/Plotinus]], [[Thinkers/Porphyry]].

## Position 2 — Iamblichus (and the Athenian School): theurgy and grace are required

The soul descends wholly; no reserved contemplative summit remains. Hence thought cannot unite us with the gods: "the vital union depends not on this, but on performance of the appropriate ritual actions" (*De Mysteriis* II.11). Union comes by the gods' voluntary bestowal of power — grace mediated through the cosmic correspondences that ground ritual ([[Concepts/Theurgy (Iamblichus)]]). [[Thinkers/Proclus]] adds the theurgic virtues faith–truth–love and providential descending *erōs*. Held by: [[Thinkers/Iamblichus]], the Athenian School.

## Stakes and Echoes

Wallis reads the whole later school as working out problems Plotinus left open; this one decides whether Neoplatonism is ultimately a philosophy or a religion. The debate prefigures Christian disputes over nature vs. grace and works vs. faith — [[Thinkers/Augustine]]'s insistence (against the "Platonists" of *Confessions* VII) that the ascent fails without the Mediator and grace is structurally Iamblichus's side of the argument, arrived at independently; Pseudo-Dionysius's sacramentalism carries the theurgic pattern into Christianity. Wallis's closing assessment sees in the Plotinus–Iamblichus opposition a still-live model of the reason-vs-spirituality dilemma.
