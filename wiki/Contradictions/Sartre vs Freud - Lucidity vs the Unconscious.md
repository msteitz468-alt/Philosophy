# Sartre vs Freud — Lucidity vs the Unconscious
> Both seek to understand the human from subjectivity and hold that the human gives itself its own form — yet they split irreconcilably over whether the mind harbours an *unconscious* or is wholly *lucid*.

## The Tension

Sartre's lifelong need to distance himself from Freud, Welten notes, betrays how much the two share: both understand the human from *subjectivity* (against objectifying psychology), and both hold that the human *makes itself* (Sartre's "project"/"original choice" answers Freud's developmental self-formation). The break is over a single thesis.

### Position A — Freud: the unconscious
The mind is split into conscious and unconscious. Neuroses trace back to *repressed* childhood memories; the lucid mind must be understood *from* the unconscious and from libidinal drives. Therapy is possible because the unconscious, though withdrawn from awareness, is accessible. Sexuality is grounded in a largely unconscious *libido*.

### Position B — Sartre: total lucidity
Consciousness is *through and through lucid*; "a consciousness that is unconscious is, for Sartre, like water that is not wet" (see [[Concepts/Consciousness as Intentionality and Néantisation (Sartre)]]). Freud's split lets the subject "hide behind itself" — "I did it unconsciously" is for Sartre just a form of acting **against better knowledge**. What Freud calls repression, Sartre re-describes as [[Concepts/Bad Faith (Mauvaise Foi) (Sartre)|bad faith]]: I can only lie to myself about what I already clearly know. Sartre accepts *nothing* as a base principle of consciousness (no libido, no unconscious) — precisely so that consciousness remains *free* and contingent. His "**existential psychoanalysis**" is therefore an analysis of *consciousness*, recovering a free **original choice**, not of a hidden past (see [[Concepts/Existential Psychoanalysis and the Original Choice (Sartre)]]). Where Freud sends the patient back to the past, Sartre stresses present and future: childhood is never a *cause*, and the child forms itself *dialectically*, in the negation of what others made of it. Sexuality, likewise, is intentional incarnation, not libido (see [[Concepts/The Look and the Other (Sartre)]]).

## Why It Matters
The whole edifice of Sartrean freedom depends on denying the unconscious: if there were a domain inaccessible to lucid choice, responsibility would no longer be total, and bad faith would collapse back into ordinary repression. Conversely, Freud's clinical practice depends on the unconscious being real. The disagreement is thus not a detail but a fork in twentieth-century theories of mind, self-knowledge, and responsibility.
