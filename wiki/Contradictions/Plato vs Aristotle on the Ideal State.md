# Plato vs Aristotle on the Ideal State

> Should the ideal city maximize unity — even to the point of abolishing private property, marriage, and family among its rulers — or is a state by nature and by right a self-sufficient plurality that maximal unity would destroy?

## The Tension

Both thinkers agree the polis exists for the good life, not mere survival, and both design an "ideal" regime to secure it — but they disagree fundamentally about what makes a *city*, as opposed to a household or an individual, one thing.

## Position A — Plato: unity through communism among the guardians

- The *Republic* holds that "the greater the unity of the state the better," and secures unity among the ruling guardian class by abolishing private property, private marriage, and private child-rearing — everyone in that class says "mine" and "not mine" of the same things simultaneously, binding the guardians into a single extended family.
- Guardian happiness is treated as subordinate to the happiness of the city as a whole, which is engineered by philosopher-rulers who possess knowledge of the Form of the Good.
- Held in: [[Thinkers/Plato]]. See [[Sources/The Republic - Plato (Cambridge, 2000)]].

## Position B — Aristotle: the state is essentially plural

- Aristotle argues excess unity is not the state's perfection but its destruction: pushed far enough, a state "becomes a family, and from being a family, an individual" and thereby ceases to be a state at all — "we ought not to attain this greatest unity even if we could."
- Self-sufficiency, which Plato and Aristotle both prize, actually favors *less* unity: a family is more self-sufficing than an individual, a city more than a family.
- "It is clearly better that property should be private, but the use of it common" — private ownership with habituated common use preserves the virtues of liberality and temperance that full communism abolishes, and avoids the free-rider/neglect problems of joint ownership ("what is common to the greatest number has the least care bestowed upon it").
- A whole cannot be happy unless its parts are — so Plato cannot coherently deny individual happiness to the guardians while claiming the city as a whole is happy.
- Held in: [[Thinkers/Aristotle]]. See [[Sources/Aristotle - Politics]] (Book II) and [[Concepts/Critique of Plato's Republic (Aristotle)]].

## Why It Matters / Downstream Effects

- This is the ancient root of the private-property-vs-communism debate that recurs across the vault's political-philosophy material; see the three-way tension with [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]], who (unlike either ancient thinker) locates the problem in private property as such rather than in its absence or its proper limitation.
- Parallels, but is distinct from, the metaphysical [[Contradictions/Plato vs Aristotle on the Forms]] — here the dispute is about the ontology of political wholes, not universals, though both trace to the same underlying disagreement about whether unity or determinate individual form is the higher principle of being.

## Attempted Resolutions

- None within the ingested classical material; Aristotle's critique is a direct, un-reconciled rebuttal rather than a synthesis. Logged as an open structural tension.
