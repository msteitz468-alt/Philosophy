# Free Will — Spinozan Necessity vs Kantian Autonomy

> Is the will an illusion produced by ignorance of the causes that determine us, or is the rational will autonomous — a genuine first cause that legislates the moral law to itself?

## The Tension

Two rationalists of the modern period reach opposite verdicts on freedom. For Spinoza freedom is *understood necessity*; for Kant freedom is *autonomous self-legislation* presupposed by morality. The dispute decides whether moral responsibility requires a will that could have done otherwise.

## Position A — Spinoza: necessitarianism, no free will

- Everything follows with geometrical necessity from the nature of the one substance, [[Concepts/Deus sive Natura - Substance Monism (Spinoza)|Deus sive Natura]]. There is no contingency and no free will in the libertarian sense.
- The feeling of free will is ignorance of the causes that determine our [[Concepts/Conatus (Spinoza)|conatus]] and [[Concepts/Affects and Active vs Passive Emotions (Spinoza)|affects]].
- "Freedom" is redefined: to be free is to act from the necessity of one's *own* nature, i.e., through adequate ideas — the [[Concepts/Freedom and Blessedness - Intellectual Love of God (Spinoza)|intellectual love of God]].
- Held in: [[Thinkers/Spinoza]]. See [[Sources/Ethics - Spinoza (Cambridge, Kisner ed. 2018)]].

## Position B — Kant: autonomy of the will

- Morality is only possible if the will is free; freedom is the *ratio essendi* of the moral law. The rational will is a genuine cause, not merely a link in nature's chain.
- Freedom = [[Concepts/Autonomy and Heteronomy of the Will (Kant)|autonomy]]: the will giving the moral law to itself ([[Concepts/Categorical Imperative - Universal Law Formulation (Kant)|categorical imperative]]), as opposed to being determined by inclination (heteronomy).
- Reconciled with natural causation only by the [[Concepts/Two Standpoints: Sensible World and Intelligible World (Kant)|two standpoints]]: as appearance I am determined, as a member of the intelligible world I am free.
- Held in: [[Thinkers/Kant]]. See [[Sources/Metaphysics of Morals - Immanuel Kant]].

## Lineage

- Spinoza's necessitarianism is the modern peak of the determinist line traced from [[Contradictions/Stoic Determinism vs Aristotelian Contingency|Stoic fate]]; Kant's autonomy is the modern defense of the contingency/responsibility line.

## Attempted Resolutions

- Kant's two-standpoints doctrine is itself an *attempt* to hold necessity (in nature) and freedom (in the intelligible self) together, but it does not satisfy the Spinozist, who admits no second standpoint. Compatibilism ([[Concepts/Compatibilism - Liberty and Necessity (Hume)]]) offers a different dissolution. Left open.
