# Subjective Idealism vs. Physical Realism

> Is the physical world composed of mind-independent material substance, or is it composed entirely of ideas within perceiving minds?

## The Tension

This is a fundamental ontological and epistemological split in early modern philosophy:
* **Physical Realism** (held by [[Thinkers/Descartes|Descartes]] and [[Thinkers/Locke|Locke]]) asserts that physical objects exist as mind-independent material substances. While we only perceive representation (ideas) of these objects in our minds, the objects themselves have an independent, actual existence in space and time.
* **Subjective Idealism** (immaterialism, held by [[Thinkers/George Berkeley|George Berkeley]]) asserts that the belief in mind-independent matter is incoherent and leads to skepticism. Instead, the existence of sensible objects consists entirely in their being perceived (*esse est percipi*); they are collections of ideas in minds.

---

## The Positions

### 1. Physical Realism (Descartes & Locke)
* **Descartes**: Argues in the *Sixth Meditation* that bodies exist as *res extensa* (extended substance), which is really distinct from *res cogitans* (thinking substance). Because God is not a deceiver, and has given us a strong natural propensity to believe our sensory ideas are caused by physical bodies, these bodies must actually exist.
* **Locke**: Argues that while we do not perceive physical substances directly, their existence is the necessary cause of our sensory ideas. He distinguishes between *primary qualities* (real properties inherent in the objects: shape, extension, solidity) and *secondary qualities* (effects produced in our minds: color, sound, taste).

### 2. Subjective Idealism / Immaterialism (Berkeley)
* **Berkeley**: Argues in the *Three Dialogues* that Locke's primary/secondary quality distinction is unsustainable, as primary qualities cannot be conceived or perceived without secondary ones. 
* He argues that because we only ever perceive ideas, we can have no evidence of anything existing beyond ideas. 
* To assert that a mind-independent substance "supports" these ideas is meaningless. The table, tree, and cherry exist, but their existence is identical to their being perceived (*esse est percipi*). The stability of the world is guaranteed not by dead matter, but by the constant perception of **God**.

---

## Implications for the Vault

This debate represents the first major challenge to the scientific assumption of a mind-independent physical universe:
* **The Epistemological Gap**: Realism creates a gap between the idea in the mind and the object in the world, opening the door to skepticism (how do we know our ideas resemble the objects?). Berkeley claims that by removing mind-independent matter, he closes this gap and destroys skepticism, since the ideas *are* the objects.
* **Simulations and Representation**: In modern cognitive science and AI, this tension re-emerges in the debate between representational theories of mind (the mind constructs a model of an external world) and radical enactivism or phenomenalism (where reality is defined entirely in terms of sensory-motor streams, akin to a virtual reality interface).
