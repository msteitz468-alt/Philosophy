# Rousseau vs. Hobbes on the State of Nature

> Is the natural state of humanity prior to government a state of peaceful compassion and freedom, or a state of constant fear and brutal war?

## The Tension

This contradiction represents the classic division in Western political philosophy regarding human nature and the purpose of the state:
* **Thomas Hobbes** argues that the state of nature is a horrific, chaotic war of all against all. Human nature is fundamentally selfish, competitive, and fearful, necessitating absolute government power to secure peace.
* **Jean-Jacques Rousseau** argues that the state of nature is a peaceful, idyllic era of solitary freedom and natural pity. Humans are naturally good, and it is civil society, property, and socialization that corrupt them and create conflict.

---

## The Positions

### 1. The Hobbesian View: War of All Against All
In *Leviathan* (1651), [[Thinkers/Thomas Hobbes|Thomas Hobbes]] outlines a mechanistic, self-interested view of human nature.
* **The Natural State**: Without a common power to keep them in awe, humans exist in a constant state of war driven by three causes: competition (for gain), diffidence (for safety), and glory (for reputation).
* **Life's Quality**: In this state, there is no industry, culture, navigation, or society, and human life is:
  > **"solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."**
* **The Remedy**: The absolute sovereign. Individuals must alienate their rights to a sovereign who will enforce contract and order through fear. See [[Concepts/State of Nature (Hobbes)]] and [[Concepts/Social Contract (Hobbes)]].

### 2. The Rousseauian View: The Noble Savage
In *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality* (1755) and *The Social Contract* (1762), [[Thinkers/Jean-Jacques Rousseau|Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] presents a direct critique of Hobbes.
* **The Natural State**: Rousseau argues that Hobbes made the mistake of projecting the vices of socialized, civilized humans back into the state of nature. Left to their natural state, humans are simple, solitary, and peaceful.
* **Natural Sentiment**: Humans are guided by self-preservation (*amour de soi*) and natural pity (*pitié*), which prevents them from harming others.
* **The Source of Conflict**: Civilization, cooperation, and the introduction of **private property** created comparison, vanity (*amour-propre*), inequality, and warfare.
* **The Remedy**: A democratic covenant. Rather than submitting to an absolute ruler to escape nature, citizens must form a society where they maintain their freedom through the **General Will**. See [[Concepts/State of Nature (Rousseau)]] and [[Concepts/General Will (Rousseau)]].

---

## Implications for the Vault

This division shapes subsequent theories of politics and ethics:
* **Human Nature**: Optimistic (Rousseau: humans are naturally good, corrupted by systems) vs. Pessimistic (Hobbes: humans are naturally dangerous, restrained by systems).
* **The Scope of State Power**: Hobbesian realism justifies state absolutism and police power as safeguards against chaos, while Rousseauian idealism demands popular sovereignty and directly democratic consent to justify any limits on natural freedom.
