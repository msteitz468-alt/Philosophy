# Rationalism vs Empiricism on Innate Ideas

> Does the mind come furnished with ideas/principles prior to experience, or is it a blank slate on which experience writes everything?

## The Tension

The defining epistemological dispute of early modern philosophy: the *source* of our concepts and of certain knowledge. It governs what counts as justification and how far reason can reach independently of the senses.

## Position A — Rationalism: innate ideas exist (Descartes)

- Some ideas are innate — not derived from sense experience: the idea of God, of the self as thinking thing, mathematical and logical truths grasped by the "natural light."
- Certainty is secured by [[Concepts/Clear and Distinct Perception (Descartes)]], not by the senses (which the [[Concepts/Method of Hyperbolic Doubt (Descartes)]] shows to be doubtable).
- The idea of an infinite, perfect God could not come from a finite mind or finite experience, so it must be innately implanted (see [[Concepts/Causal Argument for God's Existence from Objective Reality (Descartes)]]).
- Held in: [[Thinkers/Descartes]]. See [[Sources/Meditations on First Philosophy - Descartes (Oxford, 2008)]].

## Position B — Empiricism: no innate ideas (Locke, Hume)

- Locke's Book I is a sustained assault on innatism: there are [[Concepts/No Innate Principles or Ideas (Locke)|no innate principles or ideas]]. The mind is a [[Concepts/Tabula Rasa - Blank Slate or Empty Cabinet (Locke)|tabula rasa]].
- All ideas come from [[Concepts/Sensation and Reflection as the Two Fountains of Ideas (Locke)|sensation and reflection]]; "nothing in the intellect not first in the senses."
- Hume sharpens this with the [[Concepts/Impressions and Ideas - Copy Principle (Hume)|copy principle]]: every simple idea is traced to a prior impression; ideas with no such pedigree are suspect.
- Held in: [[Thinkers/Locke]], [[Thinkers/Hume]]. See [[Sources/An Essay Concerning Human Understanding - Locke]] and [[Sources/An Enquiry concerning Human Understanding - Hume (Oxford, Millican ed.)]].

## Why It Matters

- Determines whether metaphysics (God, soul, substance) can be known a priori (rationalist) or is curtailed to the bounds of experience (empiricist) — feeding directly into [[Concepts/Hume's Fork (Relations of Ideas vs Matters of Fact)]] and the [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]].

## Attempted Resolutions

- Kant's later "Copernican" turn (forms of intuition + categories supplied by the mind, content supplied by the senses) is the historical synthesis, but the ingested Kant material ([[Sources/Metaphysics of Morals - Immanuel Kant]]) is practical philosophy and does not itself adjudicate this theoretical dispute. Left open here.
