# Temporal Creation vs Eternal World

> Did the world have an absolute beginning in time, brought into being by the free choice of God — or is the ordered cosmos eternal, with no first moment, God being its eternal cause rather than its temporal originator?

## The Tension

This is the central battle of the *Tahafut* debate and the longest discussion in both books. It is at once cosmological (does time have a first moment?), theological (does God act by voluntary choice or by the necessity of His essence?), and logical (can a temporal effect proceed from an eternal cause without a *new* determinant?). The vault tracks the causal sub-dispute separately in [[Contradictions/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation]]; this page isolates the eternity/creation axis.

## Position A — Temporal Creation by an Eternal Will (al-Ghazali)

- The world was created in time by an **eternal will** that decreed its existence at the very moment it began; nonexistence "prior to this was not willed." See [[Concepts/Eternal Will and Temporal Creation (Ghazali)]].
- Will just *is* "an attribute whose function — nay, its essence — is to differentiate a thing from its similar," so it can specify one of two indiscernible moments without any further determinant (*murajjih*). The demand "why not sooner?" falsely models God on human deliberation.
- "Duration and time are both created," so there is no empty time in which God "waited." The intuition that time must precede the world is an estimative illusion, like imagining space beyond the cosmos.
- The philosophers have not *demonstrated* eternity (only made it seem plausible), so by the [[Concepts/Criterion of Demonstrability (Ghazali)|demonstrability criterion]] the Qur'anic doctrine of a created world stands. Al-Ghazali condemns the eternity thesis as outright unbelief (*kufr*).
- Held in: [[Thinkers/Al-Ghazali]]. Source: [[Sources/The Incoherence of the Philosophers - Al-Ghazali]].

## Position B — The Eternal World (Avicenna / Aristotle, defended by Ibn Rushd)

- An eternal, unchanging cause has an eternal effect; for the world to *begin* at a moment would require a new determination arising in the changeless Eternal — impossible. Time and motion have no first instant.
- "Eternal will" is an **equivocation**: it keeps the word "will" while stripping out deliberation, change, and choice between live contraries. An undetermined will that arbitrarily fixes a moment for no reason makes God neither wise nor good.
- God's priority to the world is *causal and simultaneous*, not temporal. See [[Concepts/Eternity of the World (Ibn Rushd)]].
- Held in: [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]. Source: [[Sources/Incoherence of the Incoherence - Ibn Rushd]].

## Position C — Eternity Undemonstrated, Creation on Authority + Design (Maimonides)

- A distinct middle worked out in *Guide* II.13–25. Maimonides holds that **neither** eternity **nor** creation is rationally demonstrable — and argues Aristotle himself never claimed to *prove* eternity ("his arguments and proofs were only apparent and plausible"). See [[Concepts/Creation vs Eternity (Maimonides)]].
- Because reason reaches a standoff, he accepts creation *ex nihilo* on **prophetic authority** and supports it with a **design argument** (the irregular motions and placements of the spheres bespeak a determining Will, not "fixed laws of Nature").
- Differs from **both** Ghazali and Averroes: unlike [[Thinkers/Al-Ghazali]], he does *not* claim to disprove eternity or condemn it as unbelief — only to show it unproven; unlike [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]], he refuses Aristotelian eternity despite accepting most of Aristotle's physics. He also pointedly rejects the **Kalam's** attempt to *prove* creation dialectically ([[Concepts/Critique of the Kalam (Maimonides)]]): "a proposition proved by a dialectical argument will... weaken my faith in it."
- Held in: [[Thinkers/Maimonides]]. Source: [[Sources/The Guide for the Perplexed - Maimonides]].

## Adjacent Positions in the Vault

- **[[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]:** a deliberate middle, essentially adopting Maimonides' epistemic verdict. The world's temporal beginning is true but known by *faith*, not demonstrable by reason (he grants the eternity-proofs are inconclusive — agreeing with al-Ghazali's negative point). His formula "God willed from eternity that the world should be, but not that it should be from eternity" mirrors the eternal-will doctrine while declining the takfir.
- **[[Thinkers/Augustine]]:** reaches "time is a creature" by interior phenomenology — [[Concepts/Time and Eternity - Distentio Animi (Augustine)]] — dissolving the "before creation" question Augustine famously refuses to answer with "He was preparing hell for those who pry."
- **Neoplatonism:** eternal [[Concepts/Emanation (Proodos)|emanation]] from the One — eternal procession without temporal beginning, the very scheme the *falasifa* overlay on Aristotle and al-Ghazali attacks.
- **Kant (First Antinomy):** the mature form of the impasse — reason can construct *equally cogent* proofs that the world does and does not have a beginning in time, suggesting the question outruns possible experience. This partly vindicates al-Ghazali's negative thesis (eternity undemonstrated) while denying his positive one equal rank.

## Attempted Resolutions

No reconciliation in the ingested material. The dispute turns on a prior question neither side concedes: **whether "will" can intelligibly select between indiscernibles without a reason.** Grant it (al-Ghazali) and temporal creation is coherent; deny it (the Principle of Sufficient Reason, Averroes' instinct) and eternity follows. Aquinas and Kant agree the eternity side is *not demonstrable* — the most stable common ground the vault records.
