# Critique of the Kalam (Maimonides)

> In *Guide* I.71–76, Maimonides reconstructs the method and the twelve premises of the Mutakallimun (the practitioners of Islamic *kalam*, both Ash'arite and Mu'tazilite) and rejects them root and branch. His master objection is methodological: the theologians "first considered what must be the properties of the things which should yield proof for... a certain creed; and when this was found they asserted that the thing must be endowed with those properties." Physics is bent to fit dogma. This is the precise inverse of [[Thinkers/Al-Ghazali]], who *embraced* the occasionalist atomism Maimonides demolishes.

## The Twelve Premises of the Kalam

Maimonides catalogues the Mutakallimun's foundations — most importantly:
- **Atomism**: all bodies are composed of indivisible, magnitudeless atoms, perfectly alike; with a real **vacuum** to let them combine, and **time** likewise composed of indivisible instants.
- **Continuous re-creation (occasionalism)**: accidents cannot endure two moments; God re-creates every accident at every instant. So "the dyer does not dye the cloth — God creates the color anew on contact"; the hand does not move the pen — God creates four unconnected accidents (will, power, hand-motion, pen-motion). "It must never be said that one thing is the cause of another."
- **Admissibility**: whatever the imagination can picture, the intellect must admit as possible; nature has no fixed necessity, only **"habit."**
- Denial of the infinite in every form; distrust of the senses as yielding no proof.

This is recognizably the same atomist-occasionalist edifice that underwrites [[Concepts/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation (Ghazali and Ibn Rushd)|Ghazali's denial of necessary causal connection]] — fire does not burn; God creates the burning. Maimonides treats it as the very thing a philosopher must clear away.

## Maimonides' Rejection

He rejects it decisively, on three grounds:

1. **It is bad physics.** Atomism "would be tantamount to the rejection of all that has been proved in Geometry"; the time-atom theory is so weak that "the refutation of such propositions is a mere waste of time." Their occasionalism he calls "nothing but mockery" — "Will you mock at Him, as you mock at man?"
2. **It inverts the order of inquiry.** Against the Kalam's procedure he sets Themistius' maxim: "the properties of things cannot adapt themselves to our opinions, but our opinions must be adapted to the existing properties." Their "sole object is to fashion the Universe according to their peculiar opinions and beliefs."
3. **It proves God the wrong way.** The Mutakallimun try to prove creation *first* and infer God from it — "most objectionable... because all the proofs for the creation have weak points." Maimonides' counter-method (developed in [[Concepts/Creation vs Eternity (Maimonides)|Part II]]) is to prove God's existence, unity, and incorporeality on grounds valid **even if the universe were eternal** — not because he believes in eternity, but to make the demonstration independent of the disputed creation question. Nature, for him, has real and stable forces and forms.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. [[Thinkers/Al-Ghazali]] (the sharpest contrast in the vault):** Ghazali is the great *defender* of exactly the occasionalism Maimonides scraps. Where Ghazali makes the absence of necessary natures the guarantor of miracles and divine omnipotence, Maimonides regards real natures as non-negotiable for science *and* finds the Kalam's defense of religion self-defeating — "the harm... by those who defend [religion] in a way not proper to it" is a shared worry, but they draw opposite conclusions. On the causation axis Maimonides stands with [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]] and Aristotle against Ghazali — see [[Contradictions/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation]].
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]:** allies against occasionalism, though Maimonides is more willing than Averroes to break with Aristotle on the world's eternity. Both insist denying real natures destroys knowledge.
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]:** Aquinas inherits Maimonides' commitment to real secondary causes and stable natures (against occasionalism) while building his own natural theology; he read the *Guide* (cited as "Rabbi Moses").
- **A modern echo:** the "admissibility" premise — whatever is imaginable is possible, so nature is mere habit — is the structural ancestor of the very view [[Thinkers/Hume]] would later naturalize as [[Concepts/Custom and Belief (Hume)|custom]]. Maimonides, like [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd|Averroes]], rejects the inference that regularity is "only habit."

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Maimonides rejects the Kalam's *proof* of creation yet himself affirms creation ex nihilo — on what non-Kalam basis? (His answer: scripture plus the non-demonstrability of eternity — [[Concepts/Creation vs Eternity (Maimonides)]].)
- If nature has real fixed forces, how are miracles possible? (Maimonides minimizes and naturalizes miracles, where Ghazali maximizes them.)
