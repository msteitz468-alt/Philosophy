# Subjective Idealism (Berkeley)

> The metaphysical doctrine that mind-independent physical matter does not exist, and that reality is composed entirely of perceiving minds and the ideas they perceive.

## Summary

Subjective Idealism, which [[Thinkers/George Berkeley|George Berkeley]] termed **immaterialism**, is the philosophical position that physical objects are not mind-independent substances but collections of sensible qualities (like color, shape, texture, and size) that exist only in the minds of perceivers. The core principle of this philosophy is:
> **"Esse est percipi"** (To be is to be perceived).

To resolve the obvious objection that things seem to exist when no human is perceiving them (e.g., a tree in an empty forest), Berkeley argues that God, as an infinite, omnipresent mind, constantly perceives all of creation. Thus, the physical universe remains stable and continuous because it is permanently sustained in the divine intellect.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. The Rejection of Matter
Berkeley challenges the distinction made by [[Thinkers/Locke|John Locke]] between **primary qualities** (inherent in objects: extension, shape, motion) and **secondary qualities** (mind-dependent: color, taste, sound). Berkeley argues that primary qualities cannot be conceived or perceived separately from secondary qualities (e.g., one cannot imagine a shape that has no color or texture). Since secondary qualities are universally admitted to exist only in the mind, the primary qualities must also exist only in the mind. Therefore, the concept of a mind-independent physical "substance" or "matter" is a useless, incoherent abstraction.

### 2. Esse est Percipi ("To be is to be perceived")
If material substance is rejected, the existence of sensible things is identical to their being perceived. Berkeley writes:
> *"The table I write on, I say, exists, that is, I see and feel it; and if I were out of my study I should say it existed, meaning thereby that if I was in my study I might perceive it, or that some other spirit actually does perceive it."*

For Berkeley, the universe consists of two and only two things:
1. **Spirits (Minds)**: Active, simple, indivisible substances that perceive, think, and will.
2. **Ideas**: Passive entities perceived by spirits (sensation, memory, imagination).

### 3. The Divine Guarantee of the External World
If objects are only ideas, why can we not control them like imaginary concepts, and why does the world have such stable, predictable laws?
* Berkeley explains that the ideas of sensation are not created by our own minds; they are printed on our senses by a powerful, external spirit—namely, **God**.
* The **laws of nature** are the regular, predictable sequences in which God displays these ideas to human minds (e.g., the idea of fire is followed by the idea of heat).
* The persistence of the external world when unperceived by humans is guaranteed because God's mind constantly perceives all things, keeping them in existence.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

> [!WARNING]
> Subjective Idealism stands in direct, unresolved tension with the physical realism of [[Thinkers/Descartes|Descartes]] and [[Thinkers/Locke|Locke]] (who assert that physical bodies exist as mind-independent substances). See [[Contradictions/Subjective Idealism vs. Physical Realism|Subjective Idealism vs. Physical Realism]].

### Major Critiques
1. **The Solipsism Trap**: If we only perceive our own ideas, how can we be sure other minds exist? Berkeley replies that we do not have an *idea* of other spirits (since spirits are active, not passive ideas), but we have a *notion* of them, inferred from the coordinated ideas of sensation that reflect other rational actors.
2. **The Problem of God's Sufferings**: If God perceives everything, does God perceive pain, and does God suffer? Berkeley asserts that God knows and perceives pain intellectually but does not feel it passively as humans do, since God is not subject to external passions.
