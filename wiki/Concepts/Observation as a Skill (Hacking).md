# Observation as a Skill (Hacking)

> Observation is not primarily a matter of language or bare sensory given; it is a *skill*. Some observations are pre-theoretical or minimally theoretical. We often "observe" using information transmitted by theoretically postulated instruments whose detailed theory we may not command or fully believe. Convergence across independent physical systems provides the practical warrant.

## Against Over-Rated Observation and Strong Theory-Loading

- Positivist observation: privileged, theory-free foundation. Hacking rejects this as both historically false and practically inadequate.
- Strong theory-ladenness (Hanson, strong Kuhn/Feyerabend readings): *all* observation statements are theory-loaded; we see what our paradigm or natural interpretations allow. Hacking grants that much observation is expectation- and skill-influenced, but documents important counter-cases.
- Observation without theory: real examples exist (e.g., certain astronomical or calorimetric observations before the relevant theory was available).
- "We speak of observing when we do not literally 'see' but use information transmitted by theoretically postulated objects."

## Illustrations

- William Herschel (radiant heat) and Caroline Herschel (astronomy): work showing observation as trained skill and the use of instruments to extend senses.
- Microscopes: we believe microscope images largely because *different* physical systems (ordinary light microscopes relying on different properties of light, phase-contrast, electron microscopes, acoustic microscopes using sound rather than light) produce agreeing pictures of the same structures. The convergence across independent causal chains, not deduction from one theory, underwrites trust.
- Augmenting the senses and independence: observation is active, skilled, and often mediated; the independence of the mediating channels strengthens the result.

## Philosophical Payoff

- Observation is not the passive, neutral given of classical empiricism or positivism.
- Nor is it wholly constituted by high theory in the strong incommensurability sense.
- This practical, multi-channel, skill-based view of observation supports entity realism: we come to trust entities because they function reliably in producing convergent, usable observational results across setups.
- Ties directly to creation of phenomena and the independence of experiment: stabilizing an observable phenomenon is skilled work of apparatus and procedure as much as it is application of theory.

## Relation to Other Vault Content

- Moderates the theory-ladenness claims in [[Concepts/Incommensurability (Kuhn)]] and Feyerabend's [[Concepts/Natural Interpretations and the Galileo Case (Feyerabend)]]: theory shapes observation, but not all the way down, and cross-checks by independent instruments provide a check that survives some meaning-shift.
- Vs. positivist "protocol sentences" or basic statements (Ayer, early Popper discussions): observation is skill and decision-laden, but that does not make it useless or purely conventional.
- Supports [[Concepts/Entity Realism vs Theory Realism (Hacking)]]: the entities that transmit or produce the observations we trust through multiple routes earn realist status via use.
- With Popper: shares the rejection of an incorrigible "given"; basic statements are accepted by decision and inter-subjective test, but Hacking adds the concrete history of instrumental skills and convergences.

## Vault Meta / AI

"Observation" for an agent is the output of skilled pipelines (retrieval, perception models, verifiers, cross-checkers) rather than raw input. Trust increases when multiple independent routes (different models, different search strategies, human review, code execution) converge on the same structure. Building reliable observation is a matter of training the skill (prompting, tool use, evaluation harnesses) and creating the conditions under which the phenomenon appears regularly. The wiki's "observations" are the stable, cross-linked claims that survive multiple independent edits and checks.
