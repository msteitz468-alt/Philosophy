# Principle of Non-Contradiction (Aristotle)

> "The same attribute cannot at the same time belong and not belong to the same subject in the same respect." The firmest of all principles — indemonstrable, but defended by *elenchus* (refutation): anyone who denies it must mean *something*, and in meaning something already concedes it. *Metaphysics* IV (Gamma).

## Summary

Aristotle calls the principle of non-contradiction (PNC) "the firmest principle of all" — the one about which it is impossible to be mistaken, and to which every demonstration ultimately reduces. He gives it in an ontological form (a thing cannot both be and not be F at once and in the same respect), a logical form (contradictory statements cannot both be true), and a psychological form ("it is impossible for the same man at the same time to believe the same thing to be and not to be").

Because it is the starting-point of all demonstration, PNC **cannot itself be demonstrated** — to demand a proof of it "argues want of education," since not everything can be proved on pain of infinite regress. But it can be defended by **elenctic (negative) demonstration**: the skeptic need only *say something significant* — "something which is significant both for himself and for another." The moment a word has *one* determinate meaning ("man" means *two-footed animal*), "being a man" excludes "not being a man." To deny that words signify one thing "is to have no meaning," which "annihilates" reasoning and discourse. So the very act of meaningful denial presupposes PNC.

Aristotle then refutes the deniers — **Protagoras's relativism** ("man is the measure") and the **Heraclitean flux** reading on which nothing stable can be truly said:

- **Reductio**: if contradictories were both true, "all things will be one" (a man would equally be a trireme) and "nothing really exists" — the indeterminate Anaxagorean mush.
- **The practical refutation**: no one actually lives the doctrine. "Why does a man walk to Megara and not stay at home? Why does he not walk into a well or over a precipice?" He guards against the precipice — so he plainly does *not* judge falling and not-falling alike. Action betrays universal commitment to determinate truth.
- **Degrees of truth**: even granting flux, "there is a more and a less in the nature of things"; one who thinks four is five is less wrong than one who thinks it a thousand — "there must be some truth to which the more true is nearer."
- **Against flux** (Cratylus, who "only moved his finger"): change in quantity doesn't abolish stable *form*; "it is in respect of its form that we know each thing." A thing can be **potentially** two contraries but never **actually** both ([[Concepts/Potentiality and Actuality (Aristotle)]]) — this is the metaphysical resolution of the flux objection.

The companion **Principle of Excluded Middle** (no intermediate between contradictories: of any subject one must affirm or deny any one predicate) is defended from the definitions of truth and falsity and by the self-refutation of the relativist (he who says "everything is true" makes the contrary statement true, hence his own false).

## How This Sits Among Thinkers

- **Versus [[Thinkers/Heraclitus]]**: PNC is Aristotle's direct answer to [[Concepts/Heraclitean Flux and Logos (Heraclitus)]] — stable form, not flux, is the object of knowledge.
- **Versus Protagoras/relativism**: the practical refutation anticipates every later "performative self-refutation" argument against global skepticism and relativism — compare the self-undermining Aristotle finds, and contrast [[Concepts/Perspectivism (Nietzsche)]] and [[Concepts/Pragmatic Theory of Truth (James)]], which revive measure-of-man themes Aristotle rejected.
- **Logic tradition**: PNC and excluded middle become bedrock for the whole Western logical tradition; their later questioning (intuitionism, paraconsistent logic, [[Concepts/Picture Theory of Language (Wittgenstein)]]'s bivalence) measures itself against this text.
- **Method note**: the *elenctic* defense (you must presuppose it to argue at all) is a transcendental argument form later echoed in [[Concepts/Cogito (Descartes)]] and Kant.
