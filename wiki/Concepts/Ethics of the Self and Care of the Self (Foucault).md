# Ethics of the Self and Care of the Self (Foucault)
> Foucault's late work proposes an "aesthetics of existence" — the Greco-Roman practice of giving deliberate style to one's life — as an alternative to both modern moral codes and liberation discourse.

## Summary

The final phase of Foucault's thought (c. 1980–1984), developed in the Collège de France lectures and the final two volumes of the *History of Sexuality*, reframes ethics entirely. Rather than asking "what is the moral law?" or "what should I do?", Foucault asks: **"how does one constitute oneself as an ethical subject?"** — a question he finds answered most richly in Greco-Roman *askesis* (practices of self-governance).

**Key concepts:**

- **Care of the self (*epimeleia heautou*):** The ancient injunction to attend to oneself — not in a narcissistic sense but as the primary ethical task, the cultivation of a self that can relate well to others and to the city.
- **Chresis aphrodision** ("use of pleasures"): The Greek art of governing one's sexual practice — not according to a code of permitted/prohibited acts, but according to the internal logic of active self-mastery (*enkrateia*).
- **Aesthetics of existence:** Giving style to one's life as a whole — making life a *work of art* that is recognizable as a coherent, deliberate form. Foucault traced this ideal from Greco-Roman ethics through Baudelaire's dandy to his own practice.
- **Parrhesia** (truth-telling): The courageous act of speaking truth to power despite personal risk — contrasted with rhetoric (persuasion without truth) and flattery. The final Collège lectures were entirely on parrhesia.

Foucault's personal interest in these ideas was not merely academic. He understood the American gay scene (the Castro, Christopher Street, bath-houses) as a practical laboratory for inventing new relational forms and pleasures outside the grid of sexual identity — a living ars erotica. The ethics of the self was both a scholarly project and a personal project, and his early death cut both short.

## Key Claims

- Ancient ethics was not primarily about compliance with a moral code but about *techniques of the self* — practices by which one constitutes oneself as an ethical subject with a good relation to pleasure, truth, and others.
- The care of the self is logically prior to the care of others in the ancient tradition — one must govern oneself well before one can govern a household or a city.
- Modern ethics has lost this dimension, replacing self-cultivation with either law-following (deontology) or utility-maximizing — Foucault proposes recovering the ancient form.
- Parrhesia — the Greek practice of frank truth-telling — is the ethical-political form most suited to the philosopher's role: speaking dangerously true things that others wish not to hear.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Plato]]**: The Platonic *epimeleia* appears in the Alcibiades — care of the soul as prerequisite of political governance. Foucault traces the subsequent decline of this theme as Platonic self-knowledge (know thyself) displaced self-care.
- **[[Thinkers/Nietzsche]]**: The aesthetics of existence recalls Nietzsche's ideal of giving style to character — a convergence Foucault acknowledged but distinguished from Nietzsche's focus on the exceptional individual vs. Foucault's interest in shared practices.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The late ethics of the self sits in tension with the earlier power-knowledge framework: if power constitutes subjects, does a self-constituting ethics have any purchase? Foucault never resolved whether the care of the self operates inside or outside (or across) power relations.
- The Greco-Roman *epimeleia* was available only to free men — a significant omission in a framework meant to serve broader ethical purposes.
