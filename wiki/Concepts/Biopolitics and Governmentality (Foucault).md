# Biopolitics and Governmentality (Foucault)
> Biopolitics is the governmental management of living populations as biological masses — the extension of disciplinary power from individual bodies to whole populations through technologies of health, demography, and welfare.

## Summary

**Biopolitics** (*biopouvoir*) appears in Foucault's Collège de France lectures (especially "Society Must Be Defended," 1975–76) and *La Volonté de savoir* (1976). It names a modality of power that targets *population* as its object — not the individual body (the target of disciplinary power) but the species-body: birth rates, mortality, health, life expectancy, housing, sanitation.

Biopower emerges in the 18th–19th centuries alongside the development of the human sciences: statistics, demography, epidemiology, public health. The state no longer merely lets people die or makes them die — it actively administers life ("make live and let die," in contrast to sovereign power's old formula of "make die or let live").

**Governmentality** (*gouvernementalité*) is the broader framework developed in the 1978–79 lectures. It names the rationality of governing — the ensemble of institutions, procedures, analyses, and calculations that allow the exercise of a form of power targeting population. Foucault traces the genealogy of the modern state through the "art of government" literature (reason of state, police science, liberalism), showing how governing populations according to their own internal laws (economic, demographic) becomes the dominant mode.

## Key Claims

- Biopower operates through a "calculated management of life" — it individualizes through discipline while simultaneously totalizing through regulation of populations.
- Racism becomes the mechanism by which biopower can be articulated with the sovereign's right to kill: a population is biologized, some segment designated as a threat to the race's health, and elimination of that segment is framed as protecting the living population — the logic of state racism and genocide.
- Liberal governmentality operates by constituting a domain (the market, civil society, the family) as self-regulating — governing through freedom rather than against it.
- The GIP's practical research into hospitals and prisons was, for Foucault, a direct laboratory for developing the concept of biopower.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Biopolitics and disciplinary power are analytically distinct modalities but Foucault never fully explains how they articulate with each other — a gap that subsequent theorists (Agamben, Mbembe) have tried to fill.

> [!warning] Biopolitics extends the logic of [[Concepts/Disciplinary Society and the Panopticon (Foucault)]] from individual bodies to populations. The race/death articulation in "Society Must Be Defended" is Foucault's most explicitly political moment — compare with [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]'s account of how capitalism manages labor-power as a population resource.
