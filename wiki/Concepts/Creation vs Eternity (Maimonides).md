# Creation vs Eternity (Maimonides)

> Maimonides' position in *Guide* Part II: the world's origin is **not demonstrable either way** — neither Aristotle's eternity nor creation *ex nihilo* admits of proof. Since reason reaches a standoff, he adopts creation ex nihilo on the authority of prophecy *and* argues it is the more probable view (from cosmic design), while insisting that Aristotle himself never claimed to have *demonstrated* eternity. This carves a third path between the [[Thinkers/Aristotle|Aristotelians]] (who assert eternity) and the [[Concepts/Critique of the Kalam (Maimonides)|Kalam]] (which claimed to *prove* creation).

## The Method: Grant Aristotle, Then Prove God

The Introduction to Part II lays down twenty-five Aristotelian propositions Maimonides accepts as proven, plus a twenty-sixth — the eternity of time and motion — that he adopts only *hypothetically*: "we will admit it for the present, because by doing so we shall be enabled clearly to demonstrate our own theory." The strategy is to prove an incorporeal necessary existent / prime mover on premises valid **even if the universe were eternal**: "whether we believe in the Creatio ex Nihilo, or in the Eternity of the Universe, we can prove by demonstrative arguments the existence of God." God's existence is thus made independent of the disputed creation question — the opposite of the Kalam's order.

## The Three Theories

1. **Moses / Scripture** — creation *ex nihilo*; even time is created. "It is next in importance to the principle of God's unity."
2. **Plato** — God forms the world from co-eternal, pre-existent matter ("as the clay is to the potter"). The heavens are generated, but *from something*.
3. **Aristotle** — full eternity; heavens indestructible, time and motion without beginning or end; God cannot "change His will or conceive a new desire."

## Neither Side Is Demonstrated

Maimonides' pivotal epistemic claim: the question outruns proof. And — reading Aristotle generously — he holds Aristotle knew it: "I intend to show that Aristotle was well aware that he had not proved the Eternity of the Universe... his arguments and proofs were only apparent and plausible." "What Aristotle says on the Eternity of the Universe... cannot be proved, and that Aristotle never intended to prove these things." Because the matter is undecidable by reason, "it is better that a proposition which cannot be demonstrated be received as an axiom, or that one of the two opposite solutions... be accepted on authority."

## Why Creation Ex Nihilo

Two reasons, in order:
1. **Prophetic authority** decides the standoff: "I accept the latter on the authority of Prophecy, which can teach things beyond the reach of philosophical speculation."
2. **The argument from design** makes it the more probable: "The best proof for design in the Universe I find in the different motions of the spheres, and in the fixed position of the stars" — variations of velocity and irregular placement cannot flow from "fixed laws of Nature" but bespeak a determining **Will**. The universe is "created with design and will," not a necessary emanation.

He sharply refuses the Kalam's *dialectical* proofs of creation: "the fact that a certain proposition has been proved by a dialectical argument will never induce me to accept that proposition, but, on the contrary, will weaken my faith in it." He claims only admissibility and then preferability — never demonstration.

## The Stake: Miracles and the Law

Aristotelian eternity, with its necessitated unchanging nature, "would necessarily be in opposition to the foundation of our religion, we should disbelieve all miracles and signs." Tellingly, his objection is to *necessity*, not to pre-existent matter as such: **Plato's** version "would not imply the rejection of miracles, but, on the contrary, would admit them as possible." Creation by will makes Revelation and the particularity of the Law intelligible — "He willed it so; or, His wisdom decided so."

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. [[Thinkers/Al-Ghazali]] (instructive contrast within the vault's cluster):** Both reject the Aristotelian eternal world and both deny it was *demonstrated*. But Ghazali ([[Concepts/Eternal Will and Temporal Creation (Ghazali)]]) presses on to *positive arguments* that eternity is incoherent and that an eternal will fixes a temporal beginning; Maimonides is more cautious — he thinks the philosophers' eternity is merely *unproven*, settles the matter by prophecy, and adds only a probabilistic design argument. Ghazali condemns eternity as unbelief; Maimonides treats it as a respectable but undecided opinion. See [[Contradictions/Temporal Creation vs Eternal World]].
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]:** Aquinas adopts essentially Maimonides' epistemic verdict — the world's temporal beginning is held by *faith*, not demonstrable by reason, and the eternity-proofs are inconclusive — explicitly crediting "Rabbi Moses." One of the clearest lines of transmission from the *Guide* into scholasticism.
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]:** Averroes defends Aristotelian eternity outright ([[Concepts/Eternity of the World (Ibn Rushd)]]); Maimonides accepts far more Aristotelian physics than Ghazali yet breaks with Aristotle exactly here.
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Augustine]]:** shares "time itself is created" ([[Concepts/Time and Eternity - Distentio Animi (Augustine)]]), reached by Maimonides through cosmology rather than interior phenomenology.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

> [!warning] Takes a distinct third position in [[Contradictions/Temporal Creation vs Eternal World]] — eternity *undemonstrated* (not, as Ghazali, *disproved*), creation accepted on prophecy + design

- If neither view is demonstrable, is the design argument doing real probabilistic work, or is prophecy carrying the whole weight?
- How much does conceding "God's will is homonymous with man's will" cost the design argument, which trades on a will that selects among options?
