# Enthymeme

> In Aristotle’s *Rhetoric*, the enthymeme is the central instrument of rhetorical persuasion: a deductive syllogism (or “rhetorical syllogism”) constructed from probabilities (*eikos*) and signs (*sēmeion*, especially *tekmerion* — conclusive signs). The counterpart to the example (rhetorical induction). It is the “substance” of rhetorical persuasion, as opposed to the non-essential emotional appeals emphasized in earlier manuals.

## Core Elements (from Book I)
- Rhetoric is the counterpart of dialectic and can be treated systematically as an art.
- Artistic proofs (*pisteis*): *logos* (the argument), *ethos* (speaker’s character), *pathos* (disposing the audience).
- The enthymeme is typically abbreviated (leaves out a premise that the audience can supply) and deals with the contingent and particular.
- Signs and probabilities: not certain demonstration but what is generally or usually the case.
- Three branches of oratory each have their characteristic enthymemes and *topoi* (lines of argument): deliberative (future expediency), forensic (past justice), epideictic (present praise/blame).
