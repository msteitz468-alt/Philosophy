# Distributive Justice and the Rule of Law (Aristotle)

> Political justice is proportional, not absolute, equality — equal for equals, unequal for unequals — which raises the "who should rule" aporia; Aristotle resolves the ordinary case in favor of the rule of law over the rule of any individual man, since "the law is reason unaffected by desire."

## Summary

In *Politics* Book III, Aristotle argues that "justice is thought by them to be, and is, equality, not, however, for all, but only for equals; and inequality is thought to be, and is, justice; neither is this for all, but only for unequals" — the core statement of proportional (as opposed to strictly numerical) distributive justice, matching political honors and offices to relevant merit rather than distributing them identically to all. This generates an aporia: on what basis should rule be assigned when different groups (the multitude, the wealthy, the good, the one best man) each have a plausible claim, and excluding any produces some injustice or dishonor? Aristotle floats a "wisdom of the multitude" argument — the collective judgment of many ordinary people can, pooled, surpass that of a few excellent individuals, "like a feast to which many contribute being better than a dinner provided out of a single purse" — as one resolution favoring broad participation in deliberation and judgment even where few hold the highest offices. He then poses the sharper law-vs-man debate: "he who bids the law rule may be deemed to bid God and Reason alone rule, but he who bids man rule adds an element of the beast... The law is reason unaffected by desire." Only in the extreme case of a person of transcendent, whole-surpassing virtue (*pambasileia*, see [[Concepts/Classification of Constitutions (Aristotle)]]) does the argument for rule of law yield to legitimate personal rule, since "such an one may truly be deemed a God among men... for men of pre-eminent virtue there is no law — they are themselves a law."

## Key Claims / Positions

- Justice in the political sphere is essentially proportional/distributive, not arithmetic equality applied indiscriminately.
- The "who should rule" question has no single uncontested answer among competing claimants (the many, the wealthy, the virtuous, the law) — each generates a real injustice when made absolute.
- The wisdom-of-the-multitude argument gives a qualified epistemic case for broad participation in deliberation/judgment, independent of any individual's superior virtue.
- The rule of law is generally superior to the rule of any individual man because law is impartial reason, free of the passions ("appetite") that can corrupt even a good ruler.
- The sole exception is a person of virtue so transcendent as to be incommensurable with ordinary citizens — for such a person alone, personal rule (not law) is fitting.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Downstream — constitutionalism**: the rule-of-law argument ("law is reason unaffected by desire") is a direct ancestor of the later constitutionalist tradition's preference for impersonal legal authority over discretionary personal rule, echoed in [[Thinkers/John Stuart Mill]]'s concerns about arbitrary power and in modern separation-of-powers thought.
- **Contrast with [[Thinkers/Thomas Hobbes]]**: Hobbes's sovereign is deliberately above the law it makes (legibus solutus), the opposite of Aristotle's default preference for law over personal rule — though Hobbes could be read as generalizing Aristotle's *pambasileia* exception into the ordinary case, on the grounds that only an unconstrained sovereign can end civil strife.
- **Wisdom-of-the-multitude**: an ancient anticipation of later epistemic-democracy arguments (e.g., the Condorcet Jury Theorem tradition) for why aggregating many mediocre judgments can outperform a few excellent ones.
