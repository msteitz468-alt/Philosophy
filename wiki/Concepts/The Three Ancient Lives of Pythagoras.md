# The Three Ancient Lives of Pythagoras
> The three surviving ancient biographies — Diogenes Laertius, Porphyry, and Iamblichus (all 3rd–4th c. AD) — that are the chief vehicles of the Pythagoras legend. Each reflects its author's agenda, so every biographical "fact" must be weighed against the purpose of the Life that reports it.

## Why They Matter
On the chapters by André Laks, Constantinos Macris, and Dominic O'Meara in [[Sources/A History of Pythagoreanism - Carl A. Huffman (ed.) (2014)]], these three Lives are the funnel through which the rich late tradition reaches us — and the reason the [[Contradictions/Pythagoras the Scientist vs the Religious Charismatic|source-critical problem]] is so acute. They share a third-century-AD date and common older sources (Aristoxenus, Nicomachus, an unnamed handbook), but differ sharply in form, purpose, and attitude — a spectrum from neutral compilation to outright divinization.

## Diogenes Laertius (Laks)
- Opens the **"Italic" succession** with Pythagoras (Book 8); heterogeneous, poorly organized, "late erudite Hellenistic scholarship."
- **Not apologetic** and shows no debt to Neopythagoreanism; his personal attitude is genuinely *undecidable* — the satirical counter-tradition (Timon: "Pythagoras... a whisperer of lofty speech... hunts after humans") sits beside admiring material.
- Laks' insight: the lists of **precepts (*symbola*/*acusmata*)** are the real organizing thread, and the doxography excerpted from **Alexander Polyhistor's "Pythagorean Notes" (*hypomnemata*)** *justifies* them — "it is because the universe is as it is that we should behave as Pythagoras demands." The *Notes* are eclectic (post-Academic + Stoic), datable late-4th to 1st c. BC (see [[Concepts/Pseudo-Pythagorean Writings]]).

## Porphyry (Macris)
- The *Life of Pythagoras* (*VP*) was **not a standalone work** but one chapter of Porphyry's four-book ***History of Philosophy***, which culminated in Plato; it survived by chance through Byzantine interest.
- Antiquarian, compilatory, and **Platonist**: pre-Platonic philosophy is mere "preparation" for Plato, advancing "from one mountain peak to another" (Pythagoras → Socrates → Plato).
- A "reasoned juxtaposition" of four sources (a handbook, Antonius Diogenes' novel, **Nicomachus**, **Moderatus**), yielding a *Middle-Platonic / Neopythagorean* Pythagoras. Uniformly admiring (the *theios anēr*); Porphyry's own vegetarian asceticism makes him single out **purification of the mind and [[Concepts/Metempsychosis (Transmigration of Souls)|metempsychosis]]** as Pythagoras' chief contributions — *VP* §19 is the oldest concise doctrinal summary of transmigration.

## Iamblichus (O'Meara)
- *On the Pythagorean Life* is **Part I of a larger ten-part curriculum, *On Pythagoreanism*** — protreptic and pedagogic, progressing from the Pythagorean life toward the mathematical sciences. (Recent scholarship rejects Rohde's "piteous patchwork" verdict; "Hardly patchworking! More an extensive re-weaving.")
- **Neoplatonist from the start**: Pythagoras is an "immaculate," undescended soul sent from Apollo's retinue to save humanity. The work stages a "**battle over Plato's legacy**" — against Porphyry's edition of Plotinus, Iamblichus (radicalizing Numenius and Nicomachus) argues that "**true Platonism is Pythagoreanism**."
- Best read as an **encomium / prolegomenon** praising the founder (*archēgos*) to motivate students. Iamblichus' revival "largely succeeded," transmitted via Syrianus and Proclus to the Renaissance (see [[Concepts/Pythagoras in Medieval and Renaissance Thought]]).

## The Lesson
Reading across the three: Diogenes = disorganized but relatively neutral compilation; Porphyry = scholarly Platonist admiration; Iamblichus = systematic divinization. The "biography" of Pythagoras is therefore *reception in three keys* — which is why strict source-criticism (Burkert, Zhmud) privileges the pre-Platonic fragments over anything these Lives add. See [[Schools/Neopythagoreanism]].
