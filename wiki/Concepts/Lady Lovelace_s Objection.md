# Lady Lovelace's Objection

> From Ada Lovelace's 1842 memoir on Babbage's Analytical Engine: "The Analytical Engine has no pretensions to originate anything. It can do whatever we know how to order it to perform." Turing treats this as one of the strongest and most persistent contrary views; his reply turns on the limited evidence available to Lovelace, the frequency with which machines surprise their programmers, and the fallacy (common to philosophers and mathematicians) of assuming that all consequences of a fact are seen simultaneously with the fact itself.

## The Objection (and Hartree's gloss)

Turing quotes Lovelace directly (her italics): the Engine "has no pretensions to originate anything."

He also cites Hartree (1949), who adds that while it may someday be possible to build equipment that will "think for itself" or exhibit conditioned reflexes / learning, "it did not seem that the machines constructed or projected at the time had this property."

The core claim: machines are limited to executing what we have explicitly instructed; they cannot create or originate.

A stronger variant: a machine can "never do anything really new" or "never take us by surprise."

## Turing's Replies (§6, objection 6)

1. **Evidence at the time was limited**: Lovelace and Babbage had only the (never-completed) Analytical Engine before them. "It is quite possible that the machines in question had in a sense got this property" of originating or surprising; the Countess and Babbage simply had no obligation or occasion to claim all that could be claimed.

2. **Machines do surprise Turing frequently**: "Machines take me by surprise with great frequency." This is largely because he does not do sufficient calculation to decide in advance what to expect; he takes risks and makes assumptions that turn out wrong. The result is genuine surprise for the programmer.

3. **The "all consequences seen at once" fallacy**: Philosophers and mathematicians are especially prone to assuming that "as soon as a fact is presented to a mind all consequences of that fact spring into the mind simultaneously with it." This is "a very useful assumption under many circumstances, but one too easily forgets that it is false." Once we drop the assumption, there is "no virtue in the mere working out of consequences from data and general principles" — and machines excel at that working-out, producing results the human does not anticipate.

4. **"Nothing new under the sun"**: Any "original" human work can be described as the growth of seeds planted by teaching or the following of general principles. The variant that machines cannot "take us by surprise" is met directly by experience.

Turing notes that this objection will be revisited under "learning machines."

## Relation to Learning Machines and "Supercritical" Minds

The Lovelace objection is the negative counterpart to the positive programme Turing sketches in §7. If ideas injected into a mind are usually "subcritical" (produce less than one idea in reply on average), a "supercritical" mind generates whole theories. Most human minds are subcritical; animals "very definitely" so. The question for machines: "Can a machine be made to be supercritical?"

A learning child machine + education process, with random element, punishments/rewards via unemotional channels, and imperatives inside the system, is offered precisely as the route past the "only does what we order it to perform" barrier.
