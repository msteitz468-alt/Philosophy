# Disciplinary Society and the Panopticon (Foucault)
> The shift from public torture to the penitentiary was not the humanization of punishment but the installation of a more efficient, diffuse form of power — one that produces "docile and useful" bodies through continuous surveillance and normalization.

## Summary

*Surveiller et punir: Naissance de la prison* (1975; English: *Discipline and Punish*) opens with a visceral contrast: the 1757 public torture-execution of Damiens the regicide, and a timetabled daily schedule for prisoners from 1838. The contrast is not progress but transformation. The question is: what changed, how, and why?

Foucault's answer: **disciplinary power** — a new modality of power that operates through space (cells, timetables, examined bodies), visibility (continuous monitoring), and normalization (comparison to a standard, ranked distribution of individuals). The prison is the pure form of this modality, but it operates across all modern institutions: schools, hospitals, factories, armies. The disciplinary society is one organized around the production of "docile and useful bodies" — simultaneously optimized for productivity and rendered politically manageable.

**The Panopticon** (Bentham's 1791 design for a prison in which a single central observer can see all inmates, who cannot see whether they are being watched) becomes Foucault's master model. The key insight: it does not matter whether the observer is actually present. The prisoner who *might* be watched at any moment *disciplines themselves*. Panoptic power is **internalized surveillance** — which is why it is more efficient than spectacular public punishment. Power operates through the gaze, not the blow.

## Key Claims

- The reform of punishment in the late 18th century was not motivated by humanitarian impulse but by the need to make power more efficient: "punish less, but punish better."
- Discipline operates on individual bodies through three instruments: hierarchical observation, normalizing judgment, and the examination (which combines both into a documentary trace).
- "Normalization" is the disciplinary operation that produces a standard and distributes individuals above/below it — making everyone measurable, comparable, and correctible.
- The carceral continuum: prison, school, hospital, factory, army share disciplinary techniques — punishment is not aberrant but continuous with the norms of normal social institutions.
- The "delinquent" (as opposed to the mere "criminal") is a product of the prison apparatus — a psychological identity constructed through its documentation and surveillance, which then justifies the prison's continued operation.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Jeremy Bentham**: Foucault reads Bentham's Panopticon not as a benevolent reform project but as an epitome of disciplinary logic — the good intentions of its designer are irrelevant to its functioning.
- **[[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]**: Both analyze modern capitalism as producing forms of control, but Marx focuses on exploitation in production; Foucault focuses on the production of docile bodies in disciplinary space, which is logically prior to the extraction of labor.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Foucault's account implies a relatively sudden transition to disciplinary society in the late 18th century — historians have questioned whether the disciplinary techniques he describes were as novel or as systematically coordinated as the book implies.
