# Occasionalism vs Natural Causation (Ghazali and Ibn Rushd)

> The radical theological position (Ghazali, drawing on Ash‚Äòarite theology) that there are no necessary causal connections or inherent powers in created things ‚Äî fire does not burn cotton ‚Äúby nature‚Äù; God creates the effect (burning, satiety, death, etc.) directly at each moment, usually following a customary ‚Äúhabit‚Äù but able to interrupt it at will (miracles). Opposed by Ibn Rushd (Averroes), following Aristotle: things possess real specific natures, essences, and active/passive powers from which their characteristic acts follow; observed regularities reflect genuine secondary causation sustained by a First Cause, not arbitrary divine intervention at every instant.

## Ghazali / Ash‚Äòarite Occasionalism (Natural Sciences, First Discussion)

- **Core Denial**: The connection between what is believed to be a cause and its effect is not necessary. Each thing has its own individuality. Neither the existence nor non-existence of one is implied by the other. Satisfaction does not *imply* drinking, burning does not *imply* contact with fire, etc.
- **Example (Cotton and Fire)**: It is possible (in God‚Äôs power) for cotton to contact fire without burning, or to turn to ash without contact. The agent of burning is God (directly or via angels), who creates the blackness and separation of parts. Fire is a ‚Äúdead body‚Äù with no real action.
- **Epistemological Ground**: Observation shows only simultaneity/concomitance (‚Äúhabit‚Äù), never causation. We infer causation only because we do not see the true (divine) cause.
- **Broader Implications**:
  - No real secondary causes or ‚Äúnatures‚Äù in things.
  - All events are fresh creations by God; the world is re-created moment by moment.
  - Miracles pose no special problem (no fixed nature is violated).
  - Human acts are likewise not truly ours in the causal sense (God creates them).
  - Leads to the famous ‚Äúscandalous‚Äù consequences Ghazali himself raises and tries to mitigate: at any moment a book could turn into a horse, a man into a dog, etc., consistent with God‚Äôs absolute power. Ghazali‚Äôs defense: God has created in us the knowledge that He will not do these things except at special times (prophetic miracles); habit fixes expectation.

Ghazali concedes in later replies that things can have qualities/dispositions, but still insists these need not produce their usual effects without God‚Äôs concurrent creation of the effect.

## From the Primary Text (Tahafut al-Falasifa, Seventeenth Discussion)

*Added 2026-06-29 with the ingest of al-Ghazali's own work ([[Sources/The Incoherence of the Philosophers - Al-Ghazali]]) ‚Äî previously this page reconstructed his position only through Averroes' rebuttal. His actual words:*

- **The thesis:** "The connection between what is habitually believed to be a cause and what is habitually believed to be an effect is not necessary, according to us." Their conjunction "is due to the prior decree of God, who creates them side by side, not to its being necessary in itself."
- **Fire and cotton:** "The one who enacts the burning by creating blackness in the cotton, [causing] separation in its parts, and making it cinder or ashes is God, either through the mediation of His angels or without mediation. As for fire, which is inanimate, it has no action."
- **The epistemology** anticipating [[Thinkers/Hume|Hume]]: observation "shows the occurrence [of burning] at [the time of] the contact with the fire but does not show the occurrence [of burning] *by* [the fire]." His man-born-blind example: one who first opens his eyes at daylight thinks sight alone reveals colors, until sunset teaches him the sun was the cause ‚Äî the apparent cause may be a mere "readier," the true cause the permanent "bestower of forms."
- **The "scandal" objection and the habit-reply:** the opponent jeers that occasionalism licenses a book left at home becoming a horse "that has defiled the library with its urine." Al-Ghazali: "God created for us the knowledge that He did not enact these possibilities... the continuous habit of their occurrence repeatedly... fixes unshakably in our minds the belief in their occurrence according to past habit." When a miracle comes, "these cognitions slip away from [people's] hearts." The objection is "nothing but sheer vilification."
- **Two approaches.** Crucially, the primary text shows al-Ghazali offering *two* positions: (1) the pure occasionalist denial (God acts voluntarily; He can create contact without burning); and (2) a **modified theory** "with which there is deliverance from these vilifications" ‚Äî fire is "created in such a way that" it burns like cotton, yet "we allow as possible that a prophet may be cast into the fire without being burned, either by changing the quality of the fire or by changing the quality of the prophet" (the *talc* example: a man coated in talc sits unharmed in a furnace). Here things have real dispositions, while miracles remain possible through God altering them ‚Äî provided the divine act stays voluntary. He does not claim the two are compossible; Marmura's introduction and the sequel *Al-Iqtisad* show theory (1), strict Ash'arite occasionalism, is the one he actually holds, theory (2) being advanced for the sake of argument.
- **Miracles targeted:** Abraham unburned, the staff into a serpent, reviving the dead, splitting the moon. Transformation requires *common matter* (staff‚Üíserpent, blood‚Üísperm share subsistent matter); accident‚Üîsubstance changes, lacking common matter, remain genuinely impossible ‚Äî so even the second theory has limits.

## Averroes‚Äô (Aristotelian) Defense of Natural Causation

- **Denial of Causation = Sophistry and Destruction of Intellect**: Whoever denies observed efficient causes in sensible things either denies with his tongue what he knows in his mind or is misled by sophistry. If there are no causes, there is no agent for any act, no intellect (which perceives things *with their causes*), no logic, no definitions, no essences, no science, and ultimately no knowledge ‚Äî only opinion.
- **Real Natures and Four Causes**: Things have specific natures/essences/attributes that determine their special functions and differentiate them (otherwise everything would be one, or nothing). All events have four causes (agent, form, matter, end) ‚Äî necessary for the effect. The theologians themselves recognize conditions, signs, and ‚Äúpsychological qualities‚Äù when it suits them.
- **Habit and Nature**: ‚ÄúHabit‚Äù is ambiguous. If in the agent (God), it contradicts the Qur‚Äôanic ‚Äúno alteration in the course of God.‚Äù If in things, it *is* nature. If only in our judgments, it is still an act of the soul determined by its nature. The philosophers locate the regularity in the existents themselves (nature) while affirming an immaterial First Agent whose act is the condition for the existence and operation of secondary causes.
- **Miracles and Impediments**: It is possible for fire to be brought near cotton without burning if an external impediment (e.g., talc) is present ‚Äî this does not deny fire‚Äôs power to burn when conditions are normal. Miracles can occur through God (or angels) conferring a special quality on the fire (confining its heat) or on the body (resistance), or through rapid transformations of matter that are possible in principle but unusual in habit. The philosophers do not deny the possibility of marvels; they deny that they require the total abolition of causation.
- **On Abraham in the Fire**: Only heretical Muslims claim such things are impossible for God. The learned philosophers do not publicly dispute religious principles; they are obligatory for moral and social order. Religious truths surpass full human understanding but must be acknowledged.
- **Knowledge and Wisdom**: True knowledge is of a thing as it is in reality. If opposites are always in perfect equilibrium with no preponderating nature or cause, there can be no permanent knowledge of anything. The eternal knowledge and will of God are the ultimate causes of the natures in existents; our knowledge tracks those natures.

Averroes insists that the apparent action of things on each other (preparing dispositions) is real, even if the bestowal of substantial forms ultimately traces to higher principles (active intellect / bestower of forms / heavenly souls). He rejects the idea that all causation is illusory projection or pure sequence (Hume-like).

## Relation to Aquinas and the Vault‚Äôs Synthesis

Aquinas repeatedly refutes Averroes (and Avicenna) on the *soul and intellect* (unicity, individuation, active intellect as power of the soul rather than separate), but on **causation and nature** he is far closer to the Aristotelian‚ÄìAverroist side than to Ghazali:

- **Natural Law (I-II, QQ. 90ff)**: Eternal law (God‚Äôs governance) ‚Üí natural law as the rational creature‚Äôs *participation* via ‚Äúthe light of natural reason whereby we discern what is good and what is evil.‚Äù First principle: ‚Äúgood is to be done and pursued, and evil avoided.‚Äù This presupposes stable, knowable natures in things and real secondary causation. Human law derives from it for the common good.
- **Grace Perfects Nature**: Foundational maxim. If there were no real nature or causal powers, there would be nothing for grace to perfect.
- **Hylomorphism and Soul (Part I, QQ. 75-76)**: The soul is the substantial *form* of the body (Aristotelian). This grounds personal identity (‚ÄúSocrates understands‚Äù), individuation, and the possibility of genuine knowledge by abstraction from phantasms. Directly anti-Averroist on the intellect, but the underlying commitment to real forms and causal powers in nature aligns against occasionalism.
- **Last End / Beatific Vision**: The composite human being (body + soul) is the subject; natural happiness (imperfect contemplation / virtuous activity) is real but incomplete; elevated by grace to supernatural vision. All of this requires a stable natural order.

The vault‚Äôs existing Aquinas pages already note the anti-Averroist polemics on the soul; the *Tahafut* now supplies the primary text being engaged and shows why the stakes (personal immortality, moral responsibility, possibility of science and natural ethics) were so high.
