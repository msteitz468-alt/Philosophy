# Unanimity Rule (Buchanan-Tullock)
> Unanimity is the unique collective decision-making rule that guarantees Pareto-efficient outcomes and eliminates expected external costs.

## Summary

In *The Calculus of Consent* (1962), James M. Buchanan and Gordon Tullock place the **rule of unanimity** at the center of their ethical and economic analysis of constitutional democracy.

### The Unique Properties of Unanimity

Within public choice theory, unanimity is not merely a high consensus threshold; it has unique qualitative properties:

1. **Zero External Costs**: Under the rule of unanimity, no collective action can be taken unless every single individual in the group agrees. This ensures that the state can never impose expected external costs on any minority or individual without their consent.
2. **Guaranteed Pareto Efficiency**: Reaching unanimous agreement is the **only true empirical test** of a change in the restricted Pareto sense. If every individual votes for a proposed policy, we can state with scientific certainty that at least one person's utility has increased and no person's utility has decreased. Any less-than-unanimous rule (e.g., simple majority) allows the winning coalition to benefit by imposing net damages on the losing minority, resulting in potentially negative-sum outcomes.
3. **The Compensation Test**: Unanimity forces political actors to construct compensation schemes or side-payments. If a project benefits one group but harms another, the benefiting group must pay compensation to the harmed group to win their vote. If the total gains of the project exceed the damages, the winners can easily afford to compensate the losers, leading to mutual gain.

### Knut Wicksell and the Benefit Principle

Buchanan's focus on unanimity was directly inspired by the Swedish economist Knut Wicksell's *Finanztheoretische Untersuchungen* (1896). Wicksell argued that if a public project is truly worth its cost, there must be a way to distribute the taxes to finance it such that everyone is made better off. Wicksell proposed that all tax-expenditure proposals should be voted on together as a package, requiring **relative unanimity** (e.g., a 5/6ths majority) to prevent majority coalitions from using the fiscal system to exploit minorities.

### The Holdout Problem and the Qualified Majority

If unanimity is the only efficient and ethical rule, why do democracies not use it for all decisions?

Buchanan and Tullock show that as the required voting threshold approaches unanimity, the **decision-making costs** (transaction costs) rise exponentially. 
* **The Holdout Strategy**: Individual voters realize that their single vote is necessary to pass the policy. Consequently, they have a strong incentive to "hold out" and demand exorbitant compensations or private side-payments, blocking the collective agreement.

To economize on these transaction and holdout costs, rational individuals at the constitutional stage voluntarily agree to delegate day-to-day decisions to **less-than-unanimous qualified majority rules** (or even simple majority), accepting the risk of minor external exploitation to avoid permanent political gridlock.

## Key Claims / Positions

### How Different Thinkers Approach This

- **[[Thinkers/John Stuart Mill]]**: Mill defended representative democracy and majority rule as the best means of checking power, but worried about the tyranny of the majority. Buchanan and Tullock formalize this worry: majority rule is structurally designed to impose external costs, and the only complete cure is the unanimity rule, with constitutional checkreins (like bicameralism) serving as substitutes for unanimity.
- **Knut Wicksell**: Formulated relative unanimity in public finance. Buchanan credited Wicksell with the intellectual breakthrough that enabled his own constitutional theories.
- **Pareto Welfare Economists**: Use mathematical models to define "Pareto-efficient allocations" of resources. Buchanan and Tullock point out that while economists can define these allocations in theory, in the real world, the only way to *verify* if a policy change is Pareto-improving is through Wicksellian unanimous voting.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] The rule of unanimity preserves the status quo. If the existing status quo is highly unjust or unequal (e.g., feudalism or slavery), requiring unanimity for any reform allows the benefiting elite to veto all progress.
- The use of side-payments and vote-trading to buy consent, while economically efficient, frequently clashes with moral and legal prohibitions against vote-buying in democratic politics. See [[Concepts/Logrolling and Vote-Trading (Buchanan-Tullock)]].
