# The Unmoved Mover (Aristotle)

> The eternal, immaterial, purely actual substance that moves the cosmos *as an object of love and desire* — final cause, not pusher — and whose own life is "thought thinking itself" (*noēsis noēseōs*). Aristotle's natural theology, developed in *Metaphysics* Book XII (Lambda).

## Summary

In *Metaphysics* XII Aristotle argues from the eternity of motion to a first principle that is pure **actuality** (*energeia*) with no admixture of **potentiality** ([[Concepts/Potentiality and Actuality (Aristotle)]]). The argument runs: motion and time must be eternal (they cannot themselves come to be or cease); a merely *potential* mover need not actually move, so it could not guarantee eternal motion; therefore there must be an eternal substance whose very essence is to be active. Because it is fully actual it is **immaterial** (matter is potentiality), and being immaterial it is indivisible, eternal, and one.

This first mover **"moves without being moved"**: it causes motion the way a beloved object or an object of thought moves desire — as a **final cause** (the *for-the-sake-of-which*), not as an efficient pusher. "The final cause produces motion as being loved, but all other things move by being moved." The heavens, eternally striving to imitate its perfection, rotate eternally; all sublunary change follows from that.

Its life is the highest activity, contemplation. Since in immaterial thinking the thinker and the object of thought coincide, and since the best intellect must think the best object (itself), the divine activity is **thought thinking itself** — *noēsis noēseōs*. "Life also belongs to God; for the actuality of thought is life, and God is that actuality; and God's self-dependent actuality is life most good and eternal."

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Three kinds of substance** ([[Concepts/Substance (Ousia) (Aristotle)]]): sensible-perishable (plants, animals), sensible-eternal (heavenly bodies), and non-sensible-immovable-eternal (the divine). The third is the object of *first philosophy* qua theology ([[Concepts/Being qua Being (Aristotle)]]).
- **From eternal motion to pure actuality**: a potential mover "may possibly not be" acting; eternal motion needs a mover whose essence *is* actuality, hence without matter.
- **Final causation, not efficient**: the mover is unmoved because it draws all things as the object of desire and intelligibility. This dissolves the regress of movers.
- **God as living contemplation**: eternal, best, indivisible, impassive; "a life such as the best which we enjoy, and enjoy for but a short time." We touch this state momentarily; God is always in it.
- **Plurality of movers**: each celestial sphere (Aristotle borrows Eudoxus/Callippus' 47–55 spheres) needs its own unmoved mover, but all are subordinate to the one primary mover; "there is one heaven alone."
- **The good is in the order *and* in the leader** — "as an army does; for its good is found both in its order and in its leader, and more in the latter." The cosmos is hierarchically ordered toward a transcendent good. Closing line (from Homer): **"The rule of many is not good; one ruler let there be."**

## How This Sits Among Thinkers

- **Versus the Presocratics**: corrects [[Thinkers/Anaxagoras]] (*nous* as mover, but cannot say why it began to move) and [[Thinkers/Empedocles]] (Love is confusedly both mover and matter). Against the atomists' purposeless void ([[Contradictions/Atomist Materialism vs Platonic-Aristotelian Teleology]]), motion has an eternal teleological source.
- **Versus [[Thinkers/Plato]]**: the separate Forms "will be causes of nothing — or at least not of movement." Aristotle replaces the Form of the Good with an actually existing divine intellect.
- **Downstream — [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]**: the *prima via* (argument from motion) and the act/potency metaphysics descend directly from Lambda, but Aquinas converts the final-cause mover into a creating, knowing, providential God — a major transformation (Aristotle's god neither creates nor knows particulars).
- **Downstream — [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]**: defends the eternal cosmos with an eternal mover ([[Concepts/Eternity of the World (Ibn Rushd)]]) against al-Ghazali's created-in-time world — a tension internal to the Aristotelian legacy.
- **[[Concepts/Great Chain of Being (Lovejoy)]]**: the self-sufficient contemplative God atop a graded order is a seed of the plenitude/gradation hierarchy; but note Lovejoy's tension between a *self-thinking* god (Aristotle) and an *overflowing* god (Neoplatonic emanation, [[Concepts/Emanation (Proodos)]]).

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Aristotle's god is *not* a creator and does not know or providentially govern particulars (it thinks only itself). Monotheist appropriations (Aquinas, Ibn Rushd) must add creation, knowledge, and providence — a real discontinuity, not a smooth inheritance.
- Eternity of motion conflicts with creation *ex nihilo* and temporal beginning — see [[Concepts/Eternity of the World (Ibn Rushd)]].
