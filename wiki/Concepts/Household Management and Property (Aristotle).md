# Household Management and Property (Aristotle)

> Household management (*oikonomia*) is the natural art of using the household's members and goods well; it is sharply distinguished from wealth-getting (*chrēmatistikē*), which has a natural, limited form (provisioning) and an unnatural, unlimited form (retail exchange and, at its worst, usury).

## Summary

*Politics* Book I, chs. 3, 8–13 works out the household's remaining parts after master/slave: husband/wife, father/children, and "the art of acquisition," which Aristotle treats as instrumental to household management rather than identical with it — "household management attends more to men than to the acquisition of inanimate things." He distinguishes a natural art of acquisition, bound to the provision of food and necessities and therefore inherently limited (like a craft with a determinate end), from an unnatural, unlimited pursuit of wealth via retail trade and currency exchange, where the end (money) has no natural boundary. The most unnatural mode of all is usury, "a gain out of money itself, and not from the natural object of it" — money bred from money rather than from use. Property and wealth are consistently instrumental in this account: household management is properly aimed at the virtue and good functioning of persons, not the accumulation of goods for its own sake.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Household management (*oikonomia*) and wealth-getting (*chrēmatistikē*) are distinct arts; the household properly uses, but is not defined by, acquisition.
- Natural acquisition is self-limiting (tied to a determinate need); unnatural acquisition (retail trade, usury) is unlimited because money itself has no natural terminus of sufficiency.
- Usury is condemned as the most unnatural form of gain, since it generates value from money itself rather than from any natural exchange of goods.
- The household's ultimate end is the virtue of its human members, not the quantity of its possessions.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Downstream medieval reception**: Aristotle's usury critique becomes the basis for the medieval Christian (and Thomistic) prohibition on interest-taking, later reversed by early modern economic thought.
- **Contrast with [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]**: Marx's critique of capital as self-expanding value (M-C-M') that "breeds" more money independent of use-value strikingly echoes Aristotle's usury analysis — both treat money-generating-money as an unnatural inversion of the proper (use-oriented) order of exchange, though Marx historicizes and generalizes the critique to all capital, not just interest-bearing loans, and lacks Aristotle's natural-limit teleology.
- **Contrast with [[Thinkers/John Stuart Mill]]** and classical/marginalist economics generally: modern economics treats unlimited wealth accumulation and market exchange as ordinarily natural and welfare-enhancing, rejecting Aristotle's premise that acquisition has a natural terminus set by household need.
