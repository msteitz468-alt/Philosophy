# Causal Argument for God's Existence from Objective Reality (Descartes)

> The principal proof of God's existence in the *Third Meditation*: the idea of God (an infinite, independent, supremely intelligent, powerful, creative substance) contains more "objective reality" than can be accounted for by any finite cause, including myself. By the principle that there must be at least as much reality in the total and efficient cause as in its effect (formal or eminent), this idea must have been caused by a being that actually possesses infinite reality—i.e., God. Complemented by a conservation/continuous-creation argument: I depend for my existence at every moment and cannot be the cause of myself.

## Core Argument

After classifying thoughts and ideas (innate, adventitious, factitious) and noting that ideas as modifications of thought cannot be false in themselves, Descartes applies the causal principle to their objective reality (the reality of what they represent).

"By the name 'God' I understand an infinite, independent, supremely intelligent, supremely powerful substance, by which I myself and whatever else exists (if anything else does exist) was created. But certainly, all these properties are such that, the more carefully I consider them, the less it seems possible that they can be derived from me alone. And so I must conclude that it necessarily follows from all that has been said up to now that God exists."

The key causal axiom (manifest by the natural light): "there must be at least as much reality in the total and efficient cause as in its effect." Nothing comes from nothing; what is more perfect cannot derive from what is less perfect. This holds for actual/formal reality and for objective reality in ideas.

Ideas of finite things (bodies, other humans, angels, myself) can derive from me (or eminently). The idea of God cannot: a finite substance cannot contain the idea of an infinite substance (the perception of the infinite is prior to the finite; I could not recognize my own lacks and doubts without the prior idea of a more perfect being). The idea is not materially false (it is supremely clear and distinct and contains perfections). It is not a construction from potential perfections in me (increase in knowledge is a mark of imperfection; potential being cannot produce objective being in an idea).

Additional argument from my own existence and conservation: "the same power and action is required to conserve any thing... in being during the individual moments in which it continues to exist, as would be needed to create the same thing from the start." I have no power to conserve myself; parents conserve only the body, not the thinking thing; any regress of partial or lesser causes ends at a being that has from itself the power to exist and all perfections—God.

The idea is innate: "the mark by which the craftsman makes himself known in his handiwork." "It must therefore be that the idea is innate within me, in the same way as the idea of myself is innate within me."

Since God is supremely perfect, he cannot be a deceiver.

## Clarifications (Replies, Objections)

- "Objective reality": the reality of the thing represented by the idea (as opposed to formal reality of the idea as a mode of thought, or actual reality of the thing outside the mind).
- "Eminently": the cause can contain the reality in a higher form (God eminently contains the reality of finite things).
- Not presupposing knowledge of God to prove God: the argument starts from the presence of the idea in a finite mind that can examine its own contents and causal dependencies.
- Addresses Caterus (1st Objections) on objective reality and causa sui; the 2nd Objections on whether the argument is self-undermining; Gassendi's critiques (5th).
