# Stagflation and Evolutionary Macroeconomics (Olson)
> Macroeconomic rigidities and sticky prices are the logical consequence of the slow decision-making of distributional coalitions, explaining stagflation and business cycles.

## Summary

In Chapter 7 of *The Rise and Decline of Nations* (1982), Mancur Olson presents an **evolutionary approach to macroeconomics** that seeks to provide choosing-rational micro-foundations for the key assumptions of Keynesian and monetarist macroeconomics.

### The "Hole" in Macroeconomic Theories

Olson critiques all major contemporary macroeconomic schools for being fatally incomplete:

* **Keynesianism**: Correctly identifies involuntary unemployment and relies on the assumption of **sticky wages and prices** (sluggish adjustment to market-clearing levels). However, it leaves this stickiness completely unexplained, treating it as an ad hoc empirical fact.
* **Monetarism / New Classical (Rational-Expectations)**: Assume that markets always clear and individuals make rational, optimal choices. Consequently, they are forced to deny the existence of involuntary unemployment, explaining recessions as voluntary decisions to work less (labor-leisure trade-offs) based on incorrect price expectations. Olson rejects this as absurd in the face of major depressions.

Olson aims to fill this "hole" by showing how rational microeconomic incentives naturally generate sticky prices and wages at the group level.

### The Cartel Foundation of Price Stickiness

[[Concepts/Distributional Coalitions (Olson)|Distributional coalitions]] (unions, cartels, professional lobbies) decide slowly because they rely on consensus or formal voting procedures. When aggregate demand changes, a cartel cannot adjust its prices or wages instantly.
* **Consensual Bargaining**: In small groups (like an oligopoly), members will hold out or block agreements to secure a better share of the cartel's gains, dragging out negotiations.
* **Constitutional Rules**: In larger groups (like labor unions), changing a wage scale requires formal meetings, votes, and contract expirations.

This slow coordination makes cartelized wages and prices highly rigid (**"sticky"**).

### Involuntary Unemployment

Olson defines **involuntary unemployment** strictly: a situation where a transaction between an unemployed worker and an employer would be mutually profitable (the worker's marginal cost of time is below the employer's marginal revenue product), but the transaction is **blocked**.

The block is created by coalitions of existing workers with the same or competitive skills. To protect their supracompetitive wages, unions or licensing boards bar entry. If the unemployed worker offers to work for a lower wage, the coalition uses pickets, laws, or social pressure to block the hire. Involuntary unemployment is thus the result of cartel enforcement.

### The Dynamics of Stagflation and Deflation

Olson explains stagflation and business cycles through this model:

1. **Unexpected Inflation**: If inflation is unexpected, it lowers the *real* value of cartel-fixed sticky prices and wages, bringing them closer to market-clearing levels. This reduces involuntary unemployment and stimulates output (the mechanism behind the short-run Phillips curve).
2. **Deflation / Demand Contraction**: In a stable, highly sclerotic society (with many cartels), a demand contraction is not met by rapid price cuts. Instead, rigid sectors ("fixprice" sectors) maintain their prices and wages, translating the demand drop into massive output and employment losses. 
3. **Involuntary Underemployment**: Resources displaced from the rigid "fixprice" sectors flood into the competitive "flexprice" sectors (like agriculture or casual labor), driving returns so low that workers experience "involuntary underemployment" (doing low-productivity work for survival wages).
4. **Stagflation**: As a society ages and accumulates more coalitions, its overall price responsiveness declines. In the 1970s, the accumulation of wage/price rigidities meant that stimulative monetary policy generated price increases (inflation) rather than output growth, while structural barriers maintained high unemployment — yielding **stagflation**.

### The Policy Implication

Olson's ultimate macroeconomic conclusion: **"the best macroeconomic policy is a good microeconomic policy."** Standard monetary and fiscal policies are blunt instruments. To eliminate stagflation and lower the natural rate of unemployment, a society must directly dismantle the microeconomic cartels, unions, and regulatory barriers that block market-clearing transactions.

## Key Claims / Positions

### How Different Thinkers Approach This

- **Milton Friedman**: Argued that inflation is "always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon" and co-formulated the "natural rate of unemployment." Olson agrees on the monetary transmission mechanism but shows that the "natural rate" is artificially high because of coalitional rigidities.
- **Robert Lucas (New Classical)**: Pioneered rational expectations, denying involuntary unemployment. Olson rejects this, arguing that Lucas's model fails to explain the interwar Great Depression.
- **Robert Clower / Axel Leijonhufvud**: Developed disequilibrium Keynesianism. Olson uses Clower's insight: if a non-market-clearing price exists in one sector, it propagates through general equilibrium, reducing incomes and aggregate demand throughout the economy.
- **Peter Temin & Anna Schwartz**: Compared U.S. deflations. Temin showed that real output grew during the 1839–43 deflation (when prices were highly flexible) but collapsed during the 1929–33 deflation (after rigidities had accumulated). Olson uses this to support his evolutionary sclerosis thesis.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Olson's view that unions are cartels causing involuntary unemployment is highly controversial in labor economics, where others argue that unions have positive "voice" effects that improve productivity.
- If the best macroeconomic policy is dismantling cartels, it requires a high degree of political power and consensus to overcome the lobbying resistance of the coalitions themselves.
