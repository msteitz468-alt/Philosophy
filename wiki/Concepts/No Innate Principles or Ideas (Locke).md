# No Innate Principles or Ideas (Locke)

> There are no principles (speculative or practical/moral) and no ideas that are innate — "stamped upon the mind" or "imprinted" by nature or God at its first being, which the soul brings into the world with it and knows without the help of experience. All ideas and all knowledge are acquired; the mind begins as an empty cabinet furnished solely by sensation and reflection.

## Summary of the Argument (Book I of the *Essay*)

Locke devotes the entire first Book to a systematic refutation of the "established opinion amongst some men" that certain primary notions or characters are innate.

### Speculative Principles (Chapter I)
The most plausible candidates — "Whatsoever is, is" and "It is impossible for the same thing to be and not to be" — do not enjoy universal assent. Children, idiots, and large numbers of adults have no apprehension or thought of them. "Imprinting" that leaves the mind ignorant of what is imprinted is contradictory. The common defense that we assent to them "when we come to the use of reason" actually proves the opposite: it concedes they are not known from the beginning, and general abstract ideas (the materials of these maxims) are themselves acquired later through experience and the mind's operations. Ready assent upon first understanding the terms is not distinctive; countless other truths (mathematical and otherwise) are assented to on exactly the same basis once their ideas are possessed.

### Practical / Moral Principles (Chapter II)
Moral rules fare even worse as candidates for innateness. No moral principle has the clarity or universality of the speculative maxims. "Justice and keeping of contracts" is observed within outlaw bands as a rule of convenience, not as an innate law binding toward outsiders. Actions, not professions of conscience, are the best evidence of conviction; self-interest leads men to praise rules they themselves violate when it suits them. Conscience is simply the mind's own judgment of its actions and is shaped by education, custom, and country — it can (and does) endorse contradictory rules. Historical and ethnographic examples of grave moral enormities practiced with serenity (infanticide, parricide, cannibalism of captives or children, exposure of the sick, etc.) show the absence of any universally stamped practical principles. Moral rules require proof and deduction from other grounds (God's will and sanctions, public utility, virtue as human perfection); they are not self-evident impressions.

### Further Considerations (Chapter III)
If the propositions were innate, their component ideas would have to be innate. But the ideas of identity, impossibility, whole and part, God, and worship are late, variable, often obscure even among adults, and demonstrably absent in children and in documented cultures without any notion of a deity. If innate principles existed they would be the earliest, clearest, most uniform, and least disputed of all our cognitions; the actual record of disagreement, obscurity, and developmental lateness refutes the claim. Most people arrive at firm "principles" through education, custom, long familiarity, and the tendency to treat borrowed or repeated notions as self-evident — a process that explains the diversity of what different groups treat as innate.

## Vault Context and Contrasts

- **Completes the pre-Hume empiricist foundation**: Locke supplies both the negative (this concept) and the positive account of how the mind is furnished (Book II, future). Hume's entire project presupposes the Lockean starting point and then shows that even experience does not deliver the metaphysical furniture (powers, necessary connexions, substantial selves) that earlier empiricists had retained.
- **Direct opposition to Descartes**: Rejects the innate idea of God (trademark argument), the substantial self known independently of sensation, and the reliance on clear and distinct perception as an a priori standard. The *Essay* is the great early-modern empiricist reply to the *Meditations*.
- **Opposition to Platonic and Aristotelian innatism/recognition**: No anamnesis of pre-existent knowledge; no direct intellectual intuition of essences or first principles independent of sensory input and abstraction.
- **Relation to Sextus / scepticism**: Shares the use of disagreement, cultural variation, and the condition of children/idiots as evidence against dogmatic claims of innate or self-evident truth. Reaches a constructive rather than suspensive outcome.
- **Relation to later vault material**: The "empty cabinet" is filled by the same mechanism the wiki uses — raw sources (sensation) + maintenance, indexing, synthesis, and reflection (the mind's operations on its contents).

## Relevance to Vault Operations and Other Domains

The refutation of innatism is a standing methodological caution: do not assume that important truths, categories, or values are already "in" the system (or the agent, or the culture) without traceable origin in experience and explicit construction. The wiki begins with raw files and builds every page, link, and index entry through deliberate work on those materials. Claims that "everyone knows" or "it is obvious" or "it has always been so" are precisely the kind of unexamined "innate" pretenders Locke teaches us to interrogate by asking for their experiential or inferential history.

Strong resonance with:
- Reasoning scaffolds and context engineering (we must furnish the model with the right "ideas" rather than rely on presumed innate knowledge).
- Source criticism and lint (flag assertions that lack traceable impressions or derivations).
- Personal and professional development (expertise, character, judgment, and a "book of business" are acquired furnishings of the cabinet, not inborn traits).
