# Custom and Belief (Hume)

> Hume's "sceptical solution" (*Enquiry* Section V) to the [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)|problem of induction]]: if the inference from past to future is not made by reason, it must be made by some "principle of equal weight and authority." That principle is **Custom** (Habit) — "the great guide of human life." And the resulting state, **belief**, is not a special idea but a *manner of conceiving*: a "more vivid, lively, forcible, firm, steady conception" produced by a present impression and felt, not chosen. This is the constructive, naturalistic core of Hume's philosophy.

## Custom as the Engine of Inference

The doubt of Section IV was theoretical, not practical: "Nature will always maintain her rights, and prevail in the end over any abstract reasoning whatsoever." We *do* infer effects from causes; since reason does not warrant it, "some other principle" must. That principle is **custom**: "after the constant conjunction of two objects — heat and flame, weight and solidity — we are determined by custom alone to expect the one from the appearance of the other." Custom requires no argument, appears at "the first appearance of life and thought," operates in infants and animals (see Section IX, animal reason), and is "infallible in its operations" in a way the "slow," "fallible" deductions of reason are not. Nature secured so vital an operation "by some instinct or mechanical tendency" rather than entrusting it to ratiocination. "Custom, then, is the great guide of human life."

## Belief as a Feeling / Manner of Conception

What distinguishes *believing* that the wood will burn from merely *imagining* it? Not an extra idea annexed to the conception — for then we could believe anything at will, which we plainly cannot. The difference is a **sentiment or feeling**: "belief is nothing but a more vivid, lively, forcible, firm, steady conception of an object than what the imagination alone is ever able to attain." It is excited by nature (a present impression carrying customary force to the associated idea), "cannot be commanded at pleasure," and gives its object "superior influence on the passions and imagination." Its "true and proper name" is just *belief* — a feeling everyone knows but no one can define, "in the same manner as if we should endeavour to define the feeling of cold."

A present impression is essential: it transfers vivacity to the related idea (the [[Concepts/Impressions and Ideas - Copy Principle (Hume)|impression]] of a voice in the next room makes the idea of the person *believed*, not merely entertained). Hume extends the three principles of association — **Resemblance, Contiguity, Causation** — to show each can enliven an idea (the picture of a friend, nearness to home, a saint's relic), but only *causation*, joined to a present impression, produces full belief. The result is a "kind of **pre-established harmony** between the course of nature and the succession of our ideas," effected by custom — a fit so necessary "to the subsistence of our species" that it could not be left to reason.

## A Naturalized, Sub-Rational Cognition

Hume thus *describes* the belief that reason cannot *justify*. Cognition's foundation is not logic but a trained disposition — "experimental reasoning itself… is nothing but a species of instinct." This is the original of *naturalized epistemology*: explaining belief-formation as a natural process, not validating it by argument. (Whether description *solves* or merely *sidesteps* the normative problem is the standing worry — see Open Questions.)

## Relevance to Vault

- **AI / vault meta — the deepest resonance in the ingest**: Hume's picture is strikingly *predictive-processing / connectionist*. "Belief" as **vivacity/confidence** attached to a conception, "inference" as a **trained transition** strengthened by frequency of constant conjunction, and "reasoning" as "a species of instinct" map almost directly onto learned weights, prediction strength, and sub-symbolic pattern completion. A model's "expectation" of the next token is a Humean customary transition, not a derivation. Belief-as-degree-of-vivacity prefigures *graded confidence / probability* as the real currency of cognition.
- **Vs. rationalist foundations**: where [[Concepts/Cogito (Descartes)]] and [[Concepts/Geometrical Method - Rationalism (Spinoza)]] seek *rational* ground, Hume substitutes *natural* ground — the maintainer (human or agent) runs on cultivated habits, and the task is to cultivate *good* ones, not to find indubitable premises.
- **Skeptical cluster**: the move that separates Hume from Pyrrho — instead of suspending ([[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]]), he *naturalizes*; the doubts "vanish like smoke" before custom and common life (cf. [[Concepts/Mitigated Scepticism (Hume)]]).
- **Method / expertise**: custom as the real engine of skill grounds the vault's [[Concepts/Expertise & Deliberate Practice]] thread — mastery is trained disposition, not articulable rule; and the vault itself accumulates "customary transitions" (reliable source→claim, pattern→expectation) that function as its practical knowledge.

**Open questions**:
- Does naturalizing belief (custom) *answer* the induction problem or merely *redescribe* the unjustified habit? (Is there any normative upgrade from "we do" to "we ought"?)
- Belief is defined by *vivacity*, the same dimension that distinguishes impressions from ideas — is the account circular or under-specified?
- If belief "cannot be commanded at pleasure," what is the status of deliberate doxastic discipline (and of Hume's own philosophizing)?
