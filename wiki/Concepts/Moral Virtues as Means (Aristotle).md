# Moral Virtues as Means (Aristotle)

> Books III–IV apply the doctrine of the mean to the particular virtues of character: courage (fear/confidence, defined by facing noble death), temperance (bodily touch-pleasures), liberality and magnificence (giving/spending money, at ordinary and grand scale), magnanimity/greatness of soul (claiming and deserving great honor, "the crown of the virtues"), good temper (anger), and the social virtues of friendliness, truthfulness, and wit.

## Summary

Courage (*andreia*) is the mean regarding fear and confidence, but Aristotle narrows its true domain sharply: genuine courage concerns "the greatest and noblest danger," paradigmatically death faced in battle for the sake of nobility, not merely to escape a worse pain. He catalogues five counterfeits that resemble but are not courage — the citizen-soldier's political courage (motivated by shame/legal penalty), the experienced soldier's skill-based confidence, passionate/spirited boldness, sanguine over-confidence from past success, and simple ignorance of danger — each lacking the true motive of nobility. Temperance (*sophrosynē*) is unusual among the virtues in that its domain is restricted to a single pair of senses — touch and taste, "the most slavish and brutish" senses shared with other animals — with the temperate person desiring health-promoting bodily pleasures "moderately and as he should," neither craving excess nor showing insensibility. Liberality (*eleutheriotēs*), governing the giving and taking of money, is judged relative to the giver's means and character rather than the absolute sum given — a poor man's modest gift may be more liberal than a rich man's larger one — with prodigality (excess) judged less bad than meanness/illiberality (deficiency) because it at least retains a generous disposition. Magnificence (*megaloprepeia*) is liberality operating at a grand scale requiring aesthetic judgment ("like an artist," knowing what expenditure is fitting for temples, public choruses, embassies) and typically presupposes inherited wealth or high station. Magnanimity/greatness of soul (*megalopsuchia*) — "the crown of the virtues," impossible without the others — is the mean regarding claims to honor: the great-souled man claims and deserves the greatest external good (honor) on a grand scale, is indifferent to lesser honors and to wealth/power for their own sake, remembers benefits given rather than received, and refuses to make his life "revolve round another," since that would be slavish. Good temper (*praotēs*), the mean regarding anger, is unusual in that Aristotle notes the good-tempered person tends to err toward deficiency (excessive placidity) rather than excess, since the culturally salient failure is people "not angry at the things they should be angry at." Book IV closes with brief, less fully theorized treatments of friendliness, truthfulness, and wit (*eutrapelia*) in social intercourse, and dismisses shame (*aidōs*) as not a true virtue at all, since it is "more like a feeling than a state of character" — appropriate to the young who are still liable to err, but not praiseworthy as a permanent disposition in the mature.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Courage requires the motive of nobility, not merely facing danger**: the five counterfeits (political/citizen courage, experience, passion, sanguineness, ignorance) look like courage behaviorally but fail because they lack — or substitute a lesser motive for — facing death "for the sake of nobility."
- **Temperance is exceptional among the virtues in its restricted domain**: it governs only the bodily pleasures of touch and taste, judged the "slavish and brutish" senses because shared with lower animals, unlike the more cognitively mediated pleasures of sight/hearing.
- **Liberality is a function of character relative to means, not absolute sums**: virtue lies "in the state of character of the giver," so wealth alone does not make one more or less liberal.
- **Prodigality (excess) is closer to virtue than meanness (deficiency)**: excess in giving retains the generous impulse that deficiency lacks entirely — an asymmetry Aristotle notes is not uniform across all the virtue-pairs.
- **Magnificence requires taste as well as means**: fitting large-scale expenditure (distinguishing appropriate grandeur from vulgar showiness) is itself an achievement of judgment, analogous to artistic skill.
- **Magnanimity is "the crown of the virtues... not found without them"**: because claiming great honor while actually deserving it presupposes the possession of the other virtues that make one worthy, greatness of soul cannot exist as an isolated trait.
- **Good temper is the one virtue Aristotle explicitly flags as erring toward deficiency in ordinary blame-practice**: people are more often faulted for insufficient anger at real wrongs than for excessive anger, inverting the usual "excess is the more common vice" expectation.
- **Shame is explicitly denied full virtue-status**: it is a passion (a feeling of the young, tied to fear of disgrace) rather than a stable *hexis*, so it cannot be praised as an ideal permanent character trait the way courage or temperance can.

## How Aristotle Uses This

Directly instantiates the abstract structure of [[Concepts/Doctrine of the Mean (Aristotle)]] across the concrete domain of character, and presupposes the account of voluntary choice in [[Concepts/Voluntary Action and Choice (Aristotle)]] (virtue is a state "concerned with choice"). The magnanimous man's self-sufficiency and refusal of dependency on others anticipates, and is later qualified by, the treatment of friendship in Books VIII–IX (see [[Concepts/Friendship - Philia (Aristotle)]]), where even the most self-sufficient person is shown to need virtuous friends.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- None flagged against existing wiki pages. The magnanimous man's disdain for depending on others sits in productive tension (not yet a formal Contradiction) with Book IX's insistence that even the happy, self-sufficient person needs friends — the text itself seems aware of and partially resolves this tension by making friendship a relation between self-sufficient equals rather than a form of dependency.
