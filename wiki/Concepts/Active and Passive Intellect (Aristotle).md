# Active and Passive Intellect (Aristotle)

> Thought (*nous*) receives intelligible form as sense receives sensible form, but must itself be unmixed and impassible to be capable of becoming all things. Aristotle then distinguishes a passive/potential intellect that "becomes all things" (destructible) from an active/productive intellect that "makes all things" — separable, impassible, unmixed, and "alone immortal and eternal." Desire (*orexis*), not thought alone, is what actually produces animal motion.

## Summary

*De Anima* Book III, chs. 4–5 extends the perception model to thought: "mind must be related to what is thinkable, as sense is to what is sensible." But mind must be different in kind from any bodily sense-organ, since an organ with a determinate nature could not receive *all* forms — Aristotle calls mind "the place of forms," but only potentially, not actually, prior to actual thinking, and notes (unlike sense, which is impaired by an excessively intense object) that greater intelligible objects do not damage the mind's later functioning. This sets up the treatise's most famous and most contested passage (ch. 5): mind, as so far described, "is what it is by virtue of becoming all things" — the passive or potential intellect, which is destructible ("without it nothing thinks"). But "there is another which is what it is by virtue of making all things: this is a sort of positive state like light" — the active or productive intellect (*nous poietikos*), which is "separable, impassible, unmixed, since it is in its essential nature activity." Aristotle concludes starkly: "when mind is set free from its present conditions it appears as just what it is and nothing more: this alone is immortal and eternal... mind as passive is destructible." He offers almost no further elaboration, leaving the relationship between the two intellects — whether one, many, personal, or shared across all thinkers — one of the most debated cruxes in the history of philosophy. Chapters 7–8 add that "the soul never thinks without an image" (*phantasm*): even abstract thought depends on sensible forms supplied by imagination, tying intellection back to the perceptual/imaginative apparatus of chs. 1–3. Chapters 9–11 close the work's psychology of action: desire (*orexis*, encompassing wish, appetite, and passion) — not mind or perception alone — is what actually produces locomotion; "that which moves therefore is a single faculty... the faculty of appetite," since mind never moves anything without appetite, while appetite can move an animal contrary to its own calculation (the phenomenon of weakness of will). The extant text breaks off abruptly mid-sentence in ch. 13, on the senses' contribution to well-being — the treatise as transmitted is textually incomplete.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Thought is structurally parallel to perception (reception of intelligible form) but must be unmixed/impassible, unlike any bodily sense organ, to be capable of universal receptivity.
- The passive intellect (which "becomes all things") is destructible, tied to the individual thinker and their images; the active intellect (which "makes all things") is separable, impassible, and alone declared immortal and eternal.
- Aristotle gives almost no criteria for individuating the active intellect — is it one for all humanity, or particular to each soul? — leaving this the central unresolved crux of the work.
- All thought, however abstract, depends on accompanying mental images (*phantasms*) supplied by imagination — there is no pure, image-free intellection.
- Desire, not intellect or perception in isolation, is the actual efficient cause of animal self-movement; practical reason moves an animal only via its alliance with appetite.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **The central interpretive battleground of the Aristotelian tradition**: Alexander of Aphrodisias read the active intellect as identical with God (a wholly external divine agency); Averroes (Ibn Rushd) held the radical "unicity of the intellect" thesis — a single, shared active (and even passive) intellect for all humanity, which Aquinas explicitly and vehemently refutes (see [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]], [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]) because it would destroy personal immortality, individual moral responsibility, and the plain fact that "Socrates understands" as an individual.
- **Vs. [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]**: Aquinas argues each individual human soul has its own active and passive intellect, both belonging to a single subsistent, individually immortal rational soul — his solution to the puzzle Aristotle leaves open, motivated by the demands of personal immortality and moral responsibility that Aristotle's text does not itself settle.
- **Feeds [[Concepts/Soul as Form of the Body (Hylomorphism, Aristotle)]]**: this passage is the textual root of the tension flagged there — nearly the whole soul is inseparable from body, but intellect (specifically the active intellect) is explicitly declared separable and immortal, an exception Aristotle raises but does not fully reconcile with his general hylomorphism.
- **Downstream — moral psychology**: the desire-as-mover-of-action doctrine, with mind powerless to move without appetite and appetite capable of overriding calculation, is Aristotle's own resource for explaining *akrasia* (weakness of will), a topic developed further in the *Nicomachean Ethics*.
