# Teachability of Virtue (Plato, Protagoras & Meno)

> Central Socratic/Platonic question: Can *arete* (virtue/excellence) be taught? In the *Protagoras*, Socrates debates the sophist Protagoras, who claims civic virtue can be taught through socialization, myth, and rhetoric. Socrates pushes for a unified definition of virtue and explores whether its parts (wisdom, justice, temperance, courage, holiness) are distinct or one. Introduces intellectualism: all wrongdoing is ignorance; virtue is a kind of knowledge or measurement (*episteme*). Paradoxically, this makes virtue teachable. The *Meno* introduces the theory of recollection (*anamnesis*): the soul knows and can "recollect" truths; virtue may be taught via this inner process or as true opinion "tied down" by reasoning (hypothesis method). Explores whether virtue is knowledge, true belief, or divine gift.

## Key Elements from Sources
- **Protagoras**: Sophist claims virtue is teachable via conventional education. Socrates demands definition; questions if virtues are many or one. Examines hedonism and *akrasia* (moral weakness); concludes virtue is knowledge of measurement – hence teachable in principle, though practice shows difficulties.
- **Meno**: "Can virtue be taught?" Leads to recollection (soul immortal, has seen truths before birth), hypothesis method for inquiry, and the conclusion that if virtue is knowledge it is teachable, but if only true opinion it may come by divine dispensation (like statesmen who succeed without full understanding).
- **Related dialogues**: Links to piety (Euthyphro), courage (Laches), definition and elenchus across the set.
