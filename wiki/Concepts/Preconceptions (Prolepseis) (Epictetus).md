# Preconceptions (Prolepseis) (Epictetus)

> The innate general notions of "good," "evil," "just," "fitting" that all people share — never wrong in the abstract, but the source of all conflict when misapplied to particular cases.

## Summary

*Prolēpsis* (preconception) is Epictetus's answer to where philosophy begins. Everyone possesses natural, pre-articulate general concepts — good is beneficial and to be pursued, evil is harmful and to be shunned — and no one disputes these in the abstract. Conflict arises only in the *application* of the preconception to particulars: all agree the good is to be pursued, but one man locates it in wealth, another in pleasure, another in virtue.

> "The beginning of philosophy... is the being sensible of the disagreement of men with each other." (Discourses II.11)

Philosophy proper begins when one notices this clash and seeks a *criterion* — a reliable standard, "like a balance or a square" — to adjudicate which application of the shared preconception is correct, rather than resting in mere seeming (*to dokoun*). Preconceptions thus function as the raw data of ethics; the philosopher's task is their correct articulation and fitting to cases.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Preconceptions are common and correct; misapplication is the disease.** "The preconceptions are common to all men, and one does not contradict another... the whole difficulty lies in adapting them to particulars" (II.11).
- **Disagreement is the spur to philosophy.** Recognizing that people who agree on the general clash violently on the particular reveals that "seeming" is not enough and a measure must be found.
- **A criterion is required.** Just as the balance settles disputes about weight, ethics needs a standard for fitting preconceptions to instances — for the Stoic, ultimately the dichotomy of the *prohairetic* (up to us) vs. the non-prohairetic.
- **Self-conceit blocks the work.** The obstacle is the presumption of already knowing (II.17); learning requires first feeling the want of a standard.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Epictetus / Stoics**: prolepsis is a naturally-given criterion, part of the Stoic account of how the rational cosmos furnishes mind with true common notions (*koinai ennoiai*).
- **[[Thinkers/Plato]]**: structural echo of recollection — we possess concepts prior to instruction — but for Plato the concepts point to transcendent Forms, whereas for the Stoic they are immanent, cosmically-seeded notions. Compare [[Concepts/Teachability of Virtue (Plato Protagoras Meno)]].
- **[[Thinkers/Kant]]**: another cousin — universal a priori structures the mind brings to experience — though Kant's categories are formal conditions of cognition, not moral contents; see [[Concepts/Categorical Imperative - Universal Law Formulation (Kant)]].
- **[[Thinkers/Sextus Empiricus]]**: the Skeptic weaponizes exactly Epictetus's observation (universal disagreement) not toward a criterion but toward suspension — the same datum, opposite conclusion. See [[Concepts/Equipollence (Isosthenia) and the Modes]].
