# Qualia Disqualified (Dennett)

> Dennett's sustained argument in Ch. 12 of *Consciousness Explained* (1991) that qualia — traditionally understood as the intrinsic, ineffable, private, directly apprehensible properties of conscious experience — do not exist as a philosophically distinct category. They are either reducible to reactive dispositions (the "lovely" secondary-quality account) or incoherent postulations (the "suspect" intrinsic-property account). The chapter is the philosophical core of the book.

## What Qualia Are Supposed to Be

The traditional concept of qualia (as assumed by Nagel, Jackson, Chalmers, and much of folk psychology) holds that phenomenal properties of experience have four features:
1. **Intrinsic**: they are what they are independently of any relational or dispositional properties.
2. **Ineffable**: they cannot be fully captured in functional or physical description.
3. **Private**: only the subject has direct access to them.
4. **Directly apprehensible**: the subject knows them infallibly in a way not available to third-person inquiry.

Dennett attacks all four features by showing that either (a) nothing satisfies them or (b) what does satisfy them turns out not to be the "mysterious residue" that requires special explanation.

## Colors Coevolved with Color Vision

Dennett's positive account: **colors are secondary qualities** — dispositional, ecological, observer-relative properties shaped by evolutionary history. Colors and color vision were "made for each other" simultaneously; there is no independent color-reality prior to observers against which the visual system could be assessed as accurate or inaccurate.

Analogy (the Rosenbergs' Jell-O box): Imagine shape property M (a complex mold shape) and its detector were created simultaneously and made for each other. The question "Is M really what the detector detects?" has no traction — there is no independent M prior to the detector's existence.

Colors are "lovely" (dispositional, observer-relative, ecologically grounded) not "suspect" (independent intrinsic properties for which we need a special phenomenal story). The dispositional account of secondary qualities explains everything the qualia theorist wants to explain without the mysterious residue.

## Mary the Color Scientist (Frank Jackson's Argument)

Jackson's thought experiment (1982): Mary is raised in a black-and-white room and learns all physical facts about color vision — neuroscience, physics, everything. When she first sees red, does she learn something new?

Jackson's claim: yes — she learns what it's like to see red; this "what it's like" cannot be captured in physical/functional terms; therefore physicalism is false.

**Dennett's reply**: If Mary *really* has all physical information, she already knows all the reactive dispositions — what red looks like, how people react to it, how it will strike her when she first sees it. The intuition that she learns something new arises from imagining her with *less* than all the information.

**Dennett's continuation**: Suppose you show Mary a blue banana (instead of a red apple). She says: "I knew you'd try that trick. That's blue, not red. I know what you're doing." She doesn't exclaim "So *that's* what red looks like!" because she can see immediately that it isn't red. If she truly has all physical information, she is not naively surprised by phenomenal colors — she sees through all the demonstrations.

The apparent force of the Mary argument comes from implicitly imagining Mary as knowing all the physical facts *except* the phenomenal ones — but that is precisely the question-begging assumption under attack.

## Inverted Qualia (Spectrum Inversion)

The inverted-qualia thought experiment: suppose your color experience is "inverted" relative to mine — what looks red to me looks green to you, but we've both learned to call ripe tomatoes "red." Since our behavioral dispositions are identical, no functional test can detect the difference. So there are facts about qualia that escape any functional description.

**Dennett's reply**: This thought experiment only has traction if one assumes a Cartesian Theater — a place where the "raw looks" are "shown" independently of any functional role. Without the Cartesian Theater, there is no coherent story to tell. What would it mean for X to look red to you if it plays *all* the functional roles that red-looking plays in a normal person? Nothing has been "inverted" — only the label "inversion" has been applied to a hypothetical that cannot be coherently filled in.

The inverted qualia thought experiment secretly smuggles in a viewer of the inner show. Once you abandon the Cartesian Theater, the coherence of the thought experiment dissolves.

## Epiphenomenal Qualia

There are two senses:
1. **Huxley's sense (weak)**: Mental events are nonfunctional by-products of neural events (like the steam from a locomotive — the steam doesn't drive the wheels). This is trivially physicalist and uninteresting: it concedes that qualia are fully determined by physical states, just not causally efficacious.
2. **Philosophical sense (strong)**: Qualia are properties of experience that are in principle undetectable by any instrument or indeed by the subject's own introspective mechanism — they exist "beyond" the functional level entirely.

**Dennett's reply to the strong sense**: Epiphenomenal qualia (philosophical sense) are **incoherent** — the parity argument. If qualia are undetectable by any instrument, including the brain's own introspective machinery, then the subject's very *claim* to have qualia cannot be caused by qualia (since qualia have no causal powers). But then why does the subject report them? The report is caused entirely by functional/physical states, and the qualia are causally idle. The gremlins-in-engine-cylinders analogy: if the gremlins are stipulated to have no causal powers whatsoever (they don't affect the engine), what explains the fact that we say "there are gremlins"? The word "gremlin" was introduced by the physical engine states. The qualia are explanatorily inert and evidentially ungrounded.

"**We're all zombies**" — not in the sense that no one is conscious, but in the sense that no one is conscious in the systematically mysterious way that would require epiphenomenal qualia. The light is off in the Cartesian Theater because there is no theater.

## The "Lovely" vs. "Suspect" Distinction

Dennett borrows from the philosophy of secondary qualities:
- **Lovely**: secondary qualities (colors, tastes, sounds as perceived) are dispositional, observer-relative, ecologically grounded. They are real, informative, and important — but they do not presuppose a framework of intrinsic phenomenal properties. Lovely secondary qualities cause no explanatory problems.
- **Suspect**: the "qualia" posited by the inverted-spectrum and Mary thought experiments — intrinsic, ineffable properties that are what they are independently of any functional role. These are suspect because no coherent account of them can be given without a Cartesian Theater.

The move in Ch. 12 is to show that colors (and other secondary qualities) are lovely, not suspect. Once this is established, the apparent residue that qualia theorists are pointing to simply evaporates.
