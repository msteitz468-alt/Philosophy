# Strong Artificial Intelligence (Searle)

> John Searle's term (contrasted with 'weak AI') for the view that an appropriately programmed digital computer literally has a mind — that the mind *is* a computer program. The claim is not metaphorical: the right program with the right inputs and outputs is sufficient for, and constitutive of, having thoughts, feelings, understanding, and intentionality. Searle refutes this with the Chinese Room argument (see related concept): syntax is not sufficient for semantics.

## Summary

Searle distinguishes two senses in which people speak of AI:

- **Weak AI**: Computers are powerful tools for simulating or modeling mental processes. Useful for psychology, neuroscience, etc. Uncontroversial.

- **Strong AI**: The appropriately programmed computer *literally* has a mind in the same sense that human beings do. 'The mind is to the brain as the program is to the computer hardware.' Brains happen to be one kind of hardware that can run the programs that constitute intelligence; any physical system (old beer cans powered by windmills, if suitably arranged) that runs the right program with the right inputs/outputs would have genuine mental states.

This view has radical consequences: nothing essentially biological about the mind; consciousness is just formal symbol manipulation; we will (or already do, on some claims) have machines that think and feel in exactly the same literal sense we do.

Searle attributes versions of this view to figures such as Herbert Simon ('we already have machines that can literally think'), Alan Newell (intelligence is just physical symbol manipulation with no essential connection to biology), Marvin Minsky (future computers will be so intelligent we'll be lucky if they keep us as pets), and John McCarthy (even thermostats can be said to have beliefs).

The Chinese Room (see dedicated concept) is designed as a simple, decisive refutation that does not depend on the current state of technology. Because digital computers are defined by their ability to implement formal, syntactical programs, and because minds require semantics (content, meaning, intentionality) in addition to syntax, no computer can have a mind merely by running the right program.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Strong AI makes a literal, ontological claim about the nature of mind, not a metaphorical or heuristic one.
- The claim is independent of hardware: any system that instantiates the right program has a mind.
- This implies that consciousness and intentionality are purely formal properties that can be realized in any medium capable of symbol manipulation.
- Searle rejects this as 'quite crazy from the start' and provides the Chinese Room as the refutation.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] The central target of Searle's 1984 lectures and the 1980 Chinese Room paper. Directly contradicts the strong computationalist interpretation of Turing (the brain as digital computer, mind as program) that grew out of the Imitation Game and early AI. See [[Thinkers/Alan Turing]], [[Concepts/Imitation Game (Turing)]], [[Concepts/Universal Machine (Turing)]], [[Concepts/Learning Machines (Turing)]], and the Computationalism school tags. Also challenges literal readings of information-processing and symbol-manipulation models in cognitive science (see Cognitive Science critique and Syntax/Semantics concepts).
- Creates tension with functionalism and multiple realizability taken to the extreme (anything with the right program, no matter how bizarre the hardware, has a mind).
- Searle contrasts it with his own biological naturalism: minds are biological phenomena caused by and realized in brains; the specific causal powers of the brain matter, not just abstract formal organization.
- Open: Weak AI remains valuable. The argument does not rule out future machines that duplicate brains at the biological level (molecule-for-molecule); it rules out duplication *solely* via program implementation.
