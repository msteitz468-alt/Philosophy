# Jurisdictional Integration and Sclerosis (Olson)
> Free trade, common markets, and political integration temporarily dismantle local cartels and guilds by expanding market boundaries and shifting policy decisions to a larger scale.

## Summary

In *The Rise and Decline of Nations* (1982), Mancur Olson presents a theory of **jurisdictional integration** to explain how countries can achieve rapid economic growth and bypass the retardants of [[Concepts/Institutional Sclerosis (Olson)|institutional sclerosis]] without requiring violent shocks.

### The Limits of Static Free-Trade Theory

Conventional economics calculates the gains from free trade or customs unions (like the European Economic Community) using static models of **comparative advantage** (developed by David Ricardo). Meticulous empirical studies by economists like Edwin Truman, Mordechai Kreinen, and Bela Balassa consistently showed that these static gains are tiny — typically adding 1% or 2% or less to Gross National Product.

Olson argues these static calculations miss the most important effect of integration. The primary benefit of jurisdictional integration is not the direct reallocation of goods according to comparative advantage, but rather the **dismantling of domestic cartels and lobbies**.

### The Mechanism of Integration

```mermaid
graph LR
    A["Jurisdictional Integration"] --> B["Wider Market Boundaries"]
    A --> C["Shift Locus of Decision-Making"]
    B --> D["Local Cartels Face Foreign Competition"]
    C --> E["National Lobbies Lose Coordinate Lobbying Power"]
    D & E --> F["Dismantled Domestic Monopolies & Lobbies"]
    F --> G["Rapid Economic Growth & Dynamism"]
```

Jurisdictional integration involves:
1. **Expanding Market Boundaries**: Allowing free movement of goods, labor, capital, and firms across a wider geographic area.
2. **Shifting the Locus of Decision-Making**: Moving policy decisions (like tariffs, standards, and licensing) from local or national capitals to a larger, centralized authority (e.g., from national capitals to Brussels in the EEC, or from German principalities to the Zollverein).

When these occur, domestic cartels face a double blow:
* **Market Exposure**: Local price-fixing rings and guilds are suddenly exposed to competition from foreign producers who are not members of the local collusion and cannot be easily policed or coerced.
* **Lobbying Dilution**: Small, local special-interest groups lose their coordinate lobbying power. It is far more difficult and costly for a narrow local lobby to coordinate and influence a large, multi-jurisdictional centralized government than to influence a small, local principal or municipality.

Consequently, **jurisdictional integration temporarily sweeps away the entry barriers and pricing rigidities of institutional sclerosis, stimulating rapid growth.**

### Historical Proofs

Olson traces several major historical episodes of jurisdictional integration:

* **The European Common Market (EEC)**: The rapid growth of the Six (France, West Germany, Italy, Benelux) in the 1950s and 60s was accelerated because integration undermined national price-fixing cartels and forced firms to compete.
* **The Zollverein and German Unification (19th century)**: Germany went from a backward area of tiny, guild-ridden principalities to a industrial powerhouse after the customs union (1834) and political unification (1871) expanded market boundaries and abolished local tolls.
* **The United States Constitution (1787)**: The creation of a single nationwide free-trade area out of thirteen protectionist ex-colonies abolished local tariffs (e.g., New York's duties against New Jersey) and set up the conditions for rapid US industrialization.
* **Nation-State Formation in Early Modern Europe**: The centralizing monarchs of England and France systematically eliminated local municipal and feudal tolls. In Britain, this national market integration happened much faster and more completely than in France, explaining why Britain developed the Industrial Revolution first.

## Key Claims / Positions

### How Different Thinkers Approach This

- **[[Thinkers/Adam Smith]]**: In *The Wealth of Nations*, Smith famously noted that "the division of labour is limited by the extent of the market." Olson builds on this, showing that expanding the market not only increases division of labor but also structurally undermines the merchant-lobby conspiracies (mercantilism) that Smith spent his career criticizing.
- **David Ricardo**: Formulated the theory of comparative advantage. Olson does not reject Ricardo's static gains but argues they are minor compared to the dynamic gains of cartel-dismantling that integration brings.
- **Fernand Braudel**: Documented that the Industrial Revolution was driven by small, provincial towns (like Manchester and Leeds) rather than the old, guild-entrenched capital cities. Olson uses Braudel's history to support his thesis that economic growth bypasses old capitals because they are centers of institutional sclerosis.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Jurisdictional integration is not a permanent cure. Over time, interest groups will organize at the new, larger scale (e.g., Euro-wide lobbies in Brussels). Once these continental coalitions form, the sclerosis will return at a larger scale.
- Free trade alone is not a panacea; it must be combined with proactive policies to prevent mergers, cartels, and new regulatory entry barriers from emerging at the integrated level.
