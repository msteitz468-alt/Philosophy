# Geist (Absolute Spirit)

> G.W.F. Hegel's central metaphysical concept, translated variously as **Spirit** or **Mind**. It represents the active, rational substance of the universe that develops through history, unfolding from unconscious nature into human self-consciousness, and ultimately achieving complete self-knowledge and freedom.

## The Three Stages of Spirit

Hegel traces the development of Geist through three major phases in his *Encyclopaedia of the Philosophical Sciences* (particularly in his *Philosophy of Mind*):

1.  **Subjective Spirit (*Subjektiver Geist*)**:
    *   Spirit in its immediate, individual form. It covers the human mind, consciousness, psychology, and individual cognitive/emotional faculties. It progresses from basic sensation (anthropology) through self-consciousness (phenomenology) to rational will (psychology).
2.  **Objective Spirit (*Objektiver Geist*)**:
    *   Spirit externalized into the social, political, and institutional world. It represents the shared customs, laws, and ethical systems that govern human communities. It develops through:
        *   *Abstract Right* (property, contract, punishment)
        *   *Morality* (individual conscience and subjective duty)
        *   *Sittlichkeit* or Ethical Life (the concrete realization of freedom in the Family, Civil Society, and the State).
3.  **Absolute Spirit (*Absoluter Geist*)**:
    *   The highest realization of Geist, where it frees itself from institutional limitations and directly contemplates its own eternal, rational nature. It achieves this self-knowledge through three cultural media:
        *   *Art* (sensuous intuition of the Absolute)
        *   *Religion* (representational or pictorial thought of the Absolute, e.g., the Incarnation representing the unity of divine and human)
        *   *Philosophy* (pure conceptual comprehension of the Absolute, where Spirit finally knows itself as the truth of all reality).

## Key Characteristics of Geist

-   **Social and Intersubjective**: Geist is not an individual, isolated mind. It is a collective, historical reality—an intersubjective network of shared language, culture, and institutions. As Hegel puts it in the *Phenomenology*, Geist is "I that is We, and We that is I."
-   **Historically Unfolding**: Geist is not static; it exists only in its activity and historical evolution. It must wander through history, experiencing division, alienation, and conflict, before it can return to itself in unified self-consciousness.
-   **Freedom as the Telos**: The goal of Geist is the realization of absolute freedom—a state where it recognizes everything in the universe as its own rational product, thereby becoming entirely self-determined and "at home" in the world.

## In the Vault

-   **Aristotelian Nous**: Geist is a historical, dynamic extension of Aristotle's (*Nous*), the divine intellect that is active thinking. However, while Aristotle's God is a self-absorbed, non-historical pure thinking of thinking (*noēsis noēseōs*), Hegel's Geist realizes itself *through* the physical universe and human history.
-   **Spinoza's Substance**: Hegel's system is a reaction to Spinoza's monism ([[Concepts/Deus sive Natura - Substance Monism (Spinoza)]]). Hegel famously declared that "everything depends on grasping and expressing the ultimate truth not as Substance, but as Subject." For Spinoza, substance is static and eternal; for Hegel, it must be active, self-differentiating, and conscious (Spirit).
-   **The Mind-Body Resolution**: Unlike Descartes' dualism ([[Concepts/Res Cogitans and Res Extensa (Descartes)]]), Hegel's Geist treats nature (the material world) not as a separate substance, but as "Spirit in its otherness"—a necessary, unconscious phase that Spirit must pass through to achieve conscious self-determination.
