# Akrasia - Weakness of Will (Aristotle)

> Book VII confronts the puzzle of incontinence (*akrasia*) — acting against one's own better judgment — against Socrates' denial that anyone knowingly does wrong. Aristotle's resolution: knowledge can be possessed without being actively *used*, so the incontinent person retains universal knowledge while failing to bring it to bear on the perceptual, particular knowledge relevant in the moment (like a drunk reciting philosophy without understanding). Incontinence is recoverable (like an intermittent disease) where vice, chosen deliberately, is not (like a permanent one).

## Summary

Aristotle takes seriously the Socratic puzzle: if virtue is knowledge, how can anyone act against what they know to be best? His answer distinguishes *having* knowledge from *using* it — the incontinent person is "like a city which passes all the right decrees and has good laws, but makes no use of them." More precisely, akrasia does not arise in the presence of knowledge "in the proper sense" (fully activated, syllogistically operative knowledge) but in the presence of *perceptual* knowledge of the particular case — someone overcome by appetite may retain the universal premise ("sweet things should be avoided by the sick") while failing to actively judge the particular ("this is a sweet thing, and I am sick") in a way that governs action, functioning rather like someone reciting Empedocles' verses while drunk: the words are there, but not truly possessed as operative understanding. Aristotle then distinguishes two forms: the impetuous, who act on passion without deliberating at all, and the properly weak, who deliberate, reach the right conclusion, but fail to abide by it under the pressure of appetite. He restricts incontinence in the strict sense to the bodily pleasures of touch and taste (food, drink, sex) — the same domain as temperance — treating incontinence about anger, honor, or gain as incontinence only by analogy, since these appetites are not universally natural to the same degree. A sharp line separates the incontinent from the merely vicious (self-indulgent, *akolastos*): the vicious person acts on a settled, deliberate choice of excess and feels no regret, hence is incurable "like a permanent disease," while the incontinent person acts against their own choice, under the sway of passion, and repents afterward — an intermittent, curable condition. The book closes with a first treatment of pleasure, correcting the view (attributed to Speusippus and others) that pleasure is merely a "process" or "becoming" (implicitly inferior, like the process of healing) — instead, pleasures "are activities and ends," complete in themselves, not necessarily inferior to any other good.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Akrasia vs. vice differ in kind, not degree**: incontinence is "contrary to choice," vice "is in accordance with choice" — the incontinent person retains a correct evaluative judgment they fail to follow; the vicious person's judgment itself is corrupted and endorses the wrong end.
- **Resolution of the Socratic puzzle via the having/using distinction**: one can possess knowledge without actively exercising it in the relevant moment, especially perceptual/particular knowledge as opposed to merely verbal/universal knowledge.
- **Two species of incontinence**: impetuosity (acting on passion without deliberating) and weakness proper (deliberating correctly, then failing to abide by the conclusion under pressure).
- **Incontinence's proper domain is the bodily pleasures of touch and taste** (matching temperance's domain); incontinence regarding anger, honor, or wealth is incontinence only by extended analogy.
- **Incontinence is curable, vice is not**: the incontinent person's regret and retained correct judgment make reform possible (an "intermittent disease"); the self-indulgent person's settled, unregretted choice of excess makes correction far harder (a "permanent disease").
- **Pleasure is activity/actuality, not process**: against views treating pleasure as a "filling" or "becoming" (implicitly a lesser, merely restorative good), Aristotle argues pleasures "are activities and ends" — complete in themselves at any moment, not stepping-stones to something else. (Developed further in Book X's second, more systematic treatment.)

## How Aristotle Uses This

Directly exercises the machinery of [[Concepts/Voluntary Action and Choice (Aristotle)]] (choice, deliberation, the location of the moving principle) to solve a genuine puzzle about moral psychology that pure virtue-of-character analysis (Books II–IV) could not resolve on its own — incontinence is precisely a case where action and choice come apart. It also anticipates and is completed by Book X's fuller theory of pleasure (see [[Concepts/Contemplation and the Best Life - Theoria (Aristotle)]]).
