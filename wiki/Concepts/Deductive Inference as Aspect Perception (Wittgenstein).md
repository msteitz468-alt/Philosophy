# Deductive Inference as Aspect Perception (Wittgenstein)
> The view that logical validity and deductive inference are not derived from prior apprehensions of word meaning, but are recognized through a process of aspect-seeing, where one perceives a concrete sentence as instantiating a logical schema.

## Concept Overview
In *Wittgenstein's Philosophical Investigations: A Critical Guide* (2010), Arif Ahmed formulates and defends a Wittgensteinian philosophy of logic centered on two core ideas: the **autonomy of logical rules** and the **epistemology of logical insight**.

## 1. Rule Autonomy: Thesis W
Ahmed presents "Thesis W" (and its variants):
- **Thesis W1**: We do not derive basic deductive rules from a prior grasp of word/sentence meanings. Instead, "our assigning those rules to [a sentence] just is our expression of its meaning."
- **Meaning-Bodies**: Wittgenstein rejects the mythological idea of a "meaning-body"—a postulated hidden entity behind an expression from which its rules of use are supposed to be derived. If the rule is not already in the meaning, then we cannot appeal to the meaning to justify the rule. Logic is autonomous.

## 2. Epistemology of Deduction: Aspect Perception
If deductive rules are autonomous, how do we ever recognize new valid arguments or "notice" that a rule is valid?
Ahmed argues that Wittgenstein resolves this by assimilating logical insight to **aspect perception** (aspect-seeing or gestalt shifts):
- **N3-Rules (Novel Rules)**: A rule is novel to a subject when they accept its premises but do not yet accept its conclusion, and their ultimate acceptance is non-derivative. 
- **The Schematic Gestalt**: When a subject recognizes the validity of a new inference, they do not deduce it from a hidden semantic essence. Rather, they undergo a shift in perception: they **see** the concrete, visible sentence as instantiating a schematic inference that they already treat as valid. 

For example, when looking at a complex formula, one might suddenly see it as a case of *Modus Ponens*. This dawning of an aspect is a perceptual and practical achievement: one notices a structural relation that was already open to view but had not been noticed. This provides a thoroughly non-mysterious, empirical epistemology of logic that avoids appealing to "immaterial" logical facts or semantic entities.
