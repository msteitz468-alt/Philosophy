# Eudaimonia

> In Aristotle’s *Nicomachean Ethics*, the highest human good (*to ariston*): happiness or flourishing, understood not as a state or feeling but as *activity* (*energeia*) of the soul in accordance with virtue (*arête*) over a complete life. It is complete (*teleion* — lacking nothing) and self-sufficient (*autarkes*). The “function” (*ergon*) argument identifies it with excellent performance of the characteristic human activity (rational soul).

## Core Elements (from Book I)
- Every expertise, action, and undertaking aims at some good. There is a highest good that is wished for for its own sake.
- Common views: pleasure (vulgar life of consumption), honor (political life), wealth (instrumental). All incomplete or external.
- The function argument: as the good of a flautist or sculptor lies in performing their characteristic activity well, so the human good lies in the activity of the soul in accordance with reason (or the best and most complete virtue) throughout a complete life.
- Political science is the architectonic expertise whose end is this good for the city (greater and more godlike than for one person).
- External goods (prosperity, friends, health) are necessary instruments or conditions for excellent activity; complete wretchedness precludes eudaimonia.
- Requires habituation and good upbringing to grasp the starting points (“the that”); the end is action (*praxis*), not mere knowledge.

## Completion from the Full Text (ingested 2026-07-01)

Reading the complete ten-book *Nicomachean Ethics* (rather than Book I alone) refines this page in two ways.

**Book I, more precisely**: eudaimonia is "something final and self-sufficient, and is the end of action" — the only good chosen always for itself and never for the sake of anything else, while other goods (honor, virtue, pleasure, reason) are chosen both for themselves and for happiness's sake. External goods (birth, friends, wealth, beauty, good fortune) are necessary conditions/instruments, not the substance of happiness, which lies in virtuous activity itself; great misfortune can diminish but does not simply destroy eudaimonia so long as virtuous activity persists — a more precise, resilience-oriented reading than "external goods as necessary instruments" alone suggests.

**Book X completes and specifies the account**: happiness admits of degrees. The *highest* eudaimonia is specifically **contemplative activity** (*theōria* — reason contemplating eternal truth), argued to be most continuous, most self-sufficient, most purely self-directed, most leisured, and most akin to divine activity — "we must, so far as we can, make ourselves immortal, and strain every nerve to live in accordance with the best thing in us." The practically virtuous life (justice, courage, temperance, and the rest, examined across Books II–V) remains genuinely happy but "in a secondary degree," since it depends on relations to others and worldly circumstance rather than being wholly self-sufficient. ## Aquinas' Medieval Synthesis and Elevation

Thomas Aquinas takes up the *Nicomachean Ethics* framework (function argument, eudaimonia as activity of the soul according to virtue over a complete life, critiques of pleasure/honor/wealth, need for habituation and external goods) in *Summa Theologica* I-II (Treatise on the Last End) and shows that natural eudaimonia is real and good (via acquired virtues in this life) but imperfect and incomplete.

- The ultimate last end is the uncreated Good, God.
- Perfect happiness (*beatitudo*) is the beatific vision: direct, intuitive contemplation of God in His essence by the glorified intellect (an operation of the speculative intellect; "the sole contemplation of God seen in His Essence").
- In this life: imperfect participation (through faith, hope, charity, virtuous activity ordered to God); "we call men happy, but only as men" (echoing Aristotle).
- Requires grace: "grace does not destroy nature but perfects it." Theological virtues perfect the cardinal ones; supernatural end exceeds unaided natural powers.
- Natural law (in the Treatise on Law) grounds Aristotelian ethics in eternal law (rational participation: "the light of natural reason whereby we discern what is good and what is evil").

This completes the vault's Aristotle material: eudaimonia elevated to beatitude; natural ethics synthesized with law and grace. ## Augustine's Restless Heart: Personal and Narrative Ground

Augustine's *Confessions* supplies the experiential and psychological root that Aquinas systematizes. The famous invocation ("You have made us for yourself and restless is our heart until it comes to rest in you") diagnoses why natural eudaimonia, while real, cannot fully satisfy: the soul is ordered to the infinite, uncreated Good. All striving (pleasure, honor, even virtue) is a search for this rest; sin is the inordinate turning toward finite goods or the act of turning itself (pear theft as paradigm of love of evil for its own sake).

- The "pilgrimage of grace" (Books 1–9) narrates the restless, divided will (two wills at war) healed only by grace; Neo-Platonism provides intellectual tools but not continence or stable rest.
- Book 10 turns inward: memory as the vast chamber in which the heart searches for God and self-knowledge.
- Ultimate rest = beatific vision (direct contemplation of God), imperfect in this life, perfect in heaven. "Grace does not destroy nature but perfects it."

See [[Sources/Confessions - Augustine]], [[Thinkers/Augustine]], [[Concepts/Restless Heart - Rest in God (Augustine)]], [[Concepts/Memory - Interiority and Self-Knowledge (Augustine)]].
