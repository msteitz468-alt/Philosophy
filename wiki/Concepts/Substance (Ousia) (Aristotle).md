# Substance (Ousia) (Aristotle)

> *Ousia* — the primary sense of being. To ask "what is being?" is really to ask "what is substance?" The central books (VII–IX, Zeta–Theta) test four candidates — substratum/matter, universal, composite, and form — and conclude that **substance in the primary sense is form** (the essence that makes a thing the kind of thing it is).

## Summary

"Being is said in many ways, but that which *is* primarily is the 'what' — the substance of the thing" ([[Concepts/Being qua Being (Aristotle)]]). Qualities, quantities, and the other categories are beings only derivatively, because they are *of* a substance; "to walk," "to be healthy," "to sit" are not "self-subsistent or capable of being separated from substance." Substance is prior in three ways: **in definition** (the account of any attribute must mention its substance), **in knowledge** (we know a thing best when we know *what* it is, not its qualities), and **in time** (only substance can exist on its own).

*Metaphysics* VII then asks *which* thing about a substance is its substance, weighing four candidates:

1. **The substratum (*hypokeimenon*) / matter** — that of which everything else is predicated. But matter taken alone "is neither a particular thing nor of a certain quantity nor any of the categories"; it is too indeterminate to be a *this* (*tode ti*). Since **separability** and **thisness** belong above all to substance, matter alone fails.
2. **The universal (*to katholou*)** — rejected (anti-Platonic core): "no universal is a substance," because substance is not predicable of a subject while the universal always is; and "the substance of each thing is peculiar to it," whereas the universal is common. A universal indicates a *such* (*toionde*), not a *this*.
3. **The composite** (form + matter, e.g. Socrates) — a genuine substance, indeed substance *par excellence* as the concrete individual, but posterior to and dependent on its form; it comes to be and perishes.
4. **The form / essence (*to ti ēn einai*)** — substance **in the primary sense**: the actuality and definable nature that makes the composite the kind of thing it is. See [[Concepts/Essence and Definition (Aristotle)]] and [[Concepts/Form and Matter - Hylomorphism (Aristotle)]].

Book V (Delta) records the lexicon entry: substance in its two primary senses is **(A)** the ultimate substratum, no longer predicated of anything else, and **(B)** that which is a *this* — separable, the shape or form of each thing. The central books resolve which has the better claim: form.

## Key Claims / Positions

- The question of being reduces to the question of substance; metaphysics is "what is substance?"
- **Thisness + separability** are the marks of substance — the criteria that eliminate bare matter and the universal.
- Primary substance = **form/essence**; the concrete composite is substance derivatively; matter is substance only as potential ([[Concepts/Potentiality and Actuality (Aristotle)]]).
- Form is **not generated or destroyed** (VIII) — only the composite comes to be; "it is a man that produces a man."
- Three grades of substance (XII): sensible-perishable, sensible-eternal, and the immaterial divine ([[Concepts/The Unmoved Mover (Aristotle)]]).

## How This Sits Among Thinkers

- **Versus [[Thinkers/Plato]]**: the rejection of the universal as substance is precisely the rejection of separate Forms — see [[Contradictions/Plato vs Aristotle on the Forms]] and [[Concepts/Critique of Platonic Forms (Aristotle)]]. For Aristotle the truly real is *this individual horse*, known through its immanent form, not a transcendent Horse-itself.
- **Versus the Presocratic materialists** ([[Thinkers/Thales]], [[Thinkers/Anaximenes]], the atomists): they made the material substratum the only reality; Aristotle shows matter alone cannot be substance because it lacks thisness.
- **Downstream — [[Thinkers/Locke]]**: the empiricist deflation — substance as an unknown "something I know not what" supporting qualities, and the [[Concepts/Nominal and Real Essences (Locke)]] distinction — is a direct reply to the Aristotelian real-essence/substantial-form tradition.
- **Downstream — [[Thinkers/Spinoza]]**: redefines *substance* as that which is *in itself and conceived through itself*, yielding exactly one ([[Concepts/Deus sive Natura - Substance Monism (Spinoza)]]) — the polar opposite of Aristotle's plural concrete substances. [[Thinkers/Leibniz]]'s [[Concepts/Monad (Leibniz)]] is another heir: substance as a true individual unity.
- **Descartes**: *res cogitans* / *res extensa* ([[Concepts/Res Cogitans and Res Extensa (Descartes)]]) retain substance-talk but recast it as two kinds of stuff, dropping hylomorphic form.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Tension *within* Zeta: form is said to be substance "in the primary sense," yet Aristotle also denies that the universal is substance — and form (e.g. *humanity*) looks universal. Whether Aristotelian forms are universal or particular ("this-soul") is the central unresolved scholarly debate the text itself leaves open.
