# Being and the Ontological Difference (Heidegger)
> The question "What is Being?" — once asked by the Greeks but subsequently forgotten — is the single animating question of Heidegger's philosophy, and the ontological difference between Being and beings is its foundational distinction.

## Summary

Heidegger's entire philosophical project begins from the claim that the Western tradition has *forgotten* the question of Being (*Seinsvergessenheit*). [[Thinkers/Aristotle]] asked "What is being?" and showed that "being is said in many ways" — across the categories (substance, quality, quantity, etc.) — unified not as a genus but by analogy, with substance (*ousia*) as the focal case. But subsequent philosophy buried the question under epistemology ([[Thinkers/Kant]]: "What can we know?"), subjectivism ([[Thinkers/Descartes]]: the thinking subject as foundation), and the regional sciences (physics, biology, etc.), each of which presupposes a "projection of a view of being onto entities" without interrogating that projection itself.

The **ontological difference** (*ontologische Differenz*) is the distinction between Being (*Sein*) and beings (*Seiendes*) — between the *being* of things and the things themselves. "Being is everywhere: everything is — people, hammers, towns, theories, planets, galaxies. Being is nowhere: it does not inhere in entities as a readily discernible property." Being is not itself a being; it cannot be encountered the way a hammer or a planet can. This makes the question of Being peculiarly elusive: unlike other preliminary understandings (e.g. a rough idea of biology before studying it), the preliminary understanding of Being that makes inquiry possible "cannot be abandoned for a direct encounter with being."

Heidegger goes beyond [[Thinkers/Aristotle]]'s framework by introducing a tripartite distinction: *that*-being (existence — *that* something is), *what*-being (essence — *what* something is), and *how*-being (the mode or manner of an entity's being). The third is Heidegger's crucial addition: entities differ not only in their properties but in the *way* they are. A hammer, a number, a Dasein, and a mood all *are*, but the manner of their being is radically different. Both Aristotle and Descartes homogenise being by treating all entities as *vorhanden* (present-at-hand) — as bearers of predicable properties. Heidegger denies this.

Epistemology is inadequate precisely because it presupposes a homogeneous knowing-relation between a pure subject and objects, ignoring the knower's situatedness and the diversity of ways entities can be known. The right way of knowing entities depends on the *being* of those entities; thus ontology must precede epistemology. As Heidegger puts it: epistemology "continually sharpens the knife but never gets round to cutting."

## Key Claims / Positions

### How Different Thinkers Approach This

- **[[Thinkers/Aristotle]]**: Asked "What is being?" and recognised its systematic ambiguity across the categories, unified by *pros hen* (focal) reference to substance. But for Heidegger, Aristotle still treated being primarily as *ousia* (substance = presence), which tied ontology to a single temporal mode (the present) and linked it problematically to theology (the unmoved mover). See [[Concepts/Being qua Being (Aristotle)]].
- **[[Thinkers/Descartes]]**: Reduced the world to *res extensa* and the self to *res cogitans* — both conceived as substances with essential attributes. This homogenises being by treating everything (including Dasein) as present-at-hand. Heidegger regards this as a catastrophic narrowing.
- **[[Thinkers/Kant]]**: Turned philosophy toward epistemology ("What can we know?"), treating being as what can be given in experience. Heidegger saw Kant as closer to ontology than Kant himself recognised (the transcendental imagination in the first *Critique*), but Kant retreated from his own insight.
- **[[Thinkers/Hegel]]**: "The last and greatest of the Cartesians" — accepted Aristotle's conception of time and Descartes's subjectivism. His *Phenomenology of Spirit* is a parallel project to *Being and Time* but remains within the tradition Heidegger seeks to dismantle.
- **[[Thinkers/A.J. Ayer]]**: Dismissed Heidegger's question of Being as paradigmatic "metaphysical nonsense" — a position Heidegger's supporters regard as exemplifying the very forgetfulness his philosophy diagnoses. See [[Concepts/Metaphysics as Meaningless (Ayer)]].

## From Being and Time (Primary Text)

The Introduction (§§1–4) states the question directly. Three prejudices sanction its neglect — being as the most universal, indefinable, self-evident concept — but "the indefinability of being does not dispense with the question of its meaning but forces it upon us" (§1). Being is not a genus; its universality "surpasses" generic universality (the medieval *transcendens*; Aristotle's unity of analogy "placed the problem of being on a fundamentally new basis"). The question's formal structure: what is asked about (*Gefragtes* — being), what is interrogated (*Befragtes* — beings, above all Dasein), what is to be ascertained (*Erfragtes* — the meaning of being). Dasein's priority is threefold (ontic, ontological, ontic-ontological): "the ontic distinction of Dasein lies in the fact that it is ontological" (§4). The stated aim of the whole treatise: "the interpretation of *time* as the possible horizon for any understanding whatsoever of being" — the guiding discovery being that Greek ontology read being as *ousia/parousia*, constant **presence**, i.e. tacitly from one temporal mode. The book breaks off (§83) before delivering the answer: "Does time itself reveal itself as the horizon of being?" See [[Concepts/Phenomenological Method and Destruktion (Heidegger)]] and [[Concepts/World-Time and the Vulgar Concept of Time (Heidegger)]].

Heidegger's later marginalia in his own copy (printed in the Stambaugh/Schmidt edition) repudiate parts of this apparatus: "a decision about beyng [*Seyn*] cannot be made with the help of such conceptuality"; Dasein is reinterpreted as "the site of the understanding of being," and the horizon-language is redirected toward *das Ereignis* — the vocabulary of the *Kehre*.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] The ontological difference is foundational but difficult to state without paradox: if Being is not a being, how can it be discussed? Heidegger's own language strains against the limits it identifies.
- The relationship between Being and time — the declared goal of *Being and Time* — was never fully worked out in the published sections.
