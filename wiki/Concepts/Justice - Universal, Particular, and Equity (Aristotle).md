# Justice - Universal, Particular, and Equity (Aristotle)

> Justice is both universal (complete virtue exercised toward others, coextensive with lawfulness) and particular (fairness): particular justice divides into distributive justice (geometric proportion, honors/goods allotted by merit) and corrective/rectificatory justice (arithmetic proportion, restoring equality in transactions regardless of merit). Equity (*epieikeia*) corrects the necessary generality of law for cases it could not anticipate, and is itself just, "better than one kind of justice... but not a different class of thing."

## Summary

Book V distinguishes two senses of justice. In the universal sense, "the just is the lawful and the fair, the unjust the unlawful and the unfair" — justice as complete virtue exercised in relation to others, since law prescribes actions expressing every virtue. In the particular sense, justice is one virtue among others, concerned specifically with fair shares, and divides into two structurally different operations. Distributive justice governs the allocation of honors, wealth, or other divisible goods among members of a community according to merit, using geometric proportion: shares should be equal only between equally meritorious parties, unequal between unequally meritorious ones, so that "the whole is to the whole as either part is to the corresponding part." Corrective (rectificatory) justice governs transactions between individuals — voluntary (trade) and involuntary (crime) — using arithmetic proportion instead: the judge disregards the relative merit or social standing of the parties and simply restores the equality disturbed by the transaction, "trying to equalize by means of the penalty, taking away from the gain of the assailant." Money is introduced as the conventional measure that makes such equalization commensurable across qualitatively different goods and services — "it measures all things," standing in for the underlying reality of demand. Aristotle then treats reciprocity in exchange, and specifies that political justice proper exists only among free and (proportionately or arithmetically) equal persons living under law with a view to self-sufficiency — justice within a household (parent-child, master-slave) is only justice by analogy, since these relations lack the requisite equality. The book closes with the doctrine of equity (*epieikeia*): because law is necessarily stated in universal terms, it cannot anticipate every particular case, and equity is the correction that applies "what the legislator himself would have said had he been present, and would have put into his law if he had known" — equity is not a rival to justice but justice's own self-correction, "better than the error that arises from the absoluteness of the [legal] statement."

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Dual structure of justice**: universal (= lawfulness, complete virtue toward others) and particular (= fairness, a specific virtue concerned with equitable shares) are both genuinely "justice," not homonyms.
- **Distributive justice uses geometric proportion**: shares of honor/goods track merit, so equals receive equal shares and unequals receive proportionally unequal shares — the substantive content of "merit" itself varies by regime (a point that connects to, without simply repeating, the *Politics*' treatment of competing claims to rule).
- **Corrective justice uses arithmetic proportion**: the judge ignores parties' relative merit entirely and restores the numerical equality disrupted by a transaction or crime — "the judge tries to equalize it," acting as, in Aristotle's phrase, "a sort of animate justice."
- **Money is a conventional measure (from *nomos*, law/convention) enabling commensuration** of otherwise incommensurable goods and services in exchange, representing (rather than constituting) the underlying reality of need/demand.
- **Political justice requires freedom and equality among the parties under law**; justice within unequal natural relations (household, master-slave) exists only by extension/analogy, not in the strict political sense.
- **Equity (*epieikeia*) is justice correcting itself**, not a departure from justice: "the same thing is just and equitable," equity simply supplying what the legislator would have specified had the particular case been foreseeable — necessitated by the ineliminable generality of law itself.

## How Aristotle Uses This

Reframes justice as a distinctive species of the mean doctrine ("justice is a kind of mean, but not in the same way as the other virtues... because it relates to an intermediate amount, while injustice relates to the extremes" — see [[Concepts/Doctrine of the Mean (Aristotle)]]) with its own proportional (rather than simply midpoint) structure. This is a genuinely different treatment from the vault's existing [[Concepts/Distributive Justice and the Rule of Law (Aristotle)]] page, drawn from *Politics* Book III: the *Politics* passage addresses distributive justice as a problem of *who should rule* (competing political claims of the many, the wealthy, the virtuous) and defends the rule of law over personal rule; this Book V material addresses distributive and corrective justice as a general ethical theory of fair allocation and transactional equity, applicable within any community, not specifically the problem of political office. The two pages should be read as complementary — the *Politics* extends and specifies the *Ethics*' more general proportional-justice framework to the specific case of constitutional rule.
