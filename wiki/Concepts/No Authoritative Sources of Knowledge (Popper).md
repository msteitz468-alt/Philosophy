# No Authoritative Sources of Knowledge (Popper)

> From the Introduction to *Conjectures and Refutations* ("On the Sources of Knowledge and of Ignorance"). Popper denies that knowledge has any authoritative **source** at all — neither observation (empiricism) nor intellectual intuition (rationalism). The right epistemological question is not "What are the best sources of our knowledge?" but **"How can we hope to detect and eliminate error?"**

## The Argument

- The classic quarrel between **British empiricism** (Bacon, Locke, Berkeley, Hume, Mill — the source of all knowledge is *observation*) and **Continental rationalism** (Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz — the source is *intellectual intuition of clear and distinct ideas*) hides a deeper *agreement*: both assume knowledge must be traced to, and justified by, an authoritative origin.

- Both are forms of the doctrine that **"truth is manifest"** — the optimistic belief that truth, once we clear away veils of prejudice and authority, will reveal itself plainly to the honest enquirer (Bacon's purging of "idols"; Descartes's clear and distinct ideas as a kind of God-given guarantee). On this view error is unnatural and needs special explanation.

- This breeds a dangerous shadow: the **conspiracy theory of ignorance**. If truth is manifest, then those who fail to see it must be *prevented* from seeing it — by sinister powers, class interest, repression, a corrupting press, a conspiracy. (Popper notes the Marxist who finds class bias on every page, the analyst who diagnoses "resistance.") The manifest-truth doctrine thus underwrites authoritarianism and persecution of the "wilfully blind."

- Popper's reply: **there are no ultimate sources of knowledge.** Every source — tradition, reason, imagination, observation — is to be welcomed, and every source is to be *critically examined*. No source is authoritative; none is beyond question; none guarantees truth. Observation is not the bedrock the empiricist imagines (it is theory-laden and fallible); intuition is not the infallible light the rationalist imagines (it can mislead however clearly it shows us things).

- The genuine task of epistemology is not the pedigree of beliefs but the **critical elimination of error**. Knowledge advances not by securing foundations but by exposing conjectures to refutation. ("The most important function of observation and reasoning, and even of intuition and imagination, is to help us in the critical examination of those bold conjectures which are the means by which we probe into the unknown.")

- **Fallibilist optimism**: we can never claim certainty or an authoritative source, yet we are not condemned to scepticism. "Our knowledge can only be finite, while our ignorance must necessarily be infinite" — and every solved problem reveals new ones. We learn from our mistakes.

## Relation to Other Vault Content

- **Vs. classical empiricism** ([[Thinkers/Locke]], [[Thinkers/Hume]]): rejects the [[Concepts/Tabula Rasa - Blank Slate or Empty Cabinet (Locke)]] picture of the mind passively receiving sensation as the *source*. Observation presupposes theory; the [[Concepts/Sensation and Reflection as the Two Fountains of Ideas (Locke)|"fountains of ideas"]] are not authoritative wells of truth. Yet Popper calls himself "an empiricist of sorts" — experience still *decides* (negatively, by falsification).

- **Vs. rationalism** ([[Thinkers/Descartes]], [[Thinkers/Spinoza]]): [[Concepts/Clear and Distinct Perception (Descartes)]] is exactly the "manifest truth" doctrine Popper targets — intuitive self-evidence is no guarantee of truth.

- **With Russell** ([[Concepts/Correspondence Theory of Truth (Russell)]]): Popper credits Russell's claim that epistemological *relativism* and *pragmatism* (truth = usefulness) are linked to authoritarian and totalitarian ideas — the political stakes of the theory of knowledge.

- **Contrast with scepticism** ([[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]], [[Concepts/Mitigated Scepticism (Hume)]]): like the sceptics, Popper denies certain foundations; *unlike* them, he holds that critical method yields genuine (fallible) growth toward truth.

- Underwrites [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)]] and [[Concepts/Three Views Concerning Human Knowledge (Popper)]]: theories are not read off authoritative sources but boldly conjectured and tested.
