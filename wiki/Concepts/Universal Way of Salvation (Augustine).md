# Universal Way of Salvation (Augustine)

> Porphyry, the Neoplatonist philosopher and fiercest enemy of Christians, admitted he had not found a *via universalis* — a universal road of liberation for the soul. Augustine argues in *City of God* X that Christ alone is this universal Way: because he is both God (the goal) and man (the road). The difference between the philosopher who sees the destination and the Christian who walks the road is the difference between pride and humility.

## Summary

One of the most philosophically dense arguments of the *City of God* (Books VIII–X) concerns whether Platonism — and especially Neoplatonism (Plotinus, Porphyry) — can provide a path to eternal happiness. Augustine's answer: Platonists see the destination but lack the road. They came close enough to recognize the One/God as the source of all bliss and illumination, but their pride prevents them from accepting the humble, incarnate Way by which that destination is reached.

**The pivot text** is Porphyry's admission (cited by Augustine, Bk X.32): Porphyry acknowledged he had not yet found a universal way for the liberation of the soul — not in any true philosophy, not in the moral teachings of the Indians, not in the ascent of the Chaldaean oracles. "This way has not yet come to my knowledge." Augustine responds: *this way is Christ*.

## The Philosophical Background

**Plotinus** (c. 204–270 CE): The greatest Neoplatonist, author of the *Enneads*. Established the system of the One → Intellect → Soul → matter, with the soul's return (*epistrophe*) via purification and contemplation. Augustine deeply respects Plotinus and often endorses his views: that the soul receives its illumination from God (not from itself); that the One is the source of all good; that evil is privation; that the soul must ascend beyond material attachment.

**Porphyry** (232–c.305 CE): Plotinus's student, editor of the *Enneads*, and the "most learned of philosophers" — yet also "the fiercest enemy of Christians." He attacked Christianity in his *Against the Christians* (condemned and burned by later emperors). He sought a *via universalis animae liberandae* (universal way of soul liberation) in theurgy, oracles, and ecstatic ritual — and admitted failure. Augustine regards this admission as philosophically honest and poignant: Porphyry's very candor about the inadequacy of all available paths points beyond itself to the one he rejected.

## Augustine's Argument (Books VIII–X)

**What Platonists get right** (Book VIII–IX):
- God (the One/supreme Good) is immaterial, immutable, the source of all illumination.
- The soul cannot be its own light; it receives illumination from God (Plotinus agrees with John 1:9 on this, says Augustine).
- True beatitude comes from union with God, not from union with demons or any intermediate being.
- Platonists are "raised above the rest by the glorious reputation they so thoroughly deserve" and are "near-Christians."

**Where Platonists fail** (Book X):
1. **Demonology**: Apuleius and Porphyry posit demons as mediators between men and gods. But a true mediator must share our misery (mortality) and God's beatitude. Demons are immortal but subject to passion — they share neither our death nor God's eternal happiness. They cannot mediate what they do not possess.
2. **Theurgy**: Porphyry endorsed theurgical rituals (magical rites to purify the soul) as a supplement to philosophy. Augustine argues these rites only entangle the soul with demons, not with God.
3. **Pride**: The fundamental failure is *superbia* (pride). The Neoplatonists saw "the land of peace from a wooded hilltop" but could not find the road. The road requires humility — specifically, acceptance of the incarnate God, the scandal of a divine being who became a servant, was born of a woman, was crucified.

**The image of the two travelers** (from Augustine's early works, restated in *City of God*):
> "It is one thing to descry the land of peace from a wooded hilltop and, unable to find the way to it, struggle on through trackless wastes where traitors and runaways lie in wait to attack. It is another thing to follow the high road to that land of peace." (*Confessions* VII.26)

The philosophers are like a proud man on a hilltop who can see the destination clearly but disdains the humble shepherd's directions (Christ), and instead follows an elegant impostor (theurgy, demons). He ends up lost; the humble traveler who trusted the shepherd arrives.

## Christ as the Universal Way

**Why Christ is the Way** (Book XI.2):
> "There is one road, and one only, well secured against all possibility of going astray; and this road is provided by one who is himself both God and man. As God, he is the goal; as man, he is the way."

The incarnation is not a scandal but a solution to the precise problem Porphyry identified:
- The soul needs to reach God (the One, the immutable Good) — *Christ as God* is the destination.
- But the soul, fallen into materiality, mortality, and pride, cannot ascend by its own contemplative effort — *Christ as man* descends to meet the soul where it is, and is himself the road.
- The Mediator must be both mortal (to die for us) and immortal (to be the goal): "Not mortal in his Godhead, not miserable in his humanity" — he takes on our mortality without our misery, he mediates without possessing the defect he removes.

This is the *via universalis*: universal because it is open to all — educated and uneducated, noble and slave, philosopher and fisherman. The Platonist path required years of philosophical training accessible only to the elite. Christ's way (faith, humility, love) is genuinely universal.

**The three incredibilities of Book XXII**: That Christ rose in the flesh; that the whole world believed this incredible event; that uneducated fishermen persuaded all of humanity. The very universality of Christian belief — cutting across all classes and philosophies — is itself evidence of the *via universalis* at work.

## Pride vs. Humility as the Differentiating Principle

The deepest analysis: why did the Platonists, who came so close, fail?

**Pride (*superbia*)** — the sin of the Devil and the earthly city's root. The Devil sinned not from flesh (he has none) but from choosing to live "by his own standard" rather than God's. The Platonists similarly:
- Are too proud to accept that the Eternal God became an infant, was crucified, and rose bodily.
- Prefer the dignity of philosophical ascent to the scandal of the Incarnation.
- Envy the humble access of the unlearned to truth they thought was theirs alone.
- In Augustine's image: they help others reach the harbor (through genuine philosophical insights about God) but are themselves destroyed "within sight of the fatherland" — like those who live on the "high mountain" of pride near the harbor and warn others of the dangers, but do not themselves enter.

**Humility (*humilitas*)** is Christ's characteristic way. "God resists the proud, but gives grace to the humble." (Preface, quoting James 4:6 — the keynote of the entire *City of God*.) The incarnation is itself an act of divine condescension — God stooping to meet man where man is. Accepting this requires the very opposite of the philosopher's pride: it requires trusting a humble shepherd rather than an elegant gentleman.

## Relationship to Other Vault Concepts

**[[Concepts/Return of the Soul (Epistrophe)]]**: Plotinus's return to the One via purification and contemplation is the closest pagan analogue to Augustine's vision. Augustine honors this but insists: (1) the return requires the Mediator, not just contemplation; (2) the return is bodily (resurrection), communal (City of God), and permanent (eternal Sabbath) — not individual, temporary, and cyclically repeated as in Plotinus; (3) the soul cannot accomplish the return by its own philosophical effort; grace is required.

**[[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]] (Pyrrhonism)**: Sextus Empiricus's third philosophy (neither dogmatism nor Academic skepticism, but ongoing search via suspension) is in some ways sympathetic — Augustine too acknowledges that one cannot "see" the destination by reason alone. But where Sextus suspends and lives by appearances, Augustine walks by faith: "we are saved in hope." The contrast is resolution (by grace, through Christ) vs. ongoing suspension.

**[[Concepts/Eudaimonia]]**: Book XIX's philosophical survey explicitly engages the Greek tradition's supreme-good debate. Augustine's universal way is universal precisely because it addresses the problem every philosophical school tried and failed to solve within natural resources.

**[[Thinkers/Plotinus]] and [[Concepts/The One (To Hen)]]**: Augustine's engagement is affectionate and critical throughout Books VIII–X. The One is real, is God, is the source of illumination — but the road to the One is not purely philosophical ascent. It is Christ, who descended so we might ascend.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Augustine's critique of Porphyry is sometimes read as unfair: Porphyry may not have had access to Christian sources adequate to present Christ as a credible universal way. The vault notes this asymmetry — Augustine knows more about Platonism than Porphyry knew about Christianity.
- The question of the **salvation of virtuous pagans** (good Platonists, Job-like Gentiles) is left open: Augustine allows that some outside visible Christianity belong to the City of God (Job), but his framework for how they are included is underdeveloped compared to later developments (e.g., Aquinas on natural law and implicit faith).
- **Implicit vs. explicit faith**: does the universal way require explicit confession of Christ, or is the humble orientation toward God (as seen in Platonists like Plotinus at their best) sufficient? Augustine leans toward requiring explicit faith; later tradition debates this at length.
