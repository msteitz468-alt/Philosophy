# Language-Games (Wittgenstein)
> Language is not a single mirror of reality but an open multiplicity of rule-governed activities — "language-games" — in which words and deeds are woven together; to imagine a language is to imagine a form of life.

## Summary

The **language-game** (*Sprachspiel*) is the central organizing idea of [[Thinkers/Ludwig Wittgenstein|Wittgenstein]]'s *Philosophical Investigations* (1953). It names "the whole, consisting of language and the actions into which it is woven" (§7). Wittgenstein introduces it to break the grip of one picture of language — the picture, drawn from Augustine and from his own [[Concepts/Picture Theory of Language (Wittgenstein)|*Tractatus*]], that every word names an object and every sentence is a combination of such names. That model, he argues, describes only a primitive fragment of how language works.

The opening move is the **builders' language** (§2): a complete language consisting of four words — "block," "pillar," "slab," "beam" — called out by a builder and answered by his assistant fetching the stone. It is a whole functioning language, yet nothing in it resembles the naming-and-describing essence philosophers seek. From here Wittgenstein multiplies examples to show that "there are *countless* kinds" of use, and the kinds are not fixed: "new types of language, new language-games, as we may say, come into existence, and others become obsolete and get forgotten" (§23).

## Core Mechanics

### 1. Words are tools, not labels
"Think of the tools in a tool-box: there is a hammer, pliers, a saw... The functions of words are as diverse as the functions of these objects" (§11). A word's uniform *appearance* (all words look alike, like the handles in a locomotive cab, §12) conceals the diversity of what we *do* with them — report, command, question, joke, greet, count, curse, pray. The naming-model wrongly treats this whole toolbox as if every tool were a label.

### 2. Language is woven into activity
"The term 'language-game' is meant to bring into prominence the fact that the *speaking* of language is part of an activity, or of a form of life" (§23). Commands, descriptions, and reports are not detachable strings of names; they are moves in a practice, like moves in chess. Hence "to imagine a language means to imagine a form of life" (§19). See [[Concepts/Forms of Life (Wittgenstein)]].

### 3. No single essence — only family resemblance
Because the kinds of language-game are countless and unbounded, there is no one feature common to all that makes them "language." They are united only by [[Concepts/Family Resemblance (Wittgenstein)|family resemblances]] (§§65–67). What "sentence" and "language" name is "the family of structures more or less related to one another" (§108).

### 4. Meaning flows from the game
Within a language-game a word has its function; torn out of any game it does no work. This grounds the doctrine that [[Concepts/Meaning as Use (Wittgenstein)|the meaning of a word is its use]]. The same point disciplines philosophy: when a word is wrenched from its home game and made to idle, confusion results — "philosophical problems arise when language goes on holiday" (§38).

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. the early Wittgenstein:** The *Tractatus* held that beneath surface grammar lies one hidden logical form shared by all meaningful propositions. The language-game idea replaces that single form with an irreducible plurality of practices. This is the pivot of [[Contradictions/Early vs Later Wittgenstein]].
- **vs. [[Thinkers/A.J. Ayer|Ayer]] / [[Concepts/Verification Principle (Ayer)|verificationism]]:** Logical positivism kept one master-criterion of meaning (empirical verifiability). Wittgenstein's pluralism dissolves the demand for any single criterion: religious, ethical, and mathematical talk are their own language-games, not failed attempts at empirical description.
- **Heirs — Austin and [[Thinkers/John Searle|Searle]]:** Ordinary-language philosophy and speech-act theory (illocutionary force, performatives) develop the insight that saying is doing, that utterances are moves in conventional practices.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
> [!warning] Directly overturns the single-logical-form doctrine of [[Concepts/Picture Theory of Language (Wittgenstein)]] — see [[Contradictions/Early vs Later Wittgenstein]].

- If there is no essence of language, is "language-game" itself a theory, or only an "object of comparison" (§130–131) meant to loosen a picture? Wittgenstein insists the latter — he advances no theory (§109).
