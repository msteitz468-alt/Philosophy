# Pressure Groups and Special Interests (Buchanan-Tullock)
> Lobbies and pressure groups are a rational response to expected differential advantages from the public budget, and their growth is a direct function of the expansion and lack of generality of government.

## Summary

In Chapter 19 of *The Calculus of Consent* (1962), James M. Buchanan and Gordon Tullock construct a constitutional analysis of **pressure groups** and special-interest lobbies. Rather than treating interest groups as moral aberrations or structural pathologies, they show that group lobbying is a rational consequence of utility-maximizing behavior under non-unanimous voting rules.

### The Genesis of Pressure Groups

Buchanan and Tullock outline a microeconomic explanation for interest group organization:

1. **Differential Public Budgets**: Whenever the government departs from general, non-discriminatory taxation and spending, it creates opportunities for differential advantages (subsidies, tariffs, tax loopholes, targeted regulations).
2. **Rational Mobilization**: Individuals who expect to secure these differential advantages have a strong incentive to organize into pressure groups. The group's primary purpose is to lobby the legislature to secure these targeted transfers, which are paid for by general taxpayers.
3. **The Reciprocal Loop**: As the scope and range of collective action expand, the opportunities for differential advantages multiply. This encourages the formation of more pressure groups, culminating in a **pluralistic equilibrium** where all groups are organized to exploit the state's budget, leading to mutual exploitation and economic inefficiency.

### Logrolling and Pressure Group Capture

Special-interest groups exploit the democratic process through the mechanism of **implicit and explicit logrolling**. In a legislature operating under simple majority rule, a single industry or regional lobby (e.g., sugar farmers or shipbuilders) cannot pass a protective subsidy alone. 

Instead, lobbies coordinate with legislators to trade votes across different issues. Lobbies help political parties construct winning coalitions by bundling together diverse special-interest policies, resulting in a bloated budget containing hundreds of protective loops and transfers. The general public, remaining **rationally ignorant** due to the high cost of information compared to their minuscule personal share of the cost, fails to oppose this capture.

### The Constitutional Cure

How can a society prevent pressure group exploitation? 

Buchanan and Tullock argue that standard legislative reforms (like lobbying limits or ethics rules) are blunt, ineffective instruments that target the symptoms rather than the cause. The only true cure is **constitutional design**:

* **The Generality Principle**: Restricting the legislature to passing general, non-discriminatory laws. Wicksell's **benefit principle of taxation** (requiring that tax rates be directly tied to public utility benefits) prevents majorities from using the tax system to exploit minorities or subsidize special interests.
* **Bicameralism**: Restructuring the legislature into two houses with different bases of representation (e.g., geographic vs. population). This requires pressure groups to negotiate across a wider, more diverse coalition of voters, raising the transaction costs of rent-seeking and diluting their lobbying power.
* **Qualified Majority Rules**: Raising the voting threshold beyond a simple majority makes it more difficult for small interest groups to capture the legislative majority.

## Key Claims / Positions

### How Different Thinkers Approach This

- **Pluralist Political Scientists (Bentley, Truman, Dahl)**: Held a relatively optimistic view of interest groups, arguing that the competition among groups would naturally produce a balanced public interest. Buchanan and Tullock reject this optimism, showing that the competition among self-interested groups leads to a negative-sum, over-expanded budget where everyone is taxed to pay for everyone else's subsidies.
- **[[Thinkers/Mancur Olson]]**: In *The Rise and Decline of Nations* (1982), Olson built directly on Buchanan and Tullock's pressure-group model. While Buchanan and Tullock analyzed the constitutional checks that *could* limit lobbies, Olson analyzed the historical dynamics: stable societies gradually accumulate these lobbies ("distributional coalitions"), resulting in "institutional sclerosis" that retards growth.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Constitutional limits on interest groups (like the generality principle) can limit the state's flexibility to respond to crises or help disadvantaged groups who require targeted, non-general support.
- If pressure groups are already entrenched, they will use their lobbying power to block the very constitutional reforms (like qualified majorities or generality requirements) designed to check them.
