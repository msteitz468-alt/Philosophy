# Time and Eternity - Distentio Animi (Augustine)

> "What then is time? If no one asks me, I know; if I wish to explain it to one that asketh, I know not." (Confessions, Book XI). In Books XI–XIII Augustine moves from personal memory (Book X) to the metaphysics of time and creation. Time is not an objective container or attribute of God (who is eternal) but a subjective "distention" (*distentio*) of the human soul/mind: the past exists in memory, the present in attention ("a present of things present"), the future in expectation. Time and creation are co-temporal ("In the beginning God created..." marks the beginning of time itself). This analysis is one of the most influential in Western philosophy of time, directly engaging Aristotle (time as measure of motion) while grounding it in the soul and in the Creator-creature relation.

## Summary

Augustine's treatment of time is not an aside but the necessary metaphysical ground for understanding creation, history, memory, and the possibility of rest in God. 

- God is eternal: simple, unchanging, "always working, ever at rest," possessing all at once in an abiding "now." There is no "before" or succession in God.
- Time begins with creation: "The world was made, not in time, but simultaneously with time." There was no time "before" creation in which God deliberated or waited.
- Time is subjective/distensive: measured in the soul. We "measure" times by comparing durations in memory/attention/expectation. A "long time" is a long distention; the present has no duration (it is a "point" that immediately becomes past).
- The three "presents": present of things past (memory), present of things present (attention/sight), present of things future (expectation). "Time is nothing else than distention" — of the mind.
- This resolves paradoxes of creation ("what was God doing before He created?") and grounds the possibility of history and narrative (the *Confessions* itself as a temporal telling that praises the eternal).

Books XII–XIII then apply this to exegesis of Genesis 1, showing creation *ex nihilo*, the role of formless matter, and the allegorical depths of the text (e.g., the "firmament" as Scripture dividing waters above/below = spiritual vs. carnal readers).

## Key Claims and Extracts

- **The famous question and aporia (Book XI.14)**: "What, then, is time? If no one asks me, I know; if I wish to explain it to one that asketh, I know not. Yet I say boldly that I know that if nothing passed away, there would be no past time; and if nothing were coming, there would be no future time; and if nothing were, there would be no present time."
- **Distention of the soul (Book XI.26-28)**: "It is in my own mind, then, that I measure time... The impression which passing things make upon you abides even when they are gone. This is what I measure when I measure time... Either, then, these are the times, or else I do not measure times... It seems to me that time is nothing else than distention (*distentio*): but of what I am not sure — probably of the mind itself."
- **Eternity vs. time**: "You are the Selfsame, and your years shall not fail... Your 'years' are but a day, and your day is not every day but today... Your today is eternity."
- **Creation and time co-begin**: No time existed "before" creation; the question "what was God doing before?" is meaningless because there was no "before." God created "in the beginning" — the beginning of time itself.
- **Memory, attention, expectation**: The mind "distends" itself toward past (via memory) and future (via expectation) while holding the present in attention. A recited psalm or long action illustrates this: expectation shortens as memory lengthens until the whole is "in memory" and attention passes to the next.

This is not pure subjectivism (time is real as the measure of change in creatures) but a relocation of time's "where" from the physical world or abstract container into the creaturely soul ordered to the eternal Creator.
