# Consumer Welfare Standard
> The doctrine that antitrust law should intervene only when consumers face higher prices — the ideological pivot that disarmed American anti-monopoly law from 1981 onward.

## Summary

The consumer welfare standard became the dominant framework for U.S. antitrust enforcement following the Chicago School revolution in legal and economic thought, associated particularly with Robert Bork's 1978 *The Antitrust Paradox*. Under this standard, a merger or business practice is anticompetitive — and therefore legally actionable — only if it results in higher prices for consumers. If a dominant firm provides low prices (Amazon's retail, Google's free search), antitrust regulators have no standing to act, regardless of what that firm does to suppliers, workers, competitors, or democracy.

Before 1981, U.S. antitrust law was far more expansive: mergers of firms with even 5–6% market share could be blocked; the goal was to prevent concentrated economic and *political* power, not just consumer price harms. Standard Oil was broken up when it controlled 65% of the market — not 100%.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

**Zephyr Teachout (critical):** The consumer welfare standard is the single most important intellectual error enabling modern monopoly. By reducing all antitrust analysis to the question of consumer prices, it blinds law and policy to: (a) wage suppression via employer monopsony, (b) supplier exploitation, (c) political capture and lobbying, (d) surveillance and data extraction, (e) the destruction of competitive entry. Teachout argues the standard must be replaced with a broader anti-monopoly philosophy that asks not just "are prices low?" but "who governs, and with what accountability?"

**Chicago School (affirmative — not in wiki):** Efficiency is the proper goal of antitrust; consumer prices are the best proxy for efficiency. Broader political concerns (worker power, small business survival, regional equality) are legitimate but belong in labor law, campaign finance law, and social policy — not antitrust, where they introduce dangerous subjectivity.

## The 1981 Pivot

The Reagan administration implemented Chicago School antitrust thinking, sharply narrowing enforcement. In the four decades since:
- Over 500,000 mergers were permitted globally (2019 figure)
- Mega-mergers (valued at $4T/year average 2015–2019) became routine
- Nearly every American industry became more concentrated
- Antitrust enforcement shifted from structural (breaking up power) to behavioral (fining specific practices)

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Conflicts with pre-1981 American antitrust tradition (Brandeis, Douglas) which treated concentration of economic power as a *political* threat, not merely a price-efficiency question. See [[Thinkers/Zephyr Teachout]].
- > [!warning] Connects to [[Thinkers/James M. Buchanan]]: Buchanan also opposed the consumer welfare standard but from a public choice direction — government regulators pursuing "consumer welfare" will be captured by the regulated industries anyway. Different diagnosis, overlapping concern.
