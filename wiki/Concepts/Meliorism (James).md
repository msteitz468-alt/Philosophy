# Meliorism (James)

> One-sentence hook: Between optimism (the world's salvation is *inevitable*) and pessimism (it is *impossible*), meliorism holds that salvation is a real *possibility* — one that "becomes more and more of a probability the more numerous the actual conditions of salvation become," our own acts among them.

## Overview

The culminating doctrine of Lecture VIII ("Pragmatism and Religion"). Having argued that "on pragmatic principles we cannot reject any hypothesis if consequences useful to life flow from it," James asks which *type* of religion the pragmatist should hold — the monistic ("the world *must and shall be* saved," already safe in the Absolute) or the pluralistic ("the world *may be* saved"). "The whole clash of rationalistic and empiricist religion is thus over the validity of possibility."

So he analyzes "**possible**" pragmatically. A bare possibility means only "there is nothing extant capable of preventing the thing." But most possibilities are "concretely grounded": some of the *conditions of production* actually exist. "Possible chicken means actual egg — plus actual sitting hen, or incubator… As the actual conditions approach completeness the chicken becomes a better-and-better-grounded possibility." Applied to the world: "The more of [the conditions of deliverance] there are existent, the fewer preventing conditions you can find, the better-grounded is the salvation's possibility, the more probable does the fact of the deliverance become."

**Meliorism** (Latin *melior*, "better") names the resulting attitude: "Meliorism treats salvation as neither inevitable nor impossible. It treats it as a possibility, which becomes more and more of a probability the more numerous the actual conditions of salvation become." James notes it "has hitherto figured less as a doctrine than as an attitude in human affairs."

## Key Arguments

- **Optimism / pessimism / meliorism**: "Optimism… the doctrine that thinks the world's salvation inevitable" (the regnant European doctrine, the Absolute's guarantee); "Pessimism… thinks the salvation of the world impossible" (Schopenhauer); meliorism is the midpoint. "It is clear that pragmatism must incline towards meliorism."
- **Our acts as the world's growing-places**: "Every such ideal realized will be one moment in the world's salvation." Our ideals are "live possibilities, for we are their live champions and pledges." "Does our act then create the world's salvation so far as it makes room for itself…? Why not the workshop of being, where we catch fact in the making?" This links meliorism to free will read as "a doctrine of relief" and "novelty" (Lecture III) — see the design/free-will discussion in [[Concepts/Pragmatic Method (James)]].
- **The "world's author" scenario**: God offers you, before creation, "a world not certain to be saved… a real adventure, with real danger, yet it may win through… Will you trust yourself and trust the other agents enough to face the risk?" James: "if you are normally constituted, you would… accept the offer… loyalty to our old nurse Nature would forbid us to say no."
- **Accepting real loss**: siding with the "moralistic" over the "saccharine" tender-minded view, James is "willing that there should be real losses and real losers, and no total preservation of all that is." He rejects the absolutist's "*aufgehoben*" (every evil "preserved as an element essential but overcome"); evil is escaped "by dropping it out altogether, throwing it overboard." The Greek epigram of the shipwrecked sailor who "bids you set sail."
- **Pluralistic theism**: pragmatism "can be called religious if you allow that religion can be pluralistic or merely melioristic." God is "but one helper, *primus inter pares*, in the midst of all the shapers of the great world's fate." We stand to the universe "as our dogs and cats" stand to human life — "tangents to the wider life of things," yet with ideals that may "coincide." James points to his own *Varieties of Religious Experience* as evidence "the hypothesis of God… does work."
- **It depends on your faith, not logic**: "In the end it is our faith and not our logic that decides such questions, and I deny the right of any pretended logic to veto my own faith." The two Whitman readings ("To You") — monistic "you are already safe" vs. pluralistic "your better possibilities pick their way" — both "sanctify the human flux," but the pluralistic "agrees with the pragmatic temper best, for it immediately suggests… future experience… sets definite activities in us at work."

## Contrasts / Lineage

- **Vs. Leibnizian optimism / theodicy**: explicitly the target — Leibniz's "best of all possible worlds" and the Absolute's "the existence of ill… is the condition of the perfection of the eternal order" (Royce/Bradley) are the "saccharine," "too idyllic" optimisms meliorism replaces.
- **Vs. Schopenhauer's pessimism**: the other rejected pole.
- **Vs. Stoic/Augustinian providence**: contrast [[Concepts/Tranquillitas Ordinis - Peace as Order (Augustine)]] and the Stoic [[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)]] — for the Stoic/Christian, cosmic order/Providence is *given* and one aligns with it; for James the order is *unfinished* and partly *made* by our risk-taking acts. (Both, interestingly, prescribe wholehearted engagement.)
- **Vs. [[Concepts/Conatus (Spinoza)]] / [[Concepts/Freedom and Blessedness - Intellectual Love of God (Spinoza)]]**: Spinoza's blessedness lies in understanding necessity *sub specie aeternitatis* (a completed, deterministic whole); James's lies in contributing to an open future. Direct metaphysical opposites on whether reality is finished.
- **With [[Concepts/Humanism - Reality as Malleable (Schiller and James)]] and [[Concepts/The One and the Many (James)]]**: meliorism is the ethical/religious face of the "unfinished, growing" pluralistic universe; "the ideal as an ultimate, not as an origin."
- **Kinship with [[Concepts/Will to Power (Nietzsche)]]**: both affirm an open, dangerous world and creative agency ("the workshop of being") and reject quietist absorption — though James keeps a melioristic, cooperative, theism-friendly key vs. Nietzsche's aristocratic revaluation.
