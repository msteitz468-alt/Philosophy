# Proliferation and Tenacity (Feyerabend)

> Feyerabend's principle of **theoretical pluralism**: science thrives when investigators both **proliferate** (invent and develop theories inconsistent with the accepted view *and* with the facts) and show **tenacity** (hold on to a promising theory despite anomalies). Uniformity — a single dominant theory rigorously tested — "impairs [science's] critical power" and "endangers the free development of the individual."

## The Two Principles

- **Proliferation**: "Proliferation of theories is beneficial for science." Multiply alternatives; do not require new theories to satisfy the **consistency condition** (agreement with the reigning view), which merely "preserves the older theory, and not the better theory." Crucially, a reigning theory can be *tested* properly only against rivals: many facts and many of the theory's own weaknesses become visible **only** from the standpoint of an incompatible alternative ([[Concepts/Counterinduction (Feyerabend)|counterinduction]]). So pluralism is not a luxury — it is a *precondition* of genuine criticism.
- **Tenacity**: it is also rational to *retain* a theory in the face of anomalies and apparent refutation — to give it time to develop its potential (no interesting theory ever agrees with all the facts at birth). Niels Bohr's practice is Feyerabend's exemplar.

Held **together**, proliferation + tenacity describe a science of competing, stubbornly-developed research traditions — the opposite of a single orthodoxy submitted to decisive tests.

## Sources of the Idea

Feyerabend grounds proliferation explicitly in **J. S. Mill's *On Liberty***: just as Mill argues that truth needs the free collision of opinions (even false ones sharpen and complete our grasp of the true), so science needs a maximal variety of competing views. "Pluralism of theories and metaphysical views is not only important for methodology, it is also an essential part of a humanitarian outlook." A favourite illustration is the politically enforced **dualism** of Western and traditional medicine in 20th-century China, which (he claims) produced genuine discoveries neither tradition would have reached alone.

## Relation to Other Vault Content

- **Vs. Popper and the testing tradition**: Popper also valued bold competing conjectures, but tenacity (clinging to a theory through refutation) cuts against the [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)|falsificationist]] demand to expose every theory to refutation and drop the refuted. Feyerabend makes Popper's *vice* (immunizing a theory) into a contextual *virtue*. See [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]].
- **Vs. Kuhn's normal science**: where [[Concepts/Normal Science and Puzzle-Solving (Kuhn)|Kuhn]] sees long mono-paradigm periods as the productive norm, Feyerabend wants proliferation **all the time** — he criticized "the uniformity of paradigms" before *Structure*'s reception. Tenacity, by contrast, is common ground with Kuhn (the community clings to its paradigm through anomalies).
- **Liberty / pluralism lineage**: the Millian argument links to [[Concepts/Social Cohesion vs. Individual Liberty (Russell)]] and [[Concepts/Toleration (Locke)]]; it is the epistemic engine behind [[Concepts/Separation of Science and State (Feyerabend)]] (let many traditions compete).
- **With [[Concepts/Confirmation Holism (Duhem-Quine Thesis)]]**: underdetermination makes tenacity rational (data never uniquely forces abandonment) and proliferation fruitful (multiple webs can fit the same evidence).

## Vault Meta / AI

A direct maintenance heuristic: keep **multiple competing interpretations alive** rather than collapsing to one prematurely (the vault's contradiction pages institutionalize proliferation; "tenacity" is the discipline of developing a promising synthesis before discarding it at the first anomaly). For AI/agentic reasoning: ensemble/parallel hypotheses and self-consistency sampling are proliferation; "don't prune the second-best branch too early" is tenacity. The balance matters — unbounded proliferation is noise, unbounded tenacity is dogmatism; Feyerabend wants both in tension, judged in the concrete situation.
