# Persuasion & Sales Science

> Behavioral and social science principles for ethical, effective persuasion and sales, drawn primarily from Daniel Pink's MasterClass (Sales and Persuasion) and reinforced by Chris Voss (Negotiation) and the agribusiness sales playbook already in the vault. Key concepts: ambiverts, loss aversion, information asymmetry/parity, framing, USP/one-word equity, and avoiding common cognitive traps.

## Core Concepts (from Pink + related)
- **Ambivert**: Balance of extrovert/introvert traits; often the most effective persuaders (neither overwhelming nor too passive).
- **Loss aversion**: People feel losses more acutely than equivalent gains; frame around what the prospect stands to lose by not acting.
- **Information asymmetry vs parity**: One side knowing more creates distrust or bad deals; aim for transparency that builds long-term relationships.
- **Confirmation bias & fundamental attribution error**: Tendency to interpret new info through existing beliefs or attribute behavior to personality rather than situation. Counter with evidence and context.
- **One-word equity / USP**: The single word or attribute that defines your brand or offering (Google = "search"; Volvo = "safety"). Unique Selling Proposition that sets you apart.
- **Primacy effect**: Earliest items in a list or pitch are remembered best; structure accordingly.
- History of sales from ancient coinage/barter to modern "unicorn" companies and science-backed methods.

**The modern ABCs of sales/persuasion (Attunement, Buoyancy, Clarity)**:
- **A — Attunement**: Get out of your own head and into theirs (perspective-taking). See where they’re coming from, understand what they truly want, honor their point of view. Powerful people are often worse at this — dial back your authority. Empathy complements it: help them feel the change they should want.
- **B — Buoyancy**: Stay afloat in an “ocean of rejection.” De-catastrophize “no.” Watch the Three Ps (self-biases around Permanence, Pervasiveness, Personalization of failure).
- **C — Clarity**: Help people see the situation in a fresh light and surface problems they didn’t know they had. Shift from problem-solving to problem-finding / curation (in the age of information parity). Example: customer says floors are dirty → ask about the house → root cause might be the puppy → they actually need a groomer, not (just) a vacuum.

Practical application: pitch structure, handling objections, timing, and using behavioral insights to serve the client rather than manipulate.
