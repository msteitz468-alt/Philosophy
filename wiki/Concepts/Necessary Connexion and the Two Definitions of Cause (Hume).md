# Necessary Connexion and the Two Definitions of Cause (Hume)

> Hume's analysis of causation (*Enquiry* Section VII), applying the [[Concepts/Impressions and Ideas - Copy Principle (Hume)|Copy Principle]] to the most obscure idea in metaphysics — *power, force, energy, necessary connexion*. We never observe any tie binding cause to effect; we observe only that one event *constantly* follows another. The idea of necessity copies an *internal* impression: the mind's **customary transition** from cause to effect. Hence Hume's famous **two definitions of cause**. "All events seem entirely loose and separate… They seem conjoined, but never connected."

## The Search for the Impression

To "fix the precise meaning" of *necessary connexion*, Hume looks for its source impression in every candidate:

- **External objects**: "We only find that the one does actually, in fact, follow the other." The impulse of one billiard ball is *attended* with motion in the second — "this is the whole that appears to the outward senses." No quality binds them; "solidity, extension, motion… never point out any other event."
- **The will moving the body**: we know *that* the body obeys the will only by experience; the means are hidden. We cannot explain why the will governs tongue and fingers but not heart or liver; a man with a newly paralysed limb feels the same volition yet nothing follows; anatomy shows the will's immediate object is muscles and nerves "totally different from" the intended motion. No impression of power here.
- **The will raising an idea**: equally mysterious ("a production of something out of nothing"); its command is limited, varies with health and mood, and is known only by experience.

**Occasionalism** (Malebranche: God is the only true cause, bodies and minds mere "occasions") is examined and rejected — not because it is unintelligible but because it is "too bold," carries us "into fairy land… beyond the reach of our faculties," and rests on the *same* ignorance of power it claims to resolve (we are no less ignorant of how the Supreme Mind acts than of how bodies act). This is the vault's [[Concepts/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation (Ghazali and Ibn Rushd)|Ghazali/Ibn Rushd causation debate]] reopened in an empiricist key.

## The True Source: Customary Transition

There *is* an impression — but it arises only after **constant conjunction** and is *internal*. After many uniform instances of A followed by B, "the mind is carried by habit, upon the appearance of one event, to expect its usual attendant." This felt **determination of the mind** — "this customary transition of the imagination from one object to its usual attendant" — is "the sentiment or impression from which we form the idea of power or necessary connexion. Nothing farther is in the case."

The crucial point: nothing *new* is found in the objects between the first instance and the hundredth — only that the observer's mind has *changed*, acquiring the habit of inference. "The first time a man saw the communication of motion by impulse… he could not pronounce that the one event was connected, but only that it was conjoined." Necessity is thus, in a sense, *projected* from the mind onto the world. (Cf. [[Concepts/Custom and Belief (Hume)]].)

## The Two Definitions of Cause

Because the idea is drawn "from something extraneous and foreign to" the cause itself, Hume offers two definitions, each capturing one aspect:

1. **The "philosophical" / regularity definition** (constant conjunction, third-person): *"an object, followed by another, and where all the objects similar to the first are followed by objects similar to the second."* Glossed counterfactually: *"Or, in other words, where, if the first object had not been, the second never had existed."* (This parenthetical seeds modern *counterfactual* theories of causation.)
2. **The "natural" / psychological definition** (the mind's transition, first-person): *"an object followed by another, and whose appearance always conveys the thought to that other."*

Both are "drawn from circumstances foreign to the cause"; Hume says we can form no better definition that points to the connexion *in* the cause, because "we have no idea of this connexion" at all.

## The "New Hume" Debate

Does Hume *deny that there are* real powers/necessary connexions in nature (**regularity theory** — the traditional reading), or only deny that we can *know or represent* them, while granting they may exist (**sceptical realism**, the "New Hume")? The text supports both: "all events seem entirely loose and separate" and "these words are absolutely without any meaning" pull toward regularity; persistent talk of "secret powers," "the ultimate springs and principles," and "secret connexion" pulls toward realism. The central interpretive crux of Hume scholarship.

## Relevance to Vault

- **Causation cluster**: the empiricist resolution of the debate the vault holds via Aristotle's four causes, Aquinas' real natures, and [[Concepts/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation (Ghazali and Ibn Rushd)]]. Hume agrees with the occasionalists *that* no necessary connexion is observed in bodies, but refuses the theological fix and locates "necessity" in the observer — a genuinely new option neither Ibn Rushd (real powers) nor Ghazali (God as sole cause) occupies.
- **Vs. rationalism**: kills the Principle of Sufficient Reason and a priori insight into causes; directly limits Spinoza's claim that effects follow from causes as conclusions from premises ([[Concepts/Geometrical Method - Rationalism (Spinoza)]]) and Descartes' causal proof ([[Concepts/Causal Argument for God's Existence from Objective Reality (Descartes)]]), which assumes a robust causal principle Hume has dissolved.
- **AI / vault meta**: a deflationary, *predictive* account of causation — "cause" as a learned, high-frequency transition between event-types, plus a counterfactual gloss. Anticipates the modern split between *correlation/regularity* (definition 1) and *counterfactual/interventionist* causal models, and the warning that systems trained on constant conjunction acquire *expectations*, not insight into mechanism ("conjoined, but never connected"). A caution against mistaking robust correlation for understood mechanism.
- **Method**: licenses the vault to record *constant conjunctions* (this source reliably pairs with that claim; this pattern recurs) as the real content of "causal" notes, while flagging that the underlying "power" is not directly given.

**Open questions**:
- Regularity theory vs. sceptical realism (the New Hume) — which does the text license?
- Are the two definitions *equivalent*, *complementary*, or *in tension* (one in the objects, one in the mind)?
- Does the counterfactual gloss ("if the first had not been…") smuggle back a modal notion the regularity reading forbids?
