# Extended Mind (Clark and Chalmers)
> Cognition is not bounded by skin and skull: when an external resource is reliably coupled to us and does the work a brain process would, it is literally part of the mind.

## Summary

The extended-mind thesis originates in Clark and David Chalmers's "The Extended Mind" (*Analysis*, 1998), which Clark calls "a kind of modern classic." Its core is the **parity principle**: "If, as we confront some task, a part of the world functions as a process which, were it to go on in the head, we would have no hesitation in accepting as part of the cognitive process, then that part of the world is (for that time) part of the cognitive process." The founding thought experiment is **Otto and Inga**: Inga recalls the museum's address from biological memory; Otto, with Alzheimer's, reads it from his ever-present notebook. If Inga's belief counts as standing memory, parity says Otto's notebook entry does too. Clark updates Otto's notebook to the smartphone.

In *The Experience Machine* (2023), Chapter 6 ("Beyond the Naked Brain") gives the thesis what it originally lacked — an account of *what brains do*. [[Concepts/Predictive Processing (Clark)]] supplies it: "the brain does not care whether key information is stored in [its] own internal states... or outside, in notebooks, apps, and GPS systems." Predictive brains are therefore "the perfect internal organs for the creation of extended minds," and we are "natural-born cyborgs" — "cyborg or hybrid minds created without the need for invasive implants."

## Key Claims / Positions

- **No "magical membrane."** Skin and skull do not mark a cognitive boundary (Susan Hurley); "minds are not merely what brains do. They are what brains create — distributed cognitive engines spanning brain, body, and world." The target view Clark rejects is Jerry Fodor's: "If the mind happens in space at all, it happens somewhere north of the neck."
- **Epistemic action**: "actions selected to improve our state of knowledge rather than to directly achieve some practical goals." Predictive brains, minimizing *expected future prediction error*, are "ideally poised to discover" them — unifying practical and epistemic action under one quantity (Kirsh & Maglio's Tetris study; expert bartenders; Feynman insisting his notes are not a record but "working... You have to work on paper and this is the paper").
- **Woven resources**: "a trusted nonbiological structure that is delicately and constantly coupled to the rest of the system that we recognize as 'you'" — factored into the brain's estimations like a bodily capacity (SwiftKey, Grammarly, GPS, the North Sense).
- **The recruitment puzzle solved**: recruitment of external resources is effortless and catalytic (via "knowledge budgeting"), not a deliberative act — which is why the recruited array, not the recruiting brain, is the genuine cognitive engine.
- **An ethical choice**: where we draw the self/mind boundary is "at least in part an ethical choice," with consequences for how we treat memory-impaired people and technology dependence.

## How This Relates to Prior Thinkers

- **[[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]]**: the deepest resonance in the vault. The tool "ready-to-hand" that withdraws into transparent use ([[Concepts/Readiness-to-Hand and Presence-at-Hand (Heidegger)]]) is Otto's notebook: equipment woven into coping so seamlessly it stops being an external object. [[Concepts/Being-in-the-World (Heidegger)]] anticipates the anti-internalist claim that mind is always already world-involving.
- **[[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]**: Dennett's "Joycean machine" and center-of-narrative-gravity self, culturally installed via external scaffolding (writing, talking-to-oneself), is a close ally — both make the mind a brain-world hybrid, substrate-flexible.
- **[[Thinkers/Descartes]]**: the direct antithesis of the sealed *res cogitans*.
- **[[Concepts/Knowledge as Perception of Agreement or Disagreement of Ideas (Locke)]]**: contrasts sharply — where Locke locates knowledge in inner ideas, Clark locates it partly in the world.

## From Reality+ (Chalmers, 2022, ch. 16)

Chalmers's own retrospective adds the co-author's voice. Ned Block's quip: the thesis "was false when we wrote the article in the 1990s but... has since become true" (smartphones, internet). The updated argument (Ishi/Omar on the Sydney Opera House): internal memory is genuine knowledge; external memory playing the same role is equally knowledge — denying the parity premise is "biological chauvinism, or maybe skin and skull chauvinism." Extension requires the resource be "effectively glued to us" — constantly available and trusted; even another person can qualify (Ernie/Bert). **AR is a superior mind-extender** because of seamlessness — Chalmers explicitly invokes Heidegger's readiness-to-hand — on a trajectory notebook → smartphone → AR glasses → brain-computer interfaces → uploading. Consciousness itself is *not* claimed to extend, only non-conscious states. Against tech-pessimists from Plato's Thamus ("this discovery of yours will create forgetfulness in the learners' souls") to Nicholas Carr: "Google makes us smarter, not stupider." Moral consequence: stealing a phone becomes "more akin to assault."
