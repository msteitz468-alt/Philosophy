# Chickenization of Labor
> The monopolistic business model — pioneered in the U.S. poultry industry — that concentrates power in the hands of one processor/platform while transferring all risk and responsibility downward to nominally independent contractors, who are simultaneously surveilled, isolated from one another, and deprived of genuine agency.

## Summary

"Chickenization" is Zephyr Teachout's term for a structural pattern of labor control that originated in the U.S. chicken processing industry (dominated by Tyson, Perdue, and Pilgrim's Pride) and has since spread to farming more broadly, then to the platform/gig economy (Uber, Grubhub, Seamless, Doordash), franchising (McDonald's), retail (Amazon), and even education (Pearson).

The model has four features:

1. **Monopoly or near-monopoly control of the market.** The processor/platform is the only (or overwhelmingly dominant) buyer or intermediary in a region or sector. Chicken farmers can only sell to one buyer within geographic reach; Uber drivers face no meaningful alternative platform.

2. **Isolation.** Contracts prohibit workers/farmers from comparing pay, sharing information with each other, or collectively organizing. The structure is a corporate panopticon: the processor sees all the farmers, but the farmers cannot see each other.

3. **Asymmetric risk and reward.** The farmer/driver bears all operational risk (debt, equipment, liability) but has no real freedom to make the decisions that determine outcomes. Reward flows upward; risk flows downward.

4. **One-way surveillance and non-consensual experimentation.** Monsanto uses tractor data to spy on farmers; Tyson experiments on farmers (testing new feed, dimming schedules) without disclosure; Uber uses algorithmic black boxes to adjust pay ratios invisibly; Pearson experimented on 9,300 unknowing students. In each case, the platform uses data to maximize extraction while sharing nothing with the subjects.

## Key Examples

- **Chicken farming (Tyson):** Farmers own the debt but must use Tyson's feed, medicine, and lighting schedules. Payment is hidden and variable (a "tournament"), so farmers cannot compare or bargain. Speaking out invites retaliation.
- **Uber:** Drivers bear car costs and liability; Uber sets prices and keeps an undisclosed (sometimes up to 60%) commission. Drivers can be "deactivated" without cause. The technology (e-hailing) did not require monopoly; the monopoly was a deliberate power grab.
- **Monsanto/agriculture:** Farmers must buy new seeds annually (cannot save or reuse); switching costs are prohibitive; tractor software is proprietary (farmers cannot repair their own equipment).
- **Delivery apps (Grubhub):** Restaurants have no real choice but to list; the platform charges 15–30% commissions, retains customer data, and can demote restaurants that don't pay for better visibility.

## The "Technology" Alibi

Both favorable and critical accounts of the gig economy treat technology as the cause. Teachout rejects this: chickenization is not driven by technological change but by antitrust policy changes starting with Reagan in 1981. The technology is real and valuable; the *monopoly* layered on top of it is the problem. E-hailing apps could function inside a regulated, decentralized system. Monsanto's seed R&D could exist without forbidding farmers from saving seeds.

## Political Consequences

Chickenization transforms citizens into subjects. Workers/farmers who are:
- Forbidden to communicate with each other (isolation)
- Constantly monitored (surveillance)
- Wholly dependent on one buyer (monopsony)
- Unable to speak out without risking their livelihood (political silencing)

...are not free political actors. Their private economic subordination bleeds into their public lives. This is Teachout's bridge from economics to political philosophy: chickenization is not just labor exploitation, it is the erosion of the conditions for democratic self-governance.

## Connection to Panopticon

Teachout explicitly invokes Bentham's panopticon: the processor sees all the farmers/drivers but they cannot see each other. Compare [[Thinkers/Michel Foucault]]'s elaboration of panopticism as a technology of power — diffuse, impersonal, productive of self-regulation among the surveilled.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Teachout's diagnosis (monopoly is the cause) conflicts with standard gig-economy defenses (competition drove efficiency, consumers benefit). Her counter: Uber's consumers did not benefit — fares went up and driver pay went down once Uber achieved dominance.
- > [!warning] Connects to [[Concepts/Monopsony and Wage Suppression]]: chickenization is the labor-market face of monopsony — one buyer, setting wages. The wage mystery (why did wages stagnate despite productivity growth?) is partly explained by chickenization spreading to white-collar and service-sector work.
