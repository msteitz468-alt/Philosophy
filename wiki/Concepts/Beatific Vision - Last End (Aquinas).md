# Beatific Vision / Last End (Aquinas)

> In Thomas Aquinas' *Summa Theologica* (especially Prima Secundae, QQ. 1-5 and 3), the ultimate human happiness (*beatitudo*) is the beatific vision: the direct, intuitive contemplation of God in His essence by the glorified intellect in heaven. This is man's one last end — the perfect, complete, self-sufficient good that alone fully satisfies the will. In this life, happiness is imperfect (a participation via faith, hope, charity, and virtuous activity); perfect happiness is supernatural, requiring grace, and consists in an operation of the speculative intellect (not sensitive, not in created goods or even the sciences/angels ultimately, but in seeing God "face to face").

## Core Elements from Summa I-II

**Teleology and the Last End (Q. 1)**:

- Every agent acts for an end (final cause is first in intention, last in execution).
- Proper to rational nature (man) to act for an end with knowledge and free will, directing himself.
- Human acts (voluntary, from deliberate will) are specified by their end (morality derives from the end/intention).
- There is one last end: impossible for the will to be directed to several un-ordered last ends at once (contradicts the nature of perfect good; would leave appetite unsatisfied or create infinite regress).
- All men desire "happiness" (living well, the complete good) under the aspect of good, though they disagree on the *thing* in which it consists (pleasure, honor, wealth, virtue, etc.).
- External goods (prosperity, friends, health) are necessary instruments or conditions for excellent activity but not the end itself; complete wretchedness precludes calling someone happy.

**In What Happiness Consists (Q. 2)**:

- Not in wealth (instrumental, for the sake of something else).
- Not in honor (external, depends on others; more excellent to have the virtue that deserves honor).
- Not in fame/glory (uncertain, can be false).
- Not in power (can be used for evil; does not guarantee the good use).
- Not in pleasure (shared with animals; the life of grazing cattle or Sardanapallus is slavish).
- Not in any created good or finite operation alone.
- Ultimately: in the uncreated Good, God.

**What Happiness Is (Q. 3)**:

- In its cause/object: something uncreated — God Himself (Who by infinite goodness perfectly satisfies the will; "God is happiness by His Essence").
- In its essence (for man): something created in him — the attainment, possession, use, or enjoyment of the last end.
- An *operation* (last act of the operator; perfection as actual; "happiness is an operation according to perfect virtue," per Aristotle, but completed).
- Not an operation of the sensitive part (shared with irrational animals; happiness does not consist in goods of the body).
- Of the intellective part: specifically, an operation of the *speculative* (contemplative) intellect, not (primarily) practical.
- Not in the consideration of the speculative sciences (these are means or participations).
- Not (ultimately) in the consideration of separate substances (angels).
- "Whether it consists in the sole contemplation of God seen in His Essence" — Yes. The beatific vision: direct union of the created intellect with the uncreated God.
- In this life: imperfect (discontinuous; "we call men happy, but only as men" — Aristotle). Perfect happiness is promised for the next life ("as the angels in heaven," Matt. 22:30), where the operation is one, continual, everlasting.

**How We Attain It (later QQ.)**:

- Requires grace (supernatural aid elevating nature; "grace does not destroy nature but perfects it").
- Theological virtues (faith, hope, charity) + infused moral virtues.
- In this life: a participation through virtuous activity ordered to God, contemplation, and the gifts of the Holy Spirit.

**Relation to Aristotle (explicit throughout)**:

Aquinas constantly cites "the Philosopher" (Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics*). He affirms the function argument, eudaimonia as excellent activity of the rational soul over a complete life, the need for external goods and good upbringing, the superiority of the contemplative life — but shows that natural happiness is real yet incomplete. The ultimate end exceeds what unaided nature can achieve; perfect beatitude is the vision of God.
