# Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)

> A theoretical system is empirical or scientific if and only if it is *falsifiable* — that is, if its logical form is such that it is possible to deduce from it (with the help of other statements) singular "basic statements" that contradict it. "It must be possible for an empirical scientific system to be refuted by experience." (Popper, *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*, §6). This is the proposed criterion of demarcation between science on the one hand and logic/mathematics plus metaphysics (or pseudo-science) on the other.

## The Criterion

Popper's proposal (a convention/decision, not a "discovery" of essences):

- **Not verifiability**: Universal laws and theories can never be conclusively verified or reduced to finite singular observation statements (Hume's problem). Any demand for verifiability either excludes genuine science (theoretical physics) or lets in everything.

- **Falsifiability instead**: The logical possibility of *refutation*. A statement or system is falsifiable if there exist possible basic statements that would contradict it. Example (Popper): "It will rain or it will not rain here tomorrow" is *not* falsifiable (and thus not empirical). "It will rain here tomorrow" *is* falsifiable.

- **Asymmetry**: Universal statements are never derivable from singulars but *can* be contradicted by them (via modus tollens). This is the only strictly deductive inference that moves "in the inductive direction."

- **Degree of falsifiability**: The more a theory prohibits (the larger the class of potential falsifiers), the greater its empirical content and the more it "says" about the world. Laws of nature are "laws" precisely because they prohibit.

- **Not a criterion of meaning**: Falsifiability separates two classes of *meaningful* statements: the falsifiable (empirical) and the non-falsifiable (metaphysical). Early verificationism (Wittgenstein, some positivists) that equated meaningfulness with verifiability/ reducibility to atomic observation statements was rejected by Popper as both too narrow for science and self-undermining (it eliminates the very universal laws science seeks).

## Relation to the Problem of Induction and Demarcation

- **Hume's problem ("the problem of induction")**: Accepted in full. No rational justification for inferring universals from singulars; any "principle of induction" leads to regress or apriorism. Probabilistic weakenings do not escape the difficulty.

- **Kant's problem ("the problem of demarcation")**: The more fundamental problem. Positivist reliance on induction as the mark of the scientific fails precisely because induction cannot demarcate (it either rules out science or fails to rule out metaphysics). Falsifiability provides a workable alternative convention that preserves the empirical character of theoretical science while drawing a line.

- By adopting falsifiability, the apparent contradiction between empiricism ("experience decides") and Hume's critique disappears: experience can decide *negatively* (falsification) without any inductive step. Science remains rational and empirical without needing a justifying principle of induction.

**Quinean holism as a challenge**: Quine's confirmation holism (the web of belief; see [[Concepts/Confirmation Holism (Duhem-Quine Thesis)]]) suggests that there are no isolated empirical contents for individual statements or theories. Any apparent falsifying basic statement can be absorbed by adjustments elsewhere in the web (auxiliaries, meanings, ontology, even logic). Popper's methodology remains a powerful proposal for maximizing critical exposure and avoiding ad-hoc immunizations, but holism implies that the unit of potential revision is larger than a single conjecture. The tension between localized severe testing and web-wide underdetermination is a central post-positivist issue (also engaged by Kuhn). See [[Sources/Two Dogmas of Empiricism - W.V.O. Quine (1951)]] and [[Thinkers/W.V.O. Quine]].

## Methodological Supplement (Rules of the Empirical Method)

Falsifiability alone is insufficient as a definition because it is always logically possible to evade refutation (ad hoc auxiliaries, redefinitions, refusal to accept falsifying evidence). Popper therefore supplements with methodological *rules* whose supreme rule is:

> "The other rules of scientific procedure must be designed in such a way that they do not protect any statement in science against falsification."

Examples:
- Auxiliary hypotheses are acceptable only if they increase the falsifiability (empirical content) of the system, not if they decrease it.
- A theory that has proved its mettle may not be dropped without "good reason" (better testable replacement, or actual falsification of a consequence).
- The aim is not to save untenable systems but to expose all candidates to the fiercest critical struggle.

## Distinctions and Clarifications

- **Falsifiability vs. falsification**: A theory may be falsifiable in principle yet never actually falsified (or falsified only after long survival). "We say that a theory is falsified only if we have accepted basic statements which contradict it" *and* a reproducible falsifying hypothesis has been corroborated.

- **Not conclusive falsification in practice**: Duhem-Quine type holism is acknowledged (a falsifying observation contradicts a whole system, not an isolated hypothesis). But the *logical* possibility of singling out consequences remains; the methodology aims to maximize exposure rather than guarantee certainty.

- **Metaphysics can be fruitful**: Atomism, determinism as research programmes, field ideas, etc., have guided science while not (yet) fully falsifiable. They are not banished; they simply do not count as *scientific* until they take falsifiable form.

- **Pseudo-science examples** (in the text and later popularizations): Theories that explain any outcome (Adlerian psychology, some Marxism, early psychoanalysis) by immunizing stratagems. Contrast with Einstein's general relativity, which risked refutation by the 1919 eclipse observations.

## The *Conjectures and Refutations* Illustration (Marx/Freud/Adler vs. Einstein)

The famous popular statement of the criterion comes from the 1953 Cambridge lecture "Science: Conjectures and Refutations" (in *C&R* ch. 1), recounting how Popper reached the problem in 1919–20. He contrasts two kinds of theory by the attitude their adherents take to evidence:

- **Einstein's general relativity** made a **risky, prohibitive prediction** — that light from stars near the sun would be deflected by a measurable, calculable amount — tested by **Eddington's 1919 eclipse** expedition. The theory *forbade* a definite observable outcome and could have been refuted by it. This is the mark of science.

- **Marx's theory of history, Freud's psychoanalysis, and Adler's "individual psychology"** seemed to their followers to be confirmed *everywhere*: every newspaper, every clinical case, every act fit the theory. Popper's drowning/rescuing-a-child example: Freud explains both the would-be murderer (repression) and the would-be rescuer (sublimation); Adler explains both (inferiority feelings). A theory that can explain *any conceivable* behaviour forbids nothing — and its apparent strength is in fact its weakness. (Adler: "Because of my thousandfold experience." Popper: "And with this new case, I suppose, your experience has become thousand-and-one-fold.")

The **seven conclusions** (Popper's reformulation):

1. It is easy to obtain confirmations for nearly every theory — if we look for them.
2. **Confirmations should count only if they result from *risky predictions*** — events that, but for the theory, we would have expected *not* to happen.
3. Every good scientific theory is a **prohibition**: the more it forbids, the better.
4. **Irrefutability is not a virtue of a theory but a vice.**
5. Every genuine *test* is an attempt to **falsify**; testability = falsifiability, and it comes in degrees (riskier theories are more testable).
6. Confirming evidence counts only when it is the result of a *genuine test* — i.e. a serious but **unsuccessful attempt to falsify** ("corroborating evidence").
7. Theories rescued from refutation by ad hoc auxiliary assumptions or re-interpretation are saved only by a **"conventionalist twist / stratagem"** that lowers or destroys their scientific status.

This distinguishes a *falsified* genuine theory (e.g. early testable Marxism, refuted then immunized) from a *non-testable* one (psychoanalysis, "in the manner of myths"). Astrology fails by making predictions vague enough to be irrefutable. (See [[Concepts/Critique of Historicism and Dialectic (Popper)]] for the Marxism case.)

## Legacy and Open Questions in the Vault

- **Direct impact on the induction concept**: See [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]] — Popper accepts the negative result and supplies the methodological bypass (conjecture-and-refutation) rather than custom (Hume) or a priori principle (Russell in *Problems of Philosophy*).

- **With Russell**: Russell's logical analysis and "piecemeal" scientific spirit in philosophy align; his verdict on Hume as leading to a "dead end" is answered by Popper's deductivist critical rationalism. Popper offers a way to continue the empiricist project without inductivism.

- **With Kant**: Explicitly frames demarcation as Kant's problem and the central problem of epistemology, while rejecting the synthetic a priori solution.

- **With Epicurus**: Ancient anticipation in the empiricist tolerance for multiple explanations provided none are contradicted by clear sensation. Popper rigorizes this into a full logic and methodology for modern theoretical science.

- **With Quine**: Quine's holism (see [[Concepts/Confirmation Holism (Duhem-Quine Thesis)]]) challenges the assumption that individual theories have isolable empirical content against which they can be cleanly falsified. Any "falsifying" observation can be absorbed by web-wide adjustments. This is a live tension with Popperian methodology rather than a refutation of it; the falsifiability demand remains a powerful discipline against ad-hoc protection of failing strands. See [[Sources/Two Dogmas of Empiricism - W.V.O. Quine (1951)]] and [[Thinkers/W.V.O. Quine]].

- **Vault meta / AI**: The falsifiability demand is a standing challenge to any claim in the wiki or any agent output: "What would count as a counter-example? What basic statement (observation from sources, experimental result, user feedback) would refute this?" High-falsifiability pages/concepts are preferred (precise, risky, informative). The wiki's contradiction pages and lint process are institutionalized falsification attempts. For agentic systems: generate hypotheses that are bold *and* testable; design severe tests; do not protect failing explanations with unfalsifiable patches.

- **Open tensions** (flagged):
  - The decision/conventional element in accepting basic statements: how much "objectivity" remains?
  - Historical/sociological critiques (Kuhn): actual science proceeds mostly by paradigm-protecting [[Concepts/Normal Science and Puzzle-Solving (Kuhn)|normal science]], and [[Concepts/Anomaly and Crisis (Kuhn)|anomalies are not bare falsifiers]] — Kuhn even doubts falsifying experiences exist. Now primary-sourced; see [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]] and [[Thinkers/Thomas Kuhn]].
  - Anarchist critique ([[Thinkers/Paul Feyerabend]], [[Sources/Against Method - Paul Feyerabend|*Against Method*]]): falsificationism is just one more rule that the history of science breaks; applied strictly it "would have killed Copernicanism" — Galileo advanced by [[Concepts/Counterinduction (Feyerabend)|counter-inductively]] retaining a theory that conflicted with the evidence. "Popper's critical rationalism fails for the same reasons" as positivism. See [[Concepts/Epistemological Anarchism (Feyerabend)]].
  - Verisimilitude: Popper's development (truth-content minus falsity-content) as the aim that corroboration is thought to track — now primary-sourced in [[Concepts/Verisimilitude (Truthlikeness) (Popper)]] (from *C&R* ch. 10).
  - Relation to probability and confirmation: explicit rejection of "degree of corroboration = probability."
