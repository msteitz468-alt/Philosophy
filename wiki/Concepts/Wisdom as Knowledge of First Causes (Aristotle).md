# Wisdom as Knowledge of First Causes (Aristotle)

> "All men by nature desire to know." Wisdom (*sophia*) is the highest knowledge — knowledge of the first principles and causes, pursued for its own sake. The opening of the *Metaphysics* (Book I) builds a ladder from sensation up to first philosophy. *Metaphysics* I–II.

## Summary

The *Metaphysics* opens with a claim about human nature: "All men by nature desire to know," shown by the delight we take in the senses "even apart from their usefulness." Aristotle then traces an ascending **hierarchy of cognition**:

**sensation (*aisthēsis*) → memory (*mnēmē*) → experience (*empeiria*) → art (*technē*) → wisdom (*sophia*)**

Sensation is shared by animals; memory makes some animals "intelligent"; in humans, many memories of the same thing yield *experience*; and when experience produces "one universal judgement about a class," we have *art*. The decisive epistemic divide: **experience knows *that* (*hoti*) something is so; art and science know *why* (*dioti*) — they grasp the *cause*.** The master-craftsman is wiser than the manual laborer not because he is better at doing but because "he knows the causes of the things that are done." Knowledge for its own sake outranks utility; the theoretical sciences arose "after the necessities of life were secured."

**Wisdom** is then characterized as the science that: knows all things (universally); knows what is hardest (the most universal, farthest from sense); is most exact; is most instructive (treats causes); is choiceworthy for its own sake (most free, "most divine"); and is architectonic, knowing the end (the good) for which each thing is done. Its proper objects are **the first principles and causes** ([[Concepts/The Four Causes (Aristotle)]]) — "by reason of these, and from these, all other things come to be known."

Book II adds that **philosophy is rightly called knowledge of the truth** ("the end of theoretical knowledge is truth"), that the **principles of eternal things are most true** (since they cause the truth of other things), and — load-bearing for the whole treatise — that **the causal series cannot be infinite** in any of the four kinds: there must be a first cause, else "the Good is eliminated" and knowledge, which requires grasping a finite set of causes, becomes impossible. Truth is "easy and hard": no one wholly misses it, yet "as the eyes of bats are to the blaze of day, so is the reason in our soul to the things most evident by nature." Method, finally, must fit the subject: "the minute accuracy of mathematics is not to be demanded in all cases, but only in things which have no matter."

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Innate desire to know**; the senses (esp. sight) loved for themselves.
- **Cognitive ladder** sensation→memory→experience→art→wisdom; the art/experience break = knowing *why* vs. *that*.
- **Wisdom = science of first causes and principles**, pursued for its own sake — "first philosophy."
- **No infinite regress of causes** (Book II) → a first principle exists; required for both the Good (final cause) and knowability.
- **Method proportioned to subject**: mathematical precision only where there is no matter; "it is absurd to seek at the same time knowledge and the way of attaining knowledge."

## How This Sits Among Thinkers

- **Versus [[Thinkers/Plato]]**: Aristotle grounds knowledge in *sensation and experience* rising to universals by abstraction, rather than recollection of separate Forms — the empiricist seed later radicalized by [[Thinkers/Locke]] ([[Concepts/Tabula Rasa - Blank Slate or Empty Cabinet (Locke)]], [[Concepts/Sensation and Reflection as the Two Fountains of Ideas (Locke)]]).
- **The "that" vs. "why" distinction** prefigures the descriptive/explanatory divide in philosophy of science; cf. [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)]] and the causal-explanation program of [[Concepts/Ladder of Causation (Pearl)]].
- **"Method proportioned to subject"** is the same methodological humility as in [[Sources/Aristotle - Nicomachean Ethics (Book I)]] (do not demand mathematical precision in ethics) — a unifying Aristotelian theme.
- **No-infinite-regress** is the engine that, in Book XII and in [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]], drives the argument to a first cause / [[Concepts/The Unmoved Mover (Aristotle)]].
- **Eudaimonia link**: the life of *theōria* (contemplation), here exalted as "most divine," is the activity the *Ethics* crowns as highest happiness — see [[Concepts/Eudaimonia]].
