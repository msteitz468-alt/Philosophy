# Separation of Science and State (Feyerabend)

> Feyerabend's political conclusion: **science is one tradition among many, not the one road to truth**, so in a free society it should be **separated from the state just as the churches are**. Research should be under democratic/public control, science should be taught "as one view among many," and non-scientific traditions (myth, religion, magic, traditional medicine) have the right "to stand on their own feet."

## The Argument

If there is no universal scientific method ([[Concepts/Epistemological Anarchism (Feyerabend)|epistemological anarchism]]) and "Reason" is not a tradition-independent measure, then **neither science nor rationality is a universal standard of excellence** — each is "a *particular tradition*, unaware of its historical grounding." Science's results are "objectively" on a par with those of other traditions; they "appear magnificent" only relative to certain values (the moon landings thrill "well-conditioned materialistic contemporaries," but a mystic's vision of God may, from another tradition, count for more). The excellence of science is *real but relative*, not absolute.

From this Feyerabend draws an institutional conclusion modeled on the **separation of church and state**:
- **Separation of science and state**: the state should not privilege or enforce science as *the* truth.
- **Democratic control**: "scientific institutions, research programmes, and suggestions must… be subjected to public control"; laypeople ("non-experts often know more than experts") should have a say. Government funding already treats science as "many enterprises," not one.
- **Education**: "stop the scientists from taking over education and from teaching… as 'fact'" a single worldview; teach science "as one view among many and not as the one and only road to truth and reality."
- **Defence of other traditions**: "non-scientific cultures, procedures and assumptions can also stand on their own feet and should be allowed to do so." Societies, "especially democratic societies, must be protected from science" — as science must be protected from ideologies.

The deeper structure: a **free society** is "a society in which all traditions are given equal rights"; its basic structure is "a protective structure, not an ideology… like an iron railing not… a conviction." It therefore "cannot be based on any particular creed; for example, it cannot be based on rationalism." Claims that a rational, "objective" debate should settle these matters are exposed as a rhetorical trick — the standards "only appear to be 'objective' because reference to the group that profits from their use has been omitted" ("it is rational to do…" = "I want you to do…" with the speaker hidden).

## A Note on Relativism

Feyerabend's "all traditions equal rights" sounds relativist, and he is usually read that way — but he **explicitly disowns** the label: he is "neither a populist for whom an appeal to 'the people' is the basis of all knowledge, nor a relativist for whom there are no 'truths as such.'" His thesis is *political and protective* (equal rights, free exchange), not the metaphysical claim that all views are equally true. He also warns that liberalizing "fact" "can have grave repercussions." The tension between this disclaimer and the leveling rhetoric is one of the book's open problems.

## Vault Meta / AI

A caution about **epistemic authority**: presenting one framework (or "the science," or a single model's output) as *the* uniquely rational truth omits "reference to the group that profits" and forecloses alternatives. Practical residue (bounded, not dogma): give rival traditions/sources a genuine hearing before deferring to the consensus; keep "expert" and "lay/indigenous/local" knowledge in dialogue. For AI governance: an argument by analogy for *pluralistic, publicly-controlled* deployment rather than a single enforced standard — while heeding Feyerabend's own warning that unlimited liberalization of "fact" has costs.
