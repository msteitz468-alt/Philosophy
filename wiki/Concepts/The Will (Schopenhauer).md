# The Will (Schopenhauer)

> The blind, insatiable, non-rational metaphysical force that constitutes the noumenal essence of all reality.

## Summary

In *The World as Will and Representation* (1819), [[Thinkers/Arthur Schopenhauer|Arthur Schopenhauer]] identifies the Kantian *thing-in-itself* (the ultimate reality behind sensory appearances) as **Will** (*Wille*). 

Unlike individual human willpower or conscious desire, the metaphysical Will is a single, unified, aimless force that drives all nature. It manifests at various levels of objectification—from physical gravity and chemical attractions, to biological growth and reproduction, and finally to conscious human desire. Because the Will is characterized by endless, unsatisfied striving, it is the root cause of all suffering in the world.

---

## Core Characteristics

### 1. The Noumenal Essence of the World
Schopenhauer accepts [[Thinkers/Kant|Kant]]'s division of the world into:
* **Representation** (*Vorstellung*): The world as it appears to our minds, structured by space, time, and causality (the *principium individuationis*).
* **Thing-in-Itself**: The true reality behind appearances, which Kant held to be unknowable.

Schopenhauer argues that we have a unique, direct access to the thing-in-itself through our own bodies. When we act, we do not merely observe our body moving from the outside; we feel the inner drive—the Will—that moves it. Extrapolating from this internal experience, Schopenhauer concludes that the same Will is the inner essence of *all* things.

### 2. Unity and the Principium Individuationis
Because space and time are properties of our cognitive representation (not of the noumenon), the thing-in-itself must be free from space and time. 
* Consequently, the Will is **one** and undivided. 
* The apparent multiplicity of individual things in the world is an illusion created by the cognitive filter of space and time (the *principium individuationis* or principle of individuation). 
* In reality, a stone, a tree, a tiger, and a human are all objectifications of the exact same metaphysical Will.

### 3. The Source of Suffering and Pessimism
Because the Will is unified and infinite, yet objectifies itself in finite, competing individuals, it is constantly in conflict with itself (e.g., the predator eating the prey is the Will consuming itself). 

In conscious beings, the Will manifests as **desire**. Since the Will has no final goal or satisfaction:
* Every satisfied desire is immediately replaced by boredom or new desires.
* Striving is endless, and suffering is the default state of life.
* Life is an endless oscillation between the pain of unsatisfied desire and the boredom of satisfaction.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

> [!WARNING]
> Schopenhauer's Will-driven pessimism directly contradicts the rationalist optimism of [[Thinkers/Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz|Leibniz]], who argued that the universe is governed by divine sufficient reason and is the "best of all possible worlds." See [[Contradictions/Schopenhauer's Pessimism vs. Leibnizian Optimism|Schopenhauer's Pessimism vs. Leibnizian Optimism]].

### Crux of Self-Abnegation
If the Will is the sole cause and essence of everything, how is it possible for humans to **deny** or quiet the Will (through asceticism or art)? 
* This is the central tension of Schopenhauer's ethics: the Will must somehow turn against itself. 
* Schopenhauer describes this as a rare, mysterious state where the Will, having achieved full self-knowledge through human intellect, decides to cease its own striving.
