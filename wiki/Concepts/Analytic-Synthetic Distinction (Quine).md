# Analytic-Synthetic Distinction (Quine)

> The supposed fundamental cleavage between truths which are analytic (grounded in meanings independently of matters of fact) and truths which are synthetic (grounded in fact). Quine argues that the distinction, as traditionally drawn and as relied upon by logical empiricists, cannot be made precise without circularity or without collapsing into a pragmatic/holistic picture.

## The Traditional Distinction (as Quine reconstructs it)

- **Analytic statements**: true by virtue of meanings and independently of fact. Kant: "attributes to its subject no more than is already conceptually contained in the subject." Leibniz: true in all possible worlds. Modern: whose denial is self-contradictory.
- **Synthetic statements**: true (or false) by virtue of how the world is.
- Two classes of analytic statements (Quine's reconstruction):
  1. Logically true statements (e.g., "No unmarried man is married") — remain true under all reinterpretations of their non-logical components (given the logical particles 'no', 'un-', 'is', etc.).
  2. Statements that can be turned into logical truths by substituting synonyms for synonyms (e.g., "No bachelor is married" becomes the above by replacing "bachelor" with its synonym "unmarried man").

The second class is the philosophically important and problematic one for empiricists. It requires a prior notion of **cognitive synonymy** that does not itself presuppose analyticity.

## Quine's Critique (from "Two Dogmas")

- **Meaning is not naming/reference**: Frege and Russell examples show that terms can name the same thing ("Evening Star" = "Morning Star"; "Scott" and "the author of Waverley"; "9" and "the number of planets") yet differ in meaning. Sameness of reference can be a matter of empirical discovery, not meaning.
- **For predicates**: extension (the class of entities of which the term is true) must be distinguished from meaning/intension. "Creature with a heart" and "creature with a kidney" are (perhaps) alike in extension but unlike in meaning.
- **Definition does not ground synonymy**: Lexicographers report antecedent facts of usage; they do not create the synonymy. Explication (Carnap) improves upon usage in favored contexts but still rests on pre-existing synonymies for those contexts. Only the artificial introduction of entirely new notation creates synonymy by fiat.
- **Interchangeability *salva veritate*** (everywhere without change of truth-value) is insufficient for cognitive synonymy in an extensional language: it guarantees only that the relevant biconditional is true, not that the truth is meaning-based/analytic. In a language containing an intensional adverb "necessarily" (true only of analytic statements), the test becomes circular — it presupposes that we already understand analyticity.
- **Carnap's state-descriptions and semantical rules fail to capture the second class**: State-descriptions (exhaustive assignments of truth-values to atomic sentences) reconstruct only logical truth (first class), because they assume the atomic statements are mutually independent (no extralogical synonym-pairs). Semantical rules either explicitly use the unexplained term "analytic" or arbitrarily label certain truths as "true by the semantical rules" (the heading itself does no explanatory work).
- **The distinction is a "metaphysical article of faith" for empiricists**: a boundary between analytic and synthetic statements "simply has not been drawn." The problem of making sense of "S is analytic for L" is no easier for artificial languages with explicit rules than for natural language.

## Relation to Prior Vault Content

- **Direct attack on [[Concepts/Hume's Fork (Relations of Ideas vs Matters of Fact)]]**: Hume's exhaustive division (relations of ideas = a priori, denial implies contradiction; matters of fact = contingent, contrary always conceivable) is the classical ancestor of the analytic/synthetic distinction. The positivists' analytic/synthetic + verification picture is a sharpened, linguistic descendant. Quine argues the fork (and its descendants) is not exhaustive or sharp in the way required.
- **Kant's problem**: Kant's question "How are synthetic a priori judgments possible?" presupposes that the analytic/synthetic and a priori/a posteriori distinctions can be drawn and that they are not coextensive. Quine undercuts the presupposition.
- **Vs. Carnap / logical empiricism**: The distinction (plus reductionism) was the main tool for demarcating meaningful science and eliminating metaphysics as nonsense. Quine shows the tool does not work on its own terms.

## Relevance to Vault

- **Triage of claims**: For any assertion in the wiki (or in an AI response), ask whether it is being treated as analytic/meaning-based (definitional, true by the schema or by stipulated synonymy) or as synthetic/empirically answerable to sources. Many "philosophical" or "methodological" claims in practice blur the line.
- **Schema and frontmatter as "semantical rules"**: The SCHEMA and page templates function like Carnapian semantical rules. They are useful conventions, not discoveries of a pre-existing analytic/synthetic boundary. They can (and should) be revised pragmatically when they no longer expedite integration of new sources.
- **AI / prompting**: "Analytic" constraints in prompts (type systems, schema, few-shot exemplars that function as definitions) vs. "synthetic" grounding in retrieved sources or tool outputs. Over-reliance on the former without the latter produces fluent but unmoored output (the second dogma's reductionist hope in miniature).
- **Lint heuristic**: claims that cannot be classified as either (a) true by the explicit conventions of the schema + prior syntheses or (b) directly traceable to and revisable by specific raw sources are candidates for the flames (Hume) or at least for explicit flagging as posits.
