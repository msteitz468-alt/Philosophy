# Simulation Theology (Chalmers)
> If we're simulated, the simulator is "a sort of god" — creator, powerful, knowledgeable, but "she need not be especially good": a local, natural god closest to Plato's demiurge.

## Summary

*Reality+* ch. 7. The simulator satisfies the creator/power/knowledge attributes of divinity for our *universe* while remaining a **local god, not a cosmic god** — a being inside the larger cosmos, "a god that even a naturalist can believe in." David Pearce: the simulation argument is "the most interesting argument for the existence of God in a long time." The problem of evil is no obstacle, since goodness was never claimed.

Classic theistic arguments reviewed: Anselm's ontological argument (undermined by a Gaunilo-style perfect-hamburger parody), al-Ghazali's cosmological argument, and design. The **fine-tuning argument** is "quite consonant" with a simulator-god (simulators prefer life-bearing settings), and simulation reasoning renders God and multiverse explanations compatible rather than rival.

**Statistical simulation theology**: our simulator is probably an AI rather than biological, probably a scientist rather than an entertainer, probably running a large **batch** of simulations, and probably not paying attention to us. Simulations may have **stopping criteria** (Preston Greene: termination once sims develop simulation technology). A naturalized afterlife via code transfer is possible; there is a non-negligible chance we are **simsims**, and Jonathan Schaffer's no-fundamental-level metaphysics allows "simulations all the way up." No religion follows: "Any god that demands our worship doesn't deserve it" — Chalmers's atheism reduces to "No possible being is worthy of worship." Stanisław Lem's *Non Serviam* anticipated all of this: "I am thus omnipotent with respect to them, but indeed, from this it does not follow that they owe me anything."

## How This Relates to Prior Thinkers

- **[[Thinkers/Plato]]**: the *Timaeus* demiurge ([[Sources/Timaeus and Critias - Plato (Oxford, 2008)]]) is the closest classical model — a craftsman-god working within a larger order.
- **[[Thinkers/Anselm of Canterbury]]** ([[Sources/Proslogion - Anselm of Canterbury (1078)]]) and **[[Thinkers/Al-Ghazali]]**: their arguments surveyed and set aside; the simulation argument replaces a priori proof with statistical reasoning.
- **[[Thinkers/Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz]]**: the ch. 18 "simulation theodicy" (create all worlds where happiness outweighs suffering; simulators aren't omniscient) parallels the best-of-all-possible-worlds — see [[Concepts/Ethics of Simulated Beings and Virtual Society (Chalmers)]].
- **[[Concepts/Karma and Reincarnation as Quest Engine (Virk)]]**: Virk derives a full religious cosmology from the simulation frame; Chalmers derives a deliberately thin, worship-free theology — a sharp contrast in method from the same premise.
