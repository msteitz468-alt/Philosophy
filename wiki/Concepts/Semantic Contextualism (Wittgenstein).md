# Semantic Contextualism (Wittgenstein)
> The debate surrounding whether the later Wittgenstein's remarks on proper names and family resemblances endorse modern semantic contextualism or serve to critique all systematic, constructive theories of meaning.

## Concept Overview
Semantic contextualism is the view in the philosophy of language that the truth conditions and semantic content of an utterance are shaped in deep, pervasive ways by the context of its utterance, rather than being determined solely by stable, context-independent rules. 

Commentators like Charles Travis, François Recanati, and Anne Bezuidenhout have read the later Wittgenstein as an early champion of contextualism. They point to:
1. Wittgenstein's treatment of proper names in *Philosophical Investigations* §79, where the descriptions associated with a name vary depending on context.
2. The concept of family resemblances (§65–67), which suggests concepts do not have fixed, rigid boundaries but are extended from case to case as context demands.

## The Critique: Wittgenstein vs. Contextualism
In *Wittgenstein's Philosophical Investigations: A Critical Guide* (2010), Jason Bridges presents a detailed critique of the contextualist reading of Wittgenstein:
- **Misreading §79**: Bridges argues that in §79, Wittgenstein is not proposing that the semantic sense of a proper name shifts from context to context. Rather, Wittgenstein is criticizing Russellian descriptivism. When a speaker provides different descriptions of "N" on different occasions, they are not specifying a context-dependent semantic sense; they are offering pragmatic tools to resolve specific, local doubts of the listener.
- **Shared Sensibility over Shifting Semantics**: For Wittgenstein, our ability to apply a family-resemblance concept like "game" to new cases does not show that the concept has a shifting, context-dependent meaning. Instead, it shows that we share a stable linguistic *sensibility* or practice. We agree in our judgments of what counts as a game, without needing context-shifting semantic rules.
- **The Anti-Theoretical Stance**: Bridges argues that semantic contextualism is a form of constructive **Truth-Conditional Semantics (TCS)**, attempting to build a theoretical, systematic model of how meaning is constituted. However, Wittgenstein's entire metaphilosophy is aimed at exposing the futility of such constructive theories. Wittgenstein’s appeals to "particular circumstances" are meant to show that language works without any underlying, hidden semantic mechanism that philosophy needs to explain.
