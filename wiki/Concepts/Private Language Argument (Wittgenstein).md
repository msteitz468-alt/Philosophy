# Private Language Argument (Wittgenstein)
> A language whose words referred to inner sensations only the speaker could know would be incoherent — it could have no standard of correctness, so "whatever is going to seem right to me is right" means here we cannot talk about 'right' at all.

## Summary

The **private language argument** (*Philosophical Investigations* §§243–315) is [[Thinkers/Ludwig Wittgenstein|Wittgenstein]]'s assault on a picture as old as [[Thinkers/Descartes|Descartes]] and [[Thinkers/Locke|Locke]]: that each of us first knows our own inner states directly and privately, and only secondarily, by analogy, attributes them to others — language for the inner being grounded in private acts of inner ostension. A genuinely **private language** is defined as one whose "individual words... are to refer to what can only be known to the person speaking; to his immediate private sensations. So another person cannot understand the language" (§243). Wittgenstein argues that no such language is possible. (Ordinary talk of pains and moods is *not* a private language in this sense — it is public and learned.)

---

## Core Mechanics

### 1. The diarist and the absence of a criterion (§258)
Imagine keeping a diary about the recurrence of a sensation, writing "S" each day it occurs, fixing the meaning of "S" by inwardly concentrating my attention on the sensation. But how do I check, next time, that *this* is really the same — that I am using "S" correctly? There is nothing to appeal to but memory, which is the very thing in question. "One would like to say: whatever is going to seem right to me is right. And that only means that here we can't talk about 'right'" (§258). Where no distinction between *being right* and *seeming right* can be drawn, there is no rule and no meaning — connecting directly to [[Concepts/Rule-Following (Wittgenstein)|"it is not possible to obey a rule privately"]] (§202).

### 2. Justification needs something independent
"Justification consists in appealing to something independent" (§265); checking one memory against another is "as if someone were to buy several copies of the morning paper to assure himself that what it said was true." A sign whose application answers to nothing public is an idle wheel — "a wheel that can be turned though nothing else moves with it is not part of the mechanism" (§271).

### 3. How sensation-words are really learned (§244)
Wittgenstein offers the positive picture: words for sensations are grafted onto natural expression. "Words are connected with the primitive, the natural, expressions of the sensation and used in their place... the verbal expression of pain replaces crying and does *not* describe it." A child taught to say "it hurts" is taught new pain-behaviour, not given a name for a private object. So the language is public from the start.

### 4. The beetle in a box (§293)
The decisive image. Suppose everyone has a box with something in it called a "beetle"; no one can look into anyone else's box. "Here it would be quite possible for everyone to have something different in his box... The thing in the box has no place in the language-game at all; not even as a *something*: for the box might even be empty.—No, one can 'divide through' by the thing in the box; it cancels out, whatever it is." The lesson: "if we construe the grammar of the expression of sensation on the model of 'object and designation' the object drops out of consideration as irrelevant." Public sensation-words work whatever (if anything) is privately "there."

### 5. Not behaviourism
Wittgenstein is careful: he is not denying sensations. "It is not a something, but not a nothing either!" (§304); the conclusion is only that "a *nothing* would serve just as well as a *something* about which nothing could be said." He rejects the misleading **grammar** of inner object-and-name, not the inner life. "An 'inner process' stands in need of outward criteria" (§580).

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. [[Thinkers/Descartes|Descartes]] (privileged access):** Descartes' *cogito* makes the inner the first and most certain object of knowledge. Wittgenstein replies that "only I can know whether I am really in pain" is "in one way... wrong, and in another nonsense" (§246) — others often *do* know, and strictly "it can't be said of me at all... that I *know* I am in pain." Self-knowledge of sensation is grammatically unlike knowledge of an object.
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Locke|Locke]] (ideas as private meanings):** Locke's words signify ideas in the speaker's own mind; communication works if our private ideas happen to match. The beetle-in-a-box dismantles exactly this: the private idea "cancels out" of the public language. Cf. [[Concepts/Meaning as Use (Wittgenstein)]].
- **For philosophy of mind:** the argument is a foundational text against Cartesian dualism and the "inner theatre," and a major source for functionalist and behaviour/criteria-based accounts of mental concepts. It also bears on AI and other minds — ascription of inner states rests on outward criteria, not on inspecting a private object (cf. [[Sources/Computing Machinery and Intelligence - Alan Turing (1950)|the other-minds problem in the Turing literature]]).
- **Orthodox vs. Pyrrhonian readings**: David Stern (2010) distinguishes the **Orthodox reading** (which treats the PLA as a deductive proof that private language is logically impossible) from a **Pyrrhonian reading** (which treats the remarks as a dialogical therapy showing that the very concept of a private language is an incoherent fantasy that dissolves under examination).

## Contradictions / Open Questions
> [!warning] Directly attacks the privileged-access epistemology of [[Thinkers/Descartes]] and the idea-semantics of [[Thinkers/Locke]]. See [[Contradictions/Early vs Later Wittgenstein]] for its break with the *Tractatus*'s solipsistic "limits of my language."

- Does the argument refute *all* private reference, or only the claim that private reference could found a *language*? The scope is debated.
