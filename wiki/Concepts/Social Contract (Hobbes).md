# Social Contract (Hobbes)

> The rational, collective agreement by which individuals escape the state of nature. Out of fear of violent death and a desire for peace, individuals mutually covenant to surrender their natural right to govern themselves, transferring it entirely to a central authority (the sovereign) in exchange for security, law, and order.

## Mechanism of the Contract

**Mutual Covenants**:
- Unlike later versions of contract theory (like Locke's or Rousseau's), Hobbes' social contract is made *between the individuals*, not between the individuals and the sovereign.
- Each person says, in effect: *"I authorize and give up my right of governing myself to this man, or to this assembly of men, on this condition; that thou give up thy right to him, and authorize all his actions in like manner."*

**The Creation of the Leviathan**:
- Through this contract, the multitude is unified into one person: the State, or **Leviathan**.
- The sovereign is *not* a party to the contract. Because the sovereign is not bound by the contract, the sovereign cannot breach it, and citizens have no legal right to rebellion or resistance once the contract is formed.

**The Absolute Sovereign**:
- Hobbes insists that the sovereign's power must be absolute, indivisible, and permanent:
  - If power is divided (e.g. between king and parliament), it leads to instability and civil war.
  - The sovereign has total control over the judiciary, the military, taxation, and even public doctrine and speech.
  - "Covenants, without the sword, are but words, and of no strength to secure a man at all."

## In the Vault

- **Constitutional Contrast**: John Locke's social contract is conditional: individuals delegate power to govern, but retain their rights. If the government violates their trust, they have the right to revolution. See [[Contradictions/Hobbesian Absolutism vs. Lockean Constitutionalism]].
- **Checks and Balances**: The absolute, indivisible sovereignty of Hobbes stands in direct opposition to the checks and balances design of [Federalist No. 51](file:///home/mark/Documents/Philosophy/wiki/Sources/The%20Federalist%20Papers%20-%20Publius%20(1787-1788).md) and limited constitutional authority.
- **Ethics vs. Self-Interest**: Hobbes bases the entire political structure on enlightened self-interest (the fear of death) rather than moral duty (Kant) or natural sociability (Aristotle).
