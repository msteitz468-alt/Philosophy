# Shih - Momentum, Formlessness, and Striking Weakness (Sun Tzu)

> One-sentence hook: Sun Tzu's dynamic core (Chapters V–VII) — combine the **direct (*cheng*) and indirect (*qi*)** to generate **momentum (*shih*)**, become **formless like water** so the enemy cannot grip you, and pour your concentrated strength onto his weakness: "avoid what is strong and strike at what is weak."

## Overview

Where [[Concepts/Calculation and the Five Factors (Sun Tzu)|Chapter I]] is static reckoning, Chapters V ("Energy"), VI ("Weak Points and Strong"), and VII ("Maneuvering") are *dynamic*: how advantage, once arranged, is *released*. Three interlocking ideas:

- **Direct and indirect (*cheng*/*qi*).** "In battle there are not more than two methods of attack — the direct and the indirect — yet these two in combination give rise to an endless series of maneuvers… they give rise to each other in turn… like moving in a circle, you never come to an end." The orthodox (fixing/engaging) and the unorthodox (flanking/surprising) continuously generate novelty.
- **Momentum / *shih* (勢).** "The onset of troops is like the rush of a torrent which will even roll stones along in its course"; the decision like "the swoop of a falcon… [that] strikes and destroys its victim." The good fighter "looks to the effect of **combined energy**, and does not require too much from individuals" — he "makes his ranks irresistible by the *force of momentum*," choosing his men and then exploiting *shih* (one round stone on a steep slope), not blaming individuals.
- **Formlessness and striking weakness.** "Be subtle, even to the point of **formlessness**… thus you may be the director of the opponent's fate." "**Military tactics are like unto water**; for water in its natural course runs away from high places and hastens downwards. So in war, the way is to **avoid what is strong and to strike at what is weak**. Water shapes its course according to the nature of the ground over which it flows; **the soldier works out his victory in relation to the foe whom he is facing.**" Hence "**there are no constant tactics**… he who can modify his tactics in relation to his opponent… may be called a heaven-born captain."

## Key Arguments

- **Impose your will; do not have it imposed.** "The clever combatant **imposes his will on the enemy, but does not allow the enemy's will to be imposed on him**" (Ch. VI). Formlessness (concealment + adaptability) plus concentration is how — the enemy must defend everywhere (so is weak everywhere), while you mass secretly against one point.
- **Concentration vs. dispersion.** "We can form a single united body, while the enemy must split up into fractions. Hence there will be a whole pitted against separate parts of a whole" — local superiority manufactured by formlessness and surprise, even from overall parity. ("Avoid strength, strike weakness" is the application.)
- **Tempo and rhythm (Ch. VII).** The famous *fūrinkazan* verse: "**Let your rapidity be that of the wind, your compactness that of the forest; in raiding and plundering be like fire, in immovability like a mountain… Let your plans be dark and impenetrable as night, and when you move, fall like a thunderbolt.**" Vary speed, density, ferocity, and stillness to the moment.
- **No fixed playbook.** Because the right move depends entirely on terrain and opponent ("water shapes its course according to the ground"), strategy is *adaptation*, not the application of constant rules — the dynamic complement to Ch. I's framework (which is a framework for judgment, not a recipe).

## Contrasts / Lineage

- **Taoist resonance**: water, formlessness, yielding-that-overcomes, and "no constant tactics" strongly echo Lao Tzu's *wu-wei* and the *Tao Te Ching*'s water imagery (Lao Tzu not yet in the vault) — strategy as flowing with circumstance rather than forcing it.
- **Vs. [[Concepts/Geometrical Method - Rationalism (Spinoza)|fixed-deduction systems]] and rigid rule-following**: Sun Tzu's "no constant tactics / adapt to the opponent" is the practical antithesis of deriving conduct from invariant principles — closer to [[Concepts/Pragmatic Method (James)|pragmatic]] situational judgment and [[Concepts/Custom and Belief (Hume)|context-trained instinct]].
- **Kindred with [[Thinkers/Garry Kasparov]]/chess**: *initiative* and *tempo* (≈ *shih*), the interplay of forcing (direct) and quiet/prophylactic (indirect) moves, attacking the weakest defended square, and "there are no constant tactics" (position-dependent play).
- **Vs. [[Concepts/Power, Empire, and Revolt (Thucydides)]]**: the Athenians lost *shih* at Syracuse — momentum reversed, formlessness gone, strength thrown against strength (siege) far from home.

## Vault / AI / Personal Relevance

- **Adaptive policy over fixed plan**: "there are no constant tactics; water shapes its course to the ground" is a charter for **online adaptation** — in agentic work, condition the next action on the current state/opponent, not a frozen script; momentum (*shih*) ≈ exploiting compounding advantage once it's arranged.
- **Concentration / leverage**: "avoid strength, strike weakness" = put effort where it has outsized effect (the weak point), and make the adversary defend everywhere — a general principle for resource allocation, debugging, and competitive strategy.
- **Formlessness ≈ optionality/unpredictability**: keeping one's approach flexible and concealed preserves the initiative (the strategic version of not over-committing); the defensive mirror is robustness to an opponent who is themselves formless.
- **Career/competition**: don't fight rivals where they're strong; find the underserved niche/weak point and concentrate (the agribusiness playbook's targeting/positioning); vary tempo (wind/forest/fire/mountain) — move fast on opportunity, hold firm on commitments.
