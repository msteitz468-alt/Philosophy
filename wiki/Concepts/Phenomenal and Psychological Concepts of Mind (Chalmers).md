# Phenomenal and Psychological Concepts of Mind (Chalmers)
> "On the phenomenal concept, mind is characterized by the way it feels; on the psychological concept, mind is characterized by what it does."

## Summary

*The Conscious Mind* ch. 1. Two distinct concepts of mind have been systematically conflated: the **phenomenal** (mind as conscious experience, qualia) and the **psychological** (mind as the causal/explanatory basis of behavior). Neither is "the" correct one; both are real. Everyday mental terms — pain, perception, emotion, belief — lead a "**double life**," bundling phenomenal and psychological components that co-occur empirically but are conceptually distinct.

The history of the conflation: Descartes assimilated the psychological to the phenomenal (mind as transparent to itself); Freud and behaviorism pried them apart; Ryle's logical behaviorism and Armstrong/Lewis functionalism assimilated the phenomenal to the psychological — "as great an error as Descartes's."

The mind–body problem thereby factors in two. The **psychological** mind–body problem is "for all intents and purposes dissolved" — like the non-existent life–body problem, it is a matter of explaining functions. What remains is the hard one, Jackendoff's "**mind–mind problem**": the link between psychological and phenomenal mind. "Consciousness" itself splits into phenomenal consciousness and psychological consciousness (awakeness, introspection, reportability, self-consciousness, attention, voluntary control). **Awareness** — access to information usable in behavioral control, ≈ Block's "access consciousness" — is the most general psychological counterpart of consciousness, and later carries the coherence principle of [[Concepts/Coherence Between Consciousness and Awareness (Chalmers)]].

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Descartes]]** ([[Sources/Meditations on First Philosophy - Descartes (Oxford, 2008)]]): the original assimilation in one direction — mind as essentially conscious and self-transparent.
- **Ryle / functionalism**: the opposite assimilation; Chalmers grants functional analysis full success for the psychological mind while denying it touches the phenomenal.
- **[[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]**: heterophenomenology in effect treats the psychological concept as exhaustive — the disagreement is over whether anything is left over.
- **[[Thinkers/Sigmund Freud]]**: unconscious belief and desire showed the psychological mind can come apart from the phenomenal.
- The distinction is the analytical engine behind the [[Concepts/Hard Problem of Consciousness (Chalmers)]]: zombie twins share the whole psychological mind and lack the phenomenal one.
