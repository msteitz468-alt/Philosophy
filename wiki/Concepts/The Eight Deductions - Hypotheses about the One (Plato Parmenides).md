# The Eight Deductions — Hypotheses about the One (Plato Parmenides)

> Part II of Plato's *Parmenides* (137c–166c): an austere logical "gymnastic" in which Parmenides, with young Aristoteles answering, examines the consequences of the hypotheses **"if one is"** and **"if one is not"** — for the One and for the Others, each in relation to itself and to the rest. The exercise yields **eight deductions plus a Coda**. It is the most difficult stretch of the Platonic corpus and the historical wellspring of the [[Concepts/The One (To Hen)|Neoplatonic One]] and negative theology.

## The Method (the "Gymnastic")

At 135e–136c Parmenides prescribes the training Socrates lacked (see [[Concepts/Participation Problems and the Critique of the Forms (Plato Parmenides)]]): for any hypothesis, do not only ask "if X is" but also "if X is not," and trace **all** consequences for X in relation to itself and to the others, and for the others in relation to themselves and to X. Part II performs this on "the One" itself. The method echoes Zeno's *reductio* procedure but is turned into a systematic, four-fold survey.

## Hermann's Organizing Key: the "Unit of Is"

Arnold Hermann reads the eight arguments along two axes:

- **Is the One taken *itself-by-itself* (absolute, no relation to Being) or *combined with Being* (a relational "Unit of Is")?**
- **Does the hypothesis affirm the One's being ("if one is") or deny it ("if one is not")?**

The decisive contrast is **relation to Being**. Taken absolutely, a subject has **no** predicate (qualifier: *neither/nor*); taken as a "Unit of Is" (fused with Being), it admits **all** predicates and their opposites in different respects (qualifier: *both*). Being is "the canvas the participants are painted upon, but Unity is the paint as well as the stroke. Without it, the canvas remains blank" — Oneness is the necessary **limiter** that lets anything have a determinate place in the ontological order.

## The Eight Deductions

| # | Subject | Mode | Hypothesis | Qualifier | Result |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | The One | simple (no relation) | if one is | neither/nor | Has **no** property at all — no parts, shape, place, motion/rest, sameness/difference, time; **not even "one"**; unnameable, unknowable, imperceptible (137c–142a) |
| II | The One | complex ("Unit of Is") | if one is | both | Is a whole of parts; generates number and unlimited multitude; admits **all** opposite predicates in different respects; is knowable, nameable (142b–155e) |
| — | **Coda** | complex, temporal | if one is | both + neither/nor | Change requires **the instant** (*to exaiphnes*), "in no time at all," the gateway between motion and rest and between being and not-being (155e–157b) |
| III | The Others | complex | if one is | both | Partaking of the One, the Others become limited unities: both limited and unlimited, like and unlike, at rest and in motion (157b–159b) |
| IV | The Others | simple | if one is | neither/nor | Without participation in the One, the Others are amorphous — neither one nor many, no qualification at all (159b–160b) |
| V | "One that is not" | complex | if one is not | both (contingent) | A non-being One that still refers to Being has properties: difference, likeness, both being and not-being (160b–163b) |
| VI | "One that is not" | simple | if one is not | neither/nor | A One with no reference to Being at all "is not in any state whatsoever" (163b–164b) |
| VII | The Others | complex, no One | if one is not | both (apparent) | Lacking a real One, the Others only **seem** to have limit, number, unity — all doxastic, deceptive appearance (164b–165e) |
| VIII | The Others | simple, no One | if one is not | neither/nor | Without the One, **"nothing is"** (165e–166c) |

**The instant (*to exaiphnes*)** (Coda): because a thing cannot be simultaneously in motion and at rest, and change from one to the other cannot occur *in* time, change happens in a timeless "instant" between states — Plato's device for the metaphysics of becoming.

**Final line (166c):** "If one is or if one is not, it and the others both are and are not, and both appear and do not appear to be all things in all ways." Read straight, an aporia; read with Hermann, a demonstration that **One and Being are reciprocal** — nothing can *be* without both.

## Significance / How Later Thinkers Use This

- **Neoplatonism:** Plotinus (following the Neopythagorean Moderatus) reads **Argument I** — the One that is not even one, beyond predication and knowledge — as disguised theology: the **[[Concepts/The One (To Hen)|One beyond Being]]**, source of all. **Argument II**'s "Unit of Is" maps onto **Nous/Intellect** (one-and-many). [[Thinkers/Proclus]] built his *Platonic Theology* and the [[Concepts/The Henads (Proclus)|henads]] on a hypothesis-by-hypothesis reading. This is the seedbed of Western **negative theology**. See [[Concepts/Parmenidean Being and the One (Parmenides)]].
- **The One and the Many:** the deductions are the classical *locus* for the problem of unity and plurality — cf. [[Concepts/The One and the Many (James)]], [[Concepts/Unity and the One (Aristotle)]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] Is Part II a genuine metaphysics (Neoplatonic/Hermann) or a purely formal *exercise* with no doctrinal payload (Ryle, Owen, Vlastos — *negotium dialecticum*)? The dominant interpretive fork for the whole dialogue.
