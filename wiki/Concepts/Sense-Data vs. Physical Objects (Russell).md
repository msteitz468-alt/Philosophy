# Sense-Data vs. Physical Objects (Russell)

> One-sentence hook: Russell's opening wedge in *The Problems of Philosophy* — the distinction between **sense-data** (the colours, sounds, hardnesses *immediately* known in sensation) and the **physical object** (the "real table," known only by *inference*) — and his case that, though we can never *prove* an external world, realism is the simpler hypothesis worth holding.

## Overview

Chapters I–IV. Russell starts from the homeliest object — a table — and shows that its colour, texture, shape, and hardness all vary with viewpoint, lighting, and observer, so that "there is no colour which preeminently appears to be the colour of the table." Hence "the real table, if there is one, is **not the same as what we immediately experience** by sight or touch or hearing… [it] is not immediately known to us at all, but must be an inference from what is immediately known." This is "the distinction that causes most trouble in philosophy — the distinction between **appearance and reality**."

He fixes vocabulary:
- **Sense-data**: "the things that are immediately known in sensation — such things as colours, sounds, smells, hardnesses, roughnesses."
- **Sensation**: "the experience of being immediately aware of these things." (The colour is the sense-datum; the awareness is the sensation.)
- **Physical object**: "the real table, if it exists." The collection of all physical objects is **matter**.

Two questions then drive chapters II–III: **(1) Is there a real table at all?** and **(2) If so, what is its nature?**

## Key Arguments

- **The existence of matter (ch. II)** cannot be *demonstrated* — "no logical absurdity results from the hypothesis that the world consists of myself and my thoughts and feelings and sensations." But the realist hypothesis is *simpler*: the cat that "appears at one moment in one part of the room, and at another in another" is better explained by a persisting animal that "gets hungry between one meal and the next" than by patches of colour that "spring into being." "Every principle of simplicity urges us to adopt the natural view."
- **Instinctive belief**: "It is not by argument that we originally come by our belief in an independent external world… it is what may be called an **instinctive belief**." Philosophy's job is to "show the hierarchy of our instinctive beliefs," present each "as free from irrelevant additions as possible," and show they "form a harmonious system." "There can never be any reason for rejecting one instinctive belief except that it clashes with others."
- **The nature of matter (ch. III)**: science reduces matter to motion in a **physical space** distinct from the private spaces of sight and touch. We can know the *structure/relations* of physical objects (geometry, spatial order, the eclipse) but **not their intrinsic nature** — "the physical objects themselves remain unknown in their intrinsic nature, so far at least as can be discovered by means of the senses." (Light is *not* the wave-motion science describes; the wave-motion is its *cause*. We see "the sun of eight minutes ago.")
- **Against idealism (ch. IV)**: Berkeley's *esse est percipi* rests on an **equivocation on "idea"** — conflating the *act* of apprehension (mental) with the *object* apprehended (need not be mental). Sense-data depend on us (their existence requires a perceiver), but this does *not* show they are *in the mind* in the sense of being mental. See [[Concepts/Knowledge by Acquaintance and Knowledge by Description (Russell)]].

## Contrasts / Lineage

- **Descends from [[Concepts/Primary and Secondary Qualities (Locke)]]**: Russell radicalizes Locke — where Locke kept *primary* qualities (extension, figure) as resembling the object, Russell holds *all* sense-data (including shape/space) are observer-relative appearances; only abstract *structure* survives as knowable.
- **Vs. Berkeley/idealism**: Russell concedes Berkeley's point that sense-data are mind-dependent, then blocks the slide to "matter is mental" via the act/object distinction — a realist rescue.
- **Refines [[Concepts/Method of Hyperbolic Doubt (Descartes)]]**: Russell uses Descartes' doubt as method but reaches a *probabilistic* realism (simplicity, instinctive belief) rather than God-guaranteed certainty.
- **Empiricist kin** ([[Concepts/Impressions and Ideas - Copy Principle (Hume)]]): sense-data ≈ Hume's impressions; but Russell builds a structural realism on top, not Humean scepticism.
- **Forward link**: "physical object = the cause of our sense-data, knowable only as to structure" anticipates Russell's later **structural realism** and 20th-c. debates about the manifest vs. scientific image.

## Vault / AI / Personal Relevance

- **Appearance/reality as a maintenance reflex**: the table that "is not what it seems" is a useful emblem for the vault's verify discipline — what a single source *reports* (appearance) vs. the cross-checked, inferred reality. Sense-data ≈ raw observations; physical object ≈ the inferred entity the wiki actually tracks.
- **Structure-only knowledge**: "we can know the *relations* but not the *intrinsic nature*" is a deep point for AI/representation — models capture *structure* (relations, correlations) of a domain, not its intrinsic essence; a humility clause about what any representation can deliver. Pairs with [[Concepts/Attributes and Parallelism (Spinoza)|parallelism]] and structural views of knowledge.
