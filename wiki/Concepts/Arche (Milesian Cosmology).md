# Arche (Milesian Cosmology)

> The primary substance, first principle, or ultimate source of all things in the cosmos; that from which everything is generated and to which everything eventually returns.

## Summary

The search for the *arche* (ἀρχή) was the defining project of the 6th-century BCE Milesian school. It represents the transition from mythological *theogony* (explaining the world via the genealogy of gods) to rational *cosmology* (explaining the world via the behavior of natural substances). The Milesians assumed that despite the vast variety of changing phenomena in the world, there must be a single, underlying, permanent substance that does not change.

---

## How the Milesians Used the Arche

### 1. Thales: Water
- Declared **water** to be the arche. 
- Reasoned that water is essential for life, seeds are moist, and it exists in solid (ice), liquid (water), and gaseous (steam) states.
- The earth floats upon water like a raft.

### 2. Anaximander: The Apeiron (The Boundless)
- Rejected water or any specific element because elements have opposite qualities (e.g., fire is hot/dry, water is cold/wet). If one were infinite, it would destroy the others.
- Postulated that the arche must be the **Apeiron**—an indeterminate, boundless, infinite substance that surrounds all worlds and from which all opposites separate out. See [[Concepts/Apeiron (Anaximander)]].

### 3. Anaximenes: Air (Aer)
- Reverted to a specific element, **air**, but explained physical variety through the mechanical processes of **condensation** and **rarefaction**.
- Rarefied air becomes fire; condensed air becomes wind, cloud, water, earth, and stone.

---

## Relevance to Later Philosophy

- **Conservation of Matter**: The concept of the arche is the direct ancestor of the law of conservation of mass (that matter can change form but cannot be created or destroyed). As Aristotle later noted: *"For there must be some nature, either one or more than one, from which the other things come into being, while it itself is preserved."*
- **The One and the Many**: Initiated the metaphysical problem of the One and the Many—how a single unified source can explain the plurality of changing phenomena.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] Tension between empirical visibility (Thales's water and Anaximenes's air) and logical abstraction (Anaximander's Apeiron). Later philosophers like Parmenides rejected the concept of a changing arche altogether, declaring change itself to be impossible.
