# State of Nature (Hobbes)

> Thomas Hobbes' theoretical description of the pre-social and pre-political condition of humanity. Lacking a centralized sovereign power to enforce order, the state of nature is characterized by a perpetual, structural state of war of all against all (*bellum omnium contra omnes*), in which human life is famously described as "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."

## Core Principles

**Equality and Competition**:
- Hobbes argues that nature has made humans roughly equal in body and mind. This basic equality breeds equal hope of achieving our ends, leading to mutual suspicion and competition.
- If two people desire the same thing which they cannot both enjoy, they become enemies and seek to destroy or subdue one another.

**Three Causes of Quarrel**:
1.  **Competition**: Driven by desire for gain (invading for plunder).
2.  **Diffidence (Distrust)**: Driven by fear for safety (invading for defense).
3.  **Glory**: Driven by desire for reputation (invading for trifles/honor).

**War of All Against All**:
- "War" does not merely consist of actual battle, but the known disposition to fight when there is no assurance to the contrary.
- In this condition of constant fear and danger of violent death, there is no place for industry, agriculture, navigation, science, arts, or society.
- Morality is non-existent: "Where there is no common power, there is no law; where no law, no injustice. Force and fraud are in war the two cardinal virtues."

## In the Vault

- **The Realist Pivot**: Hobbes' state of nature is the starting point for political realism, contrasting with Plato's teleological city ([Republic](file:///home/mark/Documents/Philosophy/wiki/Sources/The%20Republic%20-%20Plato%20(Cambridge,%202000).md)) and Aristotle's view of man as a naturally political animal (*zoon politikon*).
- **The Social Contract**: The unbearable horror of the state of nature is what drives rational individuals to enter into a social contract and submit to an absolute sovereign. See [[Concepts/Social Contract (Hobbes)]].
- **Contrast with Locke**: John Locke's state of nature is a state of liberty governed by natural law (reason), which only becomes a state of war if people violate others' rights.
