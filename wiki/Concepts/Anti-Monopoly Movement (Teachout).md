# Anti-Monopoly Movement (Teachout)
> Teachout's strategic prescription: a mass political movement demanding structural breakup of monopolies — analogous to the Progressive Era trust-busting movement — as the only adequate response to corporate private government.

## Summary

Teachout argues that the correct response to monopoly is not behavioral regulation, ethical consumerism, or narrow antitrust litigation, but a broad-based *political movement* that demands structural change: stop mergers, break up existing dominant firms, overturn post-1981 judicial precedent, and build new anti-monopoly institutions.

Her model is historical: Standard Oil, AT&T, and IG Farben were all broken up despite plutocratic resistance, because persistent, strategic mass activism made breakup politically unavoidable. The Progressive Era anti-monopoly movement (with its local antitrust leagues and trust-busting ideology) transformed American political economy in the early 20th century. Teachout argues nothing less is required today.

## Core Strategic Claims

1. **Boycotts reinforce corporate authority.** When activists threaten to boycott Facebook for not removing white nationalists, they implicitly accept that Facebook *has the authority* to regulate speech — they are petitioning the private government, not dismantling it. Ethical consumerism is a trap.

2. **Existing tools are sufficient for an executive with will.** Using existing statutes, a president can direct the FTC and DOJ to: (a) stop mergers at much lower concentration thresholds, (b) implement industry-specific anti-monopoly rules, (c) bring breakup suits against dominant platforms. No new legislation is required to begin.

3. **State attorneys general are underused.** State AGs can investigate, block mergers, and force divestiture. The 47-state Google/Facebook investigation (2019–20) is an example of latent state power.

4. **New legislation is the ceiling, not the floor.** Congress can pass laws overturn decades of bad Supreme Court precedent (returning to pre-1981 merger standards), mandate divestiture, regulate platforms as public utilities, ban forced arbitration, and pass campaign finance reform. These are the medium-term goals.

5. **The agenda must be unified.** Instead of fighting Pfizer on Tuesday for drug prices, Comcast on Wednesday, and Amazon on Thursday, activists should unite behind one demand: break them up. A unified anti-monopoly frame connects the dots across industries.

## Historical Precedents Cited

- Standard Oil breakup (1911) — controlled 65% of market
- AT&T breakup (1982) — "Ma Bell" divested into regional carriers
- IG Farben breakup (post-WWII)
- Progressive Era local antitrust leagues (120 years ago)
- 2019 Queens, NYC defeat of Amazon HQ2

## Tension with Collective Action Theory

[[Thinkers/Mancur Olson]] predicts that diffuse public interests will systematically lose to concentrated corporate interests in political organizing. Teachout's claim that a mass movement can win is partly an empirical claim (it has happened before) and partly a normative one (we must try). She does not fully answer the Olsonian logic.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Tension with [[Concepts/Constitutional Political Economy (Buchanan-Tullock)]]: Buchanan argues that anti-monopoly regulations will be captured by the regulated industries (regulatory capture). Teachout is aware of capture but argues structural breakup — unlike ongoing regulation — removes the power to capture.
- > [!warning] The "moral economy" vision (Ch. 12) — worker co-ops, local banking, decentralized ownership — goes beyond anti-monopoly into a positive institutional design. It's unclear whether anti-monopoly breakup naturally produces these outcomes or requires additional affirmative policy.
