# Friendship - Philia (Aristotle)

> Friendship (*philia*) is necessary for the good life even for the self-sufficient, since "without friends no one would choose to live, though he had all other goods." Of the three kinds — utility, pleasure, virtue — only virtue-friendship between equals who are good is friendship in the primary, complete, and durable sense. True friendship with another is modeled on, and an extension of, the good person's own proper self-love and internal harmony.

## Summary

Books VIII–IX (treated as a continuous inquiry) open by classifying friendship according to the three things that can be "loved" — the useful, the pleasant, and the good. Friendships of utility and of pleasure are incidental: they exist only so long as the party remains useful or pleasant, dissolve when that ceases, and involve loving the other not for who they are but for what they provide. Perfect friendship — "the friendship of men who are good, and alike in virtue" — is different in kind: such friends wish well to each other for the other's own sake (not instrumentally), and because their goodness is an enduring trait, so is their friendship; incidentally, virtue-friends are also useful and pleasant to each other, but not primarily so. Aristotle treats friendship and justice as coextensive with community as such: "in every community there is thought to be some form of justice, and friendship too," with the depth of shared life determining the depth of both — tyranny, where ruler and ruled share nothing in common, admits no friendship at all, while democracies, built on greater equality, admit more. Unequal friendships (parent-child, ruler-subject, older-younger) are reconciled with the equality friendship seems to require through *proportional* reciprocity: the superior should be loved more than they love, in proportion to their merit, while the inferior renders honor in exchange for benefit — "when the love is in proportion to the merit of the parties, then in a sense arises equality." Aristotle also observes that friendship "lies in loving rather than in being loved," illustrated by mothers who love without expectation of return, and that benefactors characteristically love those they benefit more than the reverse, since the beneficiary is, in a sense, the benefactor's own handiwork — a reminder and extension of the benefactor's own existence, which (echoed at the start of Book IX) is itself intrinsically pleasant to perceive. Book IX grounds all of this in a striking argument about self-relation: the good person is properly self-loving — not in the vulgar, reproached sense of grasping for wealth and pleasure, but in identifying with and benefiting their own rational governing part, choosing noble action "for the sake of the intellectual element in him, which is thought to be the man himself." The bad person, by contrast, suffers internal discord (appetite warring against reason), so cannot even be a friend to himself, let alone to others; the good person's internal harmony is the template friendship with another (as "another self") extends. From this it follows that even the fully self-sufficient happy person genuinely needs friends: not out of lack, but because shared life, shared contemplation, and shared awareness of existing well complete and intensify one's own perception of one's good — "man is a political creature, and one whose nature is to live with others," so "no one would choose the whole world on condition of being alone." True friendship, requiring shared living, mutual knowledge, and undivided attention, is necessarily limited in number — one cannot have "great friendship" of the primary kind toward many people at once.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Three kinds of friendship — utility, pleasure, virtue — with virtue-friendship alone primary**: utility and pleasure friendships are incidental (loving what the other *provides*, not who they *are*) and dissolve when the basis ceases; virtue-friendship, wishing well to the other "qua good," is stable because goodness is enduring.
- **Friendship and justice are coextensive with community**: the extent of shared life in any association determines both the form of justice and the degree of friendship present in it — the limiting negative case is tyranny (no shared good, hence no friendship), the limiting positive case is friendship between equals who are also fully virtuous.
- **Unequal friendships are equalized by proportional reciprocity**: love and honor exchanged in proportion to each party's merit and contribution restores a functional equality even between structurally unequal parties (parent/child, ruler/ruled).
- **Loving, not being loved, is friendship's essence**: exemplified by maternal love that persists without requital, and by benefactors who characteristically love recipients more than recipients love them (the recipient as the benefactor's own "handiwork").
- **Proper self-love is the template for friendship with others**: the good person's identification with and cultivation of their own rational part is not vice but the condition for genuine friendship — internal harmony extended outward; the bad person's internal discord precludes true friendship both with others and with himself.
- **Even the self-sufficient happy person needs friends**: not from deficiency but because shared activity and shared contemplation complete one's own awareness of living well — "to perceive that one lives is in itself pleasant... he needs, therefore, to be conscious of the existence of his friend as well."
- **True (virtue-based) friendship is necessarily limited in number**, since it requires living together, undivided attention, and mutual, particular knowledge that cannot be extended to many at once — a structural, not merely practical, limit.

## How Aristotle Uses This

Qualifies and completes the self-sufficiency implicit in [[Concepts/Moral Virtues as Means (Aristotle)]]'s treatment of magnanimity (the great-souled person's apparent disdain for depending on others): friendship between equals is shown not to be a form of dependency but an extension of self-sufficient virtue outward, resolving the apparent tension. It directly sets up Book X's final identification of the highest happiness with contemplative activity (see [[Concepts/Contemplation and the Best Life - Theoria (Aristotle)]]), since shared contemplation with a friend is explicitly named as part of what completes the good life.
