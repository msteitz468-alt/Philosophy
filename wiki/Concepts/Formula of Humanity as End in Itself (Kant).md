# Formula of Humanity as End in Itself (Kant)

## Overview

In the Second Section, after establishing the universal law formulation, Kant derives a second equivalent expression of the Categorical Imperative from the idea of an end in itself (something with absolute, unconditioned worth that can ground a categorical imperative). This is the Formula of Humanity (FH): rational nature (humanity) must always be treated as an end in itself and never merely as a means.

**Key quote:** "Act so that you use humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, always at the same time as an end, never merely as a means."

"The human being, and in general every rational being, exists as end in itself, not merely as means to the discretionary use of this or that will, but in all its actions... it must always at the same time be considered as an end."

## Key Arguments

- All objects of inclination (and inclinations themselves) have only *conditioned* worth (dependent on the existence of needs/desires). Relative ends ground only hypothetical imperatives.
- For there to be a supreme practical principle / categorical imperative, there must be something whose existence has *absolute* worth, an "end in itself" that can serve as ground of determinate laws valid for every rational will.
- Rational beings (persons) have this status by their nature: they set ends, are capable of morality, and are not things. "Rational nature exists as end in itself."
- The practical imperative: treat humanity (rational nature/capacity for morality) in oneself and others always as end and never merely as means. This limits arbitrary choice; it is an objective (not merely subjective) end.
- Examples:
  - Suicide to escape burdensome life: uses one's own person merely as means (to a bearable condition up to death); contradicts humanity as end in itself.
  - Lying promise: uses the other merely as means (for one's end) without the other being able to contain the end of the action in himself; violates the other's status as rational being who must be able to agree with the maxim.
  - Attacks on freedom/property of others similarly treat persons as mere means.
- This formula is "fundamentally the same" as the universal law formula; both express the absolutely good will. Later in the section it is connected to autonomy and the realm of ends.
- Note: "humanity" here means rational nature (capacity to set ends and give moral laws), not the biological species; the principle is meant to hold for all rational beings.

## Vault Relevance / AI / Personal

- **Alignment & agent design**: "Never treat humans (or other agents) merely as means" is a core constraint for tool-using / goal-directed systems. Optimization for user "happiness" or metrics without regard to the person's rational agency (consent, dignity, capacity to set own ends) violates FH. In practice: do not manipulate, deceive, or instrumentalize users even if it "works" for short-term goals; design for users as co-legislators/end-setters.
- **Wiki / maintenance**: Edits, syntheses, or categorizations must respect the "humanity" (rational agency, provenance, accuracy) of sources and prior contributors — never merely as means to "a nice page" or speed. Universal law + humanity together: maxims of editing/ingestion must be willable universally and must treat the rational content/persons behind sources as ends.
- **Personal/career**: In sales/consulting/insurance: clients are not merely means to premium or quota; their risk needs, autonomy, and long-term welfare must be ends in the interaction itself. "Book of business" growth that treats relationships instrumentally fails the test. Honest disclosure, suitable recommendations, and respect for client decision-making as rational agents.
- **Multi-agent / teams**: In agent swarms or human-AI teams, each participant (human or artificial) must be treated as capable of self-legislation, not mere tool. Kingdom of Ends extension (below) scales this to a community of such agents.
- **Education/ethics cultivation**: Training "respect" (not just compliance or sympathy) for the law and for rational nature parallels cultivating good will. Warns against purely outcome- or feeling-based "ethics" that reduce persons to data points or preference bundles.
