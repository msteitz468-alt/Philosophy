# Second Industrial Revolution (Wiener)

> Wiener's framework for the historical significance of the vacuum tube, feedback control, and digital computing: the **First** Industrial Revolution displaced human and animal *muscle*; the **Second** displaces human *judgment*. The automatic factory (computing machine + sense organs + effectors) is the machine equivalent of the complete animal. Key social claim: "The automatic machine is the **precise economic equivalent of slave labor**. Any labor which competes with slave labor must accept the economic conditions of slave labor." The resulting structural unemployment, if not consciously governed, will produce social disruption dwarfing the 1930s.

## Summary

### The First Industrial Revolution: Displacing Muscle

The first IR (steam engine, 1760s–1840s) replaced human and animal power:

- Watt's engine → steam-powered factories → the textile mill as prototype
- The factory required geographic concentration of workers because power transmission was mechanical (line shafting, belt and pulley from one engine to all machines)
- This **technical necessity** created the urban factory as a social institution, with all its consequences (child labor, urbanization, social disruption)
- The individual without anything but physical labor to sell "has nothing to sell which it is worth anyone's money to buy" in the post-first-IR world

The key point: the first IR displaced muscle but left human judgment intact. Workers who could adapt — operating machines, supervising processes, managing — retained economic value.

### The Second Industrial Revolution: Displacing Judgment

The second IR is constituted by three technical developments operating together:

1. **Vacuum tube (electron valve)**: makes it possible to control high-power processes with minuscule input signals. A steel-rolling mill can be regulated by circuits carrying less power than a radio set. This separates *control* from *power* — the control problem can be solved at negligible energy levels, then amplified.

2. **Feedback control**: allows machines to monitor their own outputs and correct their own behavior. The factory machine becomes self-regulating, not just powerful.

3. **Digital computing**: provides the logical/mathematical brain that can sequence operations, make decisions among alternatives, and store and retrieve information about the process state.

The combination produces the **automatic factory**: a system that has computing machine as brain + sense organs (photoelectric cells, thermometers, pH meters, pressure gauges) + effectors (servo-motors, valve actuators) + feedback loops connecting all three. "The all-over system will correspond to the complete animal with sense organs, effectors, and proprioceptors, and not, as in the ultra-rapid computing machine, to an isolated brain."

The critical distinction: the first IR machine needed a human to watch it and intervene on judgment calls. The second IR machine makes the judgment calls itself.

### The Slave-Labor Equivalence

Wiener's most provocative claim:

"Let us remember that the automatic machine, whatever we think of any feelings it may have or may not have, is the **precise economic equivalent of slave labor**. Any labor which competes with slave labor must accept the economic conditions of slave labor. It is perfectly clear that this will produce an unemployment situation, in comparison with which the present recession and even the depression of the thirties will seem a pleasant joke."

The logic:
- Slave labor has ~zero marginal cost once the slave is acquired.
- Machines that can replace clerical and judgment workers also approach zero marginal cost per unit of output.
- Any worker whose function can be replaced by such a machine must compete against zero (or near-zero) marginal cost labor.
- This drives wages toward subsistence for all such workers.
- The scope of affected labor is much broader in the second IR than the first: not just manual labor but accounting, quality control, scheduling, inventory management, routine decision-making of all kinds.

Wiener does not say this is desirable — he says it will happen unless consciously governed. "The new industrial revolution is a two-edged sword. It may be used for the benefit of humanity, but only if humanity survives long enough to enter a period in which such a benefit is possible."

### The Industrialist Incentive Problem

Wiener is pessimistic about corporate self-governance:

"We have a good deal of experience as to how the industrialists regard a new industrial potential... they have very few inhibitions when it comes to taking all the profit out of an industry that there is to be done, and then letting the public pick up the pieces. This is the history of the lumber and mining industries."

The incentive structure: industrialists will introduce automation wherever it yields immediate profit, regardless of long-term social consequences. A "sure and quick profit, and to get out before the crash touches him personally" is the rational business strategy, even if it accelerates the unemployment crisis.

Note: Wiener is *more optimistic* in the 1954 revision than the 1950 original, having seen management conferences that showed genuine awareness of social responsibility. He does not abandon the concern but modulates the pessimism.

### The Return to Cottage Industry (Technical Optimism)

Against the social pessimism, Wiener notes a genuine technical possibility. The first IR required geographic concentration (one steam engine for many machines). The electric motor reverses this: each machine can have its own motor. Power no longer requires concentration.

"We are now in a position to return to cottage industry, in places where it would otherwise be suitable." With individual motors and communication networks, the technical necessity of the large urban factory dissolves. Workers could potentially be geographically dispersed; the factory as social institution is contingent on the second-IR communication infrastructure, not technically required.

This is a 1950 anticipation of the remote-work argument: digital infrastructure makes geographic concentration of knowledge workers unnecessary (though coordination costs remain).

### Scope and Timeline

Wiener estimates (writing ~1950) that automatic factories will be broadly operational within 10–20 years in peacetime, or within 2–5 years of a major war (which would massively accelerate the development). He explicitly notes the comparison with radar: "We are already as far along in the process of developing a unified system of automatic control machines as we were in the development of radar in 1939."

He correctly identifies that the automation will not penetrate everywhere uniformly:
- **Will penetrate**: continuous-process industries (steel, paper, chemicals, oil refining, canning), assembly lines, repetitive clerical work, accounting, statistical quality control
- **Will penetrate more slowly**: small-scale operations (corner grocery, corner garage) where capital costs outweigh benefits; highly variable tasks (farming on diverse terrain, custom work)

The overall picture: a broad middle layer of repetitive judgment tasks — the backbone of the 20th-century white-collar economy — is directly in scope.

## Key Claims / Positions

- First IR displaced muscle; second IR displaces judgment — a qualitatively different and more sweeping displacement.
- Automatic machine = precise economic equivalent of slave labor → structural unemployment crisis.
- Any labor competing with slave labor must accept slave labor conditions.
- Corporate incentives will rush automation for short-term profit regardless of social consequences.
- Technical possibility exists to decentralize production (cottage industry) if social will exists to govern the transition.
- The choice is human: technology is two-edged — can benefit humanity or destroy it.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Wiener's 1950 prediction of massive structural unemployment from judgment-displacing automation has been debated for 75 years. Historical pattern: past industrial revolutions created as many jobs as they destroyed, through new categories of work. Whether the second IR (now the AI revolution) is genuinely different — whether AI closes off the "new job category" escape route — is the central empirical debate in current AI-economics. Wiener's argument does not depend on whether new jobs appear; it depends on whether the new jobs are *accessible* to workers displaced from the old ones.
- The slave-labor equivalence is provocative and not fully argued. Slave labor has social costs (coercion, oversight, resistance) that machine labor does not. But the economic argument — that competing with zero-marginal-cost production drives wages to near-zero for affected workers — is more formal and defensible than the rhetoric suggests.
