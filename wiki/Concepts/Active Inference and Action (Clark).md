# Active Inference and Action (Clark)
> Action is not the execution of motor commands but a self-fulfilling prophecy: the brain predicts the bodily sensations that *would* occur if a movement were already done, then moves the body to make those predictions come true.

## Summary

Under [[Concepts/Predictive Processing (Clark)]], action is built the same way as perception. "Ordinary daily actions are caused by predictions of the flow of bodily sensations that would occur if that very action were to be performed." There are two ways to reduce prediction error: change your guess to fit the world (perception), or **change the world to fit your guess** (action). This yields, in Clark's words, "the first fully unified treatment of perception and action" — both minimize sensory prediction error, "jointly orchestrated by the drive to eliminate errors in prediction."

To move, "the brain — when I want to move my hand — [must] gently lie to itself, forcibly downgrading genuine sensory information associated with the currently immobile state of my arm" and upgrading the prediction of the moved state. That downgrading/upgrading *is* [[Concepts/Precision-Weighting (Clark)]] in the motor domain. This resolves a long-standing anomaly (motor and sensory cortex sharing the same wiring): "one wiring diagram to rule them all."

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Ideomotor theory revived** (Hermann Lotze, William James): "the idea of the completed action is what brings the actual action about" — reversing commonsense causality. The mental representation of the finished effect is the cause.
- **Passive-motion paradigm / marionette metaphor**: the brain solves for the *endpoint*, and the lower joints and degrees of freedom fall into place automatically, as if the body were pulled toward the goal.
- **Self-tickling fails** because the predictive brain anticipates its own sensory consequences and cancels them — superseding the older "efference copy" account (Erich von Holst, 1950).
- **Active sensing / embodied expertise**: perception is itself intelligent action. The outfielder "run[s] with their eye on the ball, so that their own movement cancels out any apparent changes in the acceleration of that ball" — controlling action by predicted sensory consequences rather than computing a trajectory.
- **Temporal depth**: long-term, goal-directed behavior uses counterfactual predictions nested above short-term ones, requiring "realistic optimism" (Clark quotes Muhammad Ali: "If my mind can conceive it and my heart can believe it, then I can achieve it").
- This is the **active inference** side of Karl Friston's framework (named in Clark's notes): predictions play the role usually assigned to motor commands.

## How This Relates to Prior Thinkers

- **[[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]]**: strong convergence with [[Concepts/Readiness-to-Hand and Presence-at-Hand (Heidegger)]] and [[Concepts/Being-in-the-World (Heidegger)]] — the skilled, absorbed agent who copes without explicit representation. Active inference is a mechanistic model of Heidegger's non-thematic, world-engaged coping: the hammer's readiness-to-hand as a low-error, prediction-guided coupling.
- **[[Concepts/The Three Disciplines - Perception, Action, and Will (Hadot)]]**: Clark naturalizes the perception→action link the Stoics treated normatively; for Clark it is one continuous error-minimizing loop.
- **[[Thinkers/Descartes]]**: the unified perception–action loop dissolves the Cartesian sensory-input/motor-output separation.
