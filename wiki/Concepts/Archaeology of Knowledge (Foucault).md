# Archaeology of Knowledge (Foucault)
> Archaeology excavates the unconscious rules — the "historical a priori" — that determine what statements can be made and recognized as true in a given period, bracketing questions of meaning, authorship, and reference.

## Summary

*L'Archéologie du savoir* (1969) was Foucault's methodological self-accounting, written while he was in Tunis and intended to systematize the implicit method of his three major works: *Folie et déraison*, *Naissance de la clinique*, and *Les Mots et les choses*. It is the most abstract of his books and, by his own later assessment, the least successful.

The archaeological method operates at the level of the **statement** (*énoncé*) — a unit of discourse that is neither sentence, proposition, nor speech act, but the event of something being said within a discursive field that makes the saying possible. Archaeology maps the rules governing which statements can be made, what objects they can be about, what positions of enunciation are available, and what concepts can be combined.

Key analytical units:
- **Discursive formation:** A body of statements governed by a regular system of dispersion (not a unified theory or ideology, but a field of regularity)
- **Historical a priori:** The preconditions of possible knowledge — not transcendental (Kantian) but historical and contingent
- **Archive:** The general system determining what statements can appear, what survives, and what is transformed in a culture's total discourse
- **Positivity:** The positive, describable rules of formation — what archaeology excavates as opposed to what hermeneutics seeks to interpret

## Key Claims

- Discourse produces its own objects: medical discourse does not describe a pre-given "illness" but constitutes what counts as illness through its rules of formation.
- Authors, intentions, and meanings are epiphenomena — the level at which archaeology does not operate. What matters is the anonymous system of rules, not who said something.
- The "historical a priori" differs from Kant's transcendental a priori in being specific to a period and culture, not universal to all possible experience.
- Archaeology describes discontinuities and ruptures in discourse; it does not explain them causally — that becomes genealogy's task.

## Transition to Genealogy

By the early 1970s, Foucault recognized archaeology's limitation: it describes the rules of discourse but cannot explain why discursive formations change or how they relate to non-discursive practices (institutions, economic relations, power). **Genealogy** (Nietzschean in inspiration) supplements archaeology by tracing the contingent power struggles through which discursive formations emerge and are displaced. Archaeology asks "what are the rules?"; genealogy asks "how did these rules come to seem natural?"

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Foucault's earliest works (the Binswanger introduction, *Maladie mentale*) are explicitly phenomenological — the archaeological method was developed retrospectively and imposes false unity on the early work.
- The "historical a priori" raises the same regress problem as the episteme: how does the archaeologist's discourse escape the archive it analyzes?
