# Existence Precedes Essence (Sartre)
> The slogan of existentialism: the human being has no fixed nature given in advance — unlike the stone, which has an essence, the human first exists and only then, through free action, makes itself something.

## Summary

For Sartre, being-in-itself simply *is* what it is; it has an **essence** — the stone is hard or soft, heavy or light, with attributes clinging to it as facts. The human being, by contrast, is consciousness, which *is not* (see [[Concepts/Being-for-itself and Being-in-itself (Sartre)]]). A human therefore does not *have* an essence but **exists** — and "to exist" is taken literally as *ex-sistere*, to stand out, to perpetually step outside oneself (transcendence). When I speak about myself I necessarily go outside myself: I use a language I am not, I say what I *was* or *will be*, but I never *am*. Hence Sartre's formula: **existence precedes essence**.

Yet essence is not simply abolished. Without existence, essence would be impossible: there would be only "being," with no perspective from which being could appear. A human *with* an essence is precisely a corpse — a being that is now only an object. "Napoleon, for us today, is nothing but an essence: he is dead, that is, he exists only as something we read and write about." Because I live, and am not as the stone is, I am "a hole in being" through which being manifests itself; the human is a *lack* of being, and from this lack tries to make something of itself.

The thesis is not primarily an ethical claim but it has ethical force: there is no human nature, no base values, to fall back on. Any identity I claim ("I am a Christian," "a socialist," "a vegetarian") is never a factual description but a *task* I set myself, realised only in ongoing action — "I am what I do, and that doing is always becoming and never a fixable being." This is the hinge to [[Concepts/Radical Freedom and Situation (Sartre)|radical freedom]] and to Sartre's paradoxical *Existentialism is a Humanism*: a humanism precisely because it refuses to trap the human in any given essence.

## Key Claims / Positions
- Objects have essences; the human *exists*, i.e. has no nature given in advance.
- "To exist" = to transcend oneself perpetually; one is never simply *is*.
- Essence still requires existence (a perspective); a being with only an essence is dead/an object.
- Every claimed identity is a self-imposed task realised in action, not a fact.

### How Sartre Differs From Prior Thinkers in the Wiki
- **vs [[Thinkers/Aristotle]]** and the classical/medieval tradition: reverses the priority of essence (*to ti ên einai*, formal/final cause) over existence; there is no human *telos* or fixed form.
- **vs the [[Concepts/Great Chain of Being (Lovejoy)|Great Chain of Being]]**: no fixed rung or nature assigns the human its place; the human is self-making, not slotted into a static *scala naturae*.
- **vs [[Thinkers/Descartes]]**: the self is not a given thinking substance with a fixed nature.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] If there is no human essence and values are ungrounded, on what basis can Sartre condemn bad faith, racism, or colonialism? Welten's answer: not by appeal to values (which are arbitrary) but by appeal to the refusal to disown freedom — see [[Concepts/Radical Freedom and Situation (Sartre)]] and [[Concepts/Violence, Colonialism and Counter-Violence (Sartre and Fanon)]].
