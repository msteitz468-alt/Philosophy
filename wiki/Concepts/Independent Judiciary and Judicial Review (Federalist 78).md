# Independent Judiciary and Judicial Review (Federalist 78)

## Overview

In *Federalist No. 78*, Alexander Hamilton (Publius) defends the judiciary provisions of the proposed Constitution, particularly life tenure during good behavior and the power of courts to declare legislative acts void when contrary to the Constitution.

The judiciary "has no influence over either the sword or the purse; no direction either of the strength or of the wealth of the society; and can take no active resolution whatever. It may truly be said to have neither FORCE nor WILL, but merely judgment; and must ultimately depend upon the aid of the executive arm even for the efficacy of its judgments."

It is "beyond comparison the weakest of the three departments of power" and "can never attack with success either of the other two."

## Key Arguments

**Permanency in office (good behavior tenure) is essential**:
- In a monarchy: excellent barrier to the despotism of the prince.
- In a republic: "no less excellent barrier to the encroachments and oppressions of the representative body."
- "The standard of good behavior for the continuance in office of the judicial magistracy, is certainly one of the most valuable of the modern improvements in the practice of government."
- Complete independence "is peculiarly essential in a limited Constitution" (one containing specified exceptions to legislative authority, e.g., no bills of attainder, no ex-post-facto laws).

**Judicial review as logical necessity**:
- Every act of a delegated authority contrary to the tenor of the commission under which it is exercised is void.
- "No legislative act, therefore, contrary to the Constitution, can be valid."
- "The interpretation of the laws is the proper and peculiar province of the courts. A constitution is, in fact, and must be regarded by the judges, as a fundamental law."
- "It therefore belongs to them to ascertain its meaning, as well as the meaning of any particular act proceeding from the legislative body. If there should happen to be an irreconcilable variance between the two, that which has the superior obligation and validity ought, of course, to be preferred; or, in other words, the Constitution ought to be preferred to the statute, the intention of the people to the intention of their agents."
- This does *not* suppose superiority of the judicial to the legislative power; it supposes the power of the people is superior to both.

**Courts as bulwarks**:
- Against legislative encroachments on the Constitution.
- Against "those ill humors, which the arts of designing men, or the influence of particular conjunctures, sometimes disseminate among the people themselves" — temporary inclinations of majorities that may injure private rights or oppress minorities.
- "Though it be proper that the people... should have the power of altering or abolishing the established Constitution, whenever they find it inconsistent with their happiness," it does not follow that representatives may violate it on momentary inclination, nor that courts should connive.

**Additional reasons for permanency**:
- To secure "that independent spirit in the judges which must be essential to the faithful performance of so arduous a duty."
- To attract and retain "fit characters" — those with sufficient skill in the (voluminous) laws plus integrity. Temporary tenure would discourage qualified people from leaving lucrative practice and would throw justice into less able hands.
- "The experience of Great Britain affords an illustrious comment on the excellence of the institution."

**On inability / removal**: Age limits or other mechanical tests are crude and dangerous (arbitrary, liable to abuse, politics, or personal attachments). Insanity is a virtual disqualification; other cases better left to impeachment or the difficulty of finding fit successors.
