# Violence, Colonialism and Counter-Violence (Sartre & Fanon)
> Violence is never original or self-standing; it is always a *response*. Colonialism is the *primary* violence — a system, not a few "rotten apples" — and decolonisation requires counter-violence, the "boomerang" of the coloniser's own violence returning to him.

## Summary

Violence is central to Sartre, but he does not invoke it to banish it; his whole work understands the human from **conflict** (see [[Concepts/The Look and the Other (Sartre)]]). Conflict and the violence flowing from it are not a corruption of an originally peaceful existence — "through violence the human creates and protects his identity," for individuals and for groups alike. Sartre distinguishes **primary** from **secondary violence**: the bullied worker who finally kills colleagues commits *secondary* violence, a response to the *primary* violence of the bullying. The lesson generalises: violence never stands on its own, and "stamping out" violence regularly turns out to be the cause of secondary violence. The pacifist, blind to his own violence, is "the worst perpetrator of violence."

**Racism and anti-antisemitism.** Before the *Critique*, Sartre's dialectical view of violence appears in his analyses of racism and antisemitism. Racism reduces the human to a fixed, unchangeable identity — and it betrays not only how we see the Other but how we think of *ourselves*: the racist treats himself, in bad faith, as a thing whose fate lies in others' hands. In *Réflexions sur la question juive* (1946) the Jewish identity is analysed as a *social construction* produced through the Other: "if the Jew did not exist, the antisemite would invent him." The antisemite chooses his passion (a *logique passionnelle*) and derives his existence from it: "hatred is his faith." Antisemitism is presented as an *opinion* but is really a *feeling*; and it is a wholly *free choice* — a conception of the world.

**Terrorism.** Early on (the posthumous *Cahiers pour une morale*), Sartre rejects *individual* terrorism, because the terrorist pursues a finite end and fights for his own salvation, not for all humanity; revolution can never be forced by a select group, which is why the French Revolution remains his model. But he recognises terrorism's *legitimacy* as a stage: the colonised subject's individual "terrorist" act (e.g. the racially barred airport worker who hijacks a plane) is a refusal of the future imposed by "race," not yet risen above individual revolt toward genuine, collective liberation.

**Colonialism and Fanon.** Sartre found the framework he needed in **Frantz Fanon's** *Les damnés de la terre* (*The Wretched of the Earth*, 1961), to which he wrote the preface. Colonialism is **violence and nothing but** — Western powers, full of "good intentions," conquered and exploited the world, reducing the Other to a mere en-soi, with Western morality and religion supplying legitimacy. Colonialism is a **system**, not an ethical problem of removing a few "rotten apples": "there are no good colonialists." Fanon, a psychiatrist, argues that the cure for the colonised's lost self-consciousness is **violence**, which has a *therapeutic* function — only in violence can the oppressed recover his human status, since he already lives in a world of violence. Sartre, writing as the *European* whom Fanon's mirror terrifies, drives "the dialectic to its end: we Europeans too are being decolonised." The coloniser's violence returns: "it is the moment of the boomerang … it comes back at us, and as before we fail to see that it is our own violence." Sartre was the first to speak of **neocolonialism** — colonial arrogance persisting in a world that believes itself free of colonialism.

**Late engagement.** This shifted Sartre's attention from communism to the liberation of the colonies and Third World, always a *counter-terror* against the real occupier, the West. He supported the Algerian FLN (against Camus's failed conciliation), the May 1968 students, and ultra-left causes; he controversially defended the Palestinian Black September actions at Munich (1972) on the parallel with the FLN, and visited the imprisoned Andreas Baader (1974) — though there he disapproved of the RAF's violence, making the visit a protest over detention conditions rather than an endorsement.

## Key Claims / Positions
- Violence is never original; it is always a response (primary vs secondary violence).
- The pacifist is blind to his own violence; conflict constitutes identity.
- Racism/antisemitism fix the human as a thing and are *free choices*; the Jew is constructed by the antisemite.
- Individual terrorism is legitimate but insufficient; real liberation must be collective.
- Colonialism is a *system* of primary violence; "there are no good colonialists."
- Decolonising counter-violence (Fanon) is therapeutic; the coloniser's violence returns as a "boomerang"; Sartre names *neocolonialism*.

### How Sartre Differs / Connects in the Wiki
- **vs [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]] / Lenin**: takes up Marx's "violence is the midwife of history" (Lenin, *State and Revolution*) but routes it through the colonised subject and the dialectic of recognition rather than class economics alone.
- Builds directly on [[Concepts/Totalization, Series and Group (Sartre)|scarcity and fraternity-terror]] and on the [[Concepts/The Look and the Other (Sartre)|conflictual structure of the Other]].
- **vs natural-rights and liberal traditions** ([[Thinkers/Locke]], [[Thinkers/Kant]]): denies foundational human rights; "good intentions," "peace," and "humanism" are charged with masking systemic violence.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] Endorsing counter-violence and (qualified) terrorism while condemning the violence of colonisers raises the unresolved question of how Sartre distinguishes legitimate from illegitimate violence without the foundational values he denies — the same tension flagged at [[Concepts/Existence Precedes Essence (Sartre)]].
