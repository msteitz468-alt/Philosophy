# God as Cosmic Sustaining Cause (Pseudo-Aristotle)

> God sustains the whole universe not by direct, laborious administration of every particular, but by an effortless, remote first impulse transmitted through a graduated chain of causes — as a Persian king rules through satraps, a puppeteer moves a whole figure by one string, or a chorus obeys a single leader's signal — reaching even to the corruptible sublunary world.

## Summary

Having argued that the cosmos's permanence arises from a harmony of contraries (unity from plurality — "junctions are: wholes and not wholes," quoting Heraclitus), *De Mundo* turns to "the cause which holds all things together." It rejects the crude view that God is diffusely and literally present everywhere ("full of God") in favor of a dignity-preserving alternative: God occupies "the highest place" in the heavens and governs the whole through a single, effortless motion that propagates outward, "each acting according to its own constitution," much as one thrown object of a given shape moves according to its shape, or as different animals released together move each to its own natural element. The treatise elaborates this with a cascade of analogies — the invisible Great King of Persia ruling a vast empire through satraps, couriers, and signal-fires without personally performing any task; a puppet-showman moving many parts of a figure by pulling one string; a chorus-leader whose single signal produces a harmonized song from many different voices; the law of a city, "fixed and immutable in the minds of those who are under it," which directs the entire life of the state without any official personally supervising each act. God is accordingly identified with a long list of traditional divine names and functions (Zeus, Fate, Necessity, Destiny, Lot, Nemesis, Adrasteia), read as so many names for the single, unified, sustaining first cause — "God, then... holding the beginning and the end and the middle of all things," with Justice as his attendant avenger.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Divine governance is compatible with divine transcendence: God need not be everywhere in person to sustain everything, only to originate a single transmitted causal impulse.
- The graduated-chain model explains how a single, simple, effortless cause can produce the vast diversity and apparent independence of natural motions and events.
- Traditional polytheistic names and abstract fate-concepts (Necessity, Fate, Destiny, Nemesis) are unified under this same single sustaining-cause account — a philosophical monotheism dressed in traditional Greek religious vocabulary.
- The city-law analogy makes explicit that this is a governance-by-impersonal-principle model, not governance by continuous personal intervention.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Vs. genuine Aristotle's [[Concepts/The Unmoved Mover (Aristotle)]]**: this is a substantively different theology. Aristotle's unmoved mover causes motion only as a final cause — an object of love/desire that the heavens imitate by circular motion — and is explicitly *thought thinking itself*, wholly self-absorbed and unaware of and uninvolved with the world's particulars. *De Mundo*'s God, by contrast, is efficiently and providentially active, originating and sustaining a real causal chain that reaches down to earthly events (winds, earthquakes, seasons) — a providential, quasi-Stoic cosmic ruler far closer to Stoic *pronoia* (providence) than to Aristotle's self-contemplating first mover.
- **Vs. Stoicism generally**: the doctrine (traced by Capelle to Poseidonius) matches Stoic cosmic sympathy and providence far better than Peripatetic theology, making this treatise better read as a Stoic-Peripatetic hybrid than as authentic Aristotelian doctrine.
- **Downstream — Neoplatonism**: the graduated-transmission model (single cause, decreasing "benefit" the farther a thing is from God — "the earth... being farthest removed from the benefit which proceeds from God") strikingly anticipates the hierarchical emanation scheme of [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] ([[Concepts/Emanation (Proodos)]]: One → Nous → Soul → matter, with reality/goodness diminishing at each remove) — though *De Mundo* keeps a single personal-ish God rather than an impersonal, ineffable One beyond being.
- **Downstream — Aquinas' analogy of governance**: the general-through-particular and law-through-citizens analogies for divine providence recur in medieval treatments of how a single divine will governs a multiplicity of secondary causes without eliminating their genuine causal autonomy (cf. [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]'s primary/secondary causation, and the Ghazali/Averroes occasionalism debate at [[Concepts/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation (Ghazali and Ibn Rushd)]] — this treatise sides firmly with real secondary causation, since each moved thing acts "according to its own constitution").
