# Polity and the Middle-Class Constitution (Aristotle)

> Turning from the ideal to the practically best, Aristotle argues polity — a fusion of oligarchy and democracy anchored by a large middle class — is the most stable and generally best constitution actually achievable "for most states."

## Summary

*Politics* Book IV marks a deliberate practical turn: "the true legislator and statesman ought to be acquainted, not only with that which is best in the abstract, but also with that which is best relatively to circumstances." Aristotle surveys the real variety within each constitutional type — five forms of democracy and four of oligarchy, each series running from the most law-governed/moderate to the most lawless/despotic (the "last" democracy resembling tyranny, the "dynasty" resembling narrow despotism) — grounding these differences in the composition of a state's rich, poor, and middle elements. He defines polity technically as "a fusion of oligarchy and democracy," achievable in three ways (combining the laws of both, taking a mean between their arrangements, or borrowing one element from each), and distinguishes it from aristocracy, which blends wealth, virtue, and numbers (or two of the three) rather than merely rich and poor. The chapter's centerpiece is the middle-class argument: "the city which is composed of middle-class citizens is necessarily best constituted... those states are likely to be well-administered in which the middle class is large, and stronger if possible than both the other classes," because the very rich tend to violence and inability to obey, the very poor to servility and envy, while the middle class alone can both obey and rule, free from the plotting endemic to the extremes — "the mean condition of states is clearly best, for no other is free from faction." He therefore instructs any legislator, whatever the nominal constitution, to strengthen the middle class: "the legislator should always include the middle class in his government; if he makes his laws oligarchical, to the middle class let him look." A historical note ties the expansion of constitutional (non-royal, non-oligarchic) government to the rise of the hoplite class in early Greek city-states.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Political science must judge not only the abstract best regime but the best regime relative to a given people's actual circumstances.
- Polity is a genuine fifth "true" form, technically a fusion/mean of oligarchy and democracy, distinct from aristocracy (which also weighs virtue).
- A large middle class is the single strongest predictor of constitutional stability, across both oligarchies and democracies, because moderate property enables the combination of capacity-to-rule and willingness-to-obey that extremes lack.
- Legislators should engineer toward the middle class regardless of a constitution's nominal type — a general design principle, not merely advice for one regime form.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Downstream — republican and constitutional theory**: the middle-class stability thesis recurs throughout later political thought (e.g., concerns with faction and balanced government) and anticipates modern political-sociology claims linking a broad middle class to democratic stability.
- **Contrast with [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]**: Marx's class analysis treats the "middle class" (petite bourgeoisie) as a historically transitional, unstable formation destined to be polarized into proletariat and bourgeoisie, directly opposing Aristotle's treatment of the middle as the natural stabilizing anchor of political order.
- **Vs. Book II's critique of Plato**: the middle-class polity is Aristotle's own constructive, empirically-grounded answer to the same stability problem Plato tried to solve via guardian communism — plurality and proportion rather than engineered unity.
