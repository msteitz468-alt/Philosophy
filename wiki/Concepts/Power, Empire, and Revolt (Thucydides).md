# Power, Empire, and Revolt (Thucydides)

> From Thucydides’ *History of the Peloponnesian War* (Book III excerpt on the Mytilene revolt): analysis of how empires generate revolt among subject states when alliances shift from mutual benefit/equality to domination by the stronger party. Explores the calculations of power, the role of diplomacy and preemption, siege strategy, the rhetoric of justification (honor, necessity, justice), and the fragility of coalitions under asymmetric power.

## Core Dynamics (from the narrative)
- **Empire vs Alliance**: When a stronger party (Athens) treats “allies” as subjects, the weaker have reason to revolt. Mytilenaean envoys at Olympia argue that Greek distrust of alliance-breakers applies only to equal partnerships; oppressive empire justifies breaking away.
- **Strategy and Escalation**: Preemptive fleet dispatch, secret diplomacy (Mytilene to Sparta while negotiating with Athens), shift from limited fighting to full siege (sea blockade vs land control).
- **Rhetoric of Revolt**: Appeals mix pragmatic power analysis with moral claims (mutual goodwill, equality, honor). The powerful and the rebellious both invoke norms.
- **Wider Context**: Simultaneous operations, the role of external powers (Lacedaemonians, Boeotians), internal preparations (walls, ships, supplies) as signals of intent.
