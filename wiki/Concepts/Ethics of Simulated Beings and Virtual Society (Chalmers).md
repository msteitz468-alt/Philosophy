# Ethics of Simulated Beings and Virtual Society (Chalmers)
> "What bestows moral status is consciousness" — so simulated people matter equally, virtual assault and theft are real wrongs, and virtual worlds need political philosophy.

## Summary

*Reality+* chs. 18–19. **Moral status** (ch. 18): the zombie trolley problem (most would kill five zombies to save one conscious human) shows consciousness, not biology, grounds status. Against Bentham/Singer sentientism, the **philosophical Vulcan** — conscious but affectless — retains full status: "The right question is not 'Can they suffer?' but 'Are they conscious?'" Since simulated brains can be conscious ([[Concepts/Machine Consciousness and Mind Uploading (Chalmers)]]), "It is not acceptable to kill five simulated people to save one ordinary human" — test by reversal: would it be OK for our simulators to kill us? Creating happy simulations is permissible (like reproduction) but not lightly; a **simulation theodicy** (create all worlds where happiness outweighs suffering; simulators aren't omniscient) parallels Leibniz. Kant's Principle of Humanity becomes a **Principle of Personhood**: creating sims *merely* as means — entertainment, prediction, science — is wrong; full-scale war simulations might be banned.

**Virtual society** (ch. 19): the LambdaMOO Mr. Bungle case "was a real virtual assault that really happened to the victim"; harm scales with avatar attachment. Virtual theft is real theft (the Dutch Runescape conviction), against Wildman/McDonnell's fictionalist "puzzle of virtual theft." Luck's **gamer's dilemma** (virtual murder OK, virtual pedophilia not) is resolved by virtue ethics — what the act indicates about character. Creator ethics: Ramirez & LaBarge's **Equivalence Principle** — "If it would be wrong to allow subjects to have a certain experience in reality, then it would be wrong... in a virtually real setting." Governance: most worlds are **corporatocracies**; social contract theory fits virtual worlds *better* than nations (users actually consent and can exit); users may shift from customers to citizens. **Virtual abundance** (reduplicable space and goods) could suspend Rawls/Hume's scarcity condition on justice, yielding a post-scarcity near-utopia (more plausibly state-run; UBI at minimum) — but positional goods stay scarce, and per Anderson's relational equality and Crenshaw's intersectionality, oppression can carry over and multiply.

## How This Relates to Prior Thinkers

- **[[Thinkers/Kant]]** ([[Sources/Metaphysics of Morals - Immanuel Kant]]): the Categorical Imperative extended to digital persons.
- **Bentham / [[Thinkers/John Stuart Mill]] / Singer**: sentientism narrowed to consciousness-ism; Anscombe's virtue-ethics revival (with Confucian parallels) supplies the gamer's-dilemma solution.
- **[[Thinkers/Thomas Hobbes]]** ([[Sources/Leviathan - Thomas Hobbes (1651)]]) and Mozi: state-of-nature contract theory transplanted to worlds with genuine consent and exit.
- **[[Thinkers/Jean-Jacques Rousseau]]/Rawls**: distributive justice under abundance; [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]'s abundance premise noted.
- **[[Thinkers/Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz]]**: simulation theodicy as naturalized best-of-all-possible-worlds — cf. [[Concepts/Simulation Theology (Chalmers)]].
- **[[Sources/Superintelligence - Nick Bostrom (2014)]]**: mind-crime worries about suffering simulated minds get their ethical foundation here.
