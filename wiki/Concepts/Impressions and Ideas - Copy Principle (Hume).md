# Impressions and Ideas - Copy Principle (Hume)

> Hume's foundational empiricist principle (*Enquiry* Section II): all the perceptions of the mind divide into **impressions** (our more lively perceptions — sensations, passions, emotions, when we hear, see, feel, love, hate, desire, will) and **ideas** (the fainter copies of these in thought, memory, and imagination), distinguished only by their *force and vivacity*. The **Copy Principle**: every simple idea is a copy of a corresponding simple impression. From this follows a meaning-test that "banishes all that jargon" from metaphysics: *"from what impression is that supposed idea derived?"* — and if none can be produced, the term is empty.

## The Distinction

"The most lively thought is still inferior to the dullest sensation." The only difference between feeling pain and remembering or imagining it is one of degree — force and vivacity — not of kind. The mind's apparent boundless creativity (golden mountains, virtuous horses, the idea of God) is really only the *compounding, transposing, augmenting, or diminishing* of materials originally supplied by impressions. "All our ideas or more feeble perceptions are copies of our impressions or more lively ones."

Two arguments for the Copy Principle:
1. **Analysis**: trace any complex idea, however sublime, and it resolves into simple ideas copied from prior impressions (the idea of God = the ideas of goodness and wisdom, drawn from our own minds, augmented without limit).
2. **The deficiency argument**: someone lacking a sense lacks the corresponding ideas — a blind man has no idea of colour, a deaf man none of sound; "a Laplander or Negroe has no notion of the relish of wine." Restore the sense and the ideas become available.

## The Meaning-Test (Hume's "Microscope")

The principle's great use is critical. When a philosophical term is suspected of being employed "without any meaning or idea (as is but too frequent), we need but enquire, from what impression is that supposed idea derived?" If no impression can be assigned, the suspicion is confirmed: the term is empty noise. This is Hume's "new microscope" for the moral sciences — a tool to throw light on obscure ideas by producing their original impressions, or to expose pseudo-ideas (and a clear ancestor of later verificationist criteria of meaning; cf. the "commit it to the flames" conclusion in [[Concepts/Mitigated Scepticism (Hume)]]). It is the weapon later turned on *power/necessary connexion* in [[Concepts/Necessary Connexion and the Two Definitions of Cause (Hume)]] and on the *self*.

## The Missing Shade of Blue

Hume himself raises "one contradictory phaenomenon." Present someone, long acquainted with colours, with a graded series of shades of blue from which one particular shade is omitted; he will perceive the gap and — most will agree — be able to *raise up the idea of the missing shade from his own imagination*, though he never had the corresponding impression. This refutes the Copy Principle as a strict universal. Hume concedes it but judges the instance "so singular, that it is scarcely worth our observing, and does not merit, that for it alone we should alter our general maxim." (A famous interpretive crux: a philosopher discarding a counterexample to his own central tool.)

## Relevance to Vault

- **Anti-rationalist engine**: the Copy Principle is the direct denial of Cartesian/Platonic *innate ideas* and recollection. Where [[Thinkers/Descartes]] makes the idea of God an innate "trademark," Hume derives it by augmenting impressions of our own qualities. Contrast [[Concepts/Cogito (Descartes)]] (the self as known substance) — Hume finds no impression of an enduring self at all.
- **Empiricist lineage**: radicalizes [[Thinkers/Epicurus]]/[[Thinkers/Lucretius]] (senses as criterion; ideas from *simulacra*) and [[Thinkers/Locke]] (theory of ideas from sensation/reflection + explicit Book I anti-innatism and tabula rasa + Book II two fountains, simple ideas, primary/secondary; now primary-sourced in vault), but turns the principle sceptically against the very metaphysics earlier empiricists retained. See new [[Sources/An Essay Concerning Human Understanding - Locke]], [[Concepts/Sensation and Reflection as the Two Fountains of Ideas (Locke)]], [[Concepts/Simple Ideas (Locke)]], [[Concepts/Primary and Secondary Qualities (Locke)]], and [[Concepts/No Innate Principles or Ideas (Locke)]].
- **AI / vault meta**: a strikingly modern picture of cognition — *ideas as compressed, lower-vivacity reactivations of richer perceptual "impressions."* Resonates with embeddings/representations grounded in sensory data, the symbol-grounding problem ("no impression → no idea" ≈ "ungrounded token → no meaning"), and multimodal grounding (a model with no visual input "has no idea of colours"). The meaning-test is a usable *lint heuristic*: for any abstract claim, ask what observable matter of fact (or formal relation) it cashes out to — if neither, flag it.
- **Method**: models the discipline of tracing every wiki claim back to its source impression (the raw document), refusing free-floating ideas without provenance.

**Open questions**:
- Does the missing shade of blue fatally weaken the principle, or merely bound it?
- Is the impression/idea distinction (degree of vivacity) sharp enough to do the work asked of it — esp. since [[Concepts/Custom and Belief (Hume)|belief itself]] is defined as a difference of vivacity?
