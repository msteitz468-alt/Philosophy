# Critique of Platonic Forms (Aristotle)

> Aristotle's sustained polemic against his teacher's separate Forms — opened in Book I, resumed devastatingly in Books XIII–XIV. The Forms, he argues, double the world without explaining it: they are causally inert, generate vicious regresses (the "third man"), and collapse into incoherence when identified with numbers.

## Summary

Aristotle accepts the Socratic search for universal **definitions** and agrees that knowledge requires something stable amid Heraclitean flux. What he rejects is the Platonic inference that the universals therefore **exist apart** (*chōris*) as eternal Forms (*Ideai*) which sensibles "participate in" (*methexis*). "Socrates did not make the universals or the definitions exist apart; *they* gave them separate existence." His objections, gathered from Books I, XIII, and XIV:

1. **They double the world uselessly.** "The Forms are practically equal to — or not fewer than — the things, in trying to explain which these thinkers proceeded from them to the Forms." To explain the many you posit an equal many.
2. **The arguments for Forms fail or prove too much.** The "one over many" argument yields Forms of negations and relations; the argument from thinking of perished things yields Forms of perishables — all results Platonists themselves reject.
3. **The Third Man regress.** If Socrates and Callias are men by sharing in Man-itself, then Socrates, Callias, *and* Man-itself form a new plurality requiring a further Form, *ad infinitum*.
4. **Causal inertness — the central objection.** "What in the world do the Forms contribute to sensible things? They cause neither movement nor any change in them." They are not the substance of sensibles (else they'd be *in* them, not apart), so they explain neither the *being* nor the *becoming* of things. "To say they are patterns and the other things share in them is to use empty words and poetical metaphors" — for a thing "might come to be like another without being copied from it" (a man like Socrates could arise whether or not Socrates exists).
5. **Multiple patterns.** Man has *animal*, *two-footed*, and *man-himself* as Forms — "several patterns of the same thing," and pattern becomes copy of a higher pattern.
6. **Separation is incoherent.** "It would seem impossible that the substance and that of which it is the substance should exist apart" ([[Concepts/Essence and Definition (Aristotle)]]: a thing is one with its essence).
7. **They ignore the efficient and final cause.** Forms "have no connexion with the cause we see in the arts, that for whose sake mind and nature operate" — they leave out [[Concepts/The Four Causes (Aristotle)|efficient and final causation]].

**Books XIII–XIV** extend the attack to the **mathematicals** and **Form-numbers**. The objects of mathematics do **not** exist as separate substances (that would breed an absurd hierarchy of separated solids, planes, lines, and points, and reverse the order of priority); rather they exist by **abstraction** — the geometer studies sensible things *qua* continuous, *qua* quantity, without those quantities existing apart. As for the Platonist/Pythagorean reduction of substance to **number** (the One and the indefinite dyad / "the great and the small"): Aristotle shows it collapses — units cannot be both fully associable (then only mathematical number, no unique Forms) and inassociable (then no addition, no number-series); ideal numbers cannot be generated coherently; an eternal number with material elements would be "capable of not existing," hence not eternal. And number is **not a cause** in any of the four senses — not agent, matter, ratio-form, or end. "Honey-water is no more wholesome if mixed in the proportion three-times-three." Finally, making the One the first principle *and* the Good entails that everything but the One "partakes of the bad" — a reductio.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Forms **redundantly double** the sensible world.
- The **Third Man** regress refutes the one-over-many argument for Forms.
- Forms are **causally inert** — explain neither being nor becoming; "participation" is empty metaphor.
- A thing is **one with its essence**, so essence cannot exist *apart* as a separate Form.
- **Mathematicals exist by abstraction**, not as separate substances.
- **Number cannot be substance or cause**; the One-and-dyad derivation is incoherent and yields the One-as-Good reductio.

## How This Sits Among Thinkers

- This concept is the **primary-text engine** of [[Contradictions/Plato vs Aristotle on the Forms]] — the foundational metaphysical split of the classical tradition. Aristotle's positive alternative is immanent form ([[Concepts/Form and Matter - Hylomorphism (Aristotle)]], [[Concepts/Substance (Ousia) (Aristotle)]]).
- **Versus [[Thinkers/Pythagoras]] / [[Concepts/Number as the Essence of Reality (Pythagoras)]]**: the XIII–XIV polemic is as much against Pythagorean number-metaphysics as against Plato.
- **The realism/nominalism lineage**: this critique launches the medieval **problem of universals**; later [[Thinkers/Locke]] deflates real essences ([[Concepts/Nominal and Real Essences (Locke)]]), and [[Thinkers/Berkeley]] denies abstract general ideas altogether ([[Concepts/Subjective Idealism (Berkeley)]]).
- **Counter-current — Neoplatonism**: [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] re-transcendentalizes the Forms within Intellect (Nous) emanating from [[Concepts/The One (To Hen)]], reasserting the Platonic side Aristotle attacked.
- **Mathematics by abstraction** anticipates later anti-Platonist philosophies of mathematics (empiricism, fictionalism), against mathematical Platonism.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Aristotle's own primary substances are forms/essences that look suspiciously like universals; critics ask whether his immanent forms escape the very objections he presses against separate Forms — see the unresolved tension noted in [[Concepts/Substance (Ousia) (Aristotle)]].
