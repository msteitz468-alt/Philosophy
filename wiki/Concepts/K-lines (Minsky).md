# K-lines (Minsky)

> Minsky's mechanism for memory: a "K-line" (Knowledge-line) is a structure that attaches itself to the set of mental agents that are active at a moment of success, insight, or memorable experience. Later, activating the same K-line arouses (partially) the same agents, thereby restoring a similar "mental state" and making it easier to solve analogous problems. Memory is not separate storage and retrieval but *reactivation* of the society that originally did the work.

## Summary

The basic idea: "Whenever you 'get a good idea,' solve a problem, or have a memorable experience, you activate a K-line to 'represent' it. A K-line is a wire-like structure that attaches itself to whichever mental agents are active when you solve a problem or have a good idea. When you activate that K-line later, the agents attached to it are aroused, putting you into a 'mental state' much like the one you were in when you solved that problem or got that idea."

Analogy (from Kenneth Haase): Smear your hands with red paint while fixing a bicycle; the tools you use get red marks. Next time, activate "red" to gather the right tools quickly. Different jobs use different colors; tools (agents) can participate in many K-lines.

This keeps knowledge close to the agents that use it, solves the "how do I know what to retrieve?" problem, and explains why memory feels like "becoming more like an earlier version of yourself."

K-lines are partial (you can't record *every* detail without being larger than yourself) and context-sensitive. Policies are needed for conflicts between currently active agents and those a K-line tries to re-arouse.

## Key Claims

- Memory is reactivation of prior agent activity patterns, not a separate lookup in a passive store.
- "Re-membering" literally means re-assembling the members (agents) of a previous society.
- This makes transfer to similar problems natural and efficient.
- It explains why reminding works and why context matters so much for recall.

## Relation to Vault

- **Dennett**: Supports Multiple Drafts — memory is just more content fixation and revision among the drafts; K-lines provide a mechanism for how previous editorial states can be partially re-instantiated.
- **Hofstadter**: K-lines are a concrete way "strange loops" or tangled hierarchies can carry history and self-reference across time; reactivation creates the temporal tangledness of mind.
- **Turing**: A specific implementation detail for how child machines can usefully "remember" and build on prior learning episodes.
