# Basic Income Guarantee (Ford)

> Martin Ford's (2015) proposal for a guaranteed basic income (or basic income guarantee) as the central element of a "new economic paradigm" required to adapt to widespread technological unemployment and the erosion of the traditional link between work, wages, and consumer purchasing power. In a future where machines and algorithms perform most predictable/routine work (blue- and white-collar), jobs will no longer be sufficient to distribute the purchasing power needed to sustain a mass-market consumer economy. A basic income — an unconditional cash payment to all citizens or residents, sufficient to meet basic needs — would provide individual economic security, maintain aggregate demand, act as an automatic stabilizer against recessions, and potentially "pay for itself" through increased economic activity and tax revenue. It is presented as superior to (or a necessary evolution beyond) traditional welfare patches, education-focused retraining, or public works in the face of general-purpose automation.

## Summary (from the source)

Ford argues that the core problem is not (only) that people will lack work or meaning, but that the economy as currently structured will lack sufficient *consumers* with purchasing power. "Machines do not consume." The postwar virtuous cycle — productivity gains → higher wages → more spending → more jobs and investment — has already broken (see [[Concepts/Technological Unemployment (Ford)]]). Without a new mechanism to get money into the hands of potential consumers, demand collapses even as productive capacity (via automation) remains high or grows.

The primary solution Ford advances is a guaranteed basic income. Key elements and arguments:
- **Unconditional and universal (or near-universal)**: Paid to individuals regardless of employment status or willingness to work (though some variants phase out at higher incomes). This distinguishes it from traditional welfare, which creates high effective marginal tax rates (benefit cliffs) that discourage work.
- **Sustains consumer demand**: In an economy where capital and technology capture most income, a basic income recycles some of that income back to the population as a whole, enabling them to continue purchasing goods and services. Most recipients would spend the bulk of it, directly stimulating economic activity.
- **Economic stabilizer**: Acts as an automatic counter-cyclical force. In downturns, it prevents the kind of demand collapse that turns recessions into depressions.
- **May pay for itself (at least partially)**: By boosting consumption and growth, it could generate enough additional tax revenue (income, sales, corporate) to offset a significant portion of its cost. Reduced spending on other safety-net programs, lower costs from poverty-related social ills (crime, health, etc.), and avoidance of deeper recession costs are additional offsets.
- **Revenue sources**: Carbon tax (double dividend: revenue + emissions reduction), value-added tax (VAT — the U.S. is unusual among advanced nations in lacking one), higher corporate taxes or closing avoidance loopholes, wealth or capital taxes (referencing Piketty), financial transaction tax, land tax, or reallocation from existing programs.
- **Alternatives considered**: Wealth/capital distribution (giving people ownership stakes so they receive returns from the capital-intensive economy); expanded Earned Income Tax Credit (but limited because it requires earned income and is mostly child-focused); public works or job guarantees (Ford sees these as insufficient at the scale required and potentially less efficient than direct cash).
- **Work and incentives**: Ford addresses the concern that a basic income would cause mass withdrawal from the workforce. He argues most people would continue to work for additional income, status, meaning, or social connection. There could even be a "Peltzman effect" — with a safety net, people might be *more* willing to take entrepreneurial risks or invest in education/upskilling. Evidence from existing small-scale pilots and programs (e.g., Alaska Permanent Fund) is cited as encouraging.
- **Political and transitional realism**: Ford acknowledges the extreme political difficulty in the current U.S. environment ("nearly all American politicians are terrified to even utter the word 'tax'"). He notes that major social reforms (e.g., Social Security, Medicare, the Affordable Care Act) took decades. He argues it is not too soon to begin serious discussion and design work, and that near-term steps (EITC expansion, pilot programs, revenue-neutral carbon tax with dividend) could build toward it.

The proposal is framed as pragmatic adaptation to technological reality rather than utopian redistribution. In a world of abundance created by machines, the question is how to distribute the fruits so that the system remains stable and most people can participate as consumers and citizens.

## Key Claims / How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Ford**: Basic income is the most direct and efficient way to address the demand-side problem created by automation. It decouples basic economic security from traditional employment without requiring the government to pick specific jobs or industries to subsidize. It is likely necessary (though not sufficient) for broad-based prosperity in a highly automated future.
- **Kelly (forces context)**: The "cognifying" and "becoming" forces that make answers and many services cheap and ubiquitous also make human labor in predictable roles less necessary. A basic income can be seen as one way to manage the "particulars" of the inevitable while preserving the consumer base that allows the technium-driven economy to function. Cross to [[Sources/The Inevitable - Kevin Kelly (2016)]], [[Concepts/Cognifying (Kelly)]], [[Concepts/Becoming (Kelly)]].
- **Bostrom (risk and power concentration)**: In a world where advanced AI and automation concentrate enormous wealth and power in the hands of those who own the capital and algorithms, a basic income (or capital distribution) is one mechanism to prevent extreme inequality and the social instability that could accompany misaligned or unaccountable superintelligence paths. Cross to [[Sources/Superintelligence - Nick Bostrom (2014)]].
- **Kurzweil (abundance framing)**: Kurzweil's vision of exponential progress and the Singularity promises radical abundance. A basic income can be viewed as a transitional or complementary policy to ensure that abundance is widely shared rather than captured by a tiny elite during the transition. Cross to [[Sources/The Singularity Is Near - Ray Kurzweil (2005)]].
- **Wiener (meaning and "know-what")**: Wiener warned of the Second Industrial Revolution displacing not just muscle but judgment, and emphasized the need for human meaning and purpose beyond economic utility. A basic income frees people from pure survival labor and opens space for education, care work, creative pursuits, community, and the "know-what" that Wiener saw as essential. Cross to [[Sources/The Human Use of Human Beings - Norbert Wiener (1950)]].
- **Broader policy debates**: Connects to historical proposals (1964 Triple Revolution report, Milton Friedman negative income tax ideas, various UBI pilots and proposals). Contrasts with job-guarantee or "human-centered" work arguments that emphasize the non-economic value of employment.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **Incentive effects vs. demand/stabilizer benefits**: Will a basic income cause significant withdrawal from the labor force (reducing output and tax base) or enable more entrepreneurship, care work, education, and risk-taking that ultimately increases prosperity? Empirical evidence is limited and contested; outcomes likely depend on design (amount, phase-outs, cultural norms).
- **Funding and political feasibility**: The economic logic may be strong, but raising the required revenue in a polarized environment where "tax" is toxic is a massive barrier. Revenue-neutral designs (carbon tax + dividend) or pilots may be necessary entry points. Alternatives focused on capital ownership may face different political and economic trade-offs (e.g., risk aversion if people's only wealth is at stake).
- **With "work as meaning" views**: Some argue that jobs provide essential structure, dignity, and social connection that cash alone cannot replace. Ford acknowledges non-economic values of work but argues that in a world where there are not enough high-quality jobs, forcing people into make-work or poverty is worse. The debate is partly empirical (how many people would stop working?) and partly philosophical (what constitutes a good life in an age of abundance?).
- **Universal vs. targeted / phase-outs**: Full universality avoids stigma and cliffs but is expensive. Targeted or phased versions (e.g., negative income tax) are cheaper but reintroduce complexity and disincentives. Ford leans toward broader/unconditional designs.
- **Interaction with AI acceleration (Kelly/Kurzweil) and risks (Bostrom)**: As automation deepens, the revenue base may shift even more toward capital and away from labor income. A basic income funded by capital/wealth/carbon taxes becomes more necessary but also harder to implement politically if power concentrates further. Ties into questions of who controls the technium/AI and how its fruits are distributed.
