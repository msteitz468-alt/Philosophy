# Providence and the Problem of Evil (Maimonides)

> Maimonides' theodicy (*Guide* III.8–24): **evil has no positive existence** — all evils are privations — and most evil is self-inflicted, so the cosmos is overwhelmingly good. **Providence is graded in proportion to intellectual perfection**: it reaches human individuals according to their share in the Active Intellect, but only the *species* of other living things. The Book of Job is read as a parable staging the rival theories of providence, in which Job's redemption is an *intellectual* awakening.

## Evil as Privation

God "only produces existence, and all existence is good: whilst evils are of a negative character." Evil is attributable to God only indirectly, through His creating the corporeal substrate in which privation (death, disease, ignorance) can occur. Maimonides distinguishes **three kinds of evil**, against cosmic pessimism:
1. Natural evils from having a perishable body (deformity, disaster) — *rare*.
2. Evils people inflict on one another (war, violence) — more numerous, still not universal.
3. Evils each person inflicts on himself through vice and excess — "by far the largest class."

The pessimist's error (he targets al-Razi, who "counted all the evils one by one") is **egocentrism**: "an ignorant man believes that the whole universe only exists for him... If anything happens to him contrary to his expectation, he at once concludes that the whole universe is evil," forgetting that mankind is an "infinitesimal portion" of being. This privation theory is shared with [[Thinkers/Augustine]] and [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] (evil as the absence of good, not a rival positive principle) — a key node in the vault's [[Contradictions/Atomist Materialism vs Platonic-Aristotelian Teleology|teleology]] thread and an answer to dualist/Manichaean accounts.

## The Five Theories of Providence

Maimonides catalogues: (1) **Epicurus** — no providence, all chance; (2) **Aristotle** — providence reaches only species, individual sublunary events are chance; (3) **Ash'arites** — everything is the bare Divine Will, no free will or final cause; (4) **Mu'tazilites** — divine wisdom and justice, with compensation even for animals; (5) **the Law** — free will plus strict justice.

## His Own Graded View

Providence is **proportional to intellect**: "the action of Divine Providence is proportional to the endowment of intellect... the greater the human perfection a person has attained, the greater the benefit he derives from Divine Providence." For non-human life he sides with Aristotle — a falling leaf or a spider catching a fly is chance, providence operating only at the species level. Individual human providence is a function of one's actualized connection to the Divine Intellect; the person absorbed in God is, in that measure, shielded, while the one who turns away is "exposed to all that may befall him."

This rests on the **homonymity of divine knowledge**: God's knowledge "is not of the same kind as ours, but totally different from it and admitting of no analogy" — it is identical with His essence, does not change with its objects, and does not abolish the contingency of the possible. "The homonymity of the term 'knowledge' misled people" into the false dilemmas about foreknowledge and freedom. (This is the same divine-knowledge problem the vault tracks from the other side in [[Concepts/God's Knowledge of Particulars (Ghazali)]].)

## Job as Parable

Maimonides reads Job as a fiction dramatizing the theories. Each speaker voices one: **Job** himself begins with **Aristotle's** view (the righteous and wicked fare alike; God is indifferent to individuals); **Eliphaz** holds the **Law's** view (suffering is deserved punishment); **Bildad** the **Mu'tazilite** view (suffering earns future compensation); **Zophar** the **Ash'arite** view (inscrutable Will). **Elihu** introduces the superior teaching (angelic intercession, prophecy, learning from nature). Crucially, Job is *not* called wise at the outset, and his transformation is **intellectual**: "so long as Job's knowledge of God was based on tradition... 'I have heard of thee by the hearing of the ear; but now mine eye seeth thee.'" Once he *knows* God rather than knowing *about* Him, earthly suffering can no longer disturb his felicity. "This lesson is the principal object of the whole Book of Job... that we should not imagine His knowledge to be similar to ours."

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. [[Thinkers/Al-Ghazali]] / Ash'arism:** Maimonides explicitly names and *rejects* the Ash'arite theory (bare arbitrary Will, no free will) — consistent with his [[Concepts/Critique of the Kalam (Maimonides)|wholesale rejection of the Kalam]]. Where Ghazali's God directly wills every event, Maimonides' God governs through graded intellect and real natures, leaving sublunary individual chance and human freedom intact.
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Aristotle]]:** he adopts Aristotle's species-only providence for non-humans but *exceeds* it for the intellectually perfected human — a deliberate splice of Aristotelian naturalism with the Law.
- **vs. the Stoics / [[Contradictions/Stoic Determinism vs Aristotelian Contingency|determinism]]:** like the Stoics he ties felicity to the mind's relation to the cosmic order, but he preserves contingency and free will rather than fate.
- **vs. modern theodicy:** the privation theory and the "evils are fewer than goods" move prefigure [[Contradictions/Schopenhauer's Pessimism vs. Leibnizian Optimism|Leibniz's optimism]] (and the pessimist reply); the intellectualist resolution of Job anticipates treating the problem of evil as partly a problem of *misframed expectation*.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- If individual providence tracks intellect, are the unlearned simply abandoned to chance? Maimonides largely accepts this hard consequence.
- Does "God's knowledge is wholly homonymous with ours" actually dissolve the freedom/foreknowledge problem, or merely declare it insoluble? (Cf. al-Ghazali's opposite confidence that God knows every particular.)
