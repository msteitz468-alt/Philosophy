# The Four Precepts of the Method (Descartes)

> The four rules to which Descartes reduced the whole apparatus of logic, ancient analysis, and modern algebra (Part II of the *Discourse*) — modeled on the "long chains of simple and easy reasonings" of the geometers, and adopted with the "firm and unwavering resolution never in a single instance to fail in observing them."

## The Four Rules

1. **Evidence (the rule of clarity):** "never to accept anything for true which I did not clearly know to be such" — to avoid *precipitancy* (rushing to judgment) and *prejudice* (inherited opinion), and to include in one's judgment only what is presented "so clearly and distinctly as to exclude all ground of doubt." This is the seed of the criterion of [[Concepts/Clear and Distinct Perception (Descartes)]].
2. **Analysis (division):** "to divide each of the difficulties under examination into as many parts as possible, and as might be necessary for its adequate solution."
3. **Order (synthesis):** "to conduct my thoughts in such order that, by commencing with objects the simplest and easiest to know, I might ascend by little and little... to the knowledge of the more complex" — even assigning an order to objects that have no natural sequence.
4. **Enumeration (review):** "in every case to make enumerations so complete, and reviews so general, that I might be assured that nothing was omitted."

## Context and Rationale

Descartes rejects the three arts he had studied — logic, geometrical analysis, and algebra — as flawed: syllogistic logic serves only to *communicate* what is already known (or, "as the art of Lully," to speak without judgment), not to discover; ancient analysis fatigues the imagination with figures; modern algebra is a confused subjection to rules and formulas. "As a multitude of laws often only hampers justice, so that a state is best governed when, with few laws, these are rigidly administered," he replaces the many precepts of logic with these four, rigidly kept.

The model is mathematics: the geometers' habit of reaching difficult conclusions by long chains of certain reasonings led Descartes to imagine that *all* objects of human knowledge are "mutually connected in the same way," so that nothing is so remote or hidden that it cannot be reached, "provided only we abstain from accepting the false for the true, and always preserve... the order necessary for the deduction of one truth from another." He first applied the method to a general "mathesis" of proportions (the germ of analytic geometry), then meant to extend it to the other sciences — but recognized these depend on uncertain principles borrowed from philosophy, so he judged he must first secure philosophy's foundations (Part IV).

## Relation to the Rest of Descartes

- The four rules are the *method*; the [[Concepts/Method of Hyperbolic Doubt (Descartes)]] is the first rule pushed to its extreme (accept *nothing* that admits any doubt), and the [[Concepts/Cogito (Descartes)]] is what survives that push — the first "simplest object" from which the order of rule 3 proceeds.
- Rule 1's "clear and distinct" becomes, once God's veracity secures it, the full [[Concepts/Clear and Distinct Perception (Descartes)]] criterion of truth.
- The *Discourse* gives the rules in autobiographical brief; the fuller technical version is the earlier, unpublished *Rules for the Direction of the Mind*.

## How This Compares to Prior Thinkers

- **Vs. Aristotelian logic:** Descartes explicitly demotes the syllogism from an instrument of discovery to a mere tool of exposition — a sharp break with the scholastic logic organon that dominated his Jesuit education. Contrast [[Thinkers/Aristotle]].
- **Vs. Plato's method of division:** Rule 2 (division) echoes the diairesis of [[Concepts/Method of Division and Definition of Sophist (Plato Sophist)]], but Descartes's aim is analytic decomposition of a *problem* into solvable parts, not the classificatory carving of a *kind* at its joints.
- **Vs. Spinoza's geometrical method:** Descartes's ideal of deduction from the simplest to the complex is realized systematically by [[Concepts/Geometrical Method - Rationalism (Spinoza)]], who casts the whole *Ethics* *more geometrico* (definitions → axioms → propositions). Descartes describes the ideal; Spinoza formalizes it.
- **Vs. Bacon / the emerging empirical method:** the four rules are rationalist and a priori (order and deduction), though Part VI of the *Discourse* insists on the growing necessity of *experiment* as knowledge advances — a concession toward the experimental turn later central to the philosophy-of-science leg.

## Relevance to Vault

The four precepts are almost a charter for disciplined ingest work: **rule 1** = admit no claim to a page that is not clearly grounded in the source (no invention; flag the uncertain); **rule 2** = decompose a large source into logical sections (the Large-Volume Protocol); **rule 3** = build from simplest pages (concepts, definitions) up to syntheses and contradictions in order; **rule 4** = the lint pass and complete enumeration (nothing orphaned, every link resolved). The insistence on *few* rules "rigidly administered" mirrors the schema's preference for a lean, strictly followed set of operations over sprawling ad-hoc process.
