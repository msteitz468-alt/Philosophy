# Principle of Gradation (Lovejoy)

> The conception that all beings (or at least all natural kinds) can and should be arranged in a single, unilinear, hierarchical order (*scala naturae*, "ladder of nature" or "Chain of Being") according to their degree of "perfection," "excellence," or realization of potentiality — from the "meagerest kind of existents, which barely escape non-existence," through every possible grade, up to the *ens perfectissimum* (or the highest possible creature), with each link differing from the ones immediately above and below by the "least possible" degree of difference, and each higher order possessing all the powers/attributes of those below it plus an additional differentiating one. The third of the three unit-ideas whose fusion produces the Great Chain of Being. Primarily suggested by Aristotle and elaborated in Neoplatonism and after.

## Summary

From Lovejoy, Ch. II:

Plato has occasional intimations of hierarchical ranking among Ideas/essences, but it remains a "vague tendency, not a definitely formulated doctrine." It is Aristotle who "chiefly suggested to naturalists and philosophers of later times the idea of arranging (at least) all animals in a single graded *scala naturae* according to their degree of 'perfection.'"

Criteria Aristotle offered:
- Degree of development of offspring at birth (eleven general grades, man at top, zoophytes at bottom).
- Hierarchy of "powers of soul": nutritive (plants), sensitive (animals), rational (man, "and possibly another kind superior"); each higher possesses all of lower + one more.

More abstractly, everything except God has "privation" — unrealized potentialities in its essence; superior levels it is constitutionally incapable of attaining. "All individual things may be graded according to the degree to which they are infected with [mere] potentiality."

Combined with plenitude (all possibles realized) and continuity (no gaps, infinitesimal differences), this yields the full Chain conception: "an immense, or — by the strict but seldom rigorously applied logic of the principle of continuity — of an infinite, number of links ranging in hierarchical order... every one of them differing from that immediately above and that immediately below it by the 'least possible' degree of difference."

The metaphor (chain, series of mirrors reflecting the One downward) recurs for centuries (Macrobius as key transmitter to medievals). Pope's famous lines summarize it: "Vast chain of being! which from God began, / Natures aethereal, human, angel, man, / Beast, bird, fish, insect, what no eye can see, / No glass can reach; from Infinite to thee..."

## Key Claims / How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Aristotle**: Originator of the explicit biological/psychological *scala*; dual influence (sharp classification vs. continuity/gradation); the "privation"/potentiality framework for ontological grading.
- **Neoplatonism (Plotinus, Macrobius)**: The hierarchical emanation (One → Intellect → Soul → matter) as the metaphysical Chain; each level an image/reflection of the prior, with progressive privation/loss of unity.
- **Medieval/Christian**: The *scala* justifies the order of creation (angels, man, animals, plants, minerals); man as microcosm or middle link; natural law and the beatific vision as the highest natural/supernatural end within the hierarchy.
- **17c–18c (Leibniz, Pope, etc.)**: The Chain as the structure of the best/fullest world; moral and aesthetic implications (each being has its due place and degree; "rise in due degree").
- **18c biology**: The *scala* as program for research — fill every link, discover missing intermediates (temporalized into evolutionary series).
- **Lovejoy**: The unilinear graded hierarchy is the "result" of fusing the other two principles with Aristotelian suggestions. It is not strictly in Plato; Aristotle provides the decisive impetus. When temporalized, the static ladder becomes an ascending process (but the assumption of a single graded series persists in much pre-Darwinian and Romantic thought).

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- With Aristotle's own pluralism: he recognized multiple independent systems of classification (habitat, structure, etc.) with no necessary correlation; a creature "superior" in one respect may be "inferior" in another. The single unilinear *scala* is a later elaboration.
- With plenitude + continuity: see those pages and [[Concepts/Great Chain of Being (Lovejoy)]]. The demand for a single continuous graded series conflicts with the diversity of kinds (new *qualia* break the scale) and with the actual branching, non-unilinear character of biological and conceptual diversity.
- With modern biology/evolution: Darwinian tree (branching, not ladder); multiple independent lineages; no single scale of "perfection" (different adaptations suit different niches); "lower" forms persist and thrive alongside "higher."
- With emergence and tangled structures: non-unilinear hierarchies (Hofstadter's "tangled hierarchies" where levels loop back on themselves; Minsky's society of agents with no single top-down scale). Cross to [[Concepts/Strange Loops and Tangled Hierarchies (Hofstadter)]].
- Teleology vs. mechanism/contingency: the graded scale implies a natural telos or direction of "perfection"; later thought (including vault Phil of Science and causal models) treats order as imposed, discovered, or evolved rather than metaphysically given.
