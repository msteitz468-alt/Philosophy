# Evidence and Truth (Husserl)
> Truth is the fulfillment of intention by intuition; evidence comes in three grades — certainty, adequacy, and apodicticity — and extends beyond sense-perception to eidetic and categorial intuition.

## Summary

Husserl's epistemology is "applied phenomenology": it uses the phenomenological analysis of intentionality and intuition to develop a theory of knowledge that goes beyond both rationalism (pure logical construction) and empiricism (only sensory experience as evidence). Husserl calls phenomenology the "genuine positivism" — grounding all sciences in what is "originarily grasped," admitting all kinds of intuition as "equally valuable sources for the justification of knowledge" (*Ideas I*, §20).

## Truth as Intentional Fulfillment

A **signitive** (or "empty") intention is an act directed toward an object without the object being directly present — a belief that there is a coffee cup in the kitchen, entertained while sitting elsewhere.

An **intuitive** (or "filled") intention is one in which the object is present "itself in its bodily selfhood" — perceiving the cup directly.

**Truth as fulfillment**: A judgment is true when the intention behind it is "fulfilled" — when an intuitive experience presents the very state of affairs the judgment intends. Fulfillment is the phenomenological correlate of verification. A judgment is false when there is an "intentional conflict" — what was intended and what is presented do not match.

This model applies to all types of knowledge: mathematical intuition "fulfills" mathematical intentions; eidetic intuition "fulfills" essence-intentions; categorial intuition "fulfills" formal-ontological intentions.

## Three Grades of Evidence

**1. Certainty**: The subject does not doubt the object's existence or the state of affairs. Paradigm: ordinary sensory perception of a table. Certainty does not imply absence of possible error — perception can deceive.

**2. Adequacy**: All aspects of the intended object are fully and evidently given — nothing remains hidden, no further investigation could add to the evidence. An *ideal limit* rarely (if ever) achieved in practice. Perception of a physical thing is *inadequate* — the thing always has hidden sides, future behavior, microstructure beyond current appearances.

**3. Apodicticity** (*Cartesian Meditations*, §6): Evidence in which "the non-being of the [intended state of affairs] is absolutely unimaginable" in the face of the experience. Apodictic evidence is stronger than certainty: not only do I not doubt it, but doubting it is inconceivable. Example: phenomenological reflection on one's own current experience ("I am now thinking") — the existence of this very act cannot be doubted while it is occurring.

**Key asymmetry**: Perception is *certain* but neither *adequate* nor *apodictic*. Phenomenological self-reflection is *apodictic* but not *adequate* (my past stream of consciousness is not fully evidently given). Mathematical intuitions are apodictic for simple cases; for complex proofs, adequacy is the limiting ideal.

## Types of Intuition as Epistemic Sources

Husserl widens the empiricist account of knowledge by admitting multiple forms of intuition:

| Type | What it gives | Domain |
|---|---|---|
| **Sensory perception** | Individual physical objects | Nature |
| **Memory / imagination** | Past and possible objects (reproductive intuition) | Nature, Culture |
| **Empathy (Einfühlung)** | Others' experiences (deferred, transferred evidence) | Culture, Consciousness |
| **Eidetic intuition** | Material essences (via variation) | All material regions |
| **Categorial intuition** | Formal structures: States of Affairs, Numbers, Individuals | Formal ontology |
| **Phenomenological reflection** | One's own current experience | Consciousness |

**Categorial intuition** (Husserl's innovation in *Logical Investigations VI*): We do not merely *think* logical and mathematical structures — we intuit them. Grasping that "this rose is red" involves not only sensory intuition of the rose and its redness but a categorial intuition of the *state of affairs* (rose-being-red). This is what grounds a priori knowledge of formal ontology.

## Against Rationalism, Empiricism, and Kantianism

- **Against rationalism (Descartes, Leibniz)**: Pure rational construction is insufficient; knowledge requires intuitive fulfillment, not merely logical derivation from clear and distinct ideas.
- **Against empiricism (Hume, Mill)**: Restricting evidence to sensory experience is too narrow — eidetic and categorial intuitions are genuine epistemic sources, giving us a priori knowledge of essences and formal structures.
- **Against Kant**: Kant's unknowable *Ding an sich* (thing-in-itself) is rejected. When a perception is intuitively fulfilled, it reaches the thing *itself*, not merely a phenomenal appearance. The proper theory of intentionality was unavailable to Kant.

## The Life-World as Epistemic Ground

All knowledge presupposes the **Lebenswelt** as its unthematised background. Knowledge is not only a matter of individual acts of perception and judgment but of culturally transmitted, "sedimented" beliefs inherited from prior generations. Mathematical physics presupposes the life-world experience of space, time, and causality from which it abstracts. Forgetting this ground is the source of the "crisis" in modern science. See [[Concepts/Intersubjectivity and the Life-World (Husserl)]].

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Descartes]]**: Husserl's apodicticity echoes Cartesian indubitability but differs: Husserl's apodictic evidence is phenomenologically *local* (this experience, now) and does not extend to a priori truths about the external world via the cogito. Husserl rejects Descartes' project of rebuilding all knowledge on a solitary apodictic foundation.
- **[[Thinkers/David Hume]]**: Husserl credits Hume with being "on the right track" on abstraction (recognizing resemblance-based generalization) but charges him with lacking the distinctions needed to avoid psychologism: without separating the ideal species *Red*, the moment of redness in this object, the object, and the concept of red, Hume's "ideas" collapsed everything into mental imagery.
- **[[Thinkers/W.V.O. Quine]]**: Quine's naturalized epistemology and rejection of the a priori directly conflict with Husserl's eidetic intuition and categorial intuition. For Quine, all knowledge is in principle empirically revisable; for Husserl, material and formal essences yield genuine a priori necessary truths. See [[Concepts/Naturalized Epistemology and the Web of Belief (Quine)]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Free variation and natural-kind essences: Husserl sometimes implies imagination alone suffices to grasp material essences (Gold, Tree). In practice, empirical investigation is required. The scope of purely imaginative eidetic variation remains contested.
- > [!warning] Apodicticity vs. fallibilism: Husserl's apodictic evidence appears to conflict with the fallibilist epistemology that has dominated post-Gettier analytic philosophy. Smith argues the conflict is less severe than it looks — apodictic evidence is local and narrow — but the tension is real.
