# The Two Tests and the Animal-Machine (Descartes)

> In Part V of the *Discourse*, Descartes argues that the body — human or animal — is a machine "made by the hands of God," and that a machine could imitate an animal so perfectly as to be indistinguishable from it. But no machine (and no beast) could pass **two tests** that reveal the presence of a rational soul: the test of *language* and the test of *universal reason*. This is the *bête-machine* (beast-machine) doctrine and the direct historical ancestor of the [[Concepts/Imitation Game (Turing)|Turing Test]].

## The Argument

Having given a mechanistic account of the body (the heart as a heat-engine, the circulation of the blood, the nerves and "animal spirits" moving the muscles), Descartes says a body is a self-moving automaton "incomparably better arranged" than any human-made machine. He then poses a thought experiment: **if there were machines resembling an ape or other irrational animal, we could have no means of telling them apart** from real animals. But **machines resembling us and imitating our actions** would still fail two "most certain tests":

1. **The language test.** A machine could be built to emit words, even words prompted by what touches it — but it could never "arrange them variously so as appositely to reply to what is said in its presence, as men of the lowest grade of intellect can do." Appropriate, open-ended, meaningful verbal response to *any* subject is beyond mechanism.

2. **The universal-reason test.** Even a machine that did some things better than us "would, without doubt, fail in certain others" — because its organs "need a particular arrangement for each particular action," whereas "reason is an universal instrument that is alike available on every occasion." No finite disposition of organs can cover the open-ended range of situations that reason handles.

By these same two tests, **beasts differ from men**: even the most stupid human child can combine words into meaningful speech; no animal can (parrots utter words but do not *understand* — and it is not a defect of organs, since deaf-mutes invent signs). Descartes concludes that brutes "have none [no reason] at all": their behavior is entirely the mechanical operation of their organs (a clock "can number the hours... more exactly than we"). Hence the *bête-machine* thesis — animals are pure automata, without minds. The rational soul, by contrast, "could by no means be educed from the power of matter" and must be specially created and united to the body — not merely "lodged in the human body... like a pilot in a ship," but joined closely enough to constitute a real man.

## Ancestor of the Turing Test

Descartes's language test is the recognizable seed of Alan Turing's operational criterion three centuries later. Both take **linguistic behavior under open interrogation** as the decisive mark distinguishing a genuine thinker from a mere mechanism, precisely because appropriate conversation cannot be pre-programmed for every contingency. The pivotal difference:

- **Descartes** treats the test as proving a *metaphysical* conclusion — no machine will *ever* pass it, therefore rational soul is a non-mechanical substance really distinct from body. The test's assumed failure grounds dualism.
- **Turing** inverts the modality: he asks not what a machine *must* fail but what a machine *might succeed* at, and treats passing the [[Concepts/Imitation Game (Turing)]] as sufficient warrant for attributing thought — dissolving the demand for a hidden non-mechanical substance behind the behavior.

So Turing accepts Descartes's *criterion* (open-ended language use) while rejecting his *verdict* (that only an immaterial soul could meet it). This sharpens the standing [[Thinkers/Descartes]] ↔ [[Thinkers/Alan Turing]] contradiction over whether thought requires a *res cogitans* (see [[Concepts/Res Cogitans and Res Extensa (Descartes)]]). Descartes's universal-reason test — that mechanism cannot match reason's open, all-purpose applicability — also anticipates modern debates over the generality of intelligence (narrow AI vs. general reason).

## How This Compares to Prior Thinkers

- **Vs. Aristotle:** Aristotle grants animals a sensitive soul and shares reason (logos) with the human rational soul as one nested hierarchy of life; Descartes severs the continuity absolutely — animals have *no* soul in the sense of mind, only mechanism, and the rational soul is a distinct creation. Contrast the graded psuchē of [[Thinkers/Aristotle]].
- **Vs. the scholastic "nothing in the intellect not first in the senses":** the universal-reason test reasserts that reason is not reducible to any arrangement of sensory/bodily organs.
- **Vs. later mechanism (La Mettrie, *Man a Machine*):** La Mettrie extends the animal-machine to *man*, denying the exception Descartes reserved for the rational soul — the trajectory that runs toward computationalism and Turing.

## Relevance to Vault

This page is a hinge between the vault's early-modern and AI/Cognitive-Science legs. The two tests give the vault a canonical *pre-history* of the [[Concepts/Imitation Game (Turing)]]: the very criterion (open-ended, contextually apt language use) that Turing operationalized as a behavioral pass/fail was first isolated by Descartes as a supposed proof of the soul's immateriality. For evaluating modern LLMs, the pairing is directly useful — Descartes's two failure-modes (canned responses that cannot reply appositely across topics; brittle competence that "needs a particular arrangement for each particular action") name exactly the classic critiques of narrow or non-general systems, while the systems' actual conversational range is what makes the Cartesian verdict newly contestable.
