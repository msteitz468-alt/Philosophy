# Becoming (Deleuze)
> Becoming is the name Deleuze gives to pure transformation — movement that is always double, always in two directions at once, eluding every present moment.

## Summary

For Deleuze (drawing primarily on the Stoics, Bergson, and Nietzsche), becoming is irreducible to being — it is not a deficient form of presence but its own positive mode. The essential property of becoming is its **two-directional simultaneity**: to grow is always to grow *and* to shrink relative to what one is becoming. Becoming perpetually eludes the present; it belongs to the order of the **Aeon** (unlimited time of the event) rather than **Chronos** (measured, present-anchored time).

This ontology displaces Plato's deeper dualism. Plato's real opposition, Deleuze argues, is not between Ideas and matter, but between copies (which participate in the Idea, stabilized in the present) and **simulacra** (which are condemned because they embody pure becoming, escaping the Idea's authority). Overturning Platonism means affirming the simulacrum.

Becoming also dissolves personal identity. If verbs of becoming overtake substantives — if Alice "grows" and "shrinks" before she "is Alice" — then the "I" loses its anchor in a stable present. The only subjects of becoming are indefinite: "a child," "a woman," "an animal."

## Key Claims

- **The paradox of becoming:** "It pertains to the essence of becoming to move and to pull in both directions at once: Alice does not grow without shrinking, and vice versa."
- **Paradox vs. good sense:** Good sense picks one direction (the more probable, the future-to-past); common sense assigns fixed identities. Paradox affirms both directions simultaneously and suspends fixed identity.
- **Becoming and simulacra:** Plato condemns the simulacrum because it embodies pure becoming (Alice gets bigger and smaller simultaneously) without resembling the Idea. Deleuze affirms the simulacrum as the real locus of becoming.
- **Aeon vs. Chronos:** Becoming belongs to Aeon (the unlimited, the event, what has just happened and what is about to happen) rather than to Chronos (the present, living bodies, measured time).

## How Different Thinkers Use Becoming

- **[[Thinkers/Plato]]:** Becoming is inferior to Being; the world of becoming is the world of appearances that only approximately imitate Forms. Deleuze inverts this: becoming is primary, being is a stabilized effect.
- **[[Thinkers/Aristotle]]:** Becoming (kinesis) is change between determinate states of substance. Deleuze's becoming is pre-individual and resists determination by substances or endpoints.
- **[[Thinkers/Hegel]]:** Becoming in Hegel is the unity of Being and Nothing, resolved by Aufhebung into determinate being. Deleuze explicitly rejects this: becoming is not synthesized but sustained in its paradoxical doubleness.
- **[[Thinkers/Nietzsche]]:** The eternal return is the being *of* becoming — becoming as such returns, not the identical. Deleuze's becoming is continuous with Nietzsche's, but radicalized via the Stoics and event-ontology.
- **[[Thinkers/Heidegger]]:** Dasein has a structure of being-toward-death that temporalizes it, but Heidegger's ontology still centers a "who" (Dasein) as the locus of temporality. Deleuze's becoming has no such subject — only haecceities (speeds, affects, relations). See [[Concepts/Haecceity and Multiplicity (Deleuze)]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] "Becoming" appears to require temporal passage, yet Deleuze insists becoming eludes the present — it is always *just* past or *about* to happen. This makes becoming almost impossible to think within standard temporal logic, which is partly Deleuze's point, but also a source of interpretive difficulty.
