# Pythagoras in Medieval and Renaissance Thought
> The long Latin afterlife of Pythagoras — transmitted not as a living school but as an *iconized* authority, the patron of number, the quadrivium, and the harmony of the spheres, culminating in Ficino's recreation of "a Pythagoras in his own image."

## Late Antiquity and the Middle Ages (Hicks)
On Andrew Hicks' chapter in [[Sources/A History of Pythagoreanism - Carl A. Huffman (ed.) (2014)]], medieval "Pythagoreanism" is a *reification* of the late-ancient **"mathematical Pythagoreanism" of Nicomachus** (via Iamblichus and Proclus — see [[Schools/Neopythagoreanism]]); it lived inside an *imagined* history of philosophy, not contemporaneous debate. It reached the Latin West chiefly through three transmitters — **Calcidius, Macrobius, and Boethius** — a "Platonizing Pythagoreanism" (Apuleius: "*Plato pythagorissat in plurimis*," which the 12th century turned into "Plato was a Pythagorean").

- **Boethius** is decisive: translating Nicomachus' *Arithmetic*, he transmitted the Pythagorean **numerical basis of reality** and named the fourfold ***quadrivium*** (arithmetic, music, geometry, astronomy) — "the fourfold road... for those whom a more excellent soul leads away from the senses." His *De institutione musica* defined music theory for a millennium, dividing it into ***musica mundana / humana / instrumentalis*** and balancing *sensus* against *ratio* in the Pythagorean manner (see [[Concepts/Pythagorean Harmonics]]).
- **Veiled discourse**: Pythagorean authority was tied to *symbolic* concealment (Proclus on the *screens* hiding secret doctrine; Macrobius' *narratio fabulosa*; the 12th-century doctrine of *integumenta / involucra*).
- **Christian selectivity**: thinkers accepted the ethics and number-metaphysics (Wisdom 11:21, God ordered all "in measure, number, and weight") but rejected **metempsychosis** (John of Salisbury: "even Plato must be refuted").
- The [[Concepts/Harmony of the Spheres]] flowed from the World-Soul's numerical structure (Calcidius), until Aristotle's *De Caelo* critique reduced it to a "*Pythagoricum figmentum*" (Grocheio) — dormant until revived by Ficino. "Soul is harmony" (*anima est harmonia*) was transmitted as Pythagorean chiefly via Macrobius.

## The Early Renaissance (Allen)
On Michael J. B. Allen's chapter, Renaissance thinkers "recreated a Pythagoras in their own image," inflating him into a near-saint anticipating Christianity (Cudworth: "the most eminent of all the ancient philosophers"; Ambrose had even called him a Jew). Pythagoras was slotted into the ***prisca theologia*** — the chain of *prisci theologi*: Zoroaster → Hermes Trismegistus → Orpheus → Aglaophamus → Pythagoras → Plato.
- **Marsilio Ficino** is "the father of Quattrocento Pythagoreanism" (~50 references to Pythagoras in his *Platonic Theology*; he treated Plato and Socrates as Pythagoreans). His recovery of Iamblichus' *On the Pythagorean Life* reshaped his ideas on **musical therapy** and "attuning," modeling his own Orphic lyre recitals — fusing the Orphic and Pythagorean revivals, with Pythagoras the safer model-sage (see [[Concepts/Pythagoreanism and Orphism]]).
- He wrestled with **metempsychosis** (newly available via Traversari's translation of Diogenes Laertius — see [[Concepts/The Three Ancient Lives of Pythagoras]]), distinguishing *metensomatosis* from animal *metempsychosis* to keep souls rational.
- **Arithmology** flourished: number symbolism, the *tetractys* as "self-moving number," the Timaean *lambda*; Ficino's *On the Fatal Number* solved the number of *Republic* VIII as **1728** (see [[Concepts/Number as the Essence of Reality (Pythagoras)]]).
- The 16th-century "apotheosis of Pythagoras" (Reuchlin drawing on "the boundless sea of Kabbalah"; Paracelsus, Postel, **Kepler**) — yet Pythagoras never eclipsed Plato: it is **Neoplatonism, not Neopythagoreanism, that dominates** the Renaissance.

## Significance for the Vault
This is the channel by which the Pythagorean "reality as number" program survived to seed early modern science (Kepler's planetary harmonics) — the deep background to the mathematization of nature, even as the *historical* Pythagoras receded behind his constructed image.
