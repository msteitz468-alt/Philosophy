# Reasons for the Commandments (Maimonides)

> Maimonides' rationalist account of the Law (*Guide* III.25–54, *ta'amei ha-mitzvot*): **no precept is arbitrary** — every commandment serves the welfare of the soul (true beliefs) or the welfare of the body (a sound social order). Many ritual laws, especially sacrifice, are read as a historically-situated, *pedagogical* concession designed to wean Israel from **Sabean** star-worship. The Law's whole aim culminates in **intellectual perfection** — knowledge of God — issuing in *imitatio Dei*.

## The Law Is Rational, Not Arbitrary

"There is a cause for every commandment: every positive or negative precept serves a useful object." General reasons are knowable; only certain particulars (why a lamb and not a ram, why the number seven) are left as "tests of obedience." The Law's twofold object:
- **Welfare of the soul** — correct opinions and true beliefs (first in *rank*).
- **Welfare of the body** — orderly social and political life (first in *time*, since material needs must be met before the intellect can be cultivated).

"The sole object of the Law is to benefit us... every one of the six hundred and thirteen precepts serves to inculcate some truth, to remove some erroneous opinion, to establish proper relations in society, to diminish evil, or to train in good manners." He sorts the precepts into fourteen classes to display this rationale systematically.

## The Sabean / Anti-Idolatry Argument

Maimonides' most striking historical thesis: many ceremonial laws were given to **counter idolatry gradually**. Israel was steeped in the worship of the **Sabeans** (star-worshippers); rather than abolish all accustomed forms of cult at a stroke — which "the nature of man... would not allow" — God *redirected* them. Temple, altar, sacrifice, incense, and priesthood were the very modes by which the surrounding nations had worshipped created things; God "transferred to His service that which had formerly served as a worship of created beings... By this Divine plan the traces of idolatry were blotted out." The Law thus meets human beings where they historically stood — a developmental, accommodationist theory of ritual that treats much of the cult as means rather than end.

## Human Perfection and the Palace

The Law's deepest purpose is the **highest of four perfections** (III.54):
1. Property — external, transient, lowest.
2. Bodily constitution — shared with animals.
3. Moral perfection — high, but only a *preparation*, valuable in relation to others.
4. **Intellectual perfection** — true knowledge of God and metaphysical truth — "the only perfection exclusively one's own," conferring immortality; "on its account he is called man."

The **palace parable** (III.51) ranks people by nearness to the king: those abroad have no religion; the ignorant masses seek the palace but never see it; jurists who study only practical law circle the gate; students of physics reach the antechamber; only those with demonstrative knowledge of God enter the inner court "with the king." Progress maps onto study — logic and mathematics circle for the gate, physics enters the hall, **metaphysics** enters the royal chamber.

The closing chapter joins this intellectualism to ethics: true wisdom is to know God's "ways" — His [[Concepts/Negative Theology and Divine Attributes (Maimonides)|attributes of action]], **hesed** (loving-kindness), **mishpat** (judgment), **zedakah** (righteousness) — and then to *practice* them "in the earth," imitating God. Knowledge culminates in action: "having acquired this knowledge he will then be determined always to seek loving-kindness, judgment, and righteousness, and thus to imitate the ways of God."

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]:** Aquinas' treatment of the Old Law (its moral, ceremonial, and judicial precepts having rational and pedagogical purposes) is deeply indebted to Maimonides; both make law serve human flourishing and both rank the contemplative knowledge of God as the highest end ([[Concepts/Beatific Vision - Last End (Aquinas)]]) — though Aquinas elevates it to supernatural beatitude by grace.
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Aristotle]] / [[Concepts/Eudaimonia]]:** the four-perfections scheme is an Aristotelian ladder crowned by *theoria*; Maimonides' "true perfection" is Aristotle's contemplative life rethought as knowledge and imitation of God.
- **vs. [[Sources/Ethics - Spinoza (Cambridge, Kisner ed. 2018)|Spinoza]]:** Spinoza inherits the intellectual-love-of-God telos but strips the ceremonial law of binding force, historicizing the whole Mosaic legislation as politically expedient for the ancient Hebrew state — radicalizing Maimonides' own developmental reading of the cult.
- **vs. modern critique:** the Sabean theory is an early instance of *functional/historical* explanation of religious ritual (law as adaptive pedagogy), a forerunner of later anthropology of religion.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- If the sacrificial cult was only a concession to wean off idolatry, what is its standing once idolatry is dead? (A tension later exploited by reformers and by Spinoza.)
- Does ranking intellectual perfection above moral perfection risk subordinating ethics to metaphysics — or does the *imitatio Dei* close at III.54 reunite them?
