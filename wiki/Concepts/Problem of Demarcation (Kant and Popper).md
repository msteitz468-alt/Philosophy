# Problem of Demarcation (Kant and Popper)

> The problem of finding a criterion that distinguishes the empirical sciences from mathematics and logic on the one hand and from "metaphysical" systems on the other. Following Kant, Popper calls this "Kant's problem" and regards it as more fundamental than Hume's problem of induction. Popper's proposed solution is the criterion of falsifiability.

## Kant's Framing (as Presented by Popper)

- Hume attempted a solution (empiricist critique of causation and induction).
- With Kant the problem became central to the theory of knowledge.
- For empiricists and positivists, the hope was that an "inductive logic" or "verifiability" criterion would simultaneously solve both the problem of induction and the problem of demarcation (only what is "derived from experience" or "reducible to atomic observation statements" counts as legitimate science).

## Popper's Diagnosis of Why Inductivist Solutions Fail

- The older positivists wanted concepts "derived from experience."
- Modern positivists shifted to statements reducible to "protocol-sentences" or "atomic propositions."
- In both cases the implied criterion of demarcation is identical with the demand for an inductive logic.
- Because Popper rejects inductive logic, he must also reject these demarcation attempts. The problem of demarcation thereby gains in urgency.

- Positivists often interpreted the problem naturalistically ("discover" a pre-existing difference in the nature of things) and wanted to *annihilate* metaphysics as "nonsensical." Popper treats the criterion as a *proposal for an agreement or convention* whose suitability can be discussed rationally among parties who share the purpose of clarifying the empirical character of science.

## Popper's Proposal

See [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)]] for the detailed criterion (falsifiability, not verifiability; asymmetry of universal and singular statements; methodological rules that maximize exposure to criticism).

Key clarifications in the context of demarcation:
- The criterion allows theoretical systems (universal laws) into science while excluding systems that are compatible with every possible observation (unfalsifiable).
- It draws the line *inside* meaningful language: both falsifiable and non-falsifiable statements can be meaningful; only the former are empirical/scientific.
- Metaphysical ideas can be (and historically have been) of the greatest importance for the *advance* of science as heuristics and research programmes. They simply do not count as scientific until they are formulated in a falsifiable manner.

## Additions from *Conjectures and Refutations*

*C&R* sharpens the demarcation theme in two ways:

- **Demarcation is not a criterion of meaning (vs. Carnap)**: In "The Demarcation Between Science and Metaphysics" (ch. 11) Popper traces the failure of Carnap's successive **verificationist** theories of meaninglessness. Carnap repeatedly tried to use a criterion of *meaning* (verifiability, then translatability into the language of science) to *eliminate metaphysics as nonsensical* — and each version either readmitted metaphysics or excluded genuine scientific laws. Popper's point: falsifiability demarcates the *empirical/scientific* from the *non-empirical*, but metaphysics can be perfectly **meaningful** (and often heuristically vital). The line is drawn *inside* meaningful discourse, not around it.

- **A theory can be false *and* irrefutable** (ch. 8, "On the Status of Science and of Metaphysics"): irrefutability entails neither truth nor meaninglessness. Two incompatible theories (determinism and indeterminism) can both be irrefutable, so irrefutability cannot imply truth. **Pure (unrestricted) existential statements** — "there exists a Latin formula that cures all diseases," "there exists the philosopher's stone" — are empirically irrefutable (we can never search the whole universe) yet we are reasonable in holding them *false*. So the rationalist may, without inconsistency, call metaphysical theories both irrefutable and false. This secures the asymmetry behind the criterion: it is *refutability*, not provability or "confirmability," that marks the empirical.

## Relation to Other Vault Content

- **Hume's problem of induction**: Explicitly treated as the less fundamental of the two great problems. Popper's demarcation solution also dissolves the apparent conflict between empiricism and Hume's critique (see [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]] update).

- **Kant**: Popper adopts Kant's diagnosis that demarcation is central, while rejecting Kant's own solution (synthetic a priori). The critical method (falsifiability + rules of procedure) replaces the a priori guarantee.

- **Russell**: Russell's characterization of philosophy as the "No Man's Land between theology and science" (speculation where definite knowledge is unavailable) is congenial; Popper supplies a sharper tool for deciding when speculation has become (or can become) scientific. Russell's logical analysis is a shared commitment; Popper adds the emphasis on severe testing and growth via criticism.

- **Epicurus / ancient empiricism**: The demarcation between clear sensation (criterion) and unverified opinion prefigures the modern problem. Epicurus allows multiple explanations for the obscure provided they are not contradicted by sensation — a weak form of openness to "falsification."

- **Pragmatism and verificationism**: Both James ("corroborate and verify") and the positivists tie legitimacy too closely to positive confirmation or reducibility. Popper's line is drawn by risk of refutation.

- **Quine and the deeper critique of verificationism**: Quine's "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" (1951) attacks the background assumptions that made verificationist demarcation (Carnap) tempting in the first place. The analytic/synthetic distinction and reductionism (each statement having its own confirming sensory events) were the tools used to draw sharp lines between meaningful science and metaphysics. Quine shows both tools fail internally (circular synonymy for the first; failed translation programs like Carnap's *Aufbau* for the second), yielding confirmation holism instead: statements face experience only as a corporate body (the web of belief). Falsifiability remains a useful *proposal* for maximizing critical exposure (Popper), but holism and underdetermination mean that any apparent refutation can be absorbed by adjustments elsewhere in the web. Quine thus deepens the problem of demarcation while blurring the science/metaphysics boundary further (physical objects and gods are epistemologically on a par as posits; ontology is continuous with science).
