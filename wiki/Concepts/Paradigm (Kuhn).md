# Paradigm (Kuhn)

> Kuhn's central term: a **paradigm** is a concrete scientific achievement that for a time supplies a community of practitioners with its model problems, methods, standards, and shared assumptions — "universally recognized scientific achievements that for a time provide model problems and solutions to a community of practitioners" (*Structure*). It is what makes **normal science** possible; a change of paradigm is a **scientific revolution**.

## The Idea

A paradigm, in Kuhn's primary sense, is not a theory or a rulebook but an **exemplar** — a striking concrete solution (Newton's *Principia*, Ptolemy's *Almagest*, Lavoisier's chemistry, Franklin's electricity) that becomes the shared model for a field. Two features make an achievement a paradigm (*Structure* p. 11):

1. **"sufficiently unprecedented to attract an enduring group of adherents"** away from competing modes of activity, and
2. **open-ended** — leaving "all sorts of problems for the redefined group of practitioners to resolve."

The paradigm thereby constitutes a **scientific community**: paradigm and community "enter the book hand in hand." It carries not just laws and theories but "applications, experiment, and instrumentation" — a whole way of seeing and working. Crucially, practitioners can agree on the paradigm (the shared exemplars) **even when they cannot agree on, or fully articulate, the explicit rules** behind it — Kuhn's "**priority of paradigms**" (§V): scientists learn by grasping the exemplars and seeing new problems as "like" them, more than by absorbing stated rules. ("The student discovers a way to see his problem as like a problem he has already encountered.")

## The Two Senses (Postscript—1969)

Critics (Margaret Masterman counted **21 distinct uses** of "paradigm" in the 1962 text) pushed Kuhn to disambiguate. He admitted he had "lost control of the word" and split it:

- **Disciplinary matrix** (the *global / broad* sense): the entire shared apparatus of a community — **symbolic generalizations** (e.g. *f = ma*), **models/metaphysical commitments**, **values** (accuracy, consistency, scope, simplicity, fruitfulness), and **exemplars**.
- **Exemplar** (the *local / narrow* sense): the shared concrete problem-solutions themselves. Kuhn called this "**the central element of what I now take to be the most novel and least understood aspect of this book**," and proposed *exemplar* as the better word.

He also (in a footnote to "Second Thoughts on Paradigms") partly **recanted the "pre-paradigm" idea**: even the schools of a so-called pre-paradigm period possess paradigms of a kind; the real difference between early and mature science is not the mere presence/absence of a paradigm.

## Etymology and Lineage

From Greek **παράδειγμα (*paradeigma*)** = pattern, model, exemplar. Hacking traces it through **Aristotle's *Rhetoric*** (paradeigma = argument by a compelling example/exemplar — see [[Concepts/Enthymeme]], [[Sources/Aristotle - Rhetoric (Book I)]]), Latin *exemplum*, the grammar-textbook use ("*amo, amas, amat*" as the paradigm to imitate), and **Wittgenstein** (*Philosophical Investigations*) and the **Vienna Circle** (Schlick, Neurath) — the philosophical milieu in which Kuhn was "weaned intellectually." After Kuhn the word (and "paradigm shift") became ubiquitous, often debased to mere hype.
