# Number as the Essence of Reality (Pythagoras)

> The Pythagorean metaphysical doctrine asserting that number is the primary substance and ordering principle of the cosmos.

## Summary

The doctrine that **"all things are numbers"** marks the transition from materialist monism (seeking a physical element as the *arche*) to formalist rationalism (seeking the structural, mathematical principles of the universe). Rather than asking what the world is *made of*, Pythagoras asked how the world is *structured*. The answer lay in the mathematical ratios and proportions that govern the natural world.

---

## Core Claims and Arguments

**The Musical Discovery**:
- Pythagoras discovered that the pitch of a vibrating string depends on its length, and that consonant musical intervals correspond to simple numerical ratios:
  - **Octave** = 2:1 ratio
  - **Fifth** = 3:2 ratio
  - **Fourth** = 4:3 ratio
- This was the first historical demonstration that a qualitative sensory experience (harmony in music) is governed by quantitative mathematical laws.

**Cosmic Order and Mathematics**:
- The Pythagoreans generalized this discovery: if music is mathematical, the entire cosmos must be structured according to mathematical proportions.
- They represented numbers geometrically as points or units (e.g., 1 is a point, 2 is a line, 3 is a plane, 4 is a solid). Thus, physical space and bodies are literally constructed out of numbers.

**The Table of Opposites**:
- The Pythagoreans organized reality into ten fundamental oppositions, led by **Limit** (associated with odd numbers, light, good, male, stability) and the **Unlimited** (associated with even numbers, darkness, bad, female, motion). 
- Harmony is the imposition of **Limit** upon the **Unlimited** through mathematical proportion.

## Source-Critical Revision (Burkert 1972)
[[Sources/Lore and Science in Ancient Pythagoreanism - Walter Burkert (1972)]] sharply qualifies *whose* doctrine this is:
- **Attribution shifts from Pythagoras to [[Thinkers/Philolaus]] and Plato's Academy.** The "limiters and unlimiteds bound by harmony" cosmology is securely documented for Philolaus (late 5th c.); the *derivation of things from numbers* via the One and the indefinite dyad is a **Platonic** construction (Plato's lecture *On the Good*; Speusippus, Xenocrates), retrojected onto Pythagoras. Aristotle tellingly speaks of "the *so-called* Pythagoreans," never of Pythagoras himself.
- **Number-symbolism, not mathematics.** "All things are number" expresses *number-mysticism* (justice = 4, marriage = 5, *kairos* = 7), not demonstrative science. Burkert's verdict on whether this is mathematics: "we must answer no — in its essence mathematics is not Pythagorean but Greek." Greek *deductive* method descends from the **Eleatics** ([[Thinkers/Parmenides]], Zeno: "follow the argument"), not from Pythagoras.
- **The Tetractys is a religious oath-formula.** The sacred 1+2+3+4 = 10 ("the fount of ever-flowing nature," holding the harmonic ratios) is genuinely archaic but is an *acusma/symbolon* — see [[Concepts/Acusmata and the Two Sects (Acusmatici and Mathematici)]] — not a theorem.
- **The irrational is beside the point.** "All things are number" never asserted that all magnitudes are commensurable, so the discovery of incommensurability (which Burkert re-dates to the *late fifth century*, after Pythagoras) did not "shatter" an early Pythagorean program — arithmetic number-symbolism and deductive geometry "lie on two different planes." (This revises the older crisis story in the callout below.)

See the full debate at [[Contradictions/Pythagoras the Scientist vs the Religious Charismatic]].

## Zhmud's Counter-Reading (Zhmud 2012)
[[Sources/Pythagoras and the Early Pythagoreans - Leonid Zhmud (2012)]] splits the question in two — and lands *partly* with Burkert, *partly* against him:
- **"All things are number" was never an early Pythagorean doctrine** (with Burkert, but for a stronger reason). In the century from Pythagoras to Philolaus "there is not a trace" of the teaching that the world consists of numbers; the early Pythagoreans had ordinary Presocratic *archai* (Hippasus = fire, Hippon = moisture). The doctrine is "doubly a reconstruction": modern scholars build it from Aristotle, and Aristotle built it himself from disparate sources — he "never names a single bearer of the number-doctrine and never gives an actual example." So for Zhmud there is **nothing to retroject** — which makes Burkert's "Academy projected it backward" thesis itself misframed.
- **Number-ontology was born in the Academy**, not read back from it onto an existing Pythagorean doctrine: the perfect-decad and ideal-number metaphysics is Plato's and Speusippus' (*On Pythagorean Numbers*), and the One + indefinite dyad ascribed to "Pythagoreans" is **1st-c. BC neo-Pythagorean**. Citing Gigon: "the system of number philosophy does not in fact belong to ancient Pythagoreanism."
- **But the *mathematics and harmonics* are genuinely early** (against Burkert). The numerical ratios of the concords are a real empirical discovery (Xenocrates fr. 87), and Aristoxenus fr. 23 reports Pythagoras "likening all things to numbers" in the context of *advancing the science of numbers* — i.e. the original interest was in *logoi* (ratios), not in numbers as cosmic stuff. Greek **deductive proof** is not borrowed from the Eleatics (Zhmud rejects the Szabó thesis Burkert endorsed): indirect proof (*reductio*) is already embedded in the old Pythagorean even/odd theory (Euclid IX.30–34), so it arose *within* mathematics.
- **No "crisis of the irrational."** Like Burkert, Zhmud denies incommensurability shattered "all is number" — but he re-dates its discovery *earlier* (to [[Thinkers/Hippasus]], early–mid 5th c., not Knorr's 430s) and finds no trace of any foundational crisis (Aristoxenus is untroubled).
- **The tetractys/decad is late.** The perfect-number-10, the "Pythagorean oath," and the Sirens are Academic (Speusippus' tetrad) and Hellenistic pseudo-Pythagorica; "the number ten plays no part in harmonics, unlike nine." This *inverts* the usual "religion is older" bias.
- **Philolaus' real principles** are *limiters* and *unlimiteds* bound by *harmonia*; "to have number" (B4) means to be **knowable/structured**, an epistemological claim, not the ontological "made of numbers." See [[Thinkers/Philolaus]].

## How Aristotle Built the Doctrine (Huffman volume, 2014)
Oliver Primavesi's and Reviel Netz's chapters in [[Sources/A History of Pythagoreanism - Carl A. Huffman (ed.) (2014)]] show *how* the "number-doctrine" was manufactured:
- **It is Aristotle's historiography, not a Pythagorean quotation.** *Metaphysics* A's account of the "so-called Pythagoreans" is "a piece of historiographical reasoning" whose rationale Aristotle himself supplied — he states his premises and conclusion *twice*, each time more strongly, tracking a development from arithmology to [[Thinkers/Philolaus]]. The claim that the Pythagoreans made numbers the principles of all things "likely derives from Aristotle's *interpretation* of Philolaus" (Netz).
- ***Mathēmata* means the whole quadrivium** (arithmetic, geometry, music, astronomy — [[Thinkers/Archytas]]' canon), *not* "mathematics" narrowly; translating it "mathematics" misleads. See [[Concepts/Pythagorean Mathematics]].
- **The end-point is Philolaus' even/odd = unlimited/limited.** Alexander of Aphrodisias preserves the lost Aristotelian monograph's **arithmology**: justice = a square number, *kairos* ("due season") = 7, Athena = 7 ("motherless and forever virgin" within the decade), marriage = 5 (first odd + first even = male + female). Two directions run together — from number-features to concepts (arithmology) and from phenomena to number (the musical ratios, traced to [[Thinkers/Hippasus]]' bronze discs 12:9:8:6). The heterogeneity of these sources is exactly why Aristotle hedges with "*so-called* Pythagoreans." See [[Sources/Aristotle - Metaphysics]].

## Relevance to Later Philosophy and Science

- **Plato's Forms**: Plato adapted this doctrine to argue that physical things are imperfect copies of immaterial, eternal realities (Forms)—which are best understood mathematically.
- **Modern Physics**: Directly anticipates the Galilean-Newtonian assumption that the book of nature is written in the language of mathematics, and the modern physics view of reality as mathematical relations (e.g., string theory, quantum wavefunctions).

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] The Paradox of the Irrational: The discovery of irrational numbers (such as $\sqrt{2}$, the length of the diagonal of a unit square) was traditionally said to have *shattered* the early Pythagorean assumption that all reality is expressible in whole-number ratios. **Both Burkert and Zhmud reject this "crisis" story**: "all things are number" never asserted universal commensurability, so the discovery (attributed to [[Thinkers/Hippasus]]) deflated no early programme — though the two scholars disagree on its date (Burkert: late 5th c.; Zhmud: early–mid 5th c.). The dramatic "foundational crisis" is itself part of the later legend.
