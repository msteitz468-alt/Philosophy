# Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)

> The foundational Stoic distinction, especially in Epictetus' *Enchiridion*: between what is in our power (*eph' hēmin*, our *prohairesis* or rational faculty of choice/judgment) and what is not (externals: body, property, reputation, office, the actions of others, fate). Freedom, happiness, and virtue lie in focusing exclusively on the former; disturbance and servitude come from misdirecting desire/aversion toward the latter.

## Core Formulation
- **In our power**: thought, impulse, will to get, will to avoid – in a word, everything which is our own doing. These are by nature free, unhindered, untrammelled.
- **Not in our power**: the body, property, reputation, office – in a word, everything which is not our own doing. These are weak, servile, subject to hindrance, dependent on others.
- Proper discipline: Desire only what is in your power; be averse only to what is in your power. For everything else, practice indifference.
- Impressions: Not things themselves disturb us, but our judgments about them. Examine and withhold assent from false impressions.
- Role-playing: Accept the part assigned by nature/God and play it well, without complaint.

This structures the *Enchiridion* as a practical handbook and appears throughout the Fragments and Golden Sayings (divine providence, self as instrument of God, freedom from externals).

**Marcus Aurelius' application (from the *Meditations*)**: The emperor uses the same division under the weight of empire and war. He focuses the "ruling part" on what is up to him (justice in decisions, simplicity of life, cheerfulness amid pain and loss, governing his own opinions) while accepting campaigns, plague, senate politics, and even Commodus as "assigned." "Do every act as if it were the last" + focus on the daemon is the Meditations' version of confining desire/aversion to what is in our power. See [[Sources/Meditations - Marcus Aurelius]] and [[Thinkers/Marcus Aurelius]].

## Relevance to Vault
- **Personal and Career Agency**: Core tool for the "Personal" use case and Mack Steitz's transition. Focus energy on controllable elements (preparation, skill-building via CPCU/study/MasterClass, response to clients, personal routines, mindset) vs uncontrollables (market, specific deals closing, others' opinions, timing of promotion). Reduces anxiety, increases effectiveness – directly supports sales playbook discipline and "book of business" ownership.
- **Stoic Practice and Examined Life**: Daily application of the dichotomy is the Stoic equivalent of the examined life (Socrates) and deliberate practice (MasterClass, Aristotle *ergon*). Links to eudaimonia as activity within our power.
- **AI/Agentic and Workflows**: In agent design and tool use, distinguish what the system/agent can control (prompts, verification steps, chosen actions) vs environment (tool outputs, external data, user). Use in reasoning scaffolds: "What is in my control here?" for self-critique.
- **Classical Complement**: Pairs with Aristotle on virtue as activity in accordance with reason (what is "up to us"), Plato on justice as internal harmony, Thucydides on power (externals) vs character (internal).
