# Natural Law (Aquinas)

> In Thomas Aquinas' *Summa Theologica* (Prima Secundae, especially Treatise on Law, QQ. 90-108), natural law is the rational creature's participation in the eternal law. It is "the light of natural reason, whereby we discern what is good and what is evil" — the first principles of practical reason (most fundamental: "good is to be done and pursued, and evil avoided"). Human (positive) law derives from it for the common good and is changeable as needed; divine law (Old and New) directs to the supernatural end. Law in general is "a rule and measure of acts" (from *ligare*, to bind), pertaining to reason, promulgated by competent authority (the whole people or its viceregent), and ordered to the common good.

## Core Elements from Summa I-II (Treatise on Law)

**Essence of Law (Q. 90)**:

- "Law is a rule and measure of acts, whereby man is induced to act or is restrained from acting" (*lex* from *ligare* — it binds).
- Pertains to reason: "the rule and measure of human acts is the reason, which is the first principle of human acts." Reason directs to the end (the first principle in action).
- "Every law is ordained to the common good."
- Made by the whole people or a public personage who has care of the whole people (sovereign/viceregent). Private persons (e.g., head of household) can make ordinances or commands but not law properly so called.
- Essential that it be promulgated (made known to those subject to it).

**Kinds of Law**:

- **Eternal law**: The divine reason (or providence) by which God governs the entire universe — the exemplar and source of all law.
- **Natural law**: The eternal law as participated in by rational creatures through "the light of natural reason, whereby we discern what is good and what is evil." It is the set of first practical principles (synderesis) naturally known, most basically "good is to be done and pursued, and evil avoided." It is universal, immutable in its first principles, and the foundation for further moral reasoning. Irrational creatures participate in eternal law by natural inclination (but not "law" in the full sense, which requires reason).
- **Human law**: Positive law enacted by human authority, derived from natural law (by conclusion or determination). It is for the common good of a particular community, and can (and sometimes must) change with circumstances (Q. 97). It has coercive power to induce virtue (not just advise).
- **Divine law** (Old Law and New Law of the Gospel): Given by God (via revelation) to direct man to his *supernatural* end (which exceeds what natural law alone can achieve). The New Law is the law of grace, interior (written on the heart), and more perfect.

**Effects of Law**: To make men good (induce virtue), command/ forbid, permit, punish. Law is a kind of training in virtue for the imperfect; the perfect are a law to themselves in a higher sense.

**Relation to Aristotle and classical sources**:

Aquinas constantly engages "the Philosopher." Natural law develops and grounds Aristotle's natural right and the idea that man is a political animal by nature (ordered to the common good of the polis). It provides a rational, universal foundation accessible even to those without revelation, while allowing for the elevation by grace and divine law.

**Grace and Nature**:

Natural law pertains to the natural order (what reason can know and achieve). Grace and the New Law perfect and elevate it toward the supernatural end (beatific vision), without destroying the natural.
