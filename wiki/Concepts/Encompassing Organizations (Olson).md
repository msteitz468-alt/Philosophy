# Encompassing Organizations (Olson)
> Large interest groups representing a substantial share of a nation's resources, which incentivizes them to support overall societal efficiency and minimize the social costs of their redistributions.

## Summary

In *The Rise and Decline of Nations* (1982), Mancur Olson introduces the concept of **encompassing organizations** (or inclusive organizations) to show that not all interest groups behave like narrow, growth-repressing cartels.

### The Logic of Scale and Cost Internalization

The behavior of an interest group depends on the proportion of the society it represents:

* **Narrow Coalitions**: If an interest group represents only 1% of a society, it receives 100% of the benefits of its lobbying but bears only 1% of the societal cost (the "excess burden" or deadweight loss) of its inefficient policies. It has a powerful incentive to lobby for inefficient transfers, tariffs, and subsidies.
* **Encompassing Organizations**: If an organization represents a large share of the society — say, 50% or more (such as a peak labor federation representing half the country's workforce, or a broad business association representing most major industries) — it faces a different incentive structure. If this encompassing group lobbies for an inefficient transfer that costs the society $10 million in lost GDP, its own members will bear $5 million of that loss. 

Because encompassing organizations **internalize a significant portion of the social costs of their actions**, they are much less likely to lobby for highly inefficient policies. Instead, they have a strong interest in the overall prosperity of their nation. They will seek to redistribute income to themselves using methods that minimize deadweight losses, and they will often support policies that increase national productivity.

### Encompassing Labor and Business

Olson uses the encompassing logic to explain different growth experiences among stable countries:

* **Scandinavian Peak Associations**: In Norway and Sweden, labor unions and employers were historically organized into centralized, encompassing peak associations (e.g., the LO union federation). As Gudmund Hernes and others documented, these inclusive groups supported technological modernization, labor retraining, and free trade because their members' welfare depended directly on the health of the national economy.
* **Fragmentation in Great Britain**: In contrast, Great Britain's labor movement was fragmented into hundreds of small, narrow unions (e.g., separate unions for boilermakers, electricians, and painters within the same shipyard). These narrow groups engaged in jurisdictional disputes, restricted output, and blocked technological progress to protect their specific members' shares, indifferent to the fact that their actions collectively ruined the British shipbuilding industry.

## Key Claims / Positions

### How Different Thinkers Approach This

- **Sociologist Gudmund Hernes**: Studied the Norwegian peak associations, providing Olson with the empirical model of how centralized, inclusive interest groups can collaborate with government to support national efficiency and social consensus.
- **Classical Liberalism / Laissez-faire**: Traditional laissez-faire ideology (and economists like Milton and Rose Friedman) argues that all interest group intervention in the market is harmful and that the government that governs least governs best. Olson's encompassing theory complicates this: while narrow lobbies are harmful, broad, centralized organizations can sometimes act as stabilizers, mitigating the worst excesses of fragmented lobbying and cooperating to solve public goods problems.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Encompassing organizations are rare and difficult to establish. Because they represent large, diverse groups, they face severe internal coordination and free-rider barriers. They usually require either strong historical path-dependencies or state support to maintain their encompassing scale.
- If an encompassing organization becomes *too* powerful or bureaucratic, it may suppress internal diversity, limit competition, and block localized innovations that threaten the centralized consensus it represents.
