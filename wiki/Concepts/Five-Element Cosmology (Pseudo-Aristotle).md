# Five-Element Cosmology (Pseudo-Aristotle)

> The universe is composed of five nested elements — earth, water, air, fire, and ether — the four sublunary elements being corruptible and mutable, while ether, the substance of the heavens and stars, is a fifth, indestructible and divine element in perpetual circular motion.

## Summary

*De Mundo* describes the cosmos as concentric spheres: an immovable earth at the center, surrounded successively by water, air, fire, and finally ether, "the less being in each case surrounded by the greater." Ether is explicitly distinguished from ordinary fire — not fiery by blazing but "because it is in continual motion, revolving in a circle, being an element other than the four" — and constitutes the incorruptible substance of the heavenly bodies, in contrast to the "Fiery and Disordered Element" and the aerial region beneath it, which are subject to constant change, generation, and decay (the meteorological phenomena: clouds, rain, snow, wind, thunder, earthquakes, all cataloged in taxonomic detail). The treatise closes the cosmological survey by describing the inhabited world as effectively a single island surrounded by Ocean, with speculative mention of other, invisible continents beyond it.

## Key Claims / Positions

- The cosmos is a sharply two-tiered structure: an eternal, unchanging superlunary region (ether, heavenly bodies) versus a corruptible, ever-changing sublunary region (the four elements and their compounds).
- Ether is a genuine fifth element, not a rarefied form of fire, defined by its natural circular motion rather than any of the four classical qualities (hot/cold/wet/dry).
- Meteorological phenomena are given systematic, largely mechanistic explanations via the two exhalations (dry and moist) rather than divine intervention at the local level.

## How This Sits Among Thinkers

- **Vs. genuine Aristotle**: this doctrine closely tracks the genuine Aristotelian corpus (*On the Heavens*, *Meteorologica*) and is consistent with — though not identical in emphasis to — the substance theory of [[Sources/Aristotle - Metaphysics]]; the text's own preface notes its meteorological sections are "the most Aristotelian portion," likely because they draw on genuine Aristotelian material via Poseidonius.
- **Downstream**: the sharp sublunary/superlunary divide becomes foundational for medieval cosmology (Ptolemaic-Aristotelian synthesis) and is only overturned by the early modern unification of terrestrial and celestial physics (Galileo, Newton).
