# Virtual Realism and Virtual Digitalism (Chalmers)
> Actual VR headsets are "reality machines," not illusion machines: virtual objects are real digital objects with causal powers, and expert users perceive them accurately as in virtual space.

## Summary

*Reality+* chs. 10–13 bring simulation realism down to earth. A **virtual reality environment** is "an immersive, interactive, and computer-generated space." Against **virtual fictionalism** ("Virtual reality is fictional reality" — virtual objects like Harry Potter), Chalmers argues game-fictionality comes from *games*, not virtuality: a Second Life conversation involves no fiction. His **virtual digitalism**: "Virtual objects are digital objects — roughly, structures of bits," made of bits as physical objects are of atoms. The causal-power argument: virtual objects have causal powers; digital objects really have those powers; so virtual objects are digital objects.

A virtual kitten is like a robot kitten — a real object, not a real kitten. Categories divide into **virtual-inclusive** (library, calculator, friendship, club — informational/social, "a matter of how things and people are interconnected") and **virtual-exclusive** (chair, car, kitten — substrate-dependent), with usage able to shift by conceptual engineering. Scorecard: "80 percent realism for many ordinary virtual objects… 100 percent for others such as virtual calculators" — virtual realism is "a little weaker than my simulation realism."

**Against the illusion machine** (ch. 11): the consensus view (Artaud, Langer, Lanier's "comprehensive illusions," Gibson's "consensual hallucination," Slater's Place/Plausibility Illusions) is "fundamentally incorrect." Via the causal theory of perception we perceive real digital objects (the screen is a "way station," like seeing Obama on TV). Expert users develop a **phenomenology of virtuality** — perceiving objects as in *virtual* space, deep enough (via cognitive penetration) "to count as perception and not just as belief or judgment," on the model of expert rear-view-mirror use. Slater's illusions hold only for naive or unwitting users. "The sense that a virtual body is my virtual body needn't be an illusion."

**Augmented reality** (ch. 12): AR objects are virtually (not physically) located — "An object is virtually in a space if it functions as if it occupies that space." Multiple AR systems yield a *harmless* relativism (like time zones and special relativity): "We simply need to allow that relations are part of reality." Ordinary objective facts survive; Conway's "alternative facts" get no vindication. Milgram's "reality-virtuality continuum" should be the **physicality-virtuality continuum** — the old name "bakes in the premise that virtuality is opposed to reality."

**Deepfakes** (ch. 13) are *not* VR (not interactive): "At best they have the power to look a certain way." They raise **local**, not Cartesian, skepticism; the remedy is "authentication by a reliable source" — track records and networks of trust (Regina Rini's images as "reliable backstops," endorsed). Arendt on the totalitarian aim — "to destroy the capacity to form any" convictions — frames the stakes; critical observers in open societies can avoid *thorough* deception.

## How This Relates to Prior Thinkers

- **Susanne Langer / Peirce**: coined "virtual object"/"virtual" in the as-if, illusory sense — Chalmers keeps the words, reverses the verdict.
- **[[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]**: "Where Am I?" cited for split-body/avatar location puzzles.
- **[[Concepts/Controlled Hallucination (Clark)]]**: Clark calls *all* perception controlled hallucination; Chalmers's expert-perception argument runs the other way — even VR perception is accurate perception of digital reality. The two agree that veridicality is about tracking structure, not resembling intrinsic natures.
- **[[Concepts/Readiness-to-Hand and Presence-at-Hand (Heidegger)]]**: the expert user's transparent "sense of virtuality" is equipment-withdrawal in Heidegger's sense — skilled coping reorganizes perception itself.
