# The Best State and the Good Life (Aristotle)

> The good of the individual and the good of the state are identical: happiness as virtuous activity, not fortune or external goods. The ideal state is sized and structured — by population, territory, and citizen class — to secure the leisure (*scholē*) that virtuous activity requires.

## Summary

*Politics* Book VII opens with Aristotle's identity thesis: "the same life is best for each individual, and for states and for mankind collectively." He grounds this in the *Nicomachean Ethics*, citing his own earlier conclusion explicitly: "we maintain, and have said in the Ethics... that happiness is the realization and perfect exercise of virtue, and this not conditional, but absolute" — external and bodily goods are eligible only "for the sake of the soul," not the reverse. From this he derives the practical design of the ideal city. Population should not be maximized but limited to "the largest number which suffices for the purposes of life, and can be taken in at a single view" — self-sufficiency (*autarkeia*), not scale, is the criterion. Territory should likewise be self-sufficing and sized "to live at once temperately and liberally in the enjoyment of leisure," sited for easy egress and defensibility and connected to both sea and land. Aristotle characterizes the Hellenic race as the fortunate mean between the spirited-but-unintelligent peoples of Europe and the intelligent-but-unspirited peoples of Asia — "high-spirited and also intelligent" — as the ideal citizen stock. Citizenship proper is restricted: "the citizens must not lead the life of mechanics or tradesmen, for such a life is ignoble, and inimical to virtue," nor farm the land themselves, "since leisure is necessary both for the development of virtue and the performance of political duties." The true "parts" of the state are warriors and councillors — the same individuals across the stages of life, strength in youth and wisdom in age — while husbandmen, artisans, and traders are mere "necessary conditions" of the state's existence, not constitutive parts of it; land is owned by the ruling class, worked ideally by a distinct, unspirited laboring population. Leisure (*scholē*) is the pivot of the whole design: "peace... is the end of war, and leisure of toil," and the legislator should direct all military and other measures toward securing it, since "there is no leisure for slaves" and virtue itself is unattainable without it.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Individual and political good are strictly identical in kind — the same virtuous-activity conception of happiness applies at both scales.
- Self-sufficiency (*autarkeia*), not maximal population or wealth, is the correct criterion for right-sizing a state's population and territory.
- Full citizenship is deliberately restricted to those with leisure; manual, commercial, and agricultural labor disqualifies a person from being a "part" of the state proper, however necessary their labor is to its existence.
- Leisure is not idleness but the precondition of virtuous political and intellectual activity — the entire military/economic apparatus of the ideal state is instrumental to securing it.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Directly extends [[Concepts/Eudaimonia]]**: this is Aristotle's own cross-reference back to the *Nicomachean Ethics*' definition of happiness as virtuous activity, now applied at the level of the *polis* rather than the individual — the clearest textual link between the two works confirming Aristotle's claim (noted on [[Sources/Aristotle - Nicomachean Ethics (Book I)]]) that ethics is completed by politics.
- **Contrast with modern egalitarian/universalist thought**: Aristotle's exclusion of laborers, tradesmen, and farmers from full citizenship on the grounds that leisure is a precondition of virtue stands in sharp, unreconciled tension with [[Thinkers/John Stuart Mill]]'s and later liberal-democratic commitments to universal political participation regardless of occupation or class.
- **Downstream**: the leisure-as-precondition-of-virtue thesis recurs in later republican and civic-humanist thought about the material conditions of active citizenship.
