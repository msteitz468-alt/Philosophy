# Basic Statements and the Empirical Basis (Popper)

> "What I call a 'basic statement' or a 'basic proposition' is a statement which can serve as a premise in an empirical falsification; in brief, a statement of a singular fact." (Popper, *LScD*, §7). The empirical basis of science consists of such statements, which are inter-subjectively testable but accepted by decision rather than justified by direct experience or perceptual "givenness."

## The Problem

Falsifiability as a criterion requires that there exist singular statements capable of serving as falsifying premises. This appears to shift the demarcation problem from theories to the "empirical basis" itself: what makes a singular statement empirical / acceptable in science?

Traditional answers (perceptual experiences "manifesting" the truth of observation statements, "protocol sentences" as the given, incorrigible foundations) reintroduce psychologism and threaten objectivity.

## Popper's Solution

- **Basic statements** are singular statements describing observable events or states of affairs that can (in conjunction with a theory) contradict it.

- They are **not** justified by subjective experience, feelings of conviction, or direct "inspection." Subjective experience can be an *object* of psychological inquiry but never a *justifier* of a scientific statement.

- They **are** inter-subjectively testable: from a basic statement other testable statements can be deduced. Hence there are no ultimate, untestable statements in science.

- Acceptance of a basic statement (when doubt arises) is a **decision** or convention reached within the scientific community after testing. It is not incorrigible; basic statements can themselves be tested and, in principle, rejected.

- Result: the empirical basis is itself hypothetical and fallible. Testability is "ad infinitum" in principle. We stop testing when we are satisfied for practical purposes that the test conditions were severe enough and the observation reproducible.

## Objectivity Without Foundations

- "The objectivity of scientific statements lies in the fact that they can be inter-subjectively tested."
- Repeatability and reproducibility under specifiable conditions are what make an effect scientifically significant (vs. "occult effects" that cannot be reproduced by others following instructions).
- Even our own observations are not taken seriously until repeated/tested. This applies to basic statements as well.

- No privileged status for "protocol sentences" describing immediate experience. They occur in science only as (low-reliability, at present) psychological hypotheses.

## Contrast with Positivist / Protocol-Sentence Views

Popper criticizes views (early Carnap, Neurath, Reininger, Wittgenstein's *Tractatus* influence) that treat atomic/protocol sentences as ultimate or in need of no further confirmation, or that try to "reduce" science to them. Such views either lead back to psychologism or fail to secure the objectivity and testability of the basis itself.

## Relation to Testing Procedure

- A theory is taken as falsified only when we accept basic statements that contradict it *and* a low-level falsifying hypothesis describing a reproducible effect has itself been corroborated.
- The basic statements play a dual role: (1) they define the logical form of empirical statements (via the class of all possible basic statements); (2) accepted ones serve as the (provisional) ground for corroboration or falsification.

## Vault and Practical Relevance

- **"Basic statements" from raw sources**: When the agent reads a source and extracts a claim or observation for the wiki, that extraction functions as a basic statement — inter-subjectively checkable against the raw text (anyone can re-read the source), but accepted by decision (the summary/interpretation is not incorrigible). Future sources or closer readings can "falsify" or refine it.

- **No sacred summaries**: Even the most corroborated page or concept remains open to revision by new basic statements (new source material) or better tests (lint, contradiction discovery, user challenge).

- **AI / agentic**: When an agent cites "evidence" or "observation," that claim must be reproducible from the provided context/files and open to re-test. "I feel confident" or internal activation strength has no justificatory force (purely subjective/psychological). The equivalent of reproducibility is: another agent or the user, given the same raw + instructions, can reach and check the same extraction.

- **CPCU / risk / career**: Loss "observations" (claims data, inspection reports) are basic statements — accepted for now after verification procedures, but always open to re-inspection, audit, or new evidence. Risk models are corroborated only to the extent they have survived tests against such data; they are never "verified."

- **Obsidian / wiki practice**: Dataview queries, graph inspection, and direct re-reading of sources provide the inter-subjective test for any claim on a page. Frontmatter `sources:` lists function as the provenance of the basic statements grounding the page.

## Open Questions

- How much conventionalism is compatible with objectivity? (Popper's answer: the *rules* of the game — the methodological decisions — are what secure rationality and criticism, even if particular basic statements are decided.)
- Psychological and social dimensions of accepting basic statements (later addressed in Popper's "three worlds" and institutional analysis of science).
- Relation to underdetermination / Duhem-Quine: a falsifying basic statement contradicts a *system*; attributing the falsification to a particular hypothesis requires additional methodological decisions.
