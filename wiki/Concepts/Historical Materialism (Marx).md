# Historical Materialism (Marx)

> Karl Marx's methodological and metaphysical approach to history, economics, and society. It asserts that the ultimate driving force of human history is not the development of ideas or spirit (idealism), but rather the development of the material conditions of life—specifically the economic mode of production, which forms the "base" that determines the legal, political, and ideological "superstructure" of society.

## Core Framework

**Base and Superstructure**:
- **The Economic Base**: Composed of two parts:
  1.  *Forces of Production*: The tools, technology, raw materials, and human labor power used to produce goods.
  2.  *Relations of Production*: The social and property relations governing the ownership and distribution of the forces of production (e.g. master/slave, lord/serf, capitalist/wage-worker).
- **The Superstructure**: The political systems, laws, religions, arts, philosophies, and moral codes of a society. The superstructure arises from and serves to justify and maintain the economic base.

**The Engine of History**:
- History progresses through a dialectical conflict within the economic base: when the development of the *forces of production* reaches a point where they are restricted by the existing *relations of production*, a period of social revolution begins.
- These economic contradictions manifest socially as **Class Struggles** between the ruling class (who own the means of production) and the oppressed class. See [[Concepts/Class Struggle (Marx)]].

**Historical Epochs**:
Marx outlines a teleological progression of historical stages based on economic organization:
1.  *Primitive Communism* (classless, shared resources).
2.  *Ancient Slave Society* (master vs. slave).
3.  *Feudalism* (lord vs. serf).
4.  *Capitalism* (bourgeoisie vs. proletariat).
5.  *Socialism/Communism* (abolition of private property and class divisions).

## In the Vault

- **The Inversion of Hegel**: Historical materialism is a direct rejection of Hegelian dialectical idealism. Hegel argued that history is the self-unfolding of [Geist](file:///home/mark/Documents/Philosophy/wiki/Concepts/Geist%20(Absolute%20Spirit).md); Marx asserted that Geist is merely an ideological reflection of material class relations. See [[Contradictions/Dialectical Idealism vs. Historical Materialism]].
- **Technium and Forces**: Marx's focus on the forces of production (tools/technology) as the driver of social relations connects to Kevin Kelly's concept of the [Technium](file:///home/mark/Documents/Philosophy/wiki/Concepts/Technium%20(Kelly).md).
- **Popperian Critique**: Karl Popper launches a severe critique of historical materialism in *The Open Society and Its Enemies*, calling it a form of deterministic historicism that leads to totalitarianism. See [[Sources/Conjectures and Refutations - Karl Popper]].
