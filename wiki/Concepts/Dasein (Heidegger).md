# Dasein (Heidegger)
> The entity whose own being is an issue for it — Heidegger's term for human existence, replacing "subject," "soul," "consciousness," and "rational animal."

## Summary

*Dasein* (literally "being there/here") is Heidegger's term for the human being understood not as a thing with properties but as the entity that exists in the distinctive sense of *standing forth* into possibilities. "The essence of Dasein lies in its existence" — Dasein has no fixed "what"; its characteristics "are in each case possible ways for it to be, and no more than that" (BT 42). This marks a fundamental break with the tradition from [[Thinkers/Aristotle]] onward, which treats entities as substances bearing predicable properties.

Dasein is chosen as the entry point for the question of Being because it is the being that *asks* the question and has a "preconceptual understanding of being" that, used with caution, guides inquiry. Unlike other entities, Dasein's being is an *issue* for it: "Dasein is an entity for which, in its Being, that Being is an issue" (BT 191). A hammer does not care how or whether it is; Dasein does.

### Existence and Possibility

For Heidegger, Dasein's possibility is *prior* to its actuality — violating [[Thinkers/Aristotle]]'s ontology in which actuality (*energeia*) is prior to potentiality (*dynamis*). Dasein "is constantly more than it factually is" because it projects itself upon possibilities. The Shakespearean "To be or not to be" captures not merely the choice of whether to exist but *how* to exist.

### Existentials vs. Categories

Dasein's basic structures are not "categories" (which apply to non-Dasein entities like hammers and numbers) but **existentials** (*Existenzialien*): being-in-the-world, care, temporality, understanding, mood, fallenness. These are "equiprimordial" (*gleichursprünglich*) — no one is derived from or prior to another.

### Mineness and the They

Every Dasein is *mine* (*Jemeinigkeit*): I cannot delegate my dying, my choosing, my existing. Yet in everyday life Dasein surrenders its choices to **das Man** (the "they") — the anonymous "one" that dictates what "one does," "one thinks," "one believes." "The Self of everyday Dasein is the they-self, which we distinguish from the authentic Self" (BT 129). *Das Man* is "everyone and no one."

### Authenticity and Inauthenticity

- **Authenticity** (*Eigentlichkeit*, from *eigen*, "own"): being true to one's own self, taking over one's possibilities.
- **Inauthenticity** (*Uneigentlichkeit*): ceding decisions to *das Man*; the normal condition of everyday Dasein and "by no means an unqualified blemish."
- "Existentiality is always determined by facticity" — Dasein is *thrown* (*geworfen*) into a world it did not choose. Authenticity is not escaping thrownness but owning it.

### Embodiment

Dasein is essentially embodied, but the body is "inconspicuous" — it "lies in the background of Dasein's doings, not in the foreground." Heidegger treats the "software" (Dasein's nature and structures) as primary over the "hardware" (body), which becomes thematic only when it intrudes (illness, fatigue).

### Fallenness

Dasein has an essential tendency to "fall" (*Verfallen*) away from itself into the world, expressed through idle talk (*Gerede*), curiosity (*Neugier*), and ambiguity (*Zweideutigkeit*). Falling does not presuppose a prior unfallen state; Dasein has "always already" fallen. Even the absorbed craftsman is fallen — absorbed in daily tasks without surveying his life as a whole.

## From Being and Time (Primary Text)

§9 gives the two theses the whole analytic rests on: "The 'essence' of Dasein lies in its existence [*Existenz*]" — Dasein's characteristics are "possible ways for it to be," not attributes of something objectively present — and being is in each case *mine* (*Jemeinigkeit*), grounding authenticity/inauthenticity (inauthenticity being no "lesser" being). "Existentials and categories are the two fundamental possibilities of the characteristics of being": "Beings are a *who* (existence) or else a *what* (objective presence in the broadest sense)." The analytic must start from average everydayness, since Dasein is "ontically 'nearest' to itself, ontologically farthest away" (§5). §10 sets the analytic off from anthropology, psychology, and biology — Dilthey, Bergson, Husserl, and Scheler never ask about the *being* of the person, and Descartes investigated the *cogito* while "the *sum* he leaves completely undiscussed." Understanding as thrown project makes Dasein "constantly 'more' than it actually is," licensing the imperative "become what you are!" (§31). Selfhood is finally cashed out not as a persisting subject but as [[Concepts/Conscience, Guilt, and Resoluteness (Heidegger)|anticipatory resoluteness]]: self-constancy "means nothing other than anticipatory resoluteness" (§64), against Kant's "I think."

## Key Claims / Positions

### How Different Thinkers Approach This

- **[[Thinkers/Aristotle]]**: The human being is a "rational animal" (*zoon logon echon*) — a substance with the essential attribute of reason. Heidegger rejects this: Dasein is not a substance at all, and its "rationality" is derivative of more fundamental structures (understanding, mood, care).
- **[[Thinkers/Descartes]]**: The self is *res cogitans* (thinking substance), a self-enclosed subject that must then prove the external world exists. Heidegger: Dasein is always already *in* the world; the Cartesian starting point is an abstraction from Dasein's actual situation.
- **Husserl**: The transcendental ego is "continuously evident" to itself. Heidegger objects that Husserl "said too little about the being of the subject" and that absorbed Dasein is barely aware of itself.
- **[[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]**: The self as "Center of Narrative Gravity" — an abstraction, not a substance. Heidegger would agree that the self is not a thing, but would insist that Dasein's self-relatedness is ontologically prior to any narrative construction.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Heidegger restricts *existence* (*Existenz*) to Dasein alone. Does this unduly privilege human being, or does it correctly identify a genuine ontological distinction? The question bears on animal consciousness, AI, and the scope of phenomenology.
- The relationship between Dasein's "mineness" and its essential being-with-others (*Mitsein*) generates tension: how can being be *mine* if it is essentially shared?
