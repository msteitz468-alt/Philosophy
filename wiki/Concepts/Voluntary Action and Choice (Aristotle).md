# Voluntary Action and Choice (Aristotle)

> An action is voluntary when its "moving principle" is in the agent, who knows the particular circumstances of the act. Choice (*prohairesis*) — deliberate desire about what is in our power — is distinct from wish (which concerns ends), appetite, and mere opinion; it follows deliberation (*bouleusis*), which is restricted to contingent matters achievable by our own effort. Because we choose the actions that form our character, we are responsible for our own virtue and vice even though we cannot control character moment-by-moment once formed.

## Summary

Book III opens the question of moral responsibility by defining the voluntary not by the absence of external compulsion but by the location of the action's originating principle: an act is voluntary when "the moving principle is in the agent himself," who is aware of the particular circumstances in which he acts. Actions done from fear of a greater evil (a tyrant's threat) are "mixed" but classed as "more like voluntary" ones, since at the moment of acting the principle of motion remains within the agent. Involuntariness proper requires either compulsion (an external force overriding the agent entirely) or ignorance of the particular circumstances (as opposed to ignorance of universal moral principle, which does not excuse). Choice (*prohairesis*) is then sharply distinguished from adjacent mental states: unlike wish, which can target ends and even impossibilities, choice concerns only what is achievable by our own effort and always concerns means, not the end itself ("wish relates rather to the end, choice to the means"); unlike appetite and temper, which are shared with animals, choice is essentially rational and deliberative; unlike opinion, which can be about anything, choice is always accompanied by desire and pursuit. Deliberation (*bouleusis*), which choice follows, ranges only over contingent matters — things that "happen in a certain way for the most part, but in which the event is obscure" and that lie within our power to bring about — never over eternal or necessary truths, nor over ends themselves, which are simply given (health for the doctor, victory for the general). Crucially, Aristotle grounds moral responsibility for character in this same framework: although we cannot, once virtue or vice has become habitual, simply choose in the moment to have a different character, we are nonetheless responsible for it, because we were responsible for the earlier choices (to practice courage, to indulge appetite) that produced the settled disposition — "men make themselves responsible for being unjust or self-indulgent."

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Voluntariness is defined by the internal location of the moving principle**, not by absence of all external pressure — "the things of which the moving principle is in a man himself are in his power to do or not to do."
- **Mixed actions** (done under threat, e.g., a tyrant's coercion) are classified as more voluntary than involuntary, since the agent still originates the act at the moment of choosing, however constrained the circumstances.
- **Ignorance of particulars excuses; ignorance of universal principle (moral ignorance) does not** — a distinction that blocks using one's own vicious character as an excuse for wrongdoing.
- **Choice (*prohairesis*) concerns means, not ends**: deliberate desire that follows deliberation about *how* to achieve an already-given end, distinguishing it from wish (which can target the end itself, even impossible ones).
- **Deliberation is restricted to contingent, agent-achievable matters** — we do not deliberate about eternal truths, about what is fixed by nature, or about ends as such, only about the "obscure" middle ground of practical means.
- **Responsibility for character is retrospective, not just momentary**: we are responsible for the *habituating* choices (repeated actions of cheating, or of drinking to excess) that produce a settled vicious or virtuous disposition, even though we cannot un-choose that disposition instantaneously once formed — "everyone ceases to inquire how he is to act when he has brought the moving principle back to himself... for this is what chooses."

## How Aristotle Uses This

Provides the psychological machinery presupposed by the entire mean-doctrine of [[Concepts/Doctrine of the Mean (Aristotle)]]: virtue is a state "concerned with choice," so an account of what choice actually is (and how it differs from wish, appetite, and opinion) is a necessary prerequisite to the analysis of the particular virtues (courage, temperance, and the rest) that follows immediately in Books III–IV (see [[Concepts/Moral Virtues as Means (Aristotle)]]). The responsibility-for-character argument here also underwrites the Book VII treatment of incontinence (*akrasia*) — see [[Concepts/Akrasia - Weakness of Will (Aristotle)]] — where Aristotle must explain how someone can act against a choice they themselves made and endorse.
