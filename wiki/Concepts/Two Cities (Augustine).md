# Two Cities (Augustine)

> Augustine's master framework for all of history: two cities, defined by two loves, interwoven throughout time, separated only at the Last Judgment. The earthly city is built on love of self to the contempt of God; the City of God is built on love of God to the contempt of self.

## Summary

The central thesis of the *City of God* (Books XI–XXII, c. 413–426 CE) is that all of human history is the story of two cities — *civitas Dei* (City of God) and *civitas terrena* (earthly city) — which are not geographically distinct but spiritually distinct, mingled throughout all institutions and ages until the end of time.

**The locus classicus** (Book XIV.28): *"Two cities have been formed by two loves: the earthly city by love of self to the contempt of God; the Heavenly City by love of God to the contempt of self. The one glories in itself, the other glories in the Lord."*

The framework is simultaneously theological, psychological, political, and historical. It explains Rome's rise and fall, the role of the Church, and the final end of all things.

---

## The Two Loves

The root distinction is **love** (*amor*), not institution, ethnicity, religion, or virtue. Both cities contain sinners; many visible members of the Church belong to the earthly city at heart; some apparently outside the Church belong to the City of God.

| | **City of God** | **Earthly City** |
|---|---|---|
| **Root** | Love of God to contempt of self | Love of self to contempt of God |
| **Standard** | Lives by God's standard | Lives by man's own standard |
| **Will** | Submitted to God's will | Self-willed (the Devil's sin) |
| **Peace sought** | Eternal peace in God | Temporal peace through domination |
| **Pride/Humility** | Humility (Christ's way) | Pride (the Devil's way) |
| **True good** | Cleaving to God | Possession of finite goods |
| **Fate** | Eternal life with God | Eternal death without God |

The earthly city is not simply wicked people or secular institutions — it is the orientation of love. Pride is its root and expression. Augustine traces this directly to the Devil's sin: not from flesh (the Devil has none) but from choosing to live "by his own standard" rather than God's. This is also why Book XIV argues that "the flesh" (*sarx* in Paul) does not mean the body but the whole person turned in on themselves — even proud philosophers are "carnal" in this sense.

## Historical Instantiations

The two cities appear in every age of history (Books XV–XVIII):

- **Cain and Abel**: Cain (earthly city) kills Abel (City of God) — and then *builds a city*, while Abel has none. The earthly city is always the builder of empires; the City of God lives as pilgrim without a permanent home on earth.
- **Noah**: the Ark = the Church, carrying the remnant through the flood of judgment.
- **Abraham**: the covenant inaugurates the explicit sacred history of the City of God.
- **Israel and the nations**: the two cities run in parallel through Assyria/Babylon (earthly) and Israel (heavenly), though members of the City of God exist among the Gentiles (Job as example).
- **Rome**: the greatest earthly city; its virtues (love of honor, civic devotion) are real and were rewarded by God with temporal empire — but it never achieved true justice because it never worshipped the true God. "Remove justice, and what are kingdoms but gangs of criminals on a large scale?" (IV.4). God used Rome providentially as a vehicle for spreading Christianity, but Rome's dominion and glory are strictly temporal.
- **The Church**: contains both cities in mixture — the predestined saints and the reprobate. Like the dragnet that gathers all fish until the shore; separated only at the Last Judgment.

The two cities are **interwoven** (*permixtae*) in the present age. They share the same streets, families, laws, and institutions. Citizens of the City of God are *pilgrims* in the earthly city: they obey its laws, pay taxes, support temporal peace — but they do not take earthly peace as their ultimate good.

## The Earthly City's Peace and the Pilgrim Use of It

Even the earthly city seeks and achieves a real good: temporal peace. Augustine does not condemn the earthly city absolutely. Its order, laws, and peace are genuine goods — they are just not the *highest* good and cannot be mistaken for it.

The citizens of the City of God *use* (*uti*) temporal peace while journeying to their true homeland, rather than *enjoying* (*frui*) it as an end. This use/enjoy distinction is Augustine's practical resolution to the "pilgrim in the world" problem:

- Obey civil laws where they do not conflict with God's law.
- Pay taxes, raise families, serve in roles that promote temporal peace.
- Do not make earthly glory, power, or security the object of ultimate love.

The earthly city seeks peace *through domination* — it imposes order by force. The City of God seeks peace through submission to God and persuasion of neighbors. The earthly city's peace, however obtained, is real and preferable to disorder; but it is always imperfect and temporary.

---

## The Separation at the Last Judgment

The two cities remain intermingled until the end. Only at the Last Judgment (Books XX–XXII) will they be definitively separated:

- The City of God: eternal life, vision of God, the eternal Sabbath (rest, sight, love, praise — without end).
- The earthly city: eternal death, the "second death," punishment proportional to their rejection of God.

The eternal Sabbath is the seventh age of history: six ages of striving (Adam, Noah, Abraham, David, Babylon, Christ) → seventh age = eternal rest in God. "There we shall rest and see; we shall see and love; we shall love and we shall praise. Behold what will be, in the end, without end." (XXII.30)

## Relationship to the Confessions

The *Confessions* applies the same two-cities logic to a single soul: Augustine's divided will between love of self (concupiscence, pride, curiosity) and love of God — resolved at conversion by grace. The *City of God* scales this drama to all of human history. "The *City of God* is the application of the *Confessions* to the history of mankind." (O'Meara) Personal and civilizational dramas run on the same logic: grace rescues love from its disordered object and reorders it toward God.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Augustine's **predestination** teaching means the membership of the City of God is fixed in God's foreknowledge, but hidden in history. This creates tension: how does the Church know who is truly a citizen? Augustine's answer: it doesn't, fully, until the end. Pastoral implication: the visible Church is never simply identical with the City of God.
- **The state**: Augustine neither simply condemns nor sanctifies the state. Later interpreters disagreed sharply — some read him as proto-theocratic, others as proto-secularist. The text supports neither extreme.
- **Millenarianism**: Augustine's reading that the "thousand years" of Revelation = the current Church age (amillennialism) was a major exegetical decision that shaped Catholic and Protestant readings; contested by futurist/dispensationalist readings.
