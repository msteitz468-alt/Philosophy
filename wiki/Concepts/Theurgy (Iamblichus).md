# Theurgy (Iamblichus)

> "Divine work" (*theourgia*, opposed to mere *theologia*, talk about the gods): ritual practice, grounded in the *Chaldaean Oracles*, aimed at incarnating divine power in a material object or a human being — for Iamblichus and the Athenian School the primary means of the soul's salvation, above philosophy itself.

## Summary

Wallis defines theurgy as ritual magic justified by the magical Principle of Correspondence — each part of the universe mirrors every other, so material symbols (stones, herbs, names, rites) mirror and channel invisible powers. Its trajectory within [[Schools/Neoplatonism]]: rejected by [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] (the philosopher transcends magic through contemplation; sympathy touches only body and lower soul), treated by [[Thinkers/Porphyry]] as a limited auxiliary, vindicated by [[Thinkers/Iamblichus]] as the road to union, and institutionalized by the Athenian School ([[Thinkers/Proclus]] was a practicing theurgist).

## The Debate

- **Porphyry (*Letter to Anebo*, *De Regressu Animae*)**: ritual "works only through the sympathy governing the sensible cosmos and cannot therefore affect the higher souls of the gods" (II.5d). At best it purifies the lower soul's pneumatic envelope; it cannot elevate the soul to the Intelligible order; the philosopher does not need it; it invites abuse.
- **Iamblichus (*De Mysteriis*)**: thought does not unite us with the gods — "the vital union depends not on this, but on performance of the appropriate ritual actions" (II.11). Union comes by the gods' voluntary bestowal of power: salvation depends on divine grace, not human effort. This coheres with his denial of the undescended soul (see [[Concepts/The Undescended Soul (Plotinus vs Iamblichus)]]): a fully fallen soul cannot climb back by intellect alone.
- **Proclus** added the theurgic virtues of faith, truth, and love (*pistis, alētheia, erōs*) from the *Chaldaean Oracles*, including *erōs pronoētikos*, the providential descending love of higher for lower — a stress on grace foreign to Plotinus.

Wallis notes theurgy differs from Christian sacramentalism in relying on the natural world-order (cosmic correspondence), not supernatural intervention — yet Pseudo-Dionysius's sacramental hierarchy visibly echoes it.
