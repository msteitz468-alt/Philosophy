# Faction (Federalist 10)

## Overview

In *Federalist No. 10*, James Madison (Publius) identifies the "violence of faction" as one of the greatest dangers to popular government and presents the extended republic under the proposed Constitution as the republican remedy.

**Definition**: "By a faction, I understand a number of citizens, whether amounting to a majority or a minority of the whole, who are united and actuated by some common impulse of passion, or of interest, adversed to the rights of other citizens, or to the permanent and aggregate interests of the community."

Factions are the "mortal diseases under which popular governments have everywhere perished" and the "favorite and fruitful topics from which the adversaries to liberty derive their most specious declamations."

## Key Arguments

**Causes of faction**: Latent in the nature of man. Fallible reason combined with self-love produces different opinions. Diversity of faculties (from which property rights originate) leads to unequal distribution of property. "Those who hold and those who are without property have ever formed distinct interests in society." Creditors vs. debtors; landed, manufacturing, mercantile, moneyed interests. Zeal for different opinions concerning religion or government; attachment to ambitious leaders; frivolous distinctions. "The most common and durable source of factions has been the various and unequal distribution of property."

**Removing causes impracticable or worse than the disease**:
- Destroying liberty (essential to political life, "air is to fire").
- Giving every citizen the same opinions, passions, and interests (impracticable; as long as reason is fallible and connected to self-love, diversity persists).

**Relief only in controlling effects**:
- Minority faction: republican principle (regular vote of majority) defeats sinister views (may clog or convulse but cannot execute violence under forms of Constitution).
- Majority faction: pure democracy offers no cure (common passion felt by majority; communication and concert from the form itself; nothing checks sacrifice of weaker party).

**Republic (vs. pure democracy) provides the cure**:
1. **Representation**: Delegation to a small number of citizens elected by the rest. "Refine and enlarge the public views, by passing them through the medium of a chosen body of citizens, whose wisdom may best discern the true interest of their country, and whose patriotism and love of justice will be least likely to sacrifice it to temporary or partial considerations." (Though factious men may still intrigue and betray.)
2. **Greater number of citizens and extent of territory**: Larger sphere brings greater variety of parties and interests. "The smaller the society, the fewer probably will be the distinct parties and interests... the more frequently will a majority be found of the same party; and the smaller the number... the more easily will they concert and execute their plans of oppression. Extend the sphere, and you take in a greater variety of parties and interests; you make it less probable that a majority of the whole will have a common motive to invade the rights of other citizens; or if such a common motive exists, it will be more difficult for all who feel it to discover their own strength, and to act in unison with each other."

"The influence of factious leaders may kindle a flame within their particular States, but will be unable to spread a general conflagration through the other States. A religious sect may degenerate into a political faction in a part of the Confederacy; but the variety of sects dispersed over the entire face of it must secure the national councils against any danger from that source. A rage for paper money, for an abolition of debts, for an equal division of property, or for any other improper or wicked project, will be less apt to pervade the whole body of the Union than a particular member of it..."

Conclusion: "In the extent and proper structure of the Union, therefore, we behold a republican remedy for the diseases most incident to republican government."
