# Greatest Happiness Principle (Mill)

> The foundational moral principle of John Stuart Mill's utilitarian ethics. It asserts that actions are morally right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, and wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness—defining happiness as pleasure and the absence of pain, and unhappiness as pain and the privation of pleasure.

## Core Concepts

**The Utility Standard**:
- Mill asserts that pleasure and freedom from pain are the only things desirable as ends; all other desirable things are desirable either for the pleasure inherent in themselves or as means to the promotion of pleasure and prevention of pain.
- Morality is consequentialist: the moral worth of an action is judged entirely by its results (the amount of net happiness it produces), not by the inner motive of the actor.

**Qualitative Pleasures (Higher vs. Lower)**:
- Unlike Jeremy Bentham's purely quantitative utilitarianism ("push-pin is as good as poetry"), Mill introduces a distinction in the *quality* of pleasures:
  - **Higher Pleasures**: Intellectual, artistic, imaginative, and moral pleasures. These are intrinsically superior to bodily sensations.
  - **Lower Pleasures**: Basic physical and sensory gratifications.
- To prove this, Mill appeals to the judgment of "competent judges"—those who have experienced both and consistently prefer the higher, intellectual pleasures despite the greater capacity for suffering they bring:
  - *"It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied."*

**The Ultimate Telos**:
- The principle aims at the maximization of happiness not just for the individual agent, but for all of humanity (and indeed, all sentient creation). The agent must be as strictly impartial as a disinterested and benevolent spectator.

## In the Vault

- **Deontological Opposition**: The Greatest Happiness Principle directly conflicts with Kant's moral deontology, which argues that actions are right only if done from duty according to formal laws, regardless of consequences. See [[Contradictions/Utilitarian Consequentialism vs. Kantian Deontology]].
- **Beatitude and Virtue**: Mill's conception of happiness as the highest end is a secularized, consequentialist counterpart to Aristotle's teleological [Eudaimonia](file:///home/mark/Documents/Philosophy/wiki/Concepts/Eudaimonia.md) and Aquinas' [Beatific Vision](file:///home/mark/Documents/Philosophy/wiki/Concepts/Beatific%20Vision%20-%20Last%20End%20(Aquinas).md).
- **Conatus and Power**: Contrast with Spinoza's ethics, where the highest good is not the maximization of pleasure as an end, but the augmentation of power to act and understand ([conatus](file:///home/mark/Documents/Philosophy/wiki/Concepts/Conatus%20(Spinoza).md)).
