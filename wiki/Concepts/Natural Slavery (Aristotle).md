# Natural Slavery (Aristotle)

> Aristotle distinguishes slavery "by nature" — grounded in a permanent deficiency of the deliberative faculty — from slavery "by law or convention" (conquest), arguing the former is just and mutually advantageous while questioning the latter.

## Summary

In *Politics* Book I, chs. 4–7 and 13, Aristotle analyzes the household's first relation, master and slave, as part of his broader project of resolving the polis into its natural elements. A slave is "a living possession" and "an instrument of action" — indeed "a part of the master, a living but separated part of his bodily frame." The philosophical crux is his claim that some men are slaves *by nature*: "he who is by nature not his own but another's man, is by nature a slave," namely one who "participates in rational principle enough to apprehend, but not to have it" — body fit for use, soul fit for another's foresight but not its own. Aristotle holds that "some men are by nature free, and others slaves, and... for these latter slavery is both expedient and right," and that natural mastery, properly exercised, is advantageous and even friendly to both parties, "for they have a common interest." But he separately acknowledges slavery "by law" — the convention that captives in war become the property of the victor — and reports that this is disputed even among the wise: some hold it unjust because it rests on force rather than virtue. He extends the deliberative-faculty analysis to the household generally: "the slave has no deliberative faculty at all; the woman has, but it is without authority, and the child has, but it is immature" — a graded hierarchy of rational authority within the household that grounds differentiated (not identical) virtue for each member.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Natural slavery is defined by a specific rational deficiency (can obey but not originate rational deliberation), not by ethnicity, conquest, or misfortune.
- The natural-slavery relation is framed as reciprocally advantageous, on analogy with soul ruling body or reason ruling appetite.
- Slavery-by-convention (war captivity) is explicitly flagged as contested and potentially unjust — Aristotle reports rather than fully endorses the "might makes right" position of some contemporaries.
- The same deliberative-faculty framework subordinates women and children within the household, though with different characterizations (authority absent vs. immature, respectively) than the slave's total absence of the faculty.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Scholarly reception**: many commentators (noted even in the wiki's brief on this text) argue Aristotle's own criteria for a "natural slave" (someone with essentially no capacity for deliberation) are so stringent that no actual human plausibly satisfies them — an internal tension between the theory and the practice it was used to justify.
- **Downstream contrast with [[Thinkers/John Stuart Mill]]** and later liberal thought: Mill's harm principle and doctrine of individual liberty presuppose universal rational agency in all (competent adult) persons, directly rejecting any graded natural hierarchy of deliberative capacity as a basis for political subordination.
- **Contrast with [[Thinkers/Thomas Hobbes]]**: Hobbes grounds all subjection (including slave-like subjection to a conqueror) in consent under fear of death within the social contract, not in any natural, permanent deficiency of reason — a fundamentally different (and more universal) account of legitimate subordination.
