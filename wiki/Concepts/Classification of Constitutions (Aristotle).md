# Classification of Constitutions (Aristotle)

> A constitution (*politeia*) is "the arrangement of magistracies in a state." Aristotle sorts constitutions into three true forms — kingship, aristocracy, polity — each ruling for the common good, and their three deviant/perverted counterparts — tyranny, oligarchy, democracy — each ruling for the rulers' private advantage.

## Summary

*Politics* Book III opens by defining the citizen not by residence or legal status but by the capacity to share in "indefinite office" — deliberative or judicial power ("he who has the power to take part in the deliberative or judicial administration of any state is said by us to be a citizen of that state"). A state is accordingly "a body of citizens sufficing for the purposes of life," and sameness of state tracks sameness of constitution, not continuity of population or territory. The constitution (*politeia*) is "the arrangement of magistracies in a state, especially of the highest of all... the constitution is in fact the government." Aristotle's master classificatory criterion is for-whose-benefit: "governments which have a regard to the common interest are constituted in accordance with strict principles of justice, and are therefore true forms; but those which regard only the interest of the rulers are all defective and perverted forms." Crossed with the question of how many rule, this yields the canonical sixfold table: kingship (rule of one) degenerates into tyranny; aristocracy (rule of the few best) degenerates into oligarchy (rule of the wealthy few for their own advantage); polity (rule of the many for the common good) degenerates into democracy (rule of the poor majority for their own advantage). Aristotle notes the true mark distinguishing oligarchy/democracy is wealth versus poverty, not mere number. Book III's second half turns to kingship specifically, distinguishing several historical varieties (Laconian perpetual generalship, barbarian hereditary despotism, elective *aesymnetia*, heroic-age kingship, and household-analogous absolute rule) and raising the extreme case of *pambasileia* (absolute kingship) — legitimate, Aristotle argues, only for a man of such transcendent virtue that he stands to the community "as a whole to a part," and so should be obeyed always rather than made to rule in turn like an ordinary citizen. Book VI later elaborates the internal varieties of democracy (ranked from agricultural, the most stable, to the mechanic/trader-based "last" democracy, the least) and oligarchy (from the most law-governed, resembling polity, to outright "dynasty"), and catalogs the administrative offices any constitution requires.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Citizenship is functionally defined (sharing in office/judgment), not by birth, residence, or occupation alone.
- The true/deviant distinction turns entirely on for-whose-benefit rule is exercised — common good vs. rulers' private advantage — not directly on the number of rulers.
- The sixfold table (three true, three deviant, crossed by one/few/many) is the master taxonomy for all subsequent constitutional analysis in the work.
- Democracy and oligarchy are more accurately distinguished by the class basis (poor-free vs. wealthy-noble) of the ruling group than by pure numerical majority/minority.
- *Pambasileia* (absolute kingship) is justified only as an extreme, near-hypothetical case for a person of transcendent, whole-surpassing virtue — "for men of pre-eminent virtue there is no law — they are themselves a law."
- Each true/deviant form admits internal varieties (Book VI's four kinds of democracy, four of oligarchy) ranked by stability, correlating with the economic/military composition of the citizen body.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Vs. [[Thinkers/Plato]]**: Plato's *Republic* (Book VIII) offers its own famous sequence of regime decay (aristocracy → timocracy → oligarchy → democracy → tyranny) driven by psychological/educational corruption across generations; Aristotle's classification is synchronic and criterial (for-whose-benefit) rather than a fixed historical cycle, and he explicitly criticizes Plato's cyclical account elsewhere in the *Politics* as insufficiently empirical.
- **Downstream**: the true/deviant-by-beneficiary criterion anticipates the later republican and constitutionalist tradition's concern with rule "for the common good" as the touchstone of legitimacy, feeding into [[Thinkers/Thomas Hobbes]] and [[Thinkers/John Stuart Mill]]'s very different accounts of legitimate authority.
- The *pambasileia* discussion is the ancient source for later debates (echoed in [[Thinkers/Thomas Hobbes]]'s sovereign and in monarchist political theory) over whether any individual's virtue or capacity could justify permanent, non-reciprocal rule.
