# Stasis and Constitutional Change (Aristotle)

> Aristotle's diagnostic theory of *stasis* (civil discord/revolution): a three-part analysis (feeling, motive, occasion) rooted in the misapplication of equality — democrats assume equals-in-freedom are equal in everything, oligarchs assume unequals-in-wealth are unequal in everything — plus a parallel analysis of how tyranny is preserved.

## Summary

*Politics* Book V opens by tracing all *stasis* to a single root confusion about equality: "democracy arises out of the notion that those who are equal in any respect are equal in all respects... oligarchy is based on the notion that those who are unequal in one respect are in all respects unequal." Aristotle then analyzes revolution through three lenses: the underlying *feeling* (a desire for equality or for superiority), the *motives* (gain, honor, or avoidance of dishonor/loss), and the specific *occasions* that trigger action. General causes include insolence (*hubris*) and avarice of magistrates, which breed conspiracy "against one another and also against the constitution from which they derive their power"; honor-seeking; fear; contempt; and — his most structurally interesting cause — the disproportionate growth of one part of the state relative to the whole, likened to a body part outgrowing the body (the "four-cubit foot"), which can topple a constitution with no external shock at all. He stresses that trivial-seeming occasions (marriage quarrels, inheritance disputes, personal grievances) have triggered major overthrows, since "an error at the beginning" propagates through the whole system. Constitution-specific causes follow: democracies typically fall through the intemperance of demagogues who persecute the rich until the rich combine in self-defense (and demagogue-generals have historically become tyrants); oligarchies fall either through oligarchic oppression provoking a popular champion, or through internal rivalry among oligarchs "playing the demagogue" toward the masses or each other; aristocracies fall when too few share in honors, when high-spirited citizens judge themselves the rulers' equals, or under extremes of wealth and poverty (often war-induced). Preservation requires guarding against small illegalities, maintaining proportion, avoiding disproportionate honor to any citizen, and keeping magistrates from personal profit. The book closes with Aristotle's contrast of two strategies for preserving a tyranny: the classic oppressive method (eliminate the eminent, forbid common meals/clubs/education, deploy spies, impoverish subjects, sow mutual distrust, keep subjects incapable of concerted action) versus the "kingly" simulation strategy — appearing moderate, fiscally accountable, pious, dignified, and protective of both rich and poor, so as to rule as "a steward and a king," "half virtuous," rather than as an outright tyrant.

## Key Claims / Positions

- All *stasis* traces to the misapplication of one legitimate form of equality (proportional or numerical) as if it were the only relevant kind, in every respect.
- Revolution has an analyzable structure — feeling, motive, occasion — that lets it be diagnosed and, in principle, forestalled.
- Disproportionate growth of a single part of a constitution (a class, an office, a faction) is an internally generated threat independent of any hostile trigger.
- Small, apparently trivial disputes are frequently the proximate occasions of large-scale constitutional collapse, because early errors compound.
- Tyranny can be prolonged either by classic terror tactics or, more durably, by simulating legitimate, moderate kingship — the two are treated as genuinely distinct strategies with different degrees of success.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Vs. [[Thinkers/Plato]]**: Aristotle explicitly criticizes Plato's cyclical theory of regime decay in the *Republic* (Book VIII's fixed numerological sequence) as insufficiently empirical, preferring his own causally pluralistic, comparative-historical account grounded in observed cases (Sparta, Syracuse, Persia, etc.) — a further instance of the empiricist/rationalist methodological split with Plato.
- **Downstream**: the "moderate tyrant" strategy anticipates later analyses (e.g., in Machiavelli's *Prince*) of the difference between rule by fear and rule by simulated legitimacy; the disproportionate-growth-of-a-part diagnosis anticipates modern concerns (echoed in [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]] and in [[Sources/Teachout - Break Em Up]]'s anti-monopoly analysis) about concentrated economic or factional power destabilizing a political order from within.
