# Harm Principle (Mill)

> John Stuart Mill's primary principle of political and social liberty, formulated in *On Liberty* (1859). It asserts that the sole justification for state or social coercion over an individual's liberty is to prevent harm to other people; an individual's own physical or moral good is never a sufficient warrant for interference.

## Core Tenets

**Scope of the Principle**:
- Mill divides human actions into two categories:
  1.  **Self-regarding actions**: Actions that affect only the actor (or others who consent). These must be completely free from state or social interference.
  2.  **Other-regarding actions**: Actions that cause direct harm to others. Only these actions may be subject to legal regulation or social disapproval.

**Rejection of Paternalism and Moralism**:
- The principle explicitly forbids:
  - **Paternalism**: Coercing someone "for their own good" (e.g. seatbelt laws or drug bans, in their classical interpretation).
  - **Moralism**: Coercing someone because their actions are deemed offensive, sinful, or immoral by the community (e.g. blasphemy laws).
- "Over himself, over his own body and mind, the individual is sovereign."

**Freedom of Thought and Speech**:
- Mill argues for absolute freedom of thought and speech. Suppressing an opinion is an evil because it robs humanity: if the opinion is right, they are deprived of the opportunity of exchanging error for truth; if wrong, they lose the clearer perception of truth produced by its collision with error.
- The only limitation is when speech acts as a direct instigation to a mischievous act (e.g., telling an excited mob outside a corn-dealer's house that corn-dealers are starving the poor).

## In the Vault

- **Tyranny of the Majority**: The Harm Principle acts as a defense against the social tyranny and pressure to conform that Mill identifies as the greatest danger of modern democracies.
- **Contrast with Hobbes**: Hobbes' absolute sovereign has total control over individual speech and action to ensure security; Mill limits state interference strictly to prevention of harm.
- **Utilitarian Foundation**: Although a defense of liberty, Mill grounds the principle in *utility* in the largest sense, "grounded on the permanent interests of man as a progressive being," rather than abstract natural rights (Locke).
