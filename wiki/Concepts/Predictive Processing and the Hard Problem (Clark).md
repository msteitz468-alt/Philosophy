# Predictive Processing and the Hard Problem (Clark)
> Clark's deflationary bet: the hard problem of consciousness is "in some ways, a trick of the mind" — an artifact of our simplified, self-opaque self-models — that a science of the predicting brain will slowly dissolve rather than solve head-on.

## Summary

In the Interlude ("The Hard Problem — Predicting the Predictors?") Clark takes a **deflationist** (not eliminativist) stance: "it's not my view that conscious experience doesn't exist"; rather, "the hard problem we seem to confront is, in some ways, a trick of the mind" whose stumbling blocks are "more conceptual than scientific." His strategy is to attack David Chalmers's **meta-problem** first (*why* we are drawn to dualism and posit an explanatory gap); once that is solved, "the hard problem... will start to look different."

**Basic sentience**, Clark argues, is already largely accounted for by predictive processing: a creature is sentient when its neural world-model is in "constant two-way communication with a model of their own changing physiological state." "Bodily self-regulation, action, and temporal depth are... jointly necessary if there is to be conscious experience at all" — see [[Concepts/Interoception and Constructed Emotion (Clark)]].

The felt qualitative character is handled with Dennett's **"strange inversion"**: "the web of behaviors and responses comes first, and the puzzling, ineffable taste is really just a handy label for that web." "Tasting like honey is then simply the way I label things that predictably evoke, in me, that specific complex of (actual and possible) responses." Because we model ourselves with **simplified, self-opaque self-models** ("predicting the predictors") that lack access to our own inner processing, we *infer* that we host mysterious "qualia" — a "metaphysical inflation" of a useful shorthand. Predictions themselves should be treated as **"experientially neutral,"** which undermines the philosophical **zombie**: the more processing detail we add, the less imaginable a "full zombie" becomes. "Qualitative consciousness is real. But perhaps (just perhaps) it isn't exactly what we think."

## Key Claims / Positions

- Deflate, don't eliminate: consciousness is real; the *puzzle* is an artifact.
- Solve the meta-problem (why the gap seems unbridgeable) and the hard problem transforms.
- Self-opacity generates the illusion of intrinsic qualia.
- Sentience requires interoceptive self-modeling, action, and temporal depth.
- "I suspect that by doing a whole lot of science of this kind we will slowly dissolve the so-called hard problem."

## How This Relates to Prior Thinkers

- **[[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]**: Clark's primary ally and source (the "strange inversion," "Expecting Ourselves"; the position aligns with Keith Frankish-style illusionism, though Clark dislikes the label). This extends Dennett's [[Contradictions/Dennett vs Nagel - Multiple Drafts vs Subjective Experience|heterophenomenological, deflationary program]] with a predictive-processing mechanism.
- **Thomas Nagel & Joseph Levine** (cf. [[Thinkers/Ernest Nagel]]): the "what is it like" and "explanatory gap" formulations are treated as *conceptual* artifacts to be dissolved — Clark lands firmly on the Dennettian side of the vault's central consciousness contradiction.
- **David Chalmers**: Clark adopts Chalmers's meta-problem framing and his thought experiment of an agent with access only to end-products, but rejects Chalmers's conclusion that this "can't deliver the goods," and targets his zombie arguments. (Chalmers is also Clark's co-author on the [[Concepts/Extended Mind (Clark and Chalmers)|extended mind]] — allies on cognition, opponents on consciousness.)
- **[[Concepts/Emergence and Consciousness (Hofstadter)]]**: kindred deflation — Hofstadter's strange-loop self and Clark's self-opaque self-model both make the "I" an abstraction the system builds about itself.
- **[[Thinkers/John Searle]]**: opposed — where Searle insists consciousness has irreducible biological intrinsic features, Clark treats the sense of irreducibility as a self-modeling artifact.
