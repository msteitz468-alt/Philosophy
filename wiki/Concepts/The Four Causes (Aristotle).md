# The Four Causes (Aristotle)

> To know a thing is to know *why* it is — and "the why" comes in four irreducible kinds: the **material** (what it's made of), the **formal** (what it is, its essence), the **efficient** (what brought it about), and the **final** (what it is for). A complete explanation gives all four. *Metaphysics* I (Alpha) and V (Delta).

## Summary

Wisdom is knowledge of first causes ([[Concepts/Wisdom as Knowledge of First Causes (Aristotle)]]), and "causes are spoken of in four senses" (*aition* / *aitia*):

1. **Material cause** (*hylē*) — "the matter or substratum," that out of which a thing comes to be and persists in it (the bronze of the statue, the silver of the bowl).
2. **Formal cause** (*eidos*, *to ti ēn einai*) — "the substance, i.e. the essence," the form or definition that makes a thing what it is ("the 'why' is reducible finally to the definition"). See [[Concepts/Essence and Definition (Aristotle)]].
3. **Efficient cause** (*archē tēs metabolēs*) — "the source of the change" or of rest; the agent or mover (the sculptor, the father of the child, the adviser of the action).
4. **Final cause** (*to hou heneka*) — "the purpose and the good, for this is the end of all generation and change"; that *for the sake of which* (health is the final cause of walking).

Book V refines them: the same thing can be cause of contraries (the pilot's presence causes safety, his absence the wreck); causes can be *prior* or *posterior*, *per se* or *accidental*, *particular* or *universal*, *actual* or *potential*. Crucially, the four are **not interchangeable and not reducible to one another**, and "no science is complete" without grasping all that are relevant to its objects.

This doctrine doubles as Aristotle's **historiographical grid** for judging his predecessors (Book I): each earlier school, he argues, glimpsed one or two causes "vaguely, like one who lisps." The Milesians and atomists grasped only the **material** cause; [[Thinkers/Anaxagoras]] introduced an **efficient** cause (*nous*) but used it as a *deus ex machina*; [[Thinkers/Empedocles]] split the efficient cause into Love and Strife but applied them inconsistently; [[Thinkers/Plato]] reached the **formal** cause (the Forms) and a material principle ("the great and the small") — but **no one clearly identified the final cause** as a genuine principle of nature. Supplying the final cause (teleology) and clarifying the formal cause is Aristotle's signature contribution.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Four irreducible types of explanatory "why": material, formal, efficient, final.
- Formal and final cause often coincide with the **form/essence** ([[Concepts/Substance (Ousia) (Aristotle)]]); in living things the form *is* the end.
- **Teleology is real and central**: nature acts for ends; "that for whose sake both all mind and the whole of nature are operative."
- Causes cannot regress infinitely (Book II): there must be a first in each line, else explanation — and the Good — would be eliminated. This anticipates the first efficient/final cause, the [[Concepts/The Unmoved Mover (Aristotle)]].
- The doctrine is simultaneously a metaphysics of explanation and a yardstick for the doxography of Book I.

## How This Sits Among Thinkers

- **Versus atomist materialism**: reducing all causation to material-plus-mechanical (atoms and void) drops formal and final causation — the heart of [[Contradictions/Atomist Materialism vs Platonic-Aristotelian Teleology]].
- **The Scientific Revolution's reversal**: early-modern science ([[Thinkers/Descartes]], Galileo, [[Thinkers/Bacon]] tradition, [[Sources/The Origins of Modern Science - Herbert Butterfield]]) deliberately *expels final causes* from physics, keeping only material and efficient ("mechanical") causes. Aristotelian teleology survives in biology and is partly re-vindicated by Darwinian function-talk.
- **[[Thinkers/Hume]]**: the very notion of efficient causation/necessary connexion is challenged — [[Concepts/Necessary Connexion and the Two Definitions of Cause (Hume)]] reduces Aristotle's robust *aitia* to constant conjunction.
- **[[Thinkers/Judea Pearl]]**: the modern formal recovery of causation ([[Concepts/Ladder of Causation (Pearl)]], [[Concepts/Do-Operator and Do-Calculus (Pearl)]]) revisits interventionist (efficient) causation rigorously, while still bracketing final causes.
- **[[Thinkers/Spinoza]]**: explicitly *rejects* final causes as anthropomorphic illusion — a direct anti-Aristotelian move.
