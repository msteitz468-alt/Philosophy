# Moral Economy (Teachout)
> Teachout's positive vision for a post-monopoly economy: not merely a technically efficient market but a moral community in which economic structures support human agency, dignity, and political freedom — through decentralized ownership, worker co-ops, local banking, and medium-sized unionized firms.

## Summary

Teachout's final chapter moves from diagnosis (monopoly is tyranny) and strategy (anti-monopoly movement) to positive vision. The moral economy is not primarily defined by GDP or consumer welfare, but by whether economic structures create the *conditions for moral life* — agency, dignity, meaningful choice, and the capacity for solidarity.

## Key Claims

**Freedom requires structural support.** A worker cannot be free in the Aristotelian or republican sense if she is wholly dependent on one employer, surveilled, unable to speak, and without savings to exit a bad situation. "The ability to look someone else in the eye and say 'no thanks' to a job" requires competitive labor markets. A business owner who can say "the customer is not always right" has a kind of freedom that is both personally constitutive and politically enabling. Freedom is not a formal property of contracts — it is a structural condition of economic life.

**Moral action requires moral space.** Chickenized workers and franchise managers cannot exercise moral discretion — they follow scripts, protocols, and monitoring systems. Moral action, Teachout argues, requires the space to make choices, bear consequences, and be in genuine relationship with those affected. Large-scale financialization — where ownership is remote, mediated through spreadsheets, and maximizing-profit is the only legal obligation — destroys the conditions for moral agency at scale. "Compassion is extremely difficult at a distance in the best of circumstances."

**The positive institutional vision includes:**
- Worker-owned and producer-owned cooperatives (co-ops) at scale — not as exceptions but as the normal organizational form for many industries
- Medium-sized unionized firms with negotiated wages, benefits, and genuine collective bargaining
- Local banking and local entrepreneurship — capital that is embedded in communities and accountable to them
- An economy "started ground-up, instead of one that is dictated top-down by a few billionaires"

**Public morality is a muscle.** If workers are free in the workplace — grappling with union questions, making choices, having power — these habits of agency spill over into political life. Conversely, a person governed economically (while being told they are free) develops cognitive disarray and a tendency to disengage from politics or embrace nihilism. Political democracy requires economic democracy.

## Relationship to Anti-Monopoly Strategy

The moral economy is not the *result* of breaking up monopolies — it requires additional affirmative policy. Co-ops are currently outcompeted because monopolists squeeze them and Wall Street buys them. Building the moral economy requires: easier co-op organization law, local banking regulation, unionization rights, and limits on financialization — on top of anti-monopoly enforcement.

## Philosophical Connections

- **Aristotle:** The good life requires the material and social conditions for virtuous activity. A chickenized farmer or surveilled Uber driver cannot exercise the deliberative agency that Aristotle thought central to human flourishing. Teachout's moral economy restores those conditions. See [[Thinkers/Aristotle]].
- **Republicanism (Arendt, Madison):** Freedom as non-domination — the ability to act without being subject to the arbitrary will of another — requires structural conditions, not just the formal absence of explicit coercion. Hannah Arendt's "world held in common" (public realm enabling political speech and action) requires local economic nodes where people are genuinely free.
- **Wendell Berry** (epigraph): "The other kind of freedom is the freedom to take care of ourselves and of each other." Berry's agrarian localism resonates with Teachout's preference for embedded, community-scale economic structures over abstracted, financialized ones.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Teachout explicitly rejects being anti-market: she wants *competitive* markets, not socialist planning. But her moral economy vision (co-ops, local ownership, rejection of profit-maximization as the only valid corporate purpose) goes significantly beyond what conventional antitrust produces. The gap between "break up monopolies" and "build co-ops everywhere" is bridged by affirmative policy that Teachout endorses but does not fully develop.
- > [!warning] Connects to debates in political philosophy between market republicans (who think competitive markets produce freedom) and socialist republicans (who think wage labor is itself domination regardless of market structure). Teachout occupies the market-republican position but stresses co-ops as an alternative to wage labor.
