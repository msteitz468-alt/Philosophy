# Monad (Leibniz)

> The fundamental metaphysical concept of Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz's philosophy. A **Monad** (from the Greek *monas*, meaning unity) is a simple, indivisible, non-material substance that serves as the ultimate building block of reality. Unlike physical atoms, monads have no extension, shape, or divisibility; they are purely spiritual or mental centers of force and perception.

## Core Characteristics

**Indivisible and Immaterial**:
- Because monads are simple substances, they cannot be divided, put together, or dissolved. They cannot have any parts.
- Consequently, monads cannot begin or end naturally; they can only be created (by creation/creation out of nothing) or annihilated (by God's command) all at once.

**Windowless**:
- Monads are famously described by Leibniz as having "no windows, by which anything could come in or go out."
- There is no physical or causal interaction between different monads. A change in one monad cannot be caused by another monad. All changes in a monad arise from its own internal principle (inner development).

**Perception and Appetition**:
- Every monad is characterized by:
  1.  **Perception**: The internal representation of external things. Each monad mirrors the entire universe from its own particular point of view.
  2.  **Appetition**: The internal striving or desire that drives the monad to pass from one perception to another.
- Monads exist in a hierarchy based on the clarity of their perceptions:
  - *Bare Monads*: Have obscure, unconscious perceptions (like a dreamless sleep).
  - *Souls*: Bare monads with memory and sensation (animals).
  - *Minds/Spirits*: Souls with self-consciousness, reason, and access to eternal truths (humans).
  - *God*: The supreme monad, possessing completely clear, distinct, and infinite perceptions of all reality.

## In the Vault

- **The Pluralist-Monist Split**: Leibniz's monads represent a radical metaphysical pluralism (infinite substances), contrasting directly with Spinoza's monism (one substance, [Deus sive Natura](file:///home/mark/Documents/Philosophy/wiki/Concepts/Deus%20sive%20Natura%20-%20Substance%20Monism%20(Spinoza).md)) and Descartes' dualism.
- **Physical Atoms vs. Spiritual Monads**: Directly opposes the materialist atomism of Democritus and Lucretius ([Atomism - Atoms and Void](file:///home/mark/Documents/Philosophy/wiki/Concepts/Atomism%20-%20Atoms%20and%20Void%20(Leucippus%20and%20Democritus).md)). For Democritus, the base of reality is extended, blind matter; for Leibniz, it is non-extended, active mind/perception.
- **The Great Chain of Being**: The continuous hierarchy of monads (from bare monads to God) is a prime example of Arthur Lovejoy's principles of continuity and gradation in the history of ideas. See [[Sources/The Great Chain of Being - Arthur O. Lovejoy (1936)]].
