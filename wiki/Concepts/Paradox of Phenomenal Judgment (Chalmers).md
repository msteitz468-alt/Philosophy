# Paradox of Phenomenal Judgment (Chalmers)
> My zombie twin "spends ridiculous amounts of time hunched over a computer, writing chapter after chapter on the mysteries of consciousness" — yet has none; consciousness is explanatorily irrelevant to our claims about consciousness.

## Summary

*The Conscious Mind* ch. 5. **Phenomenal judgments** — Chalmers's deflationary, functional term ("what I and my zombie twin have in common") — come in three orders: first-order ("That's red!"), second-order ("I'm having a red sensation"), third-order ("Sensations are mysterious"). Since judgments and claims are functional states, they are reductively explainable in terms that "neither appeal to nor imply the existence of consciousness." Hence the paradox, generated by four premises Chalmers thinks are all probably true: physical causal closure; judgments supervene logically; consciousness doesn't; we know we are conscious. "The trick is to learn to live with the combination."

Indeed a reflective computational agent with direct access to its perceptual contents *would* say "I just see it" and puzzle over why red looks this way (citing Hofstadter and Winograd's SHRDLU): "Consciousness is surprising, but claims about consciousness are not." But explaining the judgments does not debunk consciousness, contra the God/UFO analogy — "consciousness is not an explanatory construct... it is a brute explanandum."

Defenses against the epistemic fallout: self-knowledge rests on **acquaintance**, not causation — "it is having the experiences that justifies the beliefs"; causal/reliabilist theories of knowledge, memory, and reference are rejected as the wrong models here (Shoemaker's argument answered; Siewert's "first-person warrant" cited). Experience even partly *constitutes* the content of qualitative concepts: inverted twins are physically identical yet differ in primary intension — "Somehow a sort of experience... is getting inside the concept and constituting its sense" (Wittgenstein's beetle-in-a-box invoked).

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]**: the chapter's main target — Dennett (1979): "There are the public reports... our judgments, and then there is—so far as introspection is concerned—darkness." Chalmers replies that Dennett describes *extrospection* and that *Consciousness Explained* trades on an ambiguity in "seem" (phenomenal vs dispositional).
- **Shoemaker**: turns the same zombie-possibility into an argument *for* reductive functionalism; Chalmers's acquaintance response is the hinge of the disagreement.
- **[[Thinkers/Douglas Hofstadter]]** ([[Sources/Gödel, Escher, Bach - Douglas Hofstadter (1979)]]): supplies the model of a self-monitoring system that would naturally make phenomenal judgments — used by Chalmers *without* the deflationary moral Hofstadter draws.
- This is the honest cost-accounting of [[Arguments/Zombie Argument (Chalmers)]] — the point deflationists press hardest (cf. [[Concepts/Predictive Processing and the Hard Problem (Clark)]]).
