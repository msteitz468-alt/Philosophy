# AI Hype Cycles (Mitchell)

> Melanie Mitchell (2019) describes the recurring pattern in AI: periods of excitement ("AI spring") fueled by new ideas or breakthroughs, leading to overpromising (imminent human-level AI, transformative applications), media hype, influx of funding/startups, followed by disappointment when breakthroughs are narrower/slower than claimed ("AI winter" — funding dries up, research slows, field gets bad reputation). This cycle has repeated roughly every 5–10 years since the field's founding. Mitchell uses it to contextualize current deep learning enthusiasm and warn against repeating past mistakes.

## Summary

From the book (history chapters):

Early example: 1950s Dartmouth conference optimism (McCarthy, Minsky, etc.) promising "every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it" within a summer (or generation). Led to funding but also early winters when general AI proved harder.

1960s–70s: Symbolic AI/expert systems hype (medical diagnosis, etc.) vs. limitations (brittleness, knowledge acquisition).

Perceptrons hype then Minsky/Papert critique + Lighthill report → major winter for neural nets in 1970s.

1980s: Connectionist revival (PDP book) + expert systems commercial boom → another bust when scaling/generalization issues hit.

1990s–2000s: "AI" term sometimes avoided in job apps due to stigma.

Mitchell notes the pattern: new technique (e.g., DL + big data + GPUs post-2010) creates optimism, promises (self-driving everywhere "soon," general AI), hype, investment (Google Brain, DeepMind acquisitions, etc.), then reality (impressive but narrow; limits in understanding/robustness) sets in.

At the 2014 Google meeting with Hofstadter, she saw the cycle in action: researchers confident, company mission "solve intelligence," but Hofstadter "terrified" by the pace and lack of caution.

The book is partly a response to hype: to inform "thinking humans" so expectations are realistic, avoiding boom-bust or harmful over-deployment.

## Key Claims

- Hype cycles are structural to AI due to the field's ambition + difficulty of general intelligence.
- Overpromising damages credibility, funding, and public trust when reality (slow, narrow progress) catches up.
- Current DL wave shows the pattern: real advances, but claims of imminent "human-level" or singularity often echo past overoptimism.
- "AI winter" is not inevitable if expectations are managed and fundamentals (common sense, etc.) addressed.
