# Zeno's Paradoxes of Motion (Zeno)

> The series of dialectical arguments devised by Zeno of Elea to prove that motion and change are logical self-contradictions.

## Summary

Zeno's paradoxes are designed to support Parmenides' monism by demonstrating that the common-sense view of the world—which assumes space and time are plural, and that motion is real—leads to insoluble logical contradictions. The paradoxes target two possible models of space and time: that they are **infinitely divisible** (continuous), or that they are composed of **discrete units** (indivisible points/instants). In either model, Zeno argues that motion cannot occur.

---

## The Four Primary Paradoxes of Motion

### 1. The Dichotomy (The Runner)
- **Premise**: Space is continuous and infinitely divisible.
- **Argument**: Before a runner can reach the end of a track, they must first travel half the distance. Before they can reach the half, they must travel a quarter, and so on *ad infinitum*.
- **Contradiction**: Traversing the track requires completing an infinite number of distinct segments. Since it is impossible to complete an infinite number of tasks in a finite time, the runner can never start or finish.

### 2. The Achilles and the Tortoise
- **Premise**: Space is infinitely divisible.
- **Argument**: Achilles, who runs ten times faster than a tortoise, gives the tortoise a 100-meter head start. To overtake the tortoise, Achilles must first run the 100 meters. By the time he arrives, the tortoise has crawled 10 meters. Achilles runs the 10 meters; the tortoise has crawled 1 meter.
- **Contradiction**: Achilles must first reach the point where the tortoise was. In each step, the tortoise moves ahead. Thus, the tortoise always retains a lead, and Achilles can never overtake it.

### 3. The Arrow
- **Premise**: Time is composed of discrete, indivisible instants.
- **Argument**: At any given instant of time, a flying arrow must occupy a space equal to its own dimensions. If it occupies a space equal to its dimensions, it is at rest at that instant (since it has no space to move into).
- **Contradiction**: If the arrow is at rest at every single instant of its flight, and time is composed of instants, the arrow is at rest throughout its entire flight. Motion is an illusion.

### 4. The Stadium (Moving Rows)
- **Argument**: Three rows of equal bodies stand in a stadium: Row A (stationary), Row B (moving right), Row C (moving left) at equal speeds. In the time Row B passes one body of Row A, it passes *two* bodies of Row C.
- **Contradiction**: The same movement takes a certain time relative to Row A, but half that time relative to Row C. If space and time are composed of indivisible units, this implies that an indivisible instant can be divided in half, which is a contradiction.

---

## Mathematical and Philosophical Resolution

- **The Crisis of the Infinite**: Zeno's paradoxes demonstrated that mathematics and physics must grapple with the nature of infinity and continuity.
- **The Limit Resolution**: Modern mathematics resolves the Dichotomy and Achilles paradoxes using the theory of infinite series and **limits** (calculus). A convergent infinite series (such as $\sum \frac{1}{2^n} = 1$) has a finite sum, proving that an infinite number of spatial intervals can be traversed in a finite time.
- **The Quantum Resolution**: Modern quantum mechanics (Planck length and time) suggests that physical space and time might indeed be discrete, meaning the continuous infinite divisibility assumed in the Dichotomy does not apply to physical reality.
