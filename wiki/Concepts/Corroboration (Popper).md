# Corroboration (Popper)

> "So long as a theory withstands detailed and severe tests and is not superseded by another theory in the course of scientific progress, we may say that it has 'proved its mettle' or that it is 'corroborated' by past experience." (Popper, *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*, §3). Corroboration is a *qualitative report* of a theory's survival under attempted refutation; it is not a probability, not verification, and not a measure of "truth" or "reliability" in the inductivist sense.

## Core Idea

After a hypothesis has been advanced and deductively tested:

- A *positive* decision (predictions borne out) provides only *temporary* support.
- The theory is said to be corroborated to the extent that it has withstood *detailed and severe* tests — especially risky tests that it was likely to fail if false, or tests that distinguish it from rivals.
- "Proved its mettle" (translation of *sich bewähren*) is deliberately non-probabilistic language. A well-corroborated theory has a good record of surviving attempts to overthrow it; this record can be reported and used in comparative evaluation, but it does not confer "probability" on the theory (universal laws typically receive zero probability on standard measures anyway).

Corroboration is always relative to the *severity* of the tests applied and to the background knowledge at the time. It is a historical report, not a timeless property or inductive projection.

## Distinctions from Related Notions

- **Vs. verification / confirmation**: No accumulation of positive instances ever verifies or makes a universal "more probable" in the required sense. Only attempted falsification can be decisive (negatively).

- **Vs. probability**: Popper argues at length (esp. Part II and appendices) that the logical probability of a universal law is zero under standard assumptions; using probability as "degree of confirmation" or "reliability" inherits the difficulties of induction. Corroboration is a different concept, tied to the methodology of severe testing.

- **Vs. pragmatic "truth happens" or satisfactoriness** (James et al.): Corroboration is specifically survival under deliberate critical attack within the rules of the empirical method, not "leading" satisfactorily in experience or being "expedient."

- **Degree of corroboration**: Can be compared across theories (a theory that has passed more severe tests, or tests that were riskier given its content, is better corroborated). Later work links this to verisimilitude (truthlikeness) as the aim: we prefer the theory that, while still fallible, has greater truth-content and less falsity-content.

## Role in the Overall Methodology

Corroboration is the positive side of the critical procedure:
- We *prefer* (tentatively) the theory that is better testable *and* better corroborated so far.
- We do not "establish" it or treat it as true/probable.
- When a better (more falsifiable, or better corroborated, or both) theory appears, the older one may be superseded even without direct falsification.
- The rule "once corroborated, a hypothesis may not be dropped without good reason" protects against arbitrary abandonment while still requiring openness to new criticism or superior alternatives.

## Additions from *Conjectures and Refutations* (Verisimilitude and the Three Requirements)

*C&R* ch. 10 supplies what the *LSD* starred notes only promised — the link from corroboration to the **aim** of science:

- **Corroboration tracks verisimilitude, not probability**: what we value in a theory is its **truthlikeness** ([[Concepts/Verisimilitude (Truthlikeness) (Popper)]] = truth-content minus falsity-content), and this is the *opposite* of probability (high probability = low content). A *bold, improbable, well-corroborated* theory is our best current conjecture at higher verisimilitude. Corroboration never *proves* greater verisimilitude, but a severely tested bold theory is the rational thing to prefer.

- **Three requirements for the growth of knowledge**: a good new theory should (1) proceed from a **simple, new, unifying idea**; (2) be **independently testable** — yield new (preferably new-kind) predictions, so it is not merely *ad hoc*; and (3) **pass some new, severe tests**. Requirements (1) and (2) are *formal* (assessable by logical analysis before testing) and *indispensable* for a theory to be worth examining; requirement (3) is *material* (empirical success) and, strikingly, **not indispensable** — a marvellous theory (Popper's example: Bohr–Kramers–Slater, 1924) may be refuted at its very first test and still be a real contribution. Corroboration is the favourable verdict on requirement (3).

This reframes corroboration as one moment in the **growth** of knowledge: bold unifying conjecture → independent testability → severe testing → (if it survives) corroboration → hoped-for gain in verisimilitude. (Cf. [[Concepts/Critique of Historicism and Dialectic (Popper)|the same trial-and-error schema applied to social knowledge]].)

## Relation to Other Vault Content

- **Hume / Problem of Induction**: Corroboration is the non-inductive replacement for "justification by experience." We do not project past conjunctions; we report that certain risky projections have not yet been overthrown.

- **Russell (Problems of Philosophy)**: Russell restates the chicken/induction problem and accepts an a priori inductive principle to assign rising probability. Popper offers corroboration (plus the demand for severity) as an alternative that stays within deductive logic and empirical testing.

- **Pragmatism (James)**: The language of "corroborate and verify" appears in James (Lecture VI), but Popper's version is tied to the critical method and asymmetry of falsification rather than processual leading or expediency.

- **Epicurus**: The ancient tolerance for multiple explanations "unless contradicted by sensation" is a weak, proto-version of preferring the account that has survived attempted refutation.

- **Vault / AI meta**: In the wiki, a claim or synthesis is "corroborated" when it has survived multiple source ingests, cross-checks, contradiction flagging, and user/agent criticism without being overturned. New sources act as potential falsifiers. "Severe tests" = deliberately seeking counter-evidence or rival interpretations. This is preferable to treating accumulated pages as inductively confirmed.

- **Career / risk**: A sales strategy or risk assessment is corroborated by surviving real-world tests (closed deals, loss ratios, client feedback) that were capable of refuting it. "Proved its mettle" language fits performance under pressure better than probabilistic language when sample sizes are small or conditions non-stationary.

## Open Questions / Later Developments

- How to measure "severity" or "degree of corroboration" formally? (Popper offers some theorems and comparisons; the *Postscript* and later papers develop this further, including the "theorem on truth-content.")
- Relation to verisimilitude: Corroboration of bold (highly falsifiable) theories is thought to track increase in verisimilitude better than corroboration of cautious ones.
- Sociological/historical objections: Scientists and communities often resist falsification ([[Concepts/Normal Science and Puzzle-Solving (Kuhn)|Kuhnian normal science]], protective belts in research programmes). Popper's methodology is normative ("how science *ought* to proceed if it is to be critical") rather than purely descriptive. Kuhn argues that requiring *severe* failure for rejection forces Popperians back into a "degree of falsification" / probability criterion — now primary-sourced; see [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]].
