# Contemplation and the Best Life - Theoria (Aristotle)

> Book X completes the treatise's account of pleasure — as a "whole" supervening on unimpeded activity, not a process or becoming — and concludes that the highest human happiness is *theōria*, the contemplative activity of reason (*nous*) grasping eternal truth: most self-sufficient, most continuous, most pleasant, and most akin to the activity of the divine. The practical life of moral virtue is happiness only "in a secondary degree." The book closes by arguing that virtue in most people requires habituation under law, transitioning explicitly to the *Politics* as the sequel inquiry.

## Summary

Returning to pleasure after Book VII's preliminary treatment, Aristotle argues pleasure is not a process (*genesis*) toward some further completion but is itself complete at any given instant, like seeing: "it does not lack anything which coming into being later will complete its form." Pleasure "completes" the activity it accompanies as a kind of supervenient bloom, differing in kind precisely as the underlying activities differ in kind, and arising most fully when both the perceiving faculty and its object are in their best condition. This sets up the treatise's final argument: since happiness is activity in accordance with the best, most complete virtue, and the highest human faculty is reason (*nous*) — "our natural ruler and guide," and either itself divine or the most divine element in us — the activity of reason contemplating eternal, unchanging truth (*theōria*) constitutes the most complete happiness available to a human being. Aristotle marshals several converging arguments: contemplation is the most continuous activity we are capable of sustaining; it is the most self-sufficient (needing no external means or partners, unlike virtuous action, which requires others to be just or brave toward); it is pursued for its own sake, with no further end beyond itself; it involves the most leisure (in the Greek sense of unforced, non-instrumental activity); and it is the activity most akin to what we ascribe to divine life, since a god's activity, being blessed, "must be a contemplative activity." In a striking exhortation, Aristotle urges that "we must not... think of human things, and, being mortal, of mortal things, but must, so far as we can, make ourselves immortal, and strain every nerve to live in accordance with the best thing in us." The practical, morally virtuous life — courage, justice, temperance, and the rest, all requiring engagement with others and worldly circumstance — remains genuinely happy, but "in a secondary degree," since its activities, however excellent, "befit our human estate" rather than express the most divine element within us. The treatise closes by conceding that argument alone cannot make most people good — "while [arguments] seem to have power to encourage and stimulate the generous-minded among our youth... they are not able to encourage the many to nobility and goodness" — so habituation, custom, and law are indispensable, and the *Ethics* explicitly hands off to a needed study of legislation and constitutions, becoming the direct prologue to the *Politics*.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Pleasure is a complete "whole," not a process**: like an instant of seeing, pleasure lacks nothing that further time would add to its form — it supervenes on and "completes" unimpeded activity rather than being a means to some further end (correcting and extending Book VII's preliminary treatment).
- **Distinct activities yield distinct kinds of pleasure**, and the measure of a pleasure's worth is the experience of the excellent (virtuous, well-formed) person, not the majority or the vicious.
- **Contemplation (*theōria*) is the supreme happiness**: activity of reason (*nous*) toward eternal truth, argued for via five converging features — greatest continuity, greatest self-sufficiency, pursuit for its own sake alone, greatest leisure, and closest likeness to divine activity.
- **"Make ourselves immortal... live in accordance with the best thing in us"**: reason, though perhaps "small in bulk" relative to the whole embodied person, "surpasses everything" in power and worth — the imperative to identify with and cultivate one's most divine capacity.
- **The practical/moral life is happiness in a secondary degree**: virtuous action (justice, courage, and the rest) genuinely constitutes eudaimonia, but a lesser, more human-scaled form than contemplative activity, since it depends on relations to others and worldly circumstance rather than being wholly self-sufficient.
- **Argument alone cannot produce virtue in most people**: only "the generous-minded among our youth" respond to rational exhortation; the many require habituation instilled by law and custom from early life — grounding the necessity of political science/legislation as the practical completion of ethical inquiry.
- **Explicit transition to the *Politics***: the *Ethics* concludes not with a final doctrine but with a research program — the study of existing constitutions and legislation needed to understand how virtuous character is actually produced and sustained in a community.

## How Aristotle Uses This

Completes and, in a way, revises the Book I account in [[Concepts/Eudaimonia]] (existing page, based only on Book I): where Book I identifies happiness generically with "activity of soul in accordance with virtue," Book X specifies that the *highest* such activity is specifically contemplative, creating explicit degrees of happiness (primary/contemplative vs. secondary/practical) not visible from Book I alone. This produces a real interpretive tension with Book VI's practical-wisdom-centered ethics (see [[Concepts/Practical Wisdom - Phronesis (Aristotle)]]) and with Book IX's insistence that even contemplative happiness requires friends to share it with (see [[Concepts/Friendship - Philia (Aristotle)]]) — scholars have long debated whether Aristotle's "two happinesses" (inclusive vs. dominant-end readings) can be fully reconciled; the vault records this as an open interpretive question rather than resolving it.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Internal interpretive tension (the classical "inclusivist vs. intellectualist" debate over Book X): does the contemplative life *include* the practical virtues as parts of a single complete happiness, or does it *supersede* them as a categorically higher good? The text supports both readings in different passages; flagged here rather than resolved, as a genuine scholarly crux rather than a wiki-page contradiction.
