# The One and the Many (James)

> One-sentence hook: Applying the pragmatic method to "the most central of all philosophic problems," James shows that the world's oneness and manyness are "absolutely coordinate" — true in as many definite respects as we can name, false in as many — so pragmatism must reject absolute monism and "range herself upon the pluralistic side."

## Overview

Lecture IV. James calls the one/many problem "the most central of all philosophic problems, central because so pregnant": "if you know whether a man is a decided monist or a decided pluralist, you perhaps know more about the rest of his opinions than if you give him any other name ending in 'ist.' To believe in the one or in the many, that is the classification with the maximum number of consequences." Yet "abstractly taken," bare oneness "is so vague as hardly to seem worth defending" — "why is 'one' more excellent than 'forty-three, or than two million and ten'?" The only way forward is pragmatic: "Granting the oneness to exist, what facts will be different in consequence? What will the unity be *known-as*? … how one?"

## The catalogue of unities (each coordinate with a manyness)

James enumerates the definite, *cashable* senses in which the world may be "one," noting that each holds only "just so far as" it obtains, and fails elsewhere:

1. **Unity of discourse** — at least one "subject of discourse": we can mean the whole by the word "universe." (But "a chaos has as much unity of discourse as a cosmos" — trivial.)
2. **Continuity** — parts "hang together" through space and time; "our whole motor life is based" on it.
3. **Lines of influence** — gravity, heat, electricity, acquaintance-networks ("Brown knows Jones…"), commercial/postal systems: "little hangings-together within the larger." But influence stops at "nonconductors"; choose wrong and "you are arrested at your very first step."
4. **Causal unity** — possibly one "first cause" or "eternal" creative act (the theistic/idealist option) vs. "an eternal self-existing many" (atoms). James leaves it "unsettled."
5. **Generic unity** — "things exist in kinds"; "the most momentous pragmatic specification" of oneness, since "logic works by predicating of the single instance what is true of all its kind." A world of pure singulars would make logic useless.
6. **Unity of purpose** — many things subserve common ends, but "our different purposes are at war"; the world "is incompletely unified teleologically and is still trying to get its unification better organized."
7. **Aesthetic union** — "things tell a story," fall into dramatic form; but "the world appears as something more epic than dramatic," "a rope of which each fibre tells a separate tale."
8. **The one Knower** — "the great monistic *denkmittel*": the Absolute as the all-enveloping knower for whom the many "form one system, tell one tale." James treats this as "an hypothesis, exactly on a par logically with the pluralist notion that there is no… focus of information… from which the entire content of the universe is visible at once." It has "practically driven out… 'Substance.'"

**Conclusion**: "The oneness and the manyness… are absolutely coordinate. Neither is primordial or more essential or excellent than the other." The world "is neither a universe pure and simple nor a multiverse pure and simple."

## Monism, mysticism, and the Absolute vs. the Ultimate

James argues that absolute monism's authority "draws its strength far less from intellectual than from mystical grounds" — "to interpret absolute monism worthily, be a mystic." He quotes the Vedantist **Vivekananda** ("there is no many"; "I am the One Existence of the universe… Kill out this superstition that there are many"), granting it "a high pragmatic value" — "a perfect sumptuosity of security" — while noting the "palpable weak places in the intellectual reasonings."

Decisively: absolute monism is "rigoristic" — "absolute unity brooks no degrees; as well might you claim absolute purity for a glass of water because it contains but a single little cholera-germ." "The independence, however infinitesimal, of a part… would be to the Absolute as fatal as a cholera-germ." **Pluralism** by contrast "has no need of this dogmatic temper": "provided you grant some… tremor of independence, some real novelty or chance, however minute, she is amply satisfied, and will allow you any amount… of real union." Hence "pragmatism, pending the final empirical ascertainment… must obviously range herself upon the pluralistic side."

A signature suggestion: the various unions may have "been successively evolved" — so "total oneness would appear at the *end* of things rather than at their origin… the notion of the 'Absolute' would have to be replaced by that of the 'Ultimate.'" Same maximal content, "time-relations positively reversed."

## Contrasts / Lineage

- **Vs. [[Concepts/Deus sive Natura - Substance Monism (Spinoza)]]** and **[[Concepts/The One (To Hen)]] / [[Thinkers/Plotinus]]**: the great monisms of the vault. Spinoza's one substance and Plotinus's One are precisely the "the many would not be, save for the One" structure James pragmatically declines to assert as necessary (granting it real but optional, "in so far forth," value).
- **Vs. the Absolute (Royce, Bradley, Hegel)**: James's running foil; he grants the Absolute its "moral-holiday" value (Lecture II) but rejects its "supernumerary features."
- **With [[Concepts/Emanation (Proodos)]] / [[Concepts/Return of the Soul (Epistrophe)]]**: James's "Ultimate" (oneness at the *end*, evolved) inverts the Neoplatonic procession-from-the-One; closer in spirit to a return/convergence than an origin.
- **With [[Concepts/Pragmatic Method (James)]]**: this is the method's showcase application — "what is the oneness *known-as*?" parallels the squirrel's "what do you practically mean?"
