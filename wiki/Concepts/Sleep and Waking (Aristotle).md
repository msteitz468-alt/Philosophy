# Sleep and Waking (Aristotle)

> Sleep is not the mere idling of the special senses but a periodic "tying up" of the single **primary/common sense-organ** (seated at the heart), caused by the upward evaporation of digested food and its cooling reflux. Waking and sleeping are contrary affections of one and the same subject — sense-perception — necessary to every animal because no natural power can be exercised without interruption.

## Summary

Aristotle asks what sleep and waking are and to which part of soul or body they belong. Since waking is the exercise of sense-perception and sleep its privation, and since perception is neither a purely psychic nor a purely bodily affair (it is an actuality of soul-through-body), sleep cannot belong to plants, which lack perception altogether, nor perpetually to any perceiving animal, since every functioning organ must eventually lose power from continuous use. The affection is located not in each special sense separately but in the **primary/common sense-organ**, closely associated with touch and seated, in blooded animals, at the heart — proven by the fact that all the senses fail together in sleep, which would be inexplicable if each sense went idle independently. Sleep is thereby distinguished from swoon, asphyxia, or unconsciousness, which are impotence arising from an external or casual cause rather than the periodic internal cause of true sleep. That internal cause is physiological: the warm evaporation released by digesting food rises to the head, cools, and sinks back down "like a tide-race in a narrow strait," overpowering the primary sense-organ and inducing the heaviness of drowsiness. Waking returns once digestion completes and the finer, purer blood separates from the coarser in the heart's central chamber. Sleep's final cause is conservation of the animal — rest is necessary because no capacity, however excellent, can function without limit.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Sleep and waking are contraries of a single subject (sense-perception), so whatever explains one explains the privation constituting the other.
- All animals sleep, since perception is definitional of animality and sleep is strictly correlative with the possession of perception.
- The affection is seated in one **common, controlling organ** (associated with touch, located at the heart), not distributed across the special senses — this explains the totality and simultaneity of sensory shutdown in sleep.
- Physiological/material cause: nutriment's evaporation rising and refluxing around the heat-source (heart/head axis); explains post-meal drowsiness, infant/dwarf sleep-proneness, narcotic and alcoholic effects, and epilepsy's affinity with sleep (both are seizures of the same organ).
- Final cause: conservation of the animal through periodic, necessary rest; waking (the exercise of perception or thought) remains the higher end.

## How Aristotle Uses This

Builds directly on the *De Anima* doctrine of the **common sense** as the single controlling perceptual faculty (see [[Concepts/Imagination and Common Sense (Aristotle)]] and [[Concepts/Theory of Perception (Aristotle)]]): sleep is presented as further evidence that a unified common organ, not a bundle of independent senses, underlies perceptual experience. It also supplies the physiological ground floor for the two treatises that follow it in the *Parva Naturalia*: dreaming is described here only in passing ("a dream is in a certain way a sense-impression") and is developed fully in [[Concepts/Dreams as Residual Sense-Movements (Aristotle)]].
