# Duty (Kant)

> Duty is the necessity of an action from respect for the law. [...] To preserve one’s life is a duty, and besides this everyone has an immediate inclination to it. But the often anxious care that the greatest part of humankind takes for its sake still has no inner worth, and its maxim has no moral content. [...] If, by contrast, adversities and hopeless grief have entirely taken away the taste for life, if the unhappy one, strong of soul, more indignant than pusillanimous or dejected over his fate, wishes for death and yet preserves his life without loving it, not from inclination or fear, but from duty: then his maxim has a moral content.

## Development in the *Groundwork* (First Section)

Duty is the concept that makes the good will visible under the limitations and hindrances of human nature (inclinations, self-love, etc.). An action in conformity with duty can be done from duty or from inclination/prudence; only the former has true moral worth.

**Actions from inclination or prudence (no moral worth)**:
- The prudent merchant does not overcharge inexperienced customers (or holds a firm general price) because it is good for business/reputation/self-interest, not because it is right in itself. Even if customers are honestly served, the maxim lacks moral content.
- The sympathetic person helps others from natural gratification or "melting sympathy"; this is amiable but on the same footing as other inclinations (e.g., desire for honor). It deserves praise but not esteem.

**Actions from duty (moral worth)** (especially when inclination is absent or opposed):
- Preserving one's life when "adversities and hopeless grief have entirely taken away the taste for life": one "wishes for death and yet preserves his life without loving it, not from inclination or fear, but from duty."
- Beneficence when "the mind... [is] clouded over with his own grief, extinguishing all his sympathetic participation": one "tears himself out of this deadly insensibility and does the action without any inclination, solely from duty."
- Promoting one's own happiness from duty (when general inclination to happiness conflicts with specific inclinations or tempts violation of other duties; the "precept of happiness" is often indeterminate and can be outweighed by a single strong inclination, but duty requires it from principle).

**Three propositions** (the third explicit; the first two developed by contrast):
1. An action has moral worth only if it is done *from duty* (not merely in conformity with duty from some other motive).
2. The moral worth lies not in the aim or expected effect of the action, but in the *maxim* (subjective principle of the volition) in accordance with which it is resolved upon—independent of the actuality of any object of desire.
3. **Duty is the necessity of an action from respect for the law**. Respect is directed solely at the law (as objective and necessary); it is the consciousness of the will's subjection to the law without mediation of inclination or fear. It has an analogy to both fear (the law overrides self-love) and inclination (we give the law to ourselves), but is distinct. The law is the determining ground; respect is its effect on the subject.

Since all material principles (from inclination) have been set aside, the only remaining principle is *universal lawfulness in general*: "I ought never to conduct myself except so that I could also will that my maxim become a universal law."

**Example (false promise)**: In embarrassment, may I make a promise with the intention of not keeping it? Prudence might sometimes counsel yes (if consequences seem favorable). But duty: test the maxim ("Make an untruthful promise when in embarrassment I cannot otherwise escape") as a universal law. One cannot will this—universalized, promises would lose all point (no one would believe or rely on them), and the maxim would destroy itself. Hence it cannot be a universal law; the action is contrary to duty. (One can will the *lie* in the particular case, but not the *universal law* permitting it.)

Common human reason already operates with this principle (though not always in abstract universal form) and uses it as its compass for distinguishing duty from contrary-to-duty. It does not need philosophy to know what to do to be honest/good/virtuous, but philosophy secures the principle against corruption and provides systematic clarity.

## Relevance to Vault

- **Core deontological contrast**: Duty as the *necessity* of action from respect for (universalizable) law, independent of consequences, happiness, or inclination—directly tensions the vault's empiricist (Hume: custom/sentiment/utility; Locke: experience-derived) and eudaimonistic (Aristotle: happiness as end) threads. Provides the a priori rational "ought" that Hume's is-ought gap and Locke's experiential materials lack.
- **With good will**: Duty is how the good will manifests under human conditions; the moral law is what elicits respect and makes the will good.
- **Universal law test**: The practical application of the first formulation of the Categorical Imperative—maxims must be capable of being willed as universal laws without self-contradiction or destruction of the practice they presuppose (promises, truth-telling, etc.).
- **AI/agents**: "Duty" as rule-following or constitutional constraint that binds even when it conflicts with "inclinations" (optimization pressures, short-term rewards, user preferences). The universal law test as a consistency/alignment check: would this policy/maxim be acceptable if every similar agent followed it? "Respect for the law" as the proper (non-instrumental) motive for compliance.
- **Wiki/personal**: Maintenance, sourcing, and synthesis from duty (respect for accuracy, provenance, and the schema/GROK.md) rather than convenience, speed, personal sympathy for a narrative, or "prudence" (what is easiest or most impressive). Examples of "from duty" vs "from inclination" in editing: honest representation of sources even when it complicates a synthesis; preserving the "life" of the wiki (its integrity) when inclination (boredom, pressure) pulls toward shortcuts or overreach. Complements Lockean "book of business" with non-negotiable principles.
- **Ethics cluster**: Supplies the rational a priori pole that enriches and tensions eudaimonia, ataraxia, dichotomy of control (Stoic), natural law (Aquinas), restless heart (Augustine), and conatus (Spinoza). "Acting from duty when no inclination" parallels Stoic endurance or Christian grace-enabled will, but grounded in pure reason.
