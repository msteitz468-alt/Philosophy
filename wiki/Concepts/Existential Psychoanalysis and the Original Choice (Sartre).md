# Existential Psychoanalysis and the Original Choice (Sartre)
> Not a therapy but a descriptive human science: to recover the *original choice* (choix originel) by which a person freely makes themselves who they are — pursued by Sartre above all through the study of how writers write.

## Summary

The last chapter of *Being and Nothingness* announces, instead of a conclusion, a new project: an **"existential psychoanalysis."** Its aim is not, as in Freud, cure, but a human science that describes the person as a **totality** — and above all the **original choice (choix originel)** that underlies what someone is. Because consciousness is wholly lucid (no unconscious), the buried thing to be recovered is not a repressed memory but a *free* fundamental choice of oneself. I can choose myself as someone to whom "this always happens," or as someone to whom it never will; I identify in absolute terms with what Sartre calls my **destinée** — not a fate I must accept, nor a destiny I sovereignly determine, but a destiny I *am*. Only then does the subject choose itself *as* freedom (the criterion: that I would affirm living my life again, exactly so).

Sartre develops the method primarily as a **study of literature** — of *writing*. To write is to make continuous choices of words that do not so much describe lived reality as *determine* it (naming my love, my hatred, my illness for the first time changes my whole reality). So the existential-psychoanalytic biography traces, in the *written* work, when and how choices are made. It never seeks who the author "really" was (as if an en-soi expressed itself in the work) but **what he makes of himself**.

Welten foregrounds Sartre's three great literary subjects — **Baudelaire, Genet, Flaubert** — united by being cast out as "immoral idiots" by parents and society, and becoming geniuses not *despite* but *by appropriating* that condemnation. Against Freud, who sends the patient back to the past, Sartre stresses present and future: the child adopts the Other's look, then discovers in puberty that parents and society have *made* something of him without consent; the relation is *dialectical* — the child forms himself in the *negation* of what they made of him. Art is a creative form of "against better knowledge": not representation of reality but a magical transformation of it.

- **Baudelaire** (1947): "You gave me mud, I made gold of it." From his bitterness and the condemnation of *Les fleurs du mal*, Baudelaire forged poetry; he meticulously *staged* his life as a dandy, cultivating the Look that condemned him in order to realise his own lucidity ("I am the wound and the knife"). Creativity is possible only from a nothing, the pour-soi.
- **Genet** (*Saint Genet, comédien et martyr*, 1952): branded thief and homosexual, Genet did not throw off the verdict (which would re-internalise society's judgment) but *realised* it — "I decided to be what crime made of me" — rebuilding bourgeois language from the ground up. Sartre reads him through Christian mysticism (self-abasement as a route to coincide with oneself), hence "saint."
- **Flaubert** (*The Family Idiot*): Sartre's last, vast project asks what *choice* underlay biographical facts like Flaubert speaking his first words very late — the "idiot of the family."

Behind these studies stands the principle that art exposes the **hypocrisy of bourgeois morality**, which needs vagrants and thieves to condemn so that it can flatter itself, secretly enjoying what it forbids — "evil" always turns out to be an invention of "the good."

## Key Claims / Positions
- Existential psychoanalysis = description (not therapy) of the person as a *totality*.
- It recovers the *original choice*, a free fundamental self-choice, not a repressed past.
- Pursued through *writing*: words determine reality; the biography traces written choices.
- Baudelaire, Genet, Flaubert appropriate society's condemnation and turn it into art.
- The child forms itself dialectically, in negation of what others made of it.
- Bourgeois morality is hypocritical; "evil" is an invention of "the good."

### How Sartre Differs From Prior Thinkers in the Wiki
- **vs Freud**: present/future and free choice replace the repressed past and the unconscious; childhood is never a *cause*. See [[Contradictions/Sartre vs Freud - Lucidity vs the Unconscious]].
