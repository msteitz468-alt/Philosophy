# Center of Narrative Gravity (Dennett)

> Dennett's theory of the self (Ch. 13 of *Consciousness Explained*, 1991): the self is an abstraction — like the center of gravity in classical mechanics — useful for organizing attributions and predictions, but not a concrete neural entity ("brain-pearl" or "soul-atom"). Humans spin narrative webs in language; the self is the abstract point that serves as the focus of those narratives. It is real as an abstraction; it is not real as a homunculus inside the brain.

## The Analogy to Center of Gravity

In classical mechanics, "center of gravity" is an extremely useful concept: it lets you calculate trajectories, predict behavior, design machinery. But there is no physical point in an object *that is* the center of gravity. The center of gravity is a useful fiction — a mathematical abstraction derived from the distribution of mass. It is real in the sense that it predicts real phenomena; it is not real in the sense of being a discrete physical thing at a location.

The self is the same kind of abstraction. It is real in the sense that attributing a self to a person generates true predictions about behavior, responsibility, preferences, plans, and autobiography. It is not real in the sense of being a discrete neural module or soul-atom located somewhere in the brain.

## Narrative Webs as the Extended Phenotype

Humans are story-telling animals. We construct narratives about ourselves — autobiography, self-conception, plans, rationalizations, self-reports — continuously and compulsively. The self is the abstract center of gravity of this narrative web.

Dennett draws an evolutionary comparison:
- Spiders spin silk webs (evolved tool use, extending the phenotype into the environment).
- Beavers build dams.
- Bowerbirds construct elaborate structures.
- Humans spin **narrative webs in language**.

The narrative web is the extended phenotype of *Homo sapiens* — just as much a biological product as the spider's silk, but implemented in language and cultural transmission. The self is what the narrative is *about* — the protagonist of the autobiography.

## Memes and the Virtual Self

The narrative web is also a product of meme-complex installation (see [[Concepts/Joycean Machine and Memes (Dennett)]]). The specific narrative structures through which we understand our selves — biographical coherence, personal identity over time, moral responsibility — are culturally transmitted memes, not hardwired neural structures. This means selves are partly cultural artifacts, not purely biological ones.

## Multiple Personality Disorder

MPD (Multiple Personality Disorder, now DID) provides a natural experiment. A person with MPD has two or more distinct selves — each with its own autobiography, preferences, voice, and behavioral repertoire, and often with no cross-access to the other's memories.

Under the Cartesian Theater view, this is deeply puzzling: how can one brain house two selves? Under the narrative gravity view, it is no more puzzling than one brain housing one self. Two selves per body is no more metaphysically extravagant than one self per body — each is an abstraction, a narrative focus point; having two such focus points is structurally no different from having one.

This also suggests that personal identity is not a simple biological fact but a narrative achievement that admits of degrees and complications.

## Split-Brain Patients

Patients whose corpus callosum has been severed (split-brain surgery) show brief bifurcations of narrative self-attribution — left and right hemispheres can produce incompatible accounts of the same action. These are not two sustained selves (as in MPD) but momentary bifurcations of a normally unified narrative center. They confirm that the unity of the self is a produced achievement (by the Joycean narrative machine) rather than a brute neural fact.

## Substrate Independence and the Information Self

The narrative-gravity account implies the self is substrate-independent. What makes you *you* is not the specific neurons in your brain but the pattern — the narrative structure, the web of beliefs, memories, values, and dispositions. If that pattern could be instantiated in silicon, it would be you (or a you — there are hard questions about branching).

Dennett's formulation: "If what you are is the program that runs on your brain's computer, then in principle you could survive the death of your brain, provided the program is preserved and implemented in another medium." This is a functionalist view of personal identity.

## Relation to Ethics and Minding

The narrative-gravity account grounds ethics without invoking mysterious qualia. Moral standing tracks the complexity and integration of the narrative self: a being capable of caring about its future, attributing its past to itself, and constructing plans is morally significant regardless of whether it has intrinsic phenomenal properties. This is consistent with the [[Concepts/Qualia Disqualified (Dennett)|Qualia Disqualified]] argument: suffering requires sufficient cognitive complexity (thwarted desires), not special phenomenal stuff.
