# Ethics Without Morality (Deleuze-Spinoza)
> Deleuze reads Spinoza as replacing morality (judgment by transcendent values) with ethics (a typology of immanent modes of existence) — and politics as the strategic use of sad passions to maintain obedience.

## Summary

Spinoza's Ethics, as Deleuze reads it, makes two foundational moves against morality:

**1. The body as philosophical model.** "We do not know what the body can do" — this ignorance is the critique of morality's claim to govern through consciousness. Consciousness is structurally illusory: it registers only effects (results of power), never causes, generating three interlocking illusions: final causes, free will, and theological transcendence.

**2. Good/bad replaces Good/Evil.** "Good" and "Evil" are transcendent moral categories referring action to an external judge. "Good" and "bad" are relational-immanent terms: good = a relation of movement that composes with mine and increases my power of acting; bad = a relation that decomposes my body and diminishes power. This is *ethology*, not morality — the study of what a body can and cannot do.

The result: "ethics, which is to say a typology of immanent modes of existence, replaces morality, which always refers existence to transcendent values. Morality is the judgment of God, the system of judgment."

**Joy as the practical criterion.** Sad passions (resentment, guilt, pity, fear) are the practical enemy — they diminish power and reinforce the illusions that sustain oppressive institutions. "Sad passions" are the political instruments of despotism: "In despotic statecraft, the supreme and essential mystery is to hoodwink the subjects, and to mask the fear, which keeps them down, with the specious garb of religion." Joy and active affection are the practical aim.

The **ethics of the event** (from the Stoics, Essay 8) extends this framework: the imperative is not "do not be unworthy of what happens to us" as passive resignation, but **counteractualization** — willing the event in its eternal dimension rather than suffering it as personal accident. The free person gathers all violence into a single Event, which breaks with resentment. "Either ethics makes no sense at all, or this is what it means and has nothing else to say: not to be unworthy of what happens to us."

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Kant]]:** Kant's categorical imperative is still a form of moral law referring action to a universal legislative form. Deleuze/Spinoza reject any transcendent criterion — the test is immanent (what does this do to my power of acting?).
- **[[Thinkers/Nietzsche]]:** Near-parallel: Nietzsche also distinguishes good/bad (noble assessment of power) from good/evil (slave resentment). Deleuze explicitly links Spinoza and Nietzsche here. The difference: Nietzsche's critique is genealogical (historical origin of values); Spinoza's is ontological (parallel of mind and body, conatus).
- **[[Thinkers/Plato]]:** Platonic ethics grounds good action in imitation of or participation in the Good (transcendent). Deleuze's Spinoza makes the Good itself an immanent composition of forces — eliminating the vertical axis entirely.
- **[[Thinkers/Aristotle]]:** Aristotle's ethics aims at eudaimonia (flourishing of the natural form of human being). Deleuze's Spinoza rejects any teleological form — the "plan" is not given by nature but constructed on the plane of consistency.
- **[[Concepts/Affects and Active vs Passive Emotions (Spinoza)]]:** This concept page carries the direct Spinoza material; the present page carries Deleuze's *transformation* of Spinoza into an anti-morality framework.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] If "good" is defined as what composes with my power, the criterion seems to risk egoism — what composes with one body may decompose another. Deleuze acknowledges this but argues that joyful compositions tend toward common notions and social cooperation. The argument remains schematic.
