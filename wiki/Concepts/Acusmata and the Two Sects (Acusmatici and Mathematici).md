# Acusmata and the Two Sects (Acusmatici and Mathematici)
> The *acusmata* ("things heard") are the cryptic ritual sayings and taboos at the religious core of early Pythagoreanism; the split between their keepers (*acusmatici*) and the pursuers of *mathēmata* (*mathematici*) is the historical fault line Burkert uses to expose how "Pythagoras the scientist" was manufactured.

## What the Acusmata Are
The *acusmata* (later called *symbola*) are short revealed precepts — taboos and oracular rules — transmitted on the authority of the Master (the famous *autos epha*, "He himself said it"). Examples Burkert discusses: "do not eat the heart" (= do not eat yourself up with grief), abstentions from certain fish and from beans, and rules of ritual purity. In [[Sources/Lore and Science in Ancient Pythagoreanism - Walter Burkert (1972)]] Burkert shows that **Aristotle** is the only early source that uses the word *acusma*, and Aristotle treats them historically — pointing to parallels in popular belief, comedy, and "barbarian" survivals — *before* the later allegorical machinery (Androcydes, Clement) turned them into riddles "clothing a lofty wisdom."

Crucially, *symbolon* originally meant a mystery-religion **password** (a token by which initiates recognize one another and are known to the gods), not a "symbol" to be decoded. The allegorizing reinterpretation — which made the silly-sounding taboos respectable — is a *later* rescue operation, evidence that the precepts began as ritual, not philosophy.

## The Two Sects
Iamblichus preserves (in *De communi mathematica scientia*, his more faithful version) a report that there were **two kinds of Pythagoreans**:
- **Acusmatici** — keepers of the acusmata; *incontestably* recognized as Pythagoreans by all.
- **Mathematici** — devoted to *mathēmata* (number, harmonics, astronomy). The acusmatici denied that the mathematici's activity came from Pythagoras at all, attributing it instead to the innovator **Hippasus**. The mathematici retorted that *they* were the truer Pythagoreans — "everything is due to *that man*" (Pythagoras).

## Burkert's Key Move: Iamblichus' Reversal
In his *Life of Pythagoras*, Iamblichus tells the **opposite** story — that the *mathematici* are the uncontested Pythagoreans and the acusmatici the breakaway sect. Burkert demonstrates this is Iamblichus' own alteration: for a Neoplatonist, the rigorous *mathēmata* simply *had* to be Pythagoras' core, so when his source said otherwise "he can only believe his eyes have deceived him, and quickly switch the two nouns." The self-consistent *Comm. math. sc.* version is therefore primary. This single editorial tell is load-bearing for the whole book: it shows the tradition *actively inverting* the evidence to put science first.

## Zhmud's Demolition: a Late Fiction (Zhmud 2012)
[[Sources/Pythagoras and the Early Pythagoreans - Leonid Zhmud (2012)]] mounts the sharpest possible attack on this entire framework — for Burkert the *key* to the Pythagorean question, for Zhmud an artefact of late sources:
- **"Acusmata" is not Pythagorean terminology.** The word was *coined by Iamblichus*; the whole prior tradition (including Porphyry) says *symbola*. "*akousmatikoi*" first appears in Clement of Alexandria; the noun "*mathēmatikoi*" post-dates Plato (Archytas said *hoi peri mathēmata*, "those concerned with the sciences").
- **The two-sects story is an Imperial-age invention.** Source-criticism traces every version (Clement, Porphyry, both of Iamblichus') back to **Nicomachus of Gerasa** (1st–2nd c. AD), with the pseudo-Pythagorean **Lysis letter**; "even if an earlier source existed, the search would be unlikely to take us back further than the first century AD." Quite possibly Nicomachus invented it. (So **Burkert's "Iamblichus reversed his source" move** loses its force: on Zhmud's view the source itself is late, and Iamblichus additionally reshuffled the groups and inserted the symbols and Hippasus material.)
- **The acusmatici cannot be found.** Since the community was a political *[[Schools/Pythagoreanism|hetairia]]*, the symbols were never a binding code: no named Pythagorean (Alcmaeon → Archytas → Xenophilus) is ever shown observing a single taboo, and comedy mocked Pythagorean *philosophy/science*, never superstitious ritualism. No source attaches reward or punishment to (non)observance — proof they were *sayings*, not rules.
- **The symbols are folklore for allegorical exegesis, not a rule of life or a password.** Against Burkert's "frightening constriction of one's freedom of action," Zhmud holds they were "religiously coloured folklore... interesting to interpret, but not obligatory to observe." Their literary life begins with **Anaximander of Miletus the Younger** (*Exegesis of Pythagorean Symbols*, c. 400) — an allegorical interpreter Aristotle used. And Zhmud denies they functioned as mystery **passwords** (contra Burkert/Müri): the Pythagoreans had no mysteries, and dozens of sayings cannot be passwords.
- **The comic "Pythagorists"** (Middle Comedy, c. 350–320, Athenian stage only) are reworkings of the stock comic Socrates/Cynic plus vegetarianism — their one real prototype being Diodorus of Aspendus, who merely *played* the Pythagorean. They are not evidence of living acusmatici.

> [!warning] This page's "Two Sects" framing is **Burkert's**, and Zhmud rejects it as a late construction. The split is preserved here because it remains central to the historiography — but read it as contested. See [[Contradictions/Pythagoras the Scientist vs the Religious Charismatic]].

## Significance
- The science-bearing *mathematici* were, on the early evidence, the **junior**, contested strand — not the original Pythagoras. This is the documentary spine of [[Contradictions/Pythagoras the Scientist vs the Religious Charismatic]].
- The acusmata anchor Pythagoras in [[Concepts/Metempsychosis (Transmigration of Souls)]] and ritual life, not in proof or measurement.
