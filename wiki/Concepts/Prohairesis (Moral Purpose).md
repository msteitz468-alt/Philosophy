# Prohairesis (Moral Purpose)

> The faculty of rational choice — assent, desire, aversion, and impulse — which for Epictetus is the *only* thing genuinely our own, and therefore the sole seat of good, evil, and freedom.

## Summary

*Prohairesis* (variously rendered will, moral purpose, choice, faculty of will) is the organizing concept of Epictetus's *Discourses*. Where the *Enchiridion* states the practical rule — distinguish what is in our power from what is not — the Discourses supply its metaphysical ground: what is "in our power" (*ta eph' hēmin*) just *is* the prohairesis and its activity. Everything else — body, property, reputation, office, our own limbs, even life — is "not our own" (*ta ouk eph' hēmin*) and hence morally indifferent. Good and evil reside nowhere but in the disposition of the will:

> "The essence of good and evil is a certain disposition of the will. What are things outward then? Materials on which the will may act, in attaining its own good or evil." (Discourses I.29)

Because the will alone is unhindered by anything external, it is the one invincible thing:

> "You will fetter my leg; but not Zeus himself can get the better of my free will." (I.1)

Tyrants, chains, exile, and death reach only the body and externals; they cannot touch the prohairesis unless it surrenders itself by a false judgment. The whole Stoic discipline is therefore the guarding and right-tuning of this one faculty.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **The dichotomy is grounded in prohairesis.** The line between what is and is not "up to us" is not arbitrary: it tracks exactly what the faculty of choice can and cannot control. This is why [[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)]] is not merely a coping heuristic but a claim about the self.
- **The self is the will, not the body.** "Thou art a little soul carrying a corpse" (Fragments); to identify with body or possessions is to mislocate oneself among things that are "another's."
- **Good and evil are internal only.** Externals are *hylē* — raw material — neither good nor bad in themselves; they become good or evil only through the will's use of them.
- **Freedom is a property of the will.** To be free is to desire only what the prohairesis controls, so that nothing can compel or frustrate one — see [[Concepts/Stoic Freedom (Eleutheria)]].
- **Moral progress (*prokopē*) is the training of the will**, not intellectual mastery of doctrine: "Progress means aligning will to nature daily; reading Chrysippus without this is worthless." (I.4)

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Epictetus** makes prohairesis the technical centre of Stoic ethics — arguably his single most original emphasis within the school.
- **[[Thinkers/Marcus Aurelius]]** inherits it directly as the "ruling faculty" (*hēgemonikon*) and inner citadel; the *Meditations* are prohairesis-discipline in private practice (see [[Concepts/Ruling Faculty and the Daemon (Marcus Aurelius)]], [[Concepts/The Inner Citadel (Hadot)]]).
- **[[Thinkers/Kant]]** offers a structural parallel and a contrast: like Epictetus he locates moral worth in the will alone ("nothing can be called good without qualification except a good will"), but grounds it in autonomous rational law-giving rather than in accord with a providential cosmic nature. See [[Concepts/Good Will (Kant)]], [[Concepts/Autonomy and Heteronomy of the Will (Kant)]].
- **[[Thinkers/Nietzsche]]** inverts the valuation: for him the Stoic "will what happens" is a disguised weakness, a making-a-virtue of impotence before necessity — contrast with [[Concepts/Will to Power (Nietzsche)]] and [[Concepts/Amor Fati (Love of Fate)]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] Freedom of prohairesis within a fully determined, providential cosmos raises the Stoic compatibilism problem — see [[Contradictions/Stoic Determinism vs Aristotelian Contingency]]. Epictetus insists the will is genuinely unhindered even though all external events are fated by Zeus.
