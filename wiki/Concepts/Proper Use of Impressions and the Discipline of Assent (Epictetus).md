# Proper Use of Impressions and the Discipline of Assent (Epictetus)

> The one power Zeus placed wholly in our hands — to test each impression (*phantasia*) before assenting to it, since "men are disturbed not by things, but by their opinions about things."

## Summary

For Epictetus the human faculty par excellence is **the right use of impressions** (*chrēsis tōn phantasiōn*). Impressions — the appearances that present themselves to the mind, including the value-judgments packed into them ("this is a disaster," "he has wronged me") — arrive unbidden. What is up to us is *assent*: whether to endorse the impression, withhold, or correct it. This is the site of philosophy:

> "This faculty of exerting the powers of pursuit and avoidance, of desire and aversion, and, in a word, the use of the appearances of things... [rightly used] thou wilt never be restrained, never be hindered." (Discourses I.1)

Because disturbance comes from the *judgment* added to an event, not the event, the discipline of assent is the lever on the whole emotional life. Logic and reasoning are therefore not idle — they are the assaying tools that distinguish true impressions from false, "as coinage assay distinguishes good coin" (I.17). But Epictetus repeatedly attacks logic pursued *for its own sake*, divorced from its purpose of governing conduct (I.17, II.19, III.24).

## Key Claims / Positions

- **The three moments:** impression → test → assent/withholding. Only the last is "ours." "Show the governing faculty of Reason a contradiction, and it will renounce it" (II.26) — the mind cannot knowingly assent to what it sees as false.
- **Judgment, not event, disturbs.** The Enchiridion's §5 ("men are disturbed not by things but by opinions") is here given its full apparatus: every troubling appearance is to be met with "test it" before "assent to it."
- **Logic is instrumental.** Reasoning is necessary as the criterion of true appearance, but is a means to right living, not an end; the vault's existing note on Epictetus warning against "logical subtleties" (I.8) belongs here.
- **The discipline of assent is the most advanced of the three fields.** In [[Concepts/The Three Fields of Training - Desire, Action, and Assent (Epictetus)]], assent (*sunkatathesis*) is reserved for the stable practitioner; beginners must first master desire.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Epictetus / Stoics**: the criterion is the *kataleptic* (cognition-grasping) impression; assent to it yields secure knowledge, assent to a false impression is the root of every passion.
- **[[Thinkers/Marcus Aurelius]]** practises it as stripping impressions to their bare description ("this is a dead fish, a dead bird") to drain them of false value — see [[Concepts/View from Above and Cosmic Perspective (Marcus Aurelius)]].
- **[[Thinkers/Sextus Empiricus]] / Pyrrhonism**: sharp contrast. Where the Stoic *tests then assents* to the kataleptic impression, the Skeptic finds impressions equipollent and *suspends* assent altogether (*epochē*). Both target tranquillity through the management of impressions, by opposite means. See [[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]], [[Concepts/Equipollence (Isosthenia) and the Modes]].
- **[[Thinkers/Descartes]]**: a structural cousin — the will's assent to clear-and-distinct perceptions, and error as assent outrunning understanding — though Descartes seeks certainty of the external world, which the Stoic treats as indifferent.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- The Stoic claim that a clearly-seen truth *compels* assent sits in tension with the moral responsibility Epictetus places on misjudgment: if error is always involuntary ignorance (the Socratic thesis, II.26, I.18 "pity, don't be angry with, the erring"), in what sense is vice blameworthy?
