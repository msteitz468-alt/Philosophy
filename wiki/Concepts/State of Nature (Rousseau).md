# State of Nature (Rousseau)

> The hypothetical prehistoric state of human existence prior to socialization, characterized by absolute freedom, peaceful solitude, self-love, and natural compassion.

## Summary

In his political and moral philosophy, particularly the *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality* (1755), [[Thinkers/Jean-Jacques Rousseau|Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] presents the **State of Nature** as a peaceful, idyllic era when humans lived as solitary, self-sufficient beings. Unlike other contractarian thinkers, Rousseau argues that humans are naturally good and that their moral corruption, inequality, and loss of freedom are the direct results of entering civil society.

This concept is often associated with the archetype of the **"Noble Savage"**—a human uncorrupted by the artificial conventions of civilization.

---

## Core Characteristics

### 1. Two Natural Sentiments
Rousseau argues that human nature in the state of nature is guided by two pre-rational sentiments:
* **Amour de soi** (Self-love / Self-preservation): A healthy, natural urge to preserve one's life, which is quiet and solitary. (This is distinct from *amour-propre* or vanity, which is a social, comparative pride that leads to conflict).
* **Pitié** (Natural Pity / Compassion): A natural aversion to seeing any other sentient being suffer, which tempers the drive for self-preservation.

### 2. Peace and Abundance
Because natural humans are solitary and have simple desires easily met by the abundance of nature, they have no reason to fight. They have no language, no houses, and no concepts of property, justice, or morality. They live in a state of immediate sensory presence, free from anxiety about the future.

### 3. The Corruption of Civilization
The transition from the state of nature to civil society is triggered by accidental changes (population growth, natural disasters) that force humans to cooperate. This cooperation leads to:
* The invention of language and stable families.
* The introduction of **private property**: *"The first man who, having enclosed a piece of ground, bethought himself of saying 'This is mine', and found people simple enough to believe him, was the real founder of civil society."*
* The rise of *amour-propre* (comparative vanity), inequality, and institutionalized slavery.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

> [!WARNING]
> Rousseau's peaceful, pity-driven State of Nature stands in direct opposition to [[Thinkers/Thomas Hobbes|Thomas Hobbes]]' view of the state of nature as a brutal war of all against all. See [[Contradictions/Rousseau vs. Hobbes on the State of Nature|Rousseau vs. Hobbes on the State of Nature]].

### Critiques
* **Historical Accuracy**: Rousseau himself admitted that the state of nature is a hypothetical construct rather than a historical fact: *"a state which no longer exists, which perhaps never existed, and probably never will."* Some critics argue that basing a political theory on a self-admitted fiction weakens its legitimacy.
* **Anti-Progress Bias**: Rousseau is often accused of wishing to return humans to an animal-like state. However, in *The Social Contract*, he clarifies that we cannot return to the state of nature; instead, we must design a legitimate civil society that preserves our natural freedom in a moral, civil form.
