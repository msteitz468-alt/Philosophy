# Dreams as Residual Sense-Movements (Aristotle)

> A dream is a presentation (*phantasma*) belonging to the faculty of *phantasia*, not to sense-perception or intellect as such: a "movement" left over from prior sense-stimulation, which persists after its object is gone and — amplified by sleep's quiet and mistaken by the dormant common sense for a fresh perception — presents itself to the sleeper as something real.

## Summary

Dreaming cannot be sense-perception proper (the eyes are shut, no object stimulates the sleeper) nor bare opinion (dreamers judge qualitative particulars — "white," "beautiful" — that opinion without perception cannot assert). Aristotle resolves this by extending a general physical principle: qualitative motions, like projectiles or heat conduction, continue propagating after their originating cause has ceased. Perceptual stimulation therefore leaves a persisting residual "movement" in the sensory organs — evidenced by afterimages, adaptation illusions (staring at one color biases the next), and the striking (if physiologically dubious) claim that a menstruating woman's gaze can cloud a polished mirror via disturbance of the intervening air. By day these residues are swamped by the far stronger movements of active perception and thought; by night, as sleep draws blood inward and quiets the sense-organs, the residues become disproportionately vivid and are received by the primary/common sense-organ as if they were present perceptions — hence the dream: an image that is "not actually Koriskos" but "like Koriskos," mistaken for the man himself because the discriminating faculty that would normally correct the error is itself quiescent.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Dreaming belongs to ***phantasia*** (the presentative faculty) specifically, described as sense-perception's activity "qua presentative" — a third category alongside perception and opinion/intellect.
- Persistence-of-stimulation physics grounds the theory: sensory qualitative change propagates and decays like a physical motion, explaining both ordinary perceptual after-effects and dream-imagery as an extreme case.
- Emotional state lowers the threshold for false identification (fear → enemies, desire → the beloved); the same graded mechanism, intensified by sleep, produces dream-content from faint residual movements — an early unified theory of waking illusion and dreaming.
- Sleep amplifies trifling internal movements because daytime "noise" (fresh perception, active thought) is absent — a proto signal-to-noise account of dream vividness.
- Not every sleep-time presentation is a dream proper: faint real perceptions during light sleep (later recognized as real) and genuine thoughts occurring in sleep are excluded; the dream proper is strictly a presentation grounded in residual sense-movement.
- Self-aware ("lucid") dreaming is acknowledged: some sleepers retain an internal voice declaring the presentation illusory.
- Excessive internal disturbance (heavy meals, infancy) can obliterate or badly distort dream-images, analogized to violently disturbed water giving no clear reflection.

## How Aristotle Uses This

Directly extends the *De Anima* account of *phantasia* as "a movement resulting from an actual exercise of a power of sense" (see [[Concepts/Imagination and Common Sense (Aristotle)]]): the dream is simply this same imaginative movement operating under the specific physiological conditions of sleep described in [[Concepts/Sleep and Waking (Aristotle)]]. The "Koriskos" example (mistaking a likeness for the man himself) reuses the picture/likeness structure that reappears, more centrally, in the account of memory (see [[Concepts/Memory and Recollection (Aristotle)]]).

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- The claim that the eye "produces an action" upon what it sees (mirror-clouding example) is in some tension with Aristotle's generally intromissive (not extramissive) theory of vision elsewhere; left as an open internal inconsistency rather than resolved by the text.
