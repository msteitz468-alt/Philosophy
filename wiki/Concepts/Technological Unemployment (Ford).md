# Technological Unemployment (Ford)

> Martin Ford's (2015) framing of the risk that accelerating information technology, machine learning, robotics, and AI will produce sustained, large-scale unemployment and underemployment that is not offset by the creation of new jobs at comparable scale or wages. Unlike prior waves of automation (which displaced specific sectors while creating new opportunities elsewhere), today's general-purpose IT is a "truly general-purpose technology" whose impact occurs "across the board." "Predictable" jobs (tasks learnable from detailed records of past performance + big data) are vulnerable at all skill levels — blue-collar (warehouse, driving, fast food) and white-collar/professional (paralegals, journalists, programmers, radiologists, office work, even some knowledge/scientific roles). The result, absent major policy adaptation, is technological unemployment/underemployment, extreme inequality, and erosion of the consumer demand that powers the mass-market economy.

## Summary (from the source)

Ford argues that the historical pattern — technological progress creates short-term disruption but long-term net job growth and rising prosperity — has broken. The postwar "Golden Age" (roughly 1945–1973) featured a near-perfect symbiosis: advancing machines raised worker productivity, which translated into higher wages, which drove consumer spending, which created more jobs. That feedback loop has decoupled since the 1970s.

Key evidence ("seven deadly trends"):
- Real average weekly earnings for typical production/nonsupervisory private-sector workers peaked in 1973 and have since declined ~13% (inflation-adjusted), even as productivity rose 107%.
- Median household income growth has lagged far behind per-capita GDP (much of the apparent gain came from increased female labor-force participation).
- "Jobless recoveries" — the first decade of the 2000s produced zero net new jobs (despite needing ~1 million/year just for workforce growth).
- Labor's share of national income has fallen; capital/owners capture the productivity gains.
- Soaring inequality (top 5% of households responsible for nearly 40% of spending and rising).
- New industries are born already highly automated and capital-intensive (Google, Facebook, WhatsApp achieved enormous scale and valuation with tiny employee counts relative to impact).
- The "predictable" nature of most work: any job where another person could learn it by studying a detailed record of past actions, or where performance can be improved by repeating prior tasks, is vulnerable to algorithms that learn from the big data exhaust of human workers.

Ford emphasizes that this is not limited to low-skill "routine" manual work. White-collar and professional jobs involving predictable, data-encapsulated tasks are now squarely targeted (e.g., legal discovery software replacing junior associates; automated journalism; image-analysis systems for radiologists who require 13+ years of training; Watson-style systems in medicine and customer service). Entry-level jobs are especially at risk, and even many college-educated workers face underemployment.

The impact is broad and potentially rapid because IT is general-purpose (affects every industry) and because new sectors incorporate labor-saving technology from inception. The historical pattern of workers displaced from one sector finding work in an emerging one is unlikely to repeat at scale.

Beyond individual hardship, Ford highlights the macroeconomic danger: jobs are the primary mechanism distributing purchasing power. If that mechanism erodes while production capacity (via automation) remains or grows, the result is insufficient consumer demand to sustain the mass-market economy — a "perfect storm" amplified by inequality, demographics, and other pressures. The parable "Machines Do Not Consume" (or alien invasion scenario) illustrates the endpoint: factories and algorithms keep producing, but there are no human buyers left.

This is not inevitable in the sense of an unstoppable force with no human response; it is the likely outcome of current trajectories *without* deliberate adaptation of economic institutions and policy.

## Key Claims / How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Ford**: Technological unemployment is already visible in data and accelerating with AI/robotics. "This time is different" because of the general-purpose nature of IT, the predictability criterion, the labor-saving birth of new industries, and the breakdown of the productivity-wage-demand loop. Education alone is not a solution. A new paradigm (basic income) is required.
- **Kelly (forces as mechanism)**: The same "cognifying" (cheap ubiquitous AI added to everything) and "becoming" (flux, services over products, perpetual upgrading) that Kelly sees as inevitable metatrends are the engines of Ford's displacement. Kelly emphasizes protopian embrace + management of particulars; Ford stresses that the aggregate effect on labor demand and consumption may require structural change. Cross to [[Sources/The Inevitable - Kevin Kelly (2016)]], [[Concepts/Cognifying (Kelly)]], [[Concepts/Becoming (Kelly)]], [[Concepts/Technium (Kelly)]].
- **Bostrom (risk substrate)**: Ford's near/medium-term focus on specialized AI and robotics displacing predictable work is the practical leading edge of the same technological substrate Bostrom analyzes for superintelligence paths, orthogonality, and control problems. Narrow AI already sufficient for economic disruption per Ford; full AGI would amplify it dramatically (see Ford's own Ch. 9). Cross to [[Sources/Superintelligence - Nick Bostrom (2014)]], [[Concepts/Superintelligence (Bostrom)]], [[Concepts/Orthogonality Thesis (Bostrom)]].
- **Kurzweil (acceleration context)**: Ford's Ch. 9 directly engages Kurzweil's Singularity vision (2045, intelligence explosion, human-machine merger) as one possible amplifier of the automation wave, while noting expert skepticism and focusing on nearer-term job/economic effects rather than transcendence. Acceleration is real; the character of the outcome depends on policy. Cross to [[Sources/The Singularity Is Near - Ray Kurzweil (2005)]], [[Concepts/Singularity (Kurzweil)]], [[Concepts/Law of Accelerating Returns (Kurzweil)]].
- **Mitchell (capabilities vs. limits)**: Ford cites rapid practical progress (Watson, deep learning, warehouse robots) as evidence that "predictable" work is automatable now. Mitchell's corrective (brittleness, lack of robust common sense/analogy/understanding in current systems) suggests that while narrow slices can be displaced effectively, full replacement of complex human roles may lag or require different architectures. The economic risk is real even if hype exceeds current understanding. Cross to [[Sources/Artificial Intelligence - Melanie Mitchell (2019)]], [[Concepts/Brittleness of Deep Learning (Mitchell)]], [[Concepts/Understanding vs Pattern Matching in AI (Mitchell)]].
- **Wiener (historical precursor)**: Ford explicitly revives Wiener's 1949 warning of cybernation/automation creating an "industrial revolution of unmitigated cruelty" by reducing the economic value of routine workers. Ford updates it with post-1973 data and extension to white-collar/general IT. Cross to [[Sources/The Human Use of Human Beings - Norbert Wiener (1950)]], [[Concepts/Second Industrial Revolution (Wiener)]].
- **Historical "Luddite fallacy" vs. data**: Challenges the mainstream economist view (postwar Golden Age as normal/permanent) by documenting the decoupling. Revives the 1964 Triple Revolution report (which called for guaranteed income amid automation fears) as prescient rather than alarmist.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **Displacement scale/timeline vs. historical resilience**: Will new jobs and sectors emerge at sufficient scale and wage levels, as in past transitions (agriculture → manufacturing → services), or is the general-purpose + born-automated nature of current IT a qualitative break? Ford's data (zero net jobs 2000s, wage-productivity divergence) vs. conventional "Luddite fallacy" arguments.
- **With Kelly protopia/embrace**: The forces Kelly describes as inevitable and ultimately beneficial (with management) are the same ones Ford sees risking systemic demand collapse and mass insecurity. Embrace + manage particulars vs. need for structural paradigm shift (basic income). Complementary warnings; different policy conclusions.
- **Narrow AI displacement vs. AGI/superintelligence risks (Bostrom/Kurzweil)**: Ford argues specialized systems + robotics are already sufficient for broad economic harm. Bostrom and parts of the Singularity discussion focus on the later, more extreme intelligence explosion. Both can be true on different timescales; the near-term wave sets the stage (and creates the wealth/power concentration) for the longer-term one.
- **Education as solution vs. limits (Mitchell)**: Conventional policy (retrain, more STEM/college) assumes human comparative advantage in non-routine work. Mitchell's analysis of current AI (strong pattern matching, weak generalization/analogy/common sense) suggests that "predictable" work can be captured without full human-like robustness, while truly novel/creative work may remain harder to automate — but may not employ enough people to absorb the displaced.
- **Basic income feasibility (political/economic)**: Strong theoretical arguments (sustains demand, stabilizer, possibly self-funding) vs. work disincentives, political toxicity, revenue challenges, and alternatives (capital distribution). Ford acknowledges the difficulties but argues discussion is urgent.
- **"Machines do not consume" endpoint**: Extreme scenario (full automation with no human purchasing power) vs. more gradual polarization (elite consumption + low-wage service economy + welfare patch). Both point to need for adaptation.
