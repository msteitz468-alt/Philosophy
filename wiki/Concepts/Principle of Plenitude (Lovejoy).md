# Principle of Plenitude (Lovejoy)

> The thesis (primarily from Plato's *Timaeus*) that the universe must contain — and a "good" (self-sufficient, non-envious) Source must produce — the maximum possible diversity of kinds of beings; that no genuine potentiality of being can remain unfulfilled; that the extent and abundance of creation must be as great as the possibility of existence and commensurate with the productive capacity of a perfect and inexhaustible Source; that the world is the better, the more things it contains. One of the three unit-ideas whose fusion constitutes the Great Chain of Being (see [[Concepts/Great Chain of Being (Lovejoy)]]). Arthur O. Lovejoy's name for a recurrent "unit-idea" whose career he traces from Plato through Neoplatonism, medieval theology, Leibniz/Spinoza, 18c optimism, and its temporalized Romantic forms.

## Summary

From Lovejoy (Ch. II, "The Genesis of the Idea in Greek Philosophy: The Three Principles"):

The principle appears first in Plato. In the *Timaeus*, the Demiurge (poetic personification of the Idea of the Good or its generative aspect) is "good" and therefore "devoid of envy"; "he desired that everything should be so far as possible like himself." This is "the sovereign originating principle of Becoming and of the cosmos." "All things" here means the sensible counterparts of every Idea (including paltry or ridiculous ones, per *Parmenides*); for living things, "it is the perfect image of the whole of which all animals — both individuals and species — are parts." The pattern contains the intelligible forms of *all* beings; nothing incomplete is beautiful.

Implications Lovejoy draws:
- Not a free or arbitrary act of choice; dialectical/ontological necessity. The Absolute would not be fully "good" or complete without producing the full range.
- Latent absolute cosmical determinism: every kind (and ultimately every particular) that is possible *must* be; "the being and attributes of all other things are derivative from this perfection because they are logically implicit in it."
- Reversal of values: the Intellectual World is deficient without the sensible embodiment; the shadows/Cave are needful to the Sun; the realm of essence lacks what is indispensable to its meaning until realized in time and multiplicity.
- "The not-so-good, not to say the bad, must be apprehended as derivative from the Idea of the Good, as involved in the essence of Perfection."

The principle passes to Neoplatonism (Plotinus: emanation as elaboration of Timaeus dialectic; the One overflows necessarily), medieval theology (Augustine: "non essent omnia, si essent aequalia"; Pseudo-Dionysius: God's "love"/goodness as inexhaustible productive energy, "non permisit manere Deum in seipso sine germine"; Aquinas), Leibniz (plenitude + sufficient reason: this is the best because fullest compossible world), Spinoza (everything possible by the nature of substance *must* be actual), and 18c optimism (Pope: "Of systems possible if 'tis confest / That wisdom infinite must form the best"; the full Chain justifies "whatever is, is right").

Temporalizing (late 18c–Romantic): the static "full" once-for-all becomes a progressive realization in time; plenitude as the "flying goal" of a Becoming rather than permanent character of a finished product (Schelling: God "is Life, and not merely being"; "Being is sensible only in becoming").

## Key Claims / How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Plato**: Core enunciation; "good" = productive without envy or need; all ideal possibilities realized in the sensible (at least for the living world as image of the whole).
- **Neoplatonists**: Systematic deduction of necessary emanation from the self-sufficient One/Good; the Chain as outflow without loss.
- **Medieval theologians**: Justifies diversity and imperfection (the full spectrum glorifies the Creator; "if all were equal, all would not be"); God's goodness primarily creative/generative rather than (or in addition to) redemptive.
- **Leibniz**: Combined with sufficient reason; every detail has a reason; this world the one realizing maximal compossible variety.
- **18c optimists**: Ethical application — the full Chain means partial evils are necessary for the universal good; "all that rises, rise in due degree."
- **Romantics (Schelling)**: Reinterpreted as process: the generative power now insufficient striving upward through history; plenitude is what the world-process *aims at* realizing, not what it already is.
- **Lovejoy**: The principle, taken with its implications and allied ideas, is a grand experiment in rational intelligibility whose consequences (when fully drawn) show the hypothesis of complete rational intelligibility to be unbelievable. It generates infinities and contradictions; a contingent world cannot be the transparent expression of a system of eternal necessary truths.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- With self-sufficiency of the Absolute: if the Good is perfectly self-contained and in need of nothing, why *must* it produce anything? (The "no envy" device masks but does not resolve the tension between otherworldly and this-worldly strains.)
- With temporality: if all possibles must be realized, why not all at once? Why a temporal order at all, and why this particular sequence and duration (arbitrary facts fatal to sufficient reason)?
- With continuity/gradation: see those concepts and the Great Chain page (plenitude pushes for maximal diversity of *kinds*, which requires qualitative leaps; the "full" universe cannot be a smooth continuum).
- With later science/philosophy: the demand for "sufficient reason" for every detail and for the world's exact content is abandoned; the world has "something arbitrary and idiosyncratic about" its laws and magnitude (Lovejoy's conclusion). Cross to Popper (no authoritative sources; conjectures), Kuhn (paradigms not dictated by pure reason), Pearl (causal structure imposed by mind on data), Gödel (incompleteness even in formal systems).
- Optimism/perfection vs. evil/contingency: the principle was the chief basis for solving the problem of evil via plenitude; its breakdown leaves the problem more stark (or reframed).
