# Constitutional Political Economy (Buchanan-Tullock)
> The choice of rules (constitutional stage under a veil of uncertainty) must be analyzed separately from choices made within those rules (operational stage).

## Summary

**Constitutional political economy** (or constitutional economics) is a subfield of economics and political theory co-founded by James M. Buchanan and Gordon Tullock. It is defined by its focus on the **choice of rules** rather than choices within rules. 

In *The Calculus of Consent* (1962), the authors construct a methodological framework to explain how rational individuals, pursuing their own utility, can agree on the fundamental rules that govern collective decision-making.

### The Two Stages of Politics

The core analytical move in constitutional political economy is the separation of political action into two distinct levels:

```
                  ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
                  │        CONSTITUTIONAL STAGE         │
                  │   - Unanimous choice of Rules       │
                  │   - Under a Veil of Uncertainty     │
                  └──────────────────┬──────────────────┘
                                     │
                                     ▼
                  ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
                  │          OPERATIONAL STAGE          │
                  │   - Ordinary legislation / actions  │
                  │   - Under non-unanimous rules       │
                  └─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

1. **The Constitutional Stage**: The level at which the "rules of the game" are selected. This choice is long-term and structural. It defines what activities will be collectivized and what voting rules (e.g., simple majority, qualified majority, bicameralism) will apply to collective choices.
2. **The Operational Stage**: The level of day-to-day politics within the chosen rules. This is where specific laws, budgets, taxes, and projects are debated and voted upon by citizens or representatives.

### The Veil of Uncertainty

How can self-interested individuals agree on a constitution that does not favor some at the expense of others? 

Buchanan and Tullock introduce the concept of the **veil of uncertainty** (a direct precursor to John Rawls's "veil of ignorance"). At the constitutional stage, individuals are choosing rules that will apply over a long, uncertain sequence of future situations. Because an individual cannot predict their own specific economic position, interest, or coalition membership on any future issue, they have a rational incentive to choose rules that are fair, general, and protect all citizens from exploitation. 

Under this uncertainty, **self-interested individuals can reach unanimous agreement on a neutral constitutional structure.**

### Politics-as-Exchange

Unlike traditional political science, which often models politics as a zero-sum struggle for power, or idealistic models that assume a collective "general will," constitutional economics models politics as a **positive-sum game of cooperative exchange**. 

The constitution is a social contract: individuals trade some of their private freedom to the state in exchange for protection from external costs (crimes, negative externalities) and the provision of public goods, using Wicksellian unanimity as the ultimate test of mutual benefit.

## Key Claims / Positions

### How Different Thinkers Approach This

- **[[Thinkers/Thomas Hobbes]]**: Hobbes's social contract theory argues that individuals, in the state of nature, must surrender their sovereignty to an absolute monarch (Leviathan) to escape the war of all against all. Buchanan and Tullock reject this authoritarian conclusion. They show that individuals can contractually establish a democratic, constitutional system with checks and balances (bicameralism, qualified majorities) that preserves individual liberty and limits the state's power.
- **[[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]**: Marx viewed the state and its constitution as the instrument of the ruling class to exploit the proletariat. Constitutional economics reinterprets this: exploitation occurs not because of class dialectics, but because the constitutional stage failed to require unanimity, allowing winning coalitions at the operational stage to impose external costs on the minority.
- **John Rawls**: In *A Theory of Justice* (1971), Rawls used the "veil of ignorance" to derive egalitarian moral principles (the difference principle). Buchanan and Tullock, writing a decade earlier, used a similar "veil of uncertainty" to derive efficiency-maximizing constitutional rules, emphasizing the interdependence of decision costs and external costs rather than distributive justice.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] The assumption of a "constitutional moment" where a society can unanimously write a new contract is historically rare. Most constitutions are born out of crisis, war, or the dominance of one group over another, raising questions about the positive applicability of the model to real-world histories.
- The distinction between constitutional rules and operational choices can blur. Political actors frequently attempt to "enshrine" their current operational preferences (e.g., specific tax rates or social bans) into the constitution itself to bind future majorities.
