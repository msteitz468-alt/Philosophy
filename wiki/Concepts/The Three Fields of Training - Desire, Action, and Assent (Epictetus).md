# The Three Fields of Training — Desire, Action, and Assent (Epictetus)

> Epictetus's own curriculum: the three *topoi* in which the student must train — the discipline of **desire**, of **action/duty**, and of **assent** — the primary-text source of Hadot's famous three-discipline reading.

## Summary

In Discourses **III.2** Epictetus lays out three fields (*topoi*) of exercise that structure the whole moral education, in a fixed order of urgency:

1. **Desire and aversion** (*orexis / ekklisis*) — the most urgent and foundational: to desire only what accords with nature and is in our power, so as never to fail of a desire or fall into what one shuns. This is the field of freedom from perturbation.
2. **Impulse to act and duty** (*hormē / kathēkonta*) — the appropriate actions arising from our roles and relationships (father, citizen, brother): to act "with reservation," fittingly and in due order (grounded in II.10 on the duties of relationships).
3. **Assent** (*sunkatathesis*) — the most advanced, reserved for the stable practitioner: to give or withhold agreement to impressions without error or precipitancy (see [[Concepts/Proper Use of Impressions and the Discipline of Assent (Epictetus)]]).

Epictetus complains that contemporary "philosophers" invert this order, plunging into the logic of assent (syllogisms, sophisms) while neglecting the desires that actually convulse them (III.2). The three fields are worked through *askēsis* — systematic training that opposes bad habit with deliberate counter-habit:

> Training is directed at controlling desire and aversion in things within the will; the true trainer "opposes habit with counter-habit," beginning by fleeing what overpowers and building strength by graduated difficulty. (Discourses III.12)

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Order matters**: desire first (it causes the passions), then action, then assent. Skipping to assent/logic is the characteristic error of the showy student (III.2, II.19, III.21).
- **Askēsis is counter-habit, not feats**: not rope-walking or endurance stunts but graduated practice in detachment from externals (III.12).
- **Training happens in action, not retreat**: living virtuously amid business is superior to withdrawn study; leisure can enslave as much as ambition (IV.4).
- **The fields map the parts of the self**: desire (the affective), impulse (the practical/volitional), assent (the cognitive) — a complete discipline of the person.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Epictetus** originates the scheme as a practical syllabus.
- **Pierre Hadot** ([[Concepts/The Three Disciplines - Perception, Action, and Will (Hadot)]]) rereads the three *topoi* as the disciplines of **assent/perception**, **desire/consent to fate**, and **action/service to the human community**, and shows they structure [[Thinkers/Marcus Aurelius]]'s *Meditations*. This concept page is the primary-text ground for Hadot's interpretation.
- **[[Thinkers/Ryan Holiday]]** popularizes Hadot's mapping as **Perception, Action, Will** in [[Sources/The Obstacle Is the Way - Ryan Holiday (2014)]].
- **[[Thinkers/Marcus Aurelius]]** practises all three as daily written exercise (see [[Concepts/The Inner Citadel (Hadot)]]).
