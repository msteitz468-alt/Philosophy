# Incorporeal Event (Deleuze-Stoics)
> The Stoics discovered that events are incorporeal — not states of affairs or accidents of substance, but surface effects that "insist" or "subsist" rather than exist, expressed by infinitives rather than nouns.

## Summary

Deleuze reads the Stoics as having made the decisive break with Platonism: rather than anchoring ethics and ontology in stable Forms, they distinguished two ontological planes:

1. **Bodies and their mixtures** — actual, present, causally interacting with each other. Governed by **Chronos** (the present as living, embodied time).
2. **Incorporeal events** — not causes but *results* and *effects* at the surface of bodies. They do not exist in the present; they "insist," "subsist," or "inhere" in bodies without being identical to them. Governed by **Aeon** (unlimited time that eluded the present: always *what has just happened* or *what is about to happen*).

Events are expressed by **infinitives**: "to green," "to cut," "to grow," "to die." Unlike nouns (denoting substances) or adjectives (denoting qualities), infinitives are indeterminate with respect to past and future — they are reversible, impersonal, and non-localizable in the present. Events do not preexist bodies; they inhere, insist, and subsist in them.

Events are **quasi-causal** among themselves (not really causal — that belongs to bodies) and form networks of sense that hover at the surface of states of affairs. This is Deleuze's "logic of sense."

## Key Claims

- **Stoic ontological distinction:** "This way of being finds itself somehow at the limit, at the surface of being, the nature of which it is not able to change: it is, in fact, neither active nor passive... It is purely and simply a result, or an effect which is not to be classified among beings." (Bréhier on Stoics, quoted by Deleuze)
- **Events and the surface:** "It is by following the border, by skirting the surface, that one passes from bodies to the incorporeal."
- **Events and ethics:** The Stoic ethical imperative — "to be worthy of what happens to us" — means willing the event in its eternal dimension (counteractualization) rather than merely suffering it as personal accident. See [[Concepts/Ethics Without Morality (Deleuze-Spinoza)]].
- **Double structure of the event:** Every event has (a) an actualization in a state of affairs (the wound, the battle) and (b) its pure incorporeal dimension (*the wound* as eternal truth, *the battle* as singular Event). The actor/free person holds both simultaneously.
- **Counteractualization:** "My wound existed before me; I was born to embody it." The ethical task is to trace the pure event across its actualization, not to identify with the accident.
- **The "fourth person singular":** Events are impersonal ("it rains," "it dies") — below the individual, beyond the universal. The free person acquires impersonality as the event's "fourth person singular."

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Aristotle]]:** Aristotle's accidents inhere in substances and are caused by them; they require a substance as ontological support. Stoic events also inhere in bodies but form an ontologically distinct plane — the incorporeal — that does not reduce to the accidental modification of substance.
- **[[Thinkers/Plato]]:** For Plato, what is real is the Form; sensible events are mere appearances. The Stoics invert the hierarchy by making events the real operators of sense (not appearances of a deeper reality, but the surface where meaning is produced).
- **[[Thinkers/Heidegger]]:** Heidegger's "event" (Ereignis) is the appropriation of Being and Dasein — still ontologically fundamental. Deleuze's event is ontologically secondary (a surface effect of bodies) but epistemologically and ethically primary.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Deleuze distinguishes the "quasi-cause" of events from the real causality of bodies, but the mechanism by which incorporeal events have "quasi-causal efficacy" back on bodies remains programmatic rather than fully worked out.
