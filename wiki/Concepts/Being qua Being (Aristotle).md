# Being qua Being (Aristotle)

> The object of *first philosophy*: the study of beings simply insofar as they are, as opposed to the special sciences that each carve off one genus of being. Its unity is secured by the *pros hen* ("toward one") doctrine — "being is said in many ways," but all with reference to one central case: substance.

## Summary

In *Metaphysics* IV (Gamma) Aristotle announces "a science which investigates being as being and the attributes which belong to this in virtue of its own nature." Unlike mathematics or physics, which "cut off a part of being," first philosophy studies what holds of anything at all just because it exists. The problem: "being" is not a single genus — it is **said in many ways** (*legetai pollachōs*). Aristotle resolves this not by mere equivocation but by **focal reference** (*pros hen legomenon*): the famous **health analogy** — a regimen, a complexion, a symptom, and a medicine are all called "healthy" by different relations to one thing, health. Likewise everything that is — qualities, quantities, processes, privations, relations — is called a *being* by some relation to **substance** (*ousia*, [[Concepts/Substance (Ousia) (Aristotle)]]). "If this is substance, it will be of substances that the philosopher must grasp the principles and the causes." So the science of being qua being turns out to be, at its core, the science of substance.

Book V (Delta) sorts the senses of "being" into four: **(1) accidental** ("the musician is a man"); **(2) per se, by the categories** (substance, quality, quantity, relation, place, time, etc.); **(3) being as truth** (a proposition's being-the-case — a mental, not real, mode, cf. VI); and **(4) potential vs. actual** being ([[Concepts/Potentiality and Actuality (Aristotle)]]). Science concerns the *per se* senses; accidental being "is practically a mere name" and admits no science (VI), since it holds "neither always nor for the most part."

Book VI (Epsilon) divides the theoretical sciences into **physics** (separable but movable beings), **mathematics** (immovable but not separable, studied by abstraction), and **theology / first philosophy** (separable *and* immovable — the divine). "If there is an immovable substance, the science of it must be prior and must be first philosophy, and universal in this way, because it is first." This is the seam where ontology (being qua being) and theology (the [[Concepts/The Unmoved Mover (Aristotle)]]) meet — the central interpretive puzzle of the *Metaphysics*.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **First philosophy is universal because it is first**: studying the primary kind of being (substance) yields the principles of all being.
- **Pros hen unity**: a middle path between Platonic univocity (one Form of Being) and bare equivocation — the categories are unified by reference to substance, not subsumed under a genus.
- **Being and unity are convertible**: "that which is one is also somehow being, and that which is being is one"; there are "exactly as many species of being as of unity" (cf. [[Concepts/Unity and the One (Aristotle)]]).
- **Neither being nor unity is itself a substance or a genus** (X, XI): both are universally predicated, but a universal cannot be the substance of things.

## How This Sits Among Thinkers

- **Versus [[Thinkers/Parmenides]]**: Parmenides made Being one and univocal, forcing him to deny change and plurality ([[Concepts/Parmenidean Being and the One (Parmenides)]]). The *pros hen* doctrine is Aristotle's escape: being is *many-ways* one, so plurality and becoming are saved without contradiction.
- **Versus [[Thinkers/Plato]]**: rejects a separate Form of Being/the One over the categories.
- **Downstream**: the analogy of being becomes central to [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]] (*analogia entis*); Aristotle's identification of first philosophy with theology shapes the whole medieval project. Contrast [[Thinkers/Spinoza]], who collapses the many beings into one substance ([[Concepts/Deus sive Natura - Substance Monism (Spinoza)]]) — the opposite of the *pros hen* pluralism.
- The senses-of-being analysis grounds the essence/accident distinction used in [[Sources/Aristotle - Modal Logic (Organon, Patterson)]] ([[Concepts/Modal Copula (Aristotelian Modality)]]).
- **Versus [[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]]**: Heidegger reads Aristotle's *pros hen* reference to substance (*ousia*) as a key moment in the history of ontology, but argues that treating *ousia* as presence (*Anwesenheit*) privileges a single temporal ecstasis (the present) and homogenises Being as *presence-at-hand* (*Vorhandenheit*). Heidegger's **ontological difference** seeks to recover the question of Being in radical distinction from any particular being or substance. See [[Concepts/Being and the Ontological Difference (Heidegger)]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Is first philosophy *general ontology* (being qua being) or *special theology* (the divine substance)? Book IV says the former, Book VI the latter. Aristotle's own reconciliation — the science of the first being is thereby the science of all being — is the perennial scholarly crux.
