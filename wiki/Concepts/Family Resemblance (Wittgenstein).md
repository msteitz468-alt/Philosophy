# Family Resemblance (Wittgenstein)
> The idea that the things falling under a general concept share no single common essence but are linked by a web of overlapping, criss-crossing similarities — like resemblances among members of a family.

## Summary

**Family resemblance** (*Familienähnlichkeit*) is [[Thinkers/Ludwig Wittgenstein|Wittgenstein]]'s answer, in the *Philosophical Investigations* (§§65–77), to a question that had organized philosophy since [[Thinkers/Plato|Plato]]: what is the one thing common to all instances of a concept — all games, all languages, all good things — in virtue of which we apply the same word? Wittgenstein's reply is that there need be no such common thing. Looking at what we call "games" — board-games, card-games, ball-games, Olympic games — we find "not something that is common to all, but similarities, relationships, and a whole series of them" (§66). Some games involve winning and losing, some amusement, some skill, some luck; the features "overlap and criss-cross" without any one feature running through them all.

The concept is therefore not bounded by a sharp definition. "Game" is "a concept with blurred edges" (§71) — and a blurred concept is still a concept, often "exactly what we need." This dissolves the demand, inherited from his own *Tractatus* and from Frege, that a genuine concept must have crystalline, exhaustively specifiable boundaries.

## Core Mechanics

### 1. "Don't think, but look!"
The decisive move is methodological. Faced with the assumption "There *must* be something common, or they would not be called 'games'," Wittgenstein insists: "don't say... but look and see whether there is anything common to all.—... don't think, but look!" (§66). The essence is not found by introspecting the concept; it is checked against the actual variety of cases — and the variety refutes the assumed unity.

### 2. The rope, not the fibre
The unity of a concept is like the strength of a thread: "the strength of the thread does not reside in the fact that some one fibre runs through its whole length, but in the overlapping of many fibres" (§67). No single thread of meaning runs through all uses of "number" or "good"; the concept holds together by overlap. Wittgenstein names the resulting kinship "family resemblances... build, features, colour of eyes, gait, temperament, etc. etc. overlap and criss-cross in the same way" (§67).

### 3. Blurred boundaries are not defects
Against Frege's view that a concept without sharp boundaries is no concept at all, Wittgenstein argues that an "indistinct picture is often exactly what we need" (§71). The extension of "game" is not closed by a frontier — yet we use the word perfectly well. A boundary *can* be drawn for a special purpose, but it is not already there waiting to be discovered. To understand a concept is to be able to give and recognize examples and apply it in new cases (§75), not to possess a hidden definition.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. [[Thinkers/Plato|Plato]] (Forms):** Plato's Socrates demands a single *eidos* — the one Form of Justice or Piety common to all just or pious things. Family resemblance is the direct denial: there is no Form, only a network of likenesses. This is one of the deepest reversals in the history of the theory of universals.
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Aristotle|Aristotle]] (essence/definition by genus and differentia):** Aristotelian definition presupposes that a kind has a real essence specifiable by necessary and sufficient conditions. Family resemblance denies that many of our most basic concepts work this way.
- **vs. the early Wittgenstein ([[Concepts/Picture Theory of Language (Wittgenstein)|Picture Theory]]):** The *Tractatus* required language to have one underlying logical form and propositions to share a definite structure with facts. Family resemblance abandons that demand: "what we call 'sentence' and 'language'... is the family of structures more or less related to one another" (§108). See [[Contradictions/Early vs Later Wittgenstein]].
- **Connection to [[Concepts/Language-Games (Wittgenstein)|Language-Games]]:** the various language-games themselves form a family — there is no essence of "language," only related practices.
- **Michael Forster's Analysis (2010)**: Forster traces the emergence of family resemblance in the early 1930s. He argues that the model is fully compatible with rule-governedness (contrary to critics who equate it with formless vagueness), since grasping a rule is exhibited by how we obey or violate it in actual cases (PI 201). Forster also shows that family-resemblance concepts have a reductive character: a family-resemblance concept applies to a case in virtue of other, more basic features applying to it.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
> [!warning] Conflicts with [[Thinkers/Plato]] and Aristotelian essentialism on whether general terms name a shared essence — see [[Contradictions/Early vs Later Wittgenstein]] for the internal reversal of the [[Concepts/Picture Theory of Language (Wittgenstein)]].

- If concepts have no sharp boundaries, how is rigorous reasoning (mathematics, law) possible? Wittgenstein's reply: boundaries can be drawn *for a purpose* without being antecedently fixed — but critics ask whether this concedes too much to vagueness.
