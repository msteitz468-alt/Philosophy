# Soul as Form of the Body (Hylomorphism, Aristotle)

> The soul is not a distinct substance temporarily inhabiting a body but "the first actuality of a natural body which has life potentially in it" — its form, applying the *Metaphysics*' hylomorphism to living things. Asking whether soul and body are "one" is as meaningless as asking whether wax and the shape stamped into it are one.

## Summary

Book I surveys and rejects predecessor theories of soul defined by self-motion or composition: Democritus's spherical fire-atoms causing motion by their own restlessness; the Pythagorean identification of soul with motes in the air or with harmony; Empedocles's like-knows-like theory ("by Earth we see Earth, by Water Water... by Love Love, and Hate by cruel Hate"); and — Aristotle's most sustained target — Plato's *Timaeus* account of soul as a "self-moving number" whose circular motion is thinking. Against self-motion, Aristotle argues a mover need not itself be moved, and that a genuinely self-moving soul would need place and natural direction, generating absurdities (a soul could leave and re-enter a body). Against harmony, a ratio of bodily contraries cannot originate movement and would multiply into "many souls" for differently-mixed body parts. Against Plato's circular-motion account, "thinking has more resemblance to a coming to rest or arrest than to a movement," since demonstration and definition are linear and terminating, not circular. Book II supplies Aristotle's own positive account: soul is "the first grade of actuality of a natural body having life potentially in it," specifically an organized natural body — the illustration is the wax and its stamped shape, or the eye and sight ("suppose the eye were an animal — sight would have been its soul... the eye being merely the matter of seeing; when seeing is removed the eye is no longer an eye, except in name"). Soul and body are thus related as actuality to potentiality, not as two separate things juxtaposed, and (with the crucial exception of mind, *nous*, "a widely different kind of soul... alone capable of existence in isolation") soul cannot exist without its proper body, since most of its actualities "are nothing but the actualities of their bodily parts." The most primitive and universal psychic power is the nutritive/reproductive soul, present in all living things including plants and defining life as such — reproduction lets a mortal individual "partake in the eternal and divine" it cannot achieve by persisting itself.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Soul is definitionally the form/actuality of a body of the appropriate kind, not a separate substance — "body without soul" and "soul in the wrong kind of body" are both unintelligible on this view.
- The soul-body relation is not a composition of two independent things but the relation of actuality to the potentiality it actualizes (wax/shape, eye/sight).
- A hierarchy of psychic capacities is nested: nutritive (all living things) ⊂ sensitive/locomotive (animals) ⊂ intellective (humans) — each higher capacity requires but is not reducible to the lower.
- Nearly all psychic capacities are inseparable from body; mind (*nous*) alone is flagged as possibly separable — the famous unresolved tension the work raises about itself.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Vs. [[Thinkers/Plato]]**: this is Aristotle's direct rejection of Platonic soul-body dualism (the *Timaeus*'s self-moving, pre-existent, mathematically-structured soul, and by extension the tripartite immortal-soul doctrine of the *Republic* and *Phaedo*) in favor of an immanent, form-of-this-body account — continuous with [[Contradictions/Plato vs Aristotle on the Forms]] but applied specifically to psychology.
- **Direct ancestor of [[Concepts/Form and Matter - Hylomorphism (Aristotle)]]**: De Anima is the paradigm application of the *Metaphysics*' general hylomorphic and act/potency framework ([[Concepts/Potentiality and Actuality (Aristotle)]]) to the special case of living substance.
- **Vs. [[Thinkers/Descartes]]**: the sharpest possible ancient counterpoint to Cartesian dualism — Descartes's real distinction of *res cogitans* and *res extensa* (two complete, separable substances) directly inverts Aristotle's claim that soul is not a substance apart from body at all, only its form. Where Aristotle's soul is essentially *of* a particular body (a soul in the wrong body is unintelligible), Descartes's thinking substance is essentially independent of any body whatsoever.
- **Downstream — [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]**: Aquinas adopts the hylomorphic soul wholesale (rational soul as substantial form of the body) but must reconcile it with Christian doctrine of personal immortality, exploiting exactly the crack Aristotle leaves open — mind's possible separability — to argue the human soul is a subsistent form capable of surviving bodily death, a synthesis Aristotle's own text does not clearly authorize.
