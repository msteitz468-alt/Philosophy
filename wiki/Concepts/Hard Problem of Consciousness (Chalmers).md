# Hard Problem of Consciousness (Chalmers)
> The easy problems (discrimination, integration, report) yield to standard cognitive science; the hard problem — "Why is all this processing accompanied by an experienced inner life?" — does not.

## Summary

*The Conscious Mind* (1996) is the book-length statement of the problem Chalmers had named in 1995. The "easy" problems of consciousness are the functional ones: explaining discrimination, integration of information, reportability, attention, behavioral control. These are hard science but methodologically tractable. The hard problem is why performance of these functions is *accompanied by experience* — why there is "something it is like" (Nagel's phrase) to be a system at all. Chalmers: "It seems to me that we are surer of the existence of conscious experience than we are of anything else in the world."

Three constraints frame the book: **take consciousness seriously** (no deflationary redefinition), **take science seriously** (accept causal closure of the physical), and treat consciousness as a **natural phenomenon** subject to laws — possibly nonphysical psychophysical laws. The upshot, argued via [[Concepts/Zombie Argument (Chalmers)]] and the failure of [[Concepts/Logical Supervenience and Reductive Explanation (Chalmers)]], is [[Concepts/Naturalistic Dualism (Chalmers)]].

Extant "theories of consciousness" (Rosenthal's higher-order thought, [[Concepts/Multiple Drafts Model (Dennett)]], Armstrong's self-scanning, Jaynes, [[Thinkers/Douglas Hofstadter]]) explain *awareness* — the functional, psychological correlate — not experience. On Dennett (1991): "nothing in the model provides an explanation of phenomenal consciousness (although Dennett would put things differently)."

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]** ([[Concepts/Qualia Disqualified (Dennett)]]): denies there is a residual hard problem once the functions are explained — the vault's sharpest live contradiction; see [[Contradictions/Dennett vs Nagel - Multiple Drafts vs Subjective Experience]].
- **[[Thinkers/Andy Clark]]** ([[Concepts/Predictive Processing and the Hard Problem (Clark)]]): hopes predictive processing dissolves or shrinks the problem — precisely the deflation Chalmers's "take consciousness seriously" constraint forbids.
- **[[Thinkers/John Searle]]**: agrees consciousness is irreducible-seeming but remains a biological naturalist, rejecting Chalmers's dualism and his [[Concepts/Organizational Invariance and the Qualia Arguments (Chalmers)]].
- **Husserl**: see [[Contradictions/Husserl vs Naturalism — Phenomenal Consciousness and the Hard Problem]] — phenomenology anticipated the datum that third-person method leaves out the first person.
- **[[Concepts/Argument from Consciousness (Jefferson)]]**: Turing's ninth objection is an ancestor of the same worry aimed at machine intelligence.
