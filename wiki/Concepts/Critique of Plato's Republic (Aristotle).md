# Critique of Plato's Republic (Aristotle)

> Aristotle's sustained attack (Politics Book II) on Plato's proposed communism of property, wives, and children: the Republic pursues too much unity, treating the state as if it should collapse into a household or an individual, when a state is by nature a self-sufficient plurality of different kinds.

## Summary

Aristotle opens Book II by isolating what he takes to be the Republic's foundational error: the premise that "the greater the unity of the state the better." He argues instead that "the nature of a state is to be a plurality," and that pursued far enough, maximal unity destroys the state as such — "a state may at length attain such a degree of unity as to be no longer a state... in tending to greater unity, from being a state, it becomes a family, and from being a family, an individual... we ought not to attain this greatest unity even if we could, for it would be the destruction of the state." Aristotle inverts Plato's own self-sufficiency argument: since a family is more self-sufficing than an individual and a city more than a family, *less* unity, not more, better serves self-sufficiency. He picks apart the logical ambiguity in Plato's "all say mine and not mine simultaneously" test for guardian unity (an equivocation between "all" distributively and collectively), and argues on empirical grounds that communal ownership of children produces universal neglect ("what is common to the greatest number has the least care bestowed upon it") and "watery" diluted love, while the scheme — meant to bind the guardians into one family — is in any case better suited to a docile subject class than to the rulers Plato assigns it to. Against communism of property, Aristotle proposes his enduring alternative: "it is clearly better that property should be private, but the use of it common," since private ownership sustains liberality and temperance while communal ownership breeds quarrels and free-riding. He identifies the Republic's deeper structural flaw as leaving the majority (husbandmen, artisans) entirely unaddressed, effectively creating "two states in one, each hostile to the other" with the guardians as an occupying garrison, and challenges the coherence of a happiness that belongs to the whole city but is explicitly denied to its guardians ("a whole cannot be happy unless its parts are"). A parallel critique of the *Laws* follows: the same core problems (common meals, shared education, freedom from labor) persist while its numerical specifics are materially impossible ("a territory as large as Babylon" for 5,000 idle citizens) and its property policy is internally inconsistent (capping wealth while leaving population unlimited).

## Key Claims / Positions

- Excess unity destroys, rather than perfects, a state — the state is essentially and rightly plural, not aiming at organismic fusion.
- Private property with common use outperforms full communism, both practically (avoids free-riding/conflict) and ethically (preserves the virtues of liberality and temperance, which communism abolishes by removing the occasion for their exercise).
- A whole's happiness requires its parts' happiness — Plato cannot coherently deny happiness to the guardians while claiming the state as a whole is happy.
- The Republic leaves the non-guardian majority without a coherent constitutional or educational place, producing a structurally divided city.
- The *Laws*' 5,000-citizen state is not merely undesirable but physically impossible given its own premises about self-sufficiency and territory.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Vs. [[Thinkers/Plato]]**: This is the paradigm ancient statement of the unity/plurality divide between the two thinkers, running parallel to but distinct from [[Contradictions/Plato vs Aristotle on the Forms]] — here the dispute concerns political metaphysics (what makes a *polis* one thing) rather than universals.
- **Method contrast**: Aristotle's critique explicitly proceeds by empirical-comparative appeal ("the results which might be expected to follow have never actually occurred," and later the "experience of ages" invoked when surveying Sparta/Crete/Carthage) against what he treats as Plato's a priori ideal construction — exemplifying the same empiricist-vs-rationalist methodological split visible throughout the vault's classical cluster.
- **Downstream**: the private-property-with-common-use formula anticipates later defenses of private property against socialist/communist critique; [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]'s critique of private property inverts Aristotle's argument by treating private ownership itself (not merely its absence) as the source of alienation and conflict — a direct three-way contradiction (Plato: abolish private property for the ruling class; Aristotle: private ownership plus common use is optimal; Marx: private property is the root problem of class society).
