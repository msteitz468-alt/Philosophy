# God's Knowledge of Particulars (Ghazali)

> The Thirteenth Discussion of the *Tahafut*, and the second of the three doctrines for which al-Ghazali condemns the philosophers as unbelievers. Avicenna held that God knows things only "in a universal way" — He knows all genera and species through a single timeless cognition but does **not** know temporal particulars *as* divisible into what was, is, and will be, since tracking a changing object would require successive thoughts and so introduce change into the divine essence. Al-Ghazali argues this is undemonstrated, self-undermining, and "uproots religious laws in their entirety."

## Avicenna's Doctrine and Its Motive

"He knows things through a universal knowledge which does not enter time and which does not change in terms of the past, the future, and the present." The motive is divine **immutability and simplicity**: to know an eclipse *as* now-beginning, now-occurring, now-past would mean three successive cognitions, hence change and plurality in God — impossible for the absolutely simple Necessary Existent. So God knows *that* an eclipse occurs (universally, as a type instantiated at a describable time) but not the eclipse *as the temporally-indexed particular event* it is. The same holds for the individual human act: God does not know the particular Zayd in his particularity.

## Al-Ghazali's Refutation

He presses the religious stakes first: this principle "uproots religious laws in their entirety, since it entails that if Zayd, for example, obeys or disobeys God, God would not know what of his states has newly come about, because He does not know Zayd in his particularity." A God ignorant of who obeys and who rebels, who did not know the moment His prophet issued the prophetic challenge, is incompatible with the Qur'anic God from whom "not even the weight of an atom in the heavens or the earth escapes."

He then dismantles the **change-from-knowing-change** argument two ways:
1. **Relation, not essence, changes.** The three temporal states can be encompassed by *one* permanent act of knowledge whose differences are mere *relations* — as a stationary person whom a moving man passes shifts from "on his right" to "on his left": the relation alters, the person's essence does not. Knowing a changing world need not change the knower.
2. **Turning their own principle against them.** If the endless differences among infinite genera and species do *not* destroy the unity of God's single universal knowledge (as the philosophers grant), then the far smaller differences of past/present/future cannot destroy it either. And even granting that some change ensues, on the philosophers' own metaphysics an eternal being can be the substrate of temporally successive states.

## Connection to the Wider Critique

This discussion is the apex of al-Ghazali's running charge (Discussions 5–13) that the philosophers' zeal for divine simplicity and necessary existence **strips God of real attributes** — knowledge, will, power, life. By reducing God to bare essence they reduce Him, in al-Ghazali's barbs, to a state "approximating that of the dead person who has no information of what takes place in the world"; "what difference is there between Him and the dead, except for His knowledge of Himself?" The doctrine of universal-only knowledge is where that abstraction collides most violently with revelation. It is bound up with [[Concepts/Eternal Will and Temporal Creation (Ghazali)|the parallel insistence]] that God is a genuine voluntary **agent**, not an impersonal necessitating cause: "Real action is that which comes about only through will."

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]:** Averroes thinks both sides err by anthropomorphizing — divine knowledge is neither universal nor particular in the human sense but the *cause* of things rather than caused by them, so the dilemma "universals or particulars" is malformed. He defends Avicenna's underlying worry about immutability while rejecting the bald claim that God is ignorant of individuals.
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]:** Aquinas confronts the identical problem and answers much as al-Ghazali's first refutation does — God knows all particulars in a single eternal act by knowing them in their cause and as present to His eternal "now," so omniscience of contingent particulars is preserved without successive divine thoughts.
- **vs. classical determinism:** The worry connects to whether complete divine foreknowledge of particular future acts is compatible with their contingency — a tension the vault tracks in [[Contradictions/Stoic Determinism vs Aristotelian Contingency]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Does the "mere relation" reply actually avoid change, or relocate it? If God's *relation* to the world genuinely alters, is that not a change in a real (relational) property of God?
- Can immutability and detailed knowledge of a changing world both be held without one collapsing into the other? Al-Ghazali and Aquinas say yes; Avicenna says no.

> [!warning] Avicenna (defended in part by [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]) holds God knows only universals to protect divine immutability; al-Ghazali condemns this as denying omniscience — see [[Sources/Incoherence of the Incoherence - Ibn Rushd]]
