# Humanism - Reality as Malleable (Schiller and James)

> One-sentence hook: F. C. S. Schiller's "Humanism," endorsed by James — "to an unascertainable extent our truths are man-made products"; reality is "malleable," and in knowing it "we add… both to the subject and to the predicate part of reality." "We receive the block of marble, but we carve the statue ourselves."

## Overview

Lecture VII ("Pragmatism and Humanism"). James attacks "*the* Truth" with a capital T as "that typical idol of the tribe… the one answer… to the one fixed enigma." Like "the Law" or "the Latin Language," "the Truth" is "an abstraction from the fact of truths in the plural, a mere useful summarizing phrase." Law, language, and truth are *results*, not antecedent principles: "Given previous law and a novel case, the judge will twist them into fresh law… Previous truth; fresh facts — and our mind finds a new truth." "Far from being antecedent principles that animate the process, law, language, truth are but abstract names for its results."

**Schiller's Humanism** generalizes this: it is "the doctrine that to an unascertainable extent our truths are man-made products." "Human motives sharpen all our questions, human satisfactions lurk in all our answers, all our formulas have a human twist." Schiller's strong formula: "The world… is what we make of it. It is fruitless to define it by what it originally was… it is what is made of it. Hence… the world is plastic." We "ought to start as if it were wholly plastic… stopping only when we are decisively rebuked."

## Key Arguments

- **Reality has three resisting parts** that our truths "must take account of" (recalled from Lecture VI): (1) **the flux of sensations** ("forced upon us, coming we know not whence… they simply *are*"); (2) **the relations among sensations** — mutable (date, place) and "eternal/essential" (likeness, etc.); (3) **previous truths** ("a much less obdurately resisting factor: it often ends by giving way").
- **But the form is ours**: "However fixed these elements may be, we still have a certain freedom." Which sensations we attend to, emphasize, and how we order them "depends on our own interests… quite different formulations of truth result. We read the same facts differently" (Waterloo = "victory" for an Englishman, "defeat" for a Frenchman). The crisp formula: **"The *that* of [reality] is its own; but the *what* depends on the *which*; and the *which* depends on us."** "Both the sensational and the relational parts of reality are dumb: they say absolutely nothing about themselves. We it is who have to speak for them" (the sensation as "a client" who must "listen to whatever account… the lawyer finds it most expedient to give").
- **We carve subjects and predicates**: We "carve out" constellations (the same stars are "Charles's Wain," "the Great Bear," "the Dipper" — "none of the names will be false"); take 27 as "3 cubed" or "26 + 1" or "100 − 73" ("one will be just as true as another," the choice fixed by use — dollars in a drawer vs. inches for a shelf). "What shall we call a thing anyhow? … we carve out everything… to suit our human purposes" (the same crowd is an "audience," or "organisms," or "cells," or "molecules"). "We create the subjects of our true as well as of our false propositions"; "we create the predicates also." "Our nouns and adjectives are all humanized heirlooms."
- **Lotze's deep suggestion**: maybe "our descriptions… [are] themselves important additions to reality," and previous reality is there "for the very purpose of stimulating our minds to such additions as shall enhance the universe's total value." "*Die Erhöhung des vorgefundenen Daseins*." "In our cognitive as well as in our active life we are creative… The world stands really malleable, waiting to receive its final touches at our hands… Man engenders truths upon it." Papini's dithyramb on "man's divinely-creative functions."
- **The structural upshot — pragmatism vs. rationalism as metaphysics, not just epistemology**: "On the pragmatist side we have only one edition of the universe, unfinished, growing in all sorts of places, especially in the places where thinking beings are at work. On the rationalist side we have a universe in many editions, one real one, the infinite folio, or edition de luxe, eternally complete." This reopens monism vs. pluralism (see [[Concepts/The One and the Many (James)]]) and sets up [[Concepts/Meliorism (James)]].
- **Not caprice**: James guards (as in Lecture VI) against Bradley's charge that humanism makes "any idea however mad… the truth if only someone is resolved to have it so." Reality "controls our thinking as an energy that must be taken account of"; we "encounter" it though we "don't possess" it. "Does the river make its banks, or do the banks make the river?" — the human and non-human factors are inseparable, like a man's two legs.

## Contrasts / Lineage

- **Vs. Kant** (the explicit comparison): humanism "sounds like Kant's view," but "between categories fulminated before nature began, and categories gradually forming themselves in nature's presence, the whole chasm between rationalism and empiricism yawns. To the genuine 'Kantianer' Schiller will always be to Kant as a satyr to Hyperion." (Contrast [[Concepts/Two Standpoints: Sensible World and Intelligible World (Kant)]], [[Concepts/Autonomy and Heteronomy of the Will (Kant)]].)
- **Vs. classical/rationalist realism**: directly opposes the adequation/correspondence view — Aristotle/[[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]], [[Concepts/Clear and Distinct Perception (Descartes)]], [[Concepts/Geometrical Method - Rationalism (Spinoza)]] (a mind-independent Nature fully intelligible *more geometrico*). For Schiller/James reality's "what" is partly our construction.
- **Echo of [[Concepts/Primary and Secondary Qualities (Locke)]] and Berkeley**: humanism radicalizes the empiricist insight that much of the perceived world is mind-contributed — but extends "the human touch" even to the relations and the "previous truths."
- **Strong kinship with [[Concepts/Perspectivism (Nietzsche)]]**: "we carve out constellations," "all our formulas have a human twist," "truth is man-made" ≈ Nietzsche's "frog perspectives," "philosophy as autobiography," and "untruth as a condition of life." Both are constructivist and anti-dogmatic; James keeps a meliorist/cooperative key, Nietzsche a will-to-power key. (See [[Concepts/Will to Power (Nietzsche)]] — "it always creates the world in its own image.")
- **With [[Concepts/Pragmatic Theory of Truth (James)]]**: humanism is the metaphysical extension of "truth is made" — not only *truth* but *reality's articulation* bears the human stamp.
