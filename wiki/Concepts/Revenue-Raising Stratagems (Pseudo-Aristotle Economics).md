# Revenue-Raising Stratagems (Pseudo-Aristotle Economics)

> Book II of the *Economics* catalogues dozens of historical fiscal expedients used by kings (Dionysius of Syracuse, Mausolus, Cotys), governors, and free cities to raise money under duress — forced "loans" never repaid, currency debasement, religious pretext for confiscation, price manipulation, and manufactured emergencies — offered value-neutrally as precedent for statecraft, in sharp practical (if not explicitly reconciled) contrast to Book I's virtue-oriented household ethics.

## Summary

After classifying political administration into four types (royal, satrapal, republican, private) with their characteristic revenue departments, the *Economics* pivots to a long anecdotal catalogue "by no means lacking in utility" for future rulers and administrators facing fiscal need. The stratagems recur in identifiable patterns: **forced or manipulated loans** (Cypselus taking a tenth of Corinthian property annually until the total vowed offering was met while citizens simultaneously replaced what they paid; Memnon of Rhodes converting successive "loans" into permanent extractions); **currency manipulation** (Dionysius of Syracuse minting tin as declared silver, reissuing debased coin, doubling face-value drachmae; Clazomenae's iron coinage backed by silver); **religious pretext for confiscation** (Dionysius claiming a visitation from Demeter to collect women's gold "on loan" from the goddess, then keeping it; the temple-suppression threats used by both Chabrias/Taos in Egypt and Cleomenes to extract priestly "contributions" to save their temples); **manufactured emergencies** (Dionysius's fictitious traitor-plot to collect two staters per citizen for a fleet, later returning early "failed-plot" contributions to build trust before a second, kept, collection); and **price/tax manipulation** (Cleomenes buying Egyptian grain from growers at a moderate fixed price and reselling at triple the rate; Byzantium's comprehensive taxation of professions, marriage rights, and even citizenship itself). The stratagems are reported as historical precedent, without explicit moral evaluation — Aristotle (or the pseudonymous author) neither endorses nor systematically condemns them, leaving the catalogue's relation to the household-management ethics of Book I an open interpretive question.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Political administration is classified into four types (royal/monarchic, satrapal/gubernatorial, republican/free-state, private) each with characteristic revenue departments (for royal administration: currency, imports, exports, expenditure).
- The catalogue is explicitly framed as practically useful precedent ("seemed to us by no means lacking in utility... capable of application by others"), not moral exemplification — a genre closer to a manual of statecraft technique than to ethical philosophy.
- Recurring techniques include: forced/manipulated loans that become de facto permanent extractions; currency debasement and reissue; religious pretext for confiscation (temple ornaments, divine "visitations," threatened temple-suppression); manufactured crises to extract "emergency" contributions later retained; and price/tax manipulation exploiting information or monopoly advantages (buying low from producers, selling high to the state or public).
- Deception of one's own citizens/subjects is treated as an ordinary tool of statecraft in this catalogue, without the virtue-ethical framing (courage, temperance, justice, the mean) that governs the *Nicomachean Ethics* and even Book I of this same work.
- The catalogue's historical range (Corinth, Syracuse, Caria, Persia-controlled Egypt, various Aegean cities) demonstrates these techniques as widespread, transregional patterns of ancient public finance under fiscal stress, not isolated anomalies.

## How Pseudo-Aristotle Uses This

Sits in explicit tension with Book I's virtue-structured household ethics (see [[Concepts/Household Management and the Ranking of Wealth-Getting (Pseudo-Aristotle)]]) — the text moves from a mean-governed, character-based account of domestic administration to an amoral, results-oriented catalogue of state fiscal technique without reconciling the two registers. This gap is itself a notable feature of the work, distinguishing "economics" in its original household sense from what becomes, in this second book, proto-public-finance/statecraft technique.
