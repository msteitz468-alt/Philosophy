# Sovereign Individual (Nietzsche)

## Overview

In the *Second Essay* of *On the Genealogy of Morals*, the "sovereign individual" appears as the "ripest fruit" on the tree of the "morality of custom" (*Sittlichkeit der Sitte*) — the long, hard, prehistoric work of making man calculable, uniform, regular, and disciplined.

**Definition**: "The sovereign individual, that resembles only himself, that has got loose from the morality of custom, the autonomous 'super-moral' individual (for 'autonomous' and 'moral' are mutually exclusive terms), — in short, the man of the personal, long, and independent will, competent to promise."

He is the one who has achieved "a proud consciousness (vibrating in every fibre), of what has been at last achieved and become vivified in him, a genuine consciousness of power and freedom, a feeling of human perfection in general." With mastery over himself he necessarily has "the mastery over circumstances, over nature, over all creatures with shorter wills, less reliable characters."

His "standard of value": looking out from himself, he honours (or despises) others exactly as he honours his peers — the strong and reliable (those who can bind themselves by promises, who promise like sovereigns: with difficulty, rarely, slowly, sparing with trusts, conferring honour by the very fact of trusting, giving their word as something that can be relied on even "in the teeth of disasters, even in the 'teeth of fate'"). He has "the heel of his foot ready for the lean and empty jackasses" and "his rod of chastisement ready for the liar."

"The proud knowledge of the extraordinary privilege of responsibility, the consciousness of this rare freedom, of this power over himself and over fate, has sunk right down to his innermost depths, and has become an instinct, a dominating instinct — what name will he give to it...? The sovereign man calls it his conscience."

This is the *proud* conscience — the late fruit of the long process that began with the cruel mnemonics of prehistory (blood, torture, "something is burnt in so as to remain in his memory").

## Key Arguments

- The breeding of an animal that *can promise* is the paradoxical task nature set for man, and the very problem of man. Requires both active forgetfulness (health, order, room for the new and noble) *and* its counter-power: an active memory of the will (continuing what has once been willed across intervening volitions, circumstances, even "fate").
- The "morality of custom" (the actual work of man on himself during the longest period of the human race) made man calculable. Its meaning and justification: at the end, the sovereign individual appears as the autonomous being who no longer needs the "social strait-waistcoats."
- Conscience in this supreme manifestation is the dominating instinct of responsibility and promising — not the bad conscience of internalization and self-torture (which is the earlier, sickly growth during the caging of the instincts).
- The process was not gentle: "How is a memory to be made for the man-animal?" Through the most dreadful sacrifices, forfeitures, mutilations, and cruel rituals. "All this is to be ascribed to" the need for a memory. "How dear is the price that they have exacted! How much blood and cruelty is the foundation of all 'good things'!"

The sovereign individual is the late, rare, "super-moral" achievement: the being who can be *trusted* because he trusts himself, who is his own security, and whose conscience is the name of his proud, unbroken will.
