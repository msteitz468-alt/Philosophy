# Know the Enemy and Know Yourself (Sun Tzu)

> One-sentence hook: Sun Tzu's most quoted maxim — "**If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles**" — making paired *self-knowledge and adversary-knowledge* the precondition of secure victory, and self-deception the root of defeat.

## Overview

The capstone of Chapter III ("Attack by Stratagem"), restated with cosmic scope in Chapter X ("Terrain"):

> "If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles. If you know yourself but not the enemy, for every victory gained you will also suffer a defeat. **If you know neither the enemy nor yourself, you will succumb in every battle.**" (III.18)

> "If you know the enemy and know yourself, your victory will not stand in doubt; **if you know Heaven and know Earth, you may make your victory complete.**" (X.31)

Knowledge is the operative variable: the three-way ladder (both / self-only / neither) maps to a clean expected-outcome gradient (secure / break-even / certain ruin). Chapter X extends the same logic to *Heaven and Earth* (timing and terrain — the impersonal conditions of [[Concepts/Calculation and the Five Factors (Sun Tzu)|the five factors]]): complete knowledge across self, enemy, and conditions makes victory "complete."

## Key Arguments

- **Self-knowledge is half the battle — and the half most neglected.** The symmetry is deliberate: you can lose by misjudging *yourself* (your strength, morale, logistics, limits) as easily as by misjudging the enemy. "Knowing yourself" is the harder, less glamorous discipline (cf. the general's **five dangerous faults** in Ch. VIII — recklessness, cowardice, hasty temper, touchy honor, over-solicitude — all failures of *self*-knowledge).
- **Knowledge is comparative.** This maxim is the conclusion the rest of the calculation serves: the **seven considerations** (Ch. I) are a structured *comparison* of self and enemy across the five factors; "know yourself and the enemy" is that comparison completed. It is also what makes [[Concepts/Winning Without Fighting (Sun Tzu)|bloodless victory]] possible — you can break an enemy's resistance only if you know exactly where it lies.
- **Knowledge must be of *this* enemy, not enemies in general.** Strategic knowledge is particular and current, which is why Ch. XIII insists [[Concepts/Foreknowledge and the Use of Spies (Sun Tzu)|foreknowledge]] "cannot be obtained inductively from experience… [but] only from other men." (See the contrast with empiricism below.)
- **Formless to the enemy, transparent to yourself.** The complement: while *you* know both sides, you keep the enemy ignorant of you ("be subtle, even to the point of formlessness," Ch. VI; the general "keeps his men in ignorance," Ch. XI). Asymmetry of knowledge *is* the advantage.

## Contrasts / Lineage

- **Self-knowledge across the vault**: a striking convergence with the philosophical "know thyself" tradition — the Delphic *gnōthi seauton*; [[Concepts/Memory - Interiority and Self-Knowledge (Augustine)|Augustine's interiority]] ("Let me know you, O my Knower"); Stoic self-examination ([[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)|knowing what is in your power]]); [[Concepts/Cogito (Descartes)|Descartes' self-certainty]]. Sun Tzu *instrumentalizes* self-knowledge for victory, where the philosophers seek it for wisdom or salvation — same discipline, different telos.
- **Vs. empiricism** ([[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]], [[Concepts/Knowledge by Acquaintance and Knowledge by Description (Russell)]]): Sun Tzu denies that knowledge of a *particular adversary* can be gotten from general experience (Hume's induction) — it must come from direct intelligence about *this* enemy. This is oddly close to Russell's principle that knowledge of a particular requires *acquaintance* (or description grounded in acquaintance), not deduction from universals.
- **Vs. [[Concepts/Power, Empire, and Revolt (Thucydides)]]**: the Athenians at Sicily failed *both* halves — overrating themselves, underrating Syracuse — the textbook "know neither → succumb."
- **[[Thinkers/Garry Kasparov]]/chess**: the purest arena for the maxim — objective assessment of one's own position *and* the opponent's resources and intentions; the player who flatters his own position or ignores the opponent's threats loses.
