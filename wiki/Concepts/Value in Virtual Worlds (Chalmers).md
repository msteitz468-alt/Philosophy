# Value in Virtual Worlds (Chalmers)
> Against Nozick: "In principle, life in virtual reality can have the same sort of value as life in nonvirtual reality... if it's bad, it won't be bad simply because it's virtual."

## Summary

*Reality+* ch. 17 answers the Value Question. Nozick's experience machine gave three reasons to refuse: we want to *do* things, to *be* a certain sort of person, and to contact deeper reality ("We want to be importantly connected to reality, not to live in a delusion"). Chalmers (with Dainton, Cogburn, Silcox) replies that VR is a **reality machine**, not an experience machine: users know they're in it, it isn't preprogrammed (interactivity makes genuine action, courage, kindness, and free choice possible — "If the physical actions are freely chosen, the virtual actions are freely chosen, too"), and it can be shared with real friends and family. Actions in VR are real actions on real digital objects ([[Concepts/Virtual Realism and Virtual Digitalism (Chalmers)]]).

He surveys four value theories — hedonism/experientialism (Bentham's "push-pin is of equal value with... poetry"; Mill's higher pleasures), desire-satisfaction (with Rawls's grass-counter objection), the Ubuntu/social view ("A person is a person through other people"), and objective-list views — and argues the positive verdict holds on all four. Genuine long-term gaps — nature, history, birth and death — are "optional values" or reachable by travel between worlds, outweighed by VR's benefits (unlimited space, new experiences, safe haven). The **Terraform Reality argument**: life in rich VR ≈ life on a terraformed planet ≈ ordinary life in value. Underlying axiology: "all value arises, one way or another, from consciousness."

## How This Relates to Prior Thinkers

- **Nozick**: main target; note the survey result Chalmers cites — 13% of philosophers would enter the experience machine, 77% would not — he is arguing against a strong intuition, not with it.
- **[[Thinkers/Plato]]**: the cave's verdict that the outside world is more valuable is exactly what Chalmers denies for virtual worlds ([[Sources/The Republic - Plato (Cambridge, 2000)]]).
- **[[Thinkers/John Stuart Mill]]** ([[Sources/Utilitarianism - John Stuart Mill (1861)]]) and Bentham: hedonist axiology accommodated, not endorsed.
- **[[Thinkers/Andy Clark]]**: Clark titled his book *The Experience Machine* to argue that ordinary perception already is one (predictive processing) — a deflation of the very virtual/real value asymmetry Nozick relied on; Chalmers reaches the same parity from metaphysics rather than neuroscience.
- **[[Concepts/Karma and Reincarnation as Quest Engine (Virk)]]**: Virk's simulation is a value-delivery engine by design; Chalmers argues value-parity without teleology.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] "All value arises from consciousness" quietly re-imports the anti-deflationist axiology — if [[Concepts/Qualia Disqualified (Dennett)]] is right, this ground of value needs restating.
