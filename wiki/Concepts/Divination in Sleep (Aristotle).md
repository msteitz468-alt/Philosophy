# Divination in Sleep (Aristotle)

> A skeptical, naturalistic verdict on prophetic dreams: they are neither straightforwardly credible nor dismissible with contempt. Most apparently prophetic dreams are pure **coincidence**; some genuinely function as **tokens** (of incipient bodily states) or **causes** (of subsequent waking action) because sleep's stillness amplifies otherwise-imperceptible "small beginnings." A god does not send dreams, since foresight in dreams disproportionately afflicts "commonplace" and even deranged persons rather than the wise.

## Summary

Aristotle frames prophetic dreams within an exhaustive threefold taxonomy — **cause**, **token (sign)**, or **coincidence** — of the event they seem to predict. Coincidences follow no universal rule, so most dramatic "fulfilled" dreams (of remote, extravagant, or agency-independent events) are simply chance, no more remarkable than an offhand remark that happens to come true. But because waking perception is dominated by comparatively strong movements, small internal bodily disturbances — the true incipient "beginnings" of disease or emotional affect — go unnoticed by day and become vivid by night; dreams can therefore be genuine **tokens** of bodily conditions, a claim Aristotle attributes even to "scientific physicians." More speculatively, a dream-image can be a **cause** of later waking action, since a nocturnal image can "pave the way" for daytime conduct just as daytime preoccupations pave the way for a corresponding dream. A divine sender is explicitly rejected: if dreams were god-sent they would come to the wise and occur equally by day, yet foresight clusters instead in mentally "vacant" or even deranged persons, whose lack of active, resistant thought lets faint alien movements register unopposed. Dreams have only a loose "divine aspect" insofar as nature itself is divinely ordered, not because they are themselves divine messages. Aristotle also explicitly rejects Democritus's rival theory of streaming "images" and "emanations" as unexplained, while adapting its underlying physics of propagating motion to his own account of nocturnal signal-amplification.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Cause / token / coincidence** as an exhaustive, general-purpose taxonomy for evaluating any purported predictive sign — a genuinely reusable epistemological tool, not specific to dreams.
- **Rejection of divine sending**: foresight in dreams belongs to the "commonplace" and the deranged rather than the wise, and does not occur equally by day — evidence against, not for, a divine source.
- **Signal amplification at night**: "the beginnings of all events are small"; sleep's quiet renders faint bodily and psychological "beginnings" disproportionately perceptible (thunder mistaken for faint ringing; honey for a drop of phlegm; fire for slight local warmth) — the mechanistic core of the whole account, medical and psychological rather than mystical.
- **Familiar-persons effect**: friends dream of one another accurately due to habitual mutual attentiveness, not mystical rapport — a sociological/attentional explanation of a folk phenomenon.
- **Rejection of Democritus's images/emanations theory** as causally unexplained, even while retaining and adapting his underlying physics of propagating motion.
- **The skilled dream-interpreter is a pattern-matcher**, discerning resemblances in blurred, disturbed presentations — likened explicitly to reading distorted reflections in agitated water, reinforcing that "prophecy" is a cognitive skill under noisy conditions, not supernatural insight.

## How Aristotle Uses This

Closes the *Parva Naturalia* sleep/dream sequence by applying the physiology of [[Concepts/Sleep and Waking (Aristotle)]] and the residual-movement theory of [[Concepts/Dreams as Residual Sense-Movements (Aristotle)]] to the popular belief in prophetic dreaming, converting a superstition-adjacent topic into a case study in his general theory of causation (cause/token/coincidence maps onto his broader efficient-cause and chance/spontaneity distinctions from the *Physics* and *Metaphysics*).

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Attributing a genuine **causal** role to some dreams (as "starting-points" of waking action) sits in mild tension with Aristotle's stricter treatment of chance/spontaneity as not a per se cause elsewhere in his corpus; the text itself restricts this to a narrow class of psychologically self-caused action (priming), so the tension is minor rather than a flat contradiction.
- No supernatural-divination source is yet ingested in the vault to anchor a dedicated Contradictions page against this naturalistic account; flagged as a candidate for one once such a source (e.g., Stoic divination, biblical prophecy) is added.
