# Formal and Material Ethics (Husserl)
> Husserl structures ethics in parallel with his pure logic: a formal axiology gives the a priori framework of value; a material ethics gives substantive norms grounded in will, practical reason, and compassion; the life-world is inherently a "value-world."

## Summary

Husserl's ethics — developed primarily in his *Lectures on Ethics and Value Theory* (1908–14) and scattered through *Ideas I* and *Ideas II* — parallels his philosophy of pure logic in structure. As pure logic is the theoretical foundation for normative/practical logic (rules of inference), **pure ethics** (formal axiology) is the theoretical foundation for practical ethics (substantive moral maxims). Both are a priori disciplines.

This is an underexplored part of Husserl's system. Smith notes that much of the account requires "reconstruction" — Husserl's ethics is less explicitly developed than his epistemology or ontology. The structural parallels are Smith's interpretive contribution, not direct quotation from Husserl.

## Formal Axiology: Pure Ethics

**Formal axiology** is the a priori theory of Value — the formal category that covers the essences *Good*, *Right*, *Better*, *Best*. Just as formal ontology studies being-as-such without specifying what exists, formal axiology studies value-as-such without specifying what is good.

**Ground norm (Grundnorm)**: Every normative domain has a ground norm — "not itself a normative proposition but a formal measure that 'defines' the normativity of all substantive norms within that domain." Kant's categorical imperative and the utilitarian principle of greatest happiness are each *ground norms* for their respective ethical spheres.

Husserl's own preferred ground norm (adapted from Brentano): **"Do the best that is attainable."** This is purely formal: what counts as "better" is determined by further material considerations. Like a Tarskian truth-definition, the ground norm specifies the formal structure of normativity without providing substantive moral content.

## Material Ethics: Substance and Love

**Material ethics** concerns the concrete, substantive content of morality. In his later lectures, Husserl holds that moral values are fundamentally **"values of love"** — constituted through love of the other, presupposing empathy (Einfühlung).

The material moral principle approaches something like "Love your neighbor as yourself." Moral worth has two levels:
1. **Formal level**: "Do the better" — acting on the best available action from among those open.
2. **Material level**: Willing motivated by both practical reason *and* compassion for others.

**Critique of competing ethics**:
- **Kantian ethics** (Verstandesethik): Excludes love and compassion from moral grounding; places all normativity in pure practical reason. Husserl: this is morally impoverished — reason without love cannot fully account for our obligations to others.
- **Utilitarian ethics**: Reduces morality to a calculus of aggregate happiness; removes the subject's will and practical engagement.
- **Humean/feeling-based ethics** (Gefühlsethik): Grounds morality in subjective feeling, losing objectivity.

All three commit a version of "psychologism" or "biologism" in ethics — reducing what should be an objective, a priori discipline to contingent facts about human nature or feeling.

## The Life-World as Value-World

A key claim from *Ideas I* (§27): The world is not experienced in the natural attitude as a mere fact-world (*Sachenwelt*) but immediately as a "value-world, goods-world, practical world" (*Wertewelt, Güterwelt, praktische Welt*). Values are features of the objects we encounter in everyday experience, not projections added on to a value-free nature.

This connects ethics to phenomenology: values are **constituted** in intentional experience — not created by will, not merely subjective, but intersubjectively accessible structures of the life-world grounded in acts of will, reason, and compassion.

**Noematic content of volition**: Just as a perceptual act has a perceptual noema (the object as perceived), a volitional act has a volitional noema with a thetic character ⟨will⟩ and a sense ⟨I hereby will to do A⟩. Pure ethics studies the ideal noematic contents and formal structures of volitional acts.

**Moral community**: *Ideas II* (§51): "Morally-practically, I treat a human being as a mere thing [Sache] if I do not take him as a moral [= morally relevant] person [Person], as a member of a moral association of persons." The moral world is constituted in moral-practical experience, presupposing the recognition of others as persons.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Immanuel Kant]]**: Husserl treats the categorical imperative as the *ground norm* of Kantian ethics — formally correct as a metanormative structure but substantively limited by excluding love/compassion. The comparison: Kant's categorical imperative functions like a Tarskian truth-definition for morality.
- **[[Thinkers/John Stuart Mill]]**: Utilitarian ethics makes happiness the ground norm. Husserl accepts this as a formally coherent structure but objects that the calculus eliminates practical engagement and treats persons as means.
- **[[Thinkers/Aristotle]]**: Husserl's material ethics — values grounded in a good human life, oriented toward love and community — has affinities with Aristotelian eudaimonia, though Husserl does not develop this connection explicitly.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Tension between objectivity and constitution: Husserl insists values are objective (not merely subjective projections) yet also "constituted" in subjective acts of will. Smith's resolution via intersubjective constitution leaves the precise mechanism underexplicated.
- > [!warning] Formal ground norm vs. material love: How the purely formal "do the better" constrains or interacts with the material imperative of love is not fully worked out in the available texts.
- > [!warning] Much of this account is Smith's reconstruction rather than explicit text from Husserl.
