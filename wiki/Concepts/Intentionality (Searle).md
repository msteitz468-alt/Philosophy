# Intentionality (Searle)

> The 'aboutness' or directedness of mental states — the fact that beliefs, desires, perceptions, intentions, etc., are *about* or *of* or *represent* something. For Searle, intentionality is the defining feature of the mental (along with consciousness and subjectivity) and is what formal symbol systems (computers) lack by themselves. The Chinese Room has syntax but no intentionality; minds have both.

## Summary

Searle (building on his earlier work in philosophy of language and action) treats intentionality as central to what minds are.

Mental states such as believing that it is raining, desiring a cold beer, seeing a tree, or intending to go to the store are intentional: they have conditions of satisfaction, they represent the world as being a certain way, they are directed at objects or states of affairs.

This semantic feature (content, meaning, aboutness) is precisely what is missing in the Chinese Room. The person (or program) manipulates symbols according to formal rules but has no intentional states with respect to the Chinese symbols — no beliefs or understandings *about* what they mean or refer to.

Searle argues that intentionality is a biological phenomenon (see Biological Naturalism). Brains have the causal powers to produce it; formal programs do not. Attributing intentionality to thermostats or computers (as some strong AI proponents do) is at best metaphorical or 'as if.'

The concept is used throughout the lectures to diagnose what strong AI and literal computational models of the mind get wrong: they capture formal structure but miss the intentional, semantic content that makes mental states mental.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Intentionality (aboutness, content) is essential to the mental.
- It is not reducible to or producible by syntax alone.
- Digital computers have no intrinsic intentionality; any apparent intentionality is derived from the intentionality of their designers and users.
- Brains produce intrinsic intentionality as a biological feature.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Core to the Chinese Room and the syntax/semantics critique of strong AI/computationalism. See [[Concepts/Chinese Room Argument (Searle)]], [[Concepts/Syntax and Semantics (Searle)]], [[Concepts/Strong Artificial Intelligence (Searle)]], and links to Turing (behavioral/functional accounts that ignore intrinsic intentionality). Contrasts with views that treat intentionality as purely functional or as something that can be ascribed on the basis of behavior or program structure alone.
- Ties to Searle's broader philosophy (speech acts, conditions of satisfaction).
- Open: How exactly do biological processes produce intrinsic intentionality? (The detailed mechanism is part of the still-incomplete neuroscience of consciousness and cognition.)
