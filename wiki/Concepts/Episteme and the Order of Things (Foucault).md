# Episteme and the Order of Things (Foucault)
> Each historical period is governed by an unconscious episteme — the deep rules that determine what kinds of statements can count as knowledge — and Western modernity's episteme of "Man" is about to dissolve.

## Summary

*Les Mots et les choses* (1966; English: *The Order of Things*) became an unexpected bestseller, selling its 3,000-copy print run within days and making Foucault a celebrity he found deeply ambivalent. The book's central concept is the **episteme**: not a worldview or ideology, but the unconscious set of rules governing the discursive space within which all knowledge of a given era is possible.

Foucault maps three successive Western epistemes:

1. **Renaissance (similitude):** Knowledge organized by resemblance, signature, and correspondence — the world is a text to be read for its hidden sympathies. Language is part of the world, not separate from it.
2. **Classical (mathesis / taxinomia):** Knowledge organized by analysis, measurement, and taxonomic classification. Language becomes transparent representation; the great tables of natural history, political economy, and general grammar are its products. The human subject does not appear here as an object of knowledge.
3. **Modern (the human sciences):** "Man" appears as both the subject who knows and the object of knowledge — an unstable doubling that generates the human sciences (psychology, sociology, anthropology). This episteme is historically contingent and, Foucault predicts, will dissolve: "Man would be erased, like a face drawn in sand at the edge of the sea."

## Key Claims

- The episteme is not caused by ideology, class, or individual genius — it is the anonymous precondition of all discourse in a period, including that of its critics.
- The transition between epistemes is a rupture, not a gradual evolution — archaeology reveals discontinuity where intellectual history sees progress.
- The "death of man" is not a value judgment but a structural prediction: the figure of Man is specific to one episteme and will not survive its dissolution.
- Foucault denied being a structuralist: his "discursive formations" used language differently from the Saussurean sign system — discourse produces its own objects rather than representing pre-given ones.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Sartre** attacked *Les Mots et les choses* as replacing "the motion picture of history with a series of static lantern slides" — the existentialist objection that Foucault's epistemes have no human agents making them.
- **Althusser** welcomed the anti-humanist thrust; the book was received (to Foucault's discomfort) as a "structuralist Bible" alongside Althusser's own anti-humanist Marxism.
- **[[Thinkers/Ian Hacking]]** develops a more localized version of epistemological discontinuity, acknowledging Canguilhem as the shared ancestor — compare his "styles of reasoning" to Foucault's episteme.
- **[[Thinkers/Thomas Kuhn]]**: paradigm shifts ≈ epistemic ruptures, but Kuhn's paradigms are local to individual sciences and driven by anomaly/crisis; Foucault's epistemes are civilization-wide and have no such internal mechanism.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- If the episteme is the unconscious precondition of all discourse in a period, how can Foucault's own analysis transcend it? The book generates a regress problem it never addresses.
- Foucault's private description of the book as "my book about signs" sits in tension with his public denial of structuralism.

> [!warning] The "death of man" thesis directly contradicts the humanist subject presupposed by [[Thinkers/Jean-Paul Sartre]]'s existentialism and by most of the vault's Enlightenment-era thinkers (Kant, Locke, Descartes). See [[Thinkers/Michel Foucault]] for broader context.
