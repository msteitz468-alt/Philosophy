# Scientific Revolution (Butterfield)

> Herbert Butterfield's framing (1949) of the "Scientific Revolution" — especially the 16th and 17th centuries, with roots reaching back to the 14th — as the pivotal event in Western history since the rise of Christianity. It "overturned the authority in science not only of the middle ages but of the ancient world", changed "the character of men’s habitual mental operations", transformed "the whole diagram of the physical universe and the very texture of human life itself", and constitutes "the real origin both of the modern world and of the modern mentality." Our conventional periodisation of European history thereby becomes "an anachronism and an encumbrance." The revolution was not a simple accumulation of correct facts or the victory of a timeless method, but a transformation in the way people thought and approached problems — a change of "thinking cap."

## Summary (Butterfield's account)

Butterfield presents the Scientific Revolution as a profound, multi-faceted intellectual and cultural transformation rather than a mere catalogue of discoveries. It involved:

- The displacement of ancient and medieval frameworks (especially Aristotelian physics and Ptolemaic astronomy, but also Galenic medicine and scholastic methods) by new ways of conceiving nature, evidence, and explanation.
- A change not only in specific doctrines but in "habitual mental operations" — how educated people in the West came to think about motion, the heavens, the body, causation, experiment, mathematics, and the very possibility of knowledge of nature.
- Pivotal but non-straightforward transitions: Copernicus' conservatism as well as his innovation; the "new" physiology of Harvey on the heart and circulation as an example of altered thinking; the methodological programmes of Bacon and Descartes; the mathematical-experimental work associated with Galileo and later Newton; the delayed maturation of chemistry (the phlogiston theory and its 18th-c. overthrow as illustration of different tempos across fields).
- Broader effects: the revolution did not stay within "science" narrowly conceived. It altered the texture of life, contributed to new attitudes toward progress and history, affected religion and theology, and by the later 17th century began to assume a "directing role" in Western civilisation as a whole.

Crucially, Butterfield stresses the need to understand the *older systems on their own terms* and to study the "intellectual knots," "hurdles," "misfires," and "blind alleys" rather than writing history as a simple march of precursors to present truth. The revolution "ended not only in the eclipse of scholastic philosophy but in the destruction of Aristotelian physics."

By the latter half of the 17th century the changes become "congested": political stability after mid-century upheavals, the intellectual power of (especially northern Protestant and Huguenot) middle-class circles, the shift of civilisational leadership from the Mediterranean (centre for millennia) northward to the Atlantic seaboard, and the emergence of science as a directing rather than merely one factor among others in culture and society. When "Western civilisation" later travels (e.g., to Japan), what is primarily transmitted is "the science, the modes of thought and all that apparatus of civilisation which were beginning to change the face of the West in the latter half of the seventeenth century."

The revolution is portrayed as a "creative product of the West" under a specific set of historical conditions that did not produce an equivalent transformation elsewhere (including in the ancient world or the Byzantine Empire despite their classical heritage).

## Key Claims / How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Butterfield (1949)**: The Scientific Revolution is the decisive break that creates modernity and the modern mentality; it outranks the Renaissance and Reformation in historical significance; it involved a change in the "diagram" of the universe and in habitual ways of thinking; proper historiography must recover the older frameworks and the actual intellectual struggles rather than anachronistic "anticipations."
- **Later historians of science (Kuhn and after)**: The "Scientific Revolution" narrative (exemplified by Butterfield and contemporaries such as Koyré) becomes the object of revision and complication. See [[Concepts/Scientific Revolutions and Paradigm Shifts (Kuhn)]]: most scientific work is "normal science" (puzzle-solving within a shared paradigm), not revolutionary; revolutions are discontinuous community conversions/gestalt switches rather than the simple triumph of superior evidence or a single, society-wide "change in thinking cap"; incommensurability means that across revolutionary divides scientists may "work in a different world" and do not simply accumulate or correct their predecessors in a linear fashion. Kuhn's work is in significant part a historiographical response to the older framing.
- **Philosophy of science more broadly**: The Butterfield narrative supplies one influential image of science as revolutionary break and progressive transformation of outlook against which 20th-c. accounts (Popper's emphasis on permanent critical rationality and falsification, Feyerabend's critique of any single "method," Hacking's turn to experimental intervention and entity realism) position themselves.
- **Relation to pre-modern thought**: The book describes (without always naming it as such) the displacement of the kind of hierarchical, continuous, teleological cosmos and epistemology traced in sources such as [[Sources/The Great Chain of Being - Arthur O. Lovejoy (1936)]] and the classical/medieval holdings.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **"One" revolution or many?** Butterfield acknowledges different fields moved at different speeds (chemistry "postponed") while insisting on an overall transformation in mentality and outlook. Later scholarship has debated the unity, timing, and even the usefulness of "the Scientific Revolution" as a single category.
- **Whig vs. contextual history**: Although Butterfield was a leading critic of Whig historiography, *Origins* has been criticised for presenting science as the triumphant emergence of modernity. The vault's Phil of Science sources (especially Kuhn) push back against overly progressive or present-centred reconstructions.
- **Continuity vs. discontinuity**: Butterfield stresses the break with ancient and medieval authority. Kuhn and others recover substantial continuities (normal science builds cumulatively within paradigms; some "revolutionary" figures retain older elements) while still affirming genuine discontinuities at the level of paradigms and "worlds."
- **Internal vs. external history**: Butterfield focuses on intellectual/mental transformations. Later work (sociology of science, externalist histories) asks how much the revolution's shape and timing depended on social, economic, religious, and political contexts — a tension already visible in the vault's Hacking and Kuhn materials.
- **"Change in thinking cap" vs. practice and community**: Butterfield's language of altered "habitual mental operations" is powerful but somewhat individual/psychological. Kuhn reframes the issue in terms of community practices, exemplars, and disciplinary matrices.

These tensions are best handled by cross-links (especially to Kuhn and Hacking) rather than a new dedicated Contradictions/ page at this stage. Flag, do not silently reconcile.
