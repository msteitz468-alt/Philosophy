# Emotive Theory of Ethics (Ayer)

> Normative ethical symbols are pseudo-concepts: they add no factual content to a sentence. "Stealing is wrong" does not describe a fact or express a proposition at all — it expresses (and excites) a feeling of moral disapproval. Ethical judgments are neither true nor false.

## The Core Argument

Ayer begins from the verification principle. Normative ethical claims ("x is good," "you ought to do y") are not analytic (their truth does not follow from definitions alone) and are not empirically verifiable (no possible observation settles whether stealing is wrong, independent of any factual claim about consequences or feelings). They are therefore *not literally significant* as propositions.

But the correct diagnosis, Ayer argues, is not that they make an unprovable claim to a non-natural moral reality (intuitionism) nor that they report the speaker's psychological states (subjectivism), but that they are **expressions of feeling**, not assertions of any kind:

> "If I say to someone, 'You acted wrongly in stealing that money,' I am not stating anything more than if I had simply said, 'You stole that money.' In adding that this action is wrong I am not making any further statement about it. I am simply evincing my moral disapproval of it."

The ethical word functions like "an exclamation mark, or a peculiar tone of horror" — it shows that the utterance is attended by certain feelings in the speaker, but *adds nothing to its literal meaning*.

A universal ethical claim like "Stealing money is wrong" expresses no proposition at all. It is equivalent to "Stealing money!!" where the punctuation marks conventionally express moral disapproval. There is *nothing here that can be true or false*.

## Four Types of Ethical Proposition

Ayer distinguishes:
1. Definitions of ethical terms / analyses of ethical concepts → **ethical philosophy** proper
2. Descriptions of moral experience and its causes → **psychology/sociology**
3. Exhortations to virtue → **ejaculations/commands**, not propositions
4. Actual ethical judgments (normative) → **expressions of feeling**, neither true nor false

Only (1) constitutes genuine ethical philosophy; (4) is the emotive theory's primary target.

## Distinction from Subjectivism

Orthodox subjectivism claims that "x is good" means "I approve of x" — a factual claim about the speaker's mental state, which would be true or false. Ayer rejects this. The difference:
- **Subjectivism**: "Tolerance is a virtue" = "I approve of tolerance" → *assertion*, can be contradicted.
- **Emotivism (Ayer)**: "Tolerance is a virtue" = evincing approval → *expression*, cannot strictly be contradicted (a man who says the opposite is expressing his opposite feelings, not contradicting a proposition).

When I say I am bored and simultaneously express boredom, the expression is not equivalent to the assertion. Expressions of feeling do not necessarily involve assertions about feeling.

## What Passes for Ethical Argument

If ethical claims cannot contradict each other in the logical sense, why do we argue about ethics? Ayer's answer: what seems to be a dispute about value is always, on inspection, a dispute about *facts*:
- Motives of the agent
- Probable consequences of the action
- Classification of the action under a general type
- Causal generalizations about what types of action tend to produce

If all factual disputes were settled and a moral divergence remained, argument would end — we would "resort to mere abuse." We cannot argue about pure questions of value because there is nothing to argue about: no claim is being made.

## Aesthetics

The same analysis applies to aesthetic judgments. "'Beautiful' is used to express certain feelings and evoke a certain response." A scientific treatment of aesthetics would explain why societies produce and admire what they do — ordinary psychology and sociology. Aesthetic criticism communicates emotion rather than transmitting knowledge.

## Theology Parallel

The same move applies to theology: "There is a transcendent God" is not merely unproven but meaningless as a proposition; therefore neither atheism ("there is no God") nor agnosticism ("it is uncertain whether there is a God") is appropriate. See [[Concepts/Metaphysics as Meaningless (Ayer)]].

## Ayer's 1946 Revisions

In the Appendix, Ayer acknowledges the theory is presented "in a very summary way." He adds:
- The common objects of moral approval are *types* of action, not individual acts; so many apparent moral judgments are actually factual classifications.
- A convinced utilitarian may actually *mean* by "right" something about general happiness — in which case their moral claim is empirically verifiable.
- The theory correctly identifies *purely normative* uses, but not all ethical language is purely normative.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Conflicts with [[Concepts/Duty (Kant)]] and the entire Kantian moral system: Kant argues moral judgments are *synthetic a priori* claims about what reason commands, with genuine truth-values. Ayer says they are neither analytic nor empirically verifiable, hence not truth-apt at all. The clash is fundamental.
- > [!warning] Conflicts with Aristotle's and Aquinas's natural-law ethics: both hold that moral truths are rationally cognizable facts about human nature, flourishing, or divine order — not expressions of feeling. See [[Thinkers/Aristotle]], [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]], [[Concepts/Natural Law (Aquinas)]].
- > [!warning] The Moore objection (which Ayer addresses): Moore argued that if subjectivism were right, ethical disputes would be impossible. Ayer concedes the same applies to his theory — but bites the bullet: we never really do dispute *values*, only *facts*.
- > [!warning] Ayer's theory makes moral progress hard to account for: if "slavery is wrong" expresses a feeling rather than states a fact, how can we say moral progress has occurred? This is a standard post-Ayer objection in metaethics (R.M. Hare, J.L. Austin, P.F. Strawson pressed this).

## Relevance to Vault

- **Metaethics cluster**: emotivism is one of three major 20th-c. metaethical positions alongside moral realism and cognitivist subjectivism. It represents the most radical consequence of the logical empiricist program.
- **Career/negotiation**: recognizing when a disagreement is about facts vs. values is a practical skill for sales and negotiation ([[Concepts/Persuasion & Sales Science]]). Ayer's sharp distinction between factual and evaluative is practically useful even if the underlying theory is contested.
