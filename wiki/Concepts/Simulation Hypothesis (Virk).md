# Simulation Hypothesis (Virk)

> The claim that what we call physical reality is a computed, video-game-like simulation — Virk's "Great Simulation" — with two crucially different versions: the NPC version (we are simulated consciousnesses) and the RPG version (we are conscious players inhabiting simulated bodies).

## Summary

Rizwan Virk's *The Simulation Hypothesis* (2019) presents the hypothesis as a convergence of three independent lines of evidence: (1) video game technology's trajectory toward the [[Concepts/Road to the Simulation Point (Virk)|Simulation Point]]; (2) quantum physics, whose indeterminacy and observer-dependence look like [[Concepts/Conditional Rendering and Quantum Indeterminacy (Virk)|conditional rendering]]; and (3) mystical and religious traditions East and West, whose doctrines of maya, karma, and the afterlife read as descriptions of a multiplayer game (see [[Concepts/Karma and Reincarnation as Quest Engine (Virk)]]).

The **Great Simulation** is Virk's name for the universe as a giant, consciousness-responsive video game combining MMORPG architecture (persistent shared world state stored outside the rendered world), VR-grade immersion, and mind-interface technology. The unifying ontological move is **information-first**: game state is pure information stored apart from its rendering, and both materialist neuroscience ("neural patterns = consciousness") and spiritual traditions ("consciousness enters the body at birth and leaves at death") converge, on Virk's reading, on the claim that "consciousness is really a set of information and a processing of that information."

## The NPC vs RPG Versions

- **NPC version** (Bostrom's default in the [[Arguments/Simulation Argument (Bostrom)]]): the simulated beings "are really artificially intelligent characters in simulations rather than conscious entities that have presence in the real world." Everyone we meet is already passing the Turing Test.
- **RPG version** (Virk's preferred): "there are billions of players who are inhabiting our consciousness" — conscious beings outside the simulation playing characters within it, though "nothing... says some percentage of the 'characters' we come in contact with couldn't be simulated consciousness."

This PC-vs-NPC distinction is the book's load-bearing philosophical fork: it determines whether the hypothesis is deflationary about consciousness (everything simulated) or a modern dualism (consciousness outside, avatar inside).

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Nick Bostrom]]** supplies the statistical engine (ancestor simulations, the trilemma) but is read by Virk as committed to the NPC version.
- **Philip K. Dick** (1977 Metz speech): "We are living in a computer-programmed reality, and the only clue we have to it is when some variable is changed."
- **Elon Musk** (2016): chances we are *not* in a simulation are "one in billions," from the extrapolation of game realism.
- **[[Thinkers/George Berkeley]]**: conditional rendering is a computational cousin of *esse est percipi* — the unobserved is not (fully) rendered. See [[Contradictions/Subjective Idealism vs. Physical Realism]].
- **[[Thinkers/Plato]]**: Virk closes the book with the Allegory of the Cave as the oldest statement of rendered-vs-base reality.
- **[[Thinkers/Ray Kurzweil]]**: Stage 10 "downloadable consciousness" is Kurzweil's [[Concepts/Mind Uploading (Kurzweil)]] repurposed; "there will be no distinction, post-Singularity... between physical and virtual reality."
- **[[Thinkers/Alan Turing]]**: Stage 9 (AI NPCs) is defined by passing the Turing Test plus embodied spatial competence.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] The RPG version's "conscious player outside the simulation" conflicts with [[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]'s deflationary account of consciousness and sides functionally with substance dualism (Descartes) — while the NPC version conflicts with [[Thinkers/John Searle]]'s claim that simulated minds are not minds ([[Contradictions/Hofstadter vs Searle - Strange Loops vs Chinese Room]]).
- Virk's "both paradigms agree consciousness is information" glosses over the hard problem: information-processing accounts are precisely what Chalmers-style arguments target.
- The hypothesis risks unfalsifiability; Virk's response is the empirical program in [[Concepts/Evidence of Computation (Virk)]].
- > [!warning] Opposite existential valence from [[Concepts/Simulation Hypothesis (Chalmers)]]: Virk treats simulation as a revelation that ordinary reality is game-like (with karma engine and quest structure); Chalmers argues that even if we're simulated, "Virtual objects really exist" and nothing ordinary is debunked ([[Concepts/Simulation Realism (Chalmers)]]). Chalmers also holds the *perfect* simulation hypothesis is untestable in principle — a direct challenge to Virk's evidence program.
