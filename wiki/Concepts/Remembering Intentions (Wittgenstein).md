# Remembering Intentions (Wittgenstein)
> The view that retrospective reports of intentions (e.g., "I was going to say...") are primitive, ungrounded descriptions of the past, justified not by recalling occurrent mental experiences but by the agent's dispositional states at the time.

## Concept Overview
In *Philosophical Investigations* §§633–693, Wittgenstein investigates how we remember past intentions and meanings (for example, knowing what you were going to say before you were interrupted, or knowing what you meant when you uttered an ambiguous remark). He challenges two standard philosophical views:
1. **Experiential Realism**: The view that our memory of an intention is true because we recall a specific, conscious, experiential state (a "logical germ" or mental rehearsal) that we were in at the time.
2. **Interpretivism**: The view that we figure out what we intended by retrospectively interpreting our past behavior and context.

Wittgenstein argues that when we say "I was going to say X," we do not recall any conscious mental event. Instead, our retrospective report is a **primitive, ungrounded report** within a shared language-game.

## Realism vs. Anti-Realism about Past Intentions
In *Wittgenstein's Philosophical Investigations: A Critical Guide* (2010), William Child analyzes this debate:
- **Anti-Realism (Crispin Wright)**: Wright argues that past intentions are not independent facts. Instead, they are retrospectively constituted by the agent's subsequent, sincere judgments. There is no fact of the matter about what I intended in the past prior to my current self-ascription.
- **Dispositional Realism (William Child)**: Child rejects Wright's anti-realism and argues that Wittgenstein is a dispositionalist realist. What makes a past self-ascription of intention true is not the current report, nor a past conscious experience, but the **whole complex of abilities, dispositions, and situational connections** the agent had at the past time. 

When an agent reports a past intention, they are describing a pre-existing connection (grounded in their past dispositions) while simultaneously making a new connection (in the act of retrospective judgment). This explains how intentions are real and objective without requiring a superstitious, inner "occult medium" to store them.
