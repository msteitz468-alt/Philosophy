# Analytic Propositions as Tautologies (Ayer)

> All propositions that are necessarily and certainly true are analytic — true solely in virtue of the definitions of the symbols they contain. They say nothing about the world; they record our linguistic conventions. Logic, mathematics, and geometry are all tautological. This defeats both Kant's synthetic a priori and Mill's inductivist account of mathematics.

## The Empiricist Difficulty

Every consistent empiricist must account for the *necessity and certainty* of logic and mathematics. If all knowledge comes from experience, how can we be certain that 7+5=12, or that no two things can be in the same place at the same time? Two options:
1. Say these truths are *not* necessary — just very probable inductive generalizations (Mill's view). But this is wrong: no experience could even in principle confute "7+5=12."
2. Say they are necessary but have *no factual content* — they are analytic, tautological, recording our linguistic conventions, not describing the world. This is Ayer's solution.

If there were a third option — necessary truths that also say something about the world (*synthetic a priori*) — then empiricism would be refuted. Ayer's task is to show that no such propositions exist: all apparent examples are really analytic in disguise.

## The Definition of Analytic

Ayer's criterion: **a proposition is analytic when its validity depends solely on the definitions of the symbols it contains**. Equivalently: it is a tautology — it cannot be denied without sinning against the rules which govern the use of language, i.e., without contradicting oneself.

(Emendation of Kant's definition: Kant gave both a psychological criterion ("predicate not thought in subject") and a logical criterion ("rests on the principle of contradiction alone") — these are not equivalent. Ayer retains only the logical criterion, made precise as: true in virtue of the meanings of the constituent symbols.)

## Why All A Priori Is Analytic

**Logic and mathematics**:
- Mathematical truths (e.g., "7+5=12") are defended against confutation by *adjusting the empirical scenario*, not by revising the mathematical proposition. If I count five pairs and get nine objects, I conclude I made a mistake in counting or observation — never that multiplication tables are wrong. This shows mathematics functions as a *tautological framework*, not as an empirical generalization.
- "The truths of logic and mathematics are true universally simply because we *never allow them to be anything else.*" They cannot be denied without violating the rules that govern the language.

**Geometry**:
- The existence of non-Euclidean geometries shows that the axioms of a geometry are simply *definitions* and its theorems are logical consequences of those definitions. A geometry is not "about" physical space — whether it applies to physical space is an empirical question *separate from* the geometry.
- This defeats Kant's view that pure geometry is synthetic a priori knowledge of the form of outer intuition. The necessity of geometry is logical, not transcendental.

**Against Kant's synthetic a priori generally**:
- Kant uses a psychological criterion (can one think the predicate without the subject?) to claim 7+5=12 is *synthetic*. But this conflates what is *easy to see* with what the proposition's validity *depends on*. Once you accept the definitions of 7, 5, and 12, their equation is a necessary consequence — no further appeal to intuition is required.
- "We show that the 'facts' which [the transcendental aesthetic] purports to explain are not facts at all." All supposedly synthetic a priori truths, properly analyzed, are tautologies.

**Against Mill**:
- Mathematical truths are not inductive generalizations from instances. The counter-instances Mill fears (twice five = nine in some case) are explained away by saying we miscounted, not by revising arithmetic. No finite series of observations could confute a mathematical truth — which shows it is not an empirical hypothesis.

## What Tautologies Do and Don't Do

**They do not**:
- Describe any matter of fact or any feature of the world.
- Give information about what exists.
- Extend our empirical knowledge.

**They do**:
- Call attention to the *implications of our linguistic usages*.
- Reveal previously unnoticed consequences of our definitions (hence: discovery and invention in mathematics are possible).
- Serve as *rules for the tautological transformation* of symbolic expressions (calculation = mechanized tautological transformation).
- Allow us to make our list of empirical information *complete and consistent*.

A being of infinite intellect would need no logic — it would see all consequences at once. Our limited intellects find tautologies *useful and surprising* because we cannot survey all implications simultaneously. The existence of logical/mathematical error is explained by the complexity of calculation, not by any non-tautological content.

## The A Priori Is Not About Language

In the 1946 Appendix, Ayer clarifies: analytic propositions are **not** empirical descriptions of how people happen to use words (that would make them contingent and revisable by survey). They *presuppose* linguistic conventions but are not identical with them. Given the rules for English "earlier," the proposition "if A earlier than B and B earlier than C, then A earlier than C" becomes necessary — the empirical convention presupposed is contingent, but the proposition, given that convention, is not.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Quine's "Two Dogmas" attacks the analytic/synthetic distinction directly: the notion of analyticity cannot be clarified without circular appeal to synonymy, meaning, or semantic rules — all of which are equally in need of explanation. If Quine is right, Ayer's whole program collapses (no clean analytic/empirical division, no verification criterion properly defined). See [[Concepts/Analytic-Synthetic Distinction (Quine)]].
- > [!warning] Hume's Fork already draws this distinction (relations of ideas vs. matters of fact), but leaves open whether it is *exhaustive*. Kant's synthetic a priori is precisely an attempt to exploit a gap in the exhaustiveness. Ayer closes the gap by re-classifying all apparent synthetic a priori as analytic — but this re-classification requires accepting Ayer's logical criterion, which Quine later refuses.
- > [!warning] The status of mathematics in light of Gödel's incompleteness theorems (1931 — just five years before LTL) is awkward: if arithmetic cannot be both complete and consistent, and if some arithmetic truths are not provable within arithmetic itself, is Ayer's reduction of all mathematical truth to "recording our linguistic conventions" tenable? Ayer does not engage this.

- **Epistemology leg**: the synthetic/analytic divide is the fundamental fault line of the Philosophy of Science leg. Ayer defends it, Quine destroys it, and Popper sidesteps it by making demarcation a methodological rather than semantic issue.
