# Biological Naturalism (Searle)

> John Searle's position on the mind-body problem: mental phenomena (consciousness, intentionality, subjectivity) are higher-level biological features of the brain. They are caused by neurophysiological processes in the brain and are realized in the brain (as features of the whole biological system). This is a form of naturalism (everything is part of the natural world) but insists on the irreducibility and reality of the mental as biological. It rejects both Cartesian dualism (mental substance) and the various forms of materialism that deny the reality of consciousness or reduce it to something non-mental.

## Summary

Searle presents biological naturalism as the commonsense, scientifically informed answer to the mind-body problem, consistent with what we know about biology and the rest of nature.

- Mental states are *caused by* the behavior of elements of the brain (lower-level neurophysiological processes).
- They are *realized in* the structure made up of those elements (higher-level features of the brain system as a whole).
- Analogy: just as liquidity is caused by and realized in the behavior of H₂O molecules, or solidity is caused by and realized in lattice structures, consciousness is caused by and realized in brain processes. No 'mental substance' is needed; no reduction that eliminates the mental is required.

This view accepts that the brain is a physical system but insists that the specific causal powers of biological brains are what produce consciousness and other mental phenomena. It is not committed to the idea that any system with the right functional organization (regardless of biology) would have a mind — that is the functionalist/computationalist view Searle rejects via the Chinese Room.

Biological naturalism explains the relationship without dualism (two kinds of substances) and without denying the obvious reality of our inner, subjective, semantic mental life.

## Key Claims / Positions

- The mind-body problem has a relatively simple solution once we take biology seriously: mental phenomena are biological.
- Causation + realization in the brain, not identity or elimination.
- Consciousness and intentionality are real, higher-level features of biological systems.
- This is consistent with modern science (no 'spooky' substances) while preserving what is obvious about the mental.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Direct alternative to the dualism Searle criticizes in the Cartesian tradition and to the strong AI / computationalist / functionalist views that treat the mind as purely formal and multiply realizable in any hardware (see [[Concepts/Strong Artificial Intelligence (Searle)]], [[Concepts/Chinese Room Argument (Searle)]], [[Concepts/Syntax and Semantics (Searle)]], and the Computationalism school on Turing concepts). Contrasts with purely physicalist or eliminativist pictures that would deny the distinctive reality of subjectivity and semantics. Links to [[Thinkers/Descartes]] (rejects substance dualism but keeps the mental as real and biological).
- Open: We still lack detailed mechanisms for exactly how specific brain processes produce specific conscious experiences (the 'how' question). Searle emphasizes our ignorance of brain function in many areas. Implications for whether non-biological systems could ever produce consciousness (Searle is skeptical on current evidence but the position is not dogmatic a priori).
