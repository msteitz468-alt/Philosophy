# History of Madness (Foucault)
> Foucault argues that "madness exists only within a society" — mental illness is not a natural category but the product of reason's institutional exclusion of unreason.

## Summary

*Folie et déraison: Histoire de la folie à l'âge classique* (1961) was Foucault's doctoral thesis, drafted primarily during his Uppsala years (1954–58) using the Carolina Rediva medical history collection. The book traces how "madness" became a distinct object of knowledge — and of confinement — not through the progressive advance of psychiatry, but through a series of historical ruptures.

The central argument: madness does not pre-exist its social and institutional definitions. "Reason" produced "unreason" as its constitutive outside. Once the category was created, the mad were confined — first alongside the poor and criminal in the *Hôpital Général* (founded 1656, "the Great Confinement"), then increasingly in purpose-built asylums governed by the new psychiatric profession. What appeared as Enlightenment liberation (Pinel famously unchaining the mad in 1794) was in fact the installation of a more insidious control: patients were now required to *internalize* the judgment of their own madness as a condition of treatment.

The book traces three historical periods:
1. **Renaissance:** Madness is adjacent to wisdom — the Ship of Fools (*Narrenschiff*), Erasmus's *Praise of Folly*, the figure of the wise fool. Unreason has a voice.
2. **Classical Age (17th–18th c.):** The Great Confinement. Madness is silenced by institutional exclusion — lumped with poverty, idleness, and criminality. Reason defines itself against unreason.
3. **Modern (post-Pinel):** Psychiatry claims to *liberate* the mad by giving them medical treatment — but in fact substitutes a subtler moral subjugation (confession of madness, therapeutic authority of the doctor).

## Key Claims

- Madness is a social construction, not a natural fact — its content and contours are historically variable.
- The Great Confinement (1656) was a socioeconomic as much as medical event: it swept up the unproductive poor alongside the mentally ill.
- Pinel's "liberation" paradox: removing physical chains imposed the stronger chains of therapeutic authority and the requirement to confess one's unreason.
- The history of psychiatry is not a history of progressively more accurate understanding but of shifting power relations between reason and its excluded other.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Derrida's 1963 critique: archaeological analysis of madness's silencing is itself an act of silencing — reason cannot speak for unreason. Foucault never fully answered this charge; his eventual hostile response sidestepped the epistemological problem.
- The book was rejected by Uppsala's Stirn Lindroth for being "too speculative" against positivist norms — suggesting the book's method was as transgressive as its content.
