# Genealogy (Foucault)
> Genealogy is a "history of the present" — a Nietzschean method that tracks the contingent power struggles through which norms, institutions, and forms of subjectivity came to seem natural and necessary.

## Summary

Foucault's decisive encounter with Nietzsche occurred in the summer of 1953, when he read the *Untimely Meditations* in Italy. Nietzsche's challenge — "where would you find a history of love, of avarice, of envy, of conscience, of tradition, or of cruelty?" — functioned as a program for Foucault's entire career. The genealogical method was fully deployed in the works of the 1970s, especially *Surveiller et punir* (1975) and the Collège de France lecture series.

Genealogy differs from archaeology in two respects:
1. **Power is central:** Genealogy traces how power relations invest discursive formations, not just the internal rules of discourse. The question becomes: whose interests does this knowledge serve, and through what mechanisms?
2. **Materiality:** Genealogy works on bodies, practices, institutions, and texts — not exclusively on discourse. It studies what Foucault calls "the effective history" (*historia effectiva*) — the actual consequences and uses of ideas and practices in specific power relations.

Genealogy is explicitly **anti-teleological:** it refuses to read history as progress toward a goal. What appears as liberation or reform is typically a transformation and intensification of control. This is the central irony of *Surveiller et punir*: the shift from public torture to the penitentiary is not humanization but the installation of more diffuse, efficient, and productive power.

## Key Claims

- There are no origins, only beginnings and their distortions: genealogy finds not foundations but accidents, power plays, and contingent victories.
- Normalization is the genealogist's primary target — the process by which historically particular norms become naturalized as universal or necessary.
- The "specific intellectual" (not the universal one) is the political form suited to genealogy: one who speaks from concrete knowledge of a specific institution, not in the name of universal humanity.
- Genealogy is explicitly motivated by present-day problems — it is a "history of the present" that aims to denaturalize current arrangements and show their contingency.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Nietzsche]]**: Foucault drew the genealogical method directly from Nietzsche's *On the Genealogy of Morality* and *Untimely Meditations* — tracing how "values" are produced by historical power relations, especially *ressentiment*. Foucault's genealogy retains Nietzsche's anti-essentialism and suspicion of progress narratives.
- **[[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]**: Marxist historiography also traces how ruling ideas serve ruling-class interests. Foucault's genealogy differs: power is not reducible to class, it is dispersed through micro-practices, and the primary site of analysis is the body/subject rather than the mode of production.
- **[[Thinkers/Ian Hacking]]**: Hacking's "historical ontology" shares genealogy's project of showing how human kinds are produced by historical processes — but is more locally focused and less suspicious of the possibility of genuine progress.
