# Return of the Soul (Epistrophe)

> In Plotinus' Neoplatonism (*Enneads*), the return (Greek *epistrophe*, "reversion" or "turning back") of the Soul is the natural, complementary movement to emanation (*proodos*). Having proceeded from the One through Intellect into the sensible world (descent, embodiment, multiplicity, potential "forgetting"), the individual soul retains an innate orientation toward its source and is drawn to purify itself, ascend through virtue and dialectic, and ultimately achieve mystical union (*henosis*, "oneness") with the One. This is the "flight of the alone to the Alone" (Ennead VI.9) — the true happiness (*eudaimonia*) of the philosopher, achieved not primarily by external action but by contemplative purification and "likeness to God." The material world and body are not evil prisons but the lowest image; the soul can and should turn back.

## Core Elements from the Source and Context

- **Descent and the Soul's Dual Nature**: Soul emanates from Intellect and "descends" to inform and govern the body/world (World Soul animates the cosmos; individual souls enter bodies). This introduces time, change, passion, and the risk of identifying with the lower (body, externals, multiplicity) — a kind of forgetting of origins (echoes Plato's Cave and Phaedo "forgetfulness").
- **Purification (Catharsis) and Virtue**: The return begins with separation from the body and passions. Virtue has stages: "civic" or political virtues (order in the lower self, justice in action) prepare for "purificatory" virtues (detachment, focus on the higher self/intellect). Virtue is "likeness to the divine" (assimilation, *homoiōsis theōi*).
- **Dialectic, Contemplation, and Henosis**: Philosophy (elenchus, hypothesis, ascent through the Forms) purifies and orients the soul. The highest is *theoria* (contemplation) — direct, non-discursive vision of Intellect and, ultimately, union with the One (beyond even Intellect's thinking). This is henosis: temporary or transformative oneness in which the soul "becomes" what it contemplates.
- **Not Escape but Fulfillment**: The return is the soul's *natural* motion and telos. Even while embodied, the philosopher lives the higher life inwardly. Action in the world can be informed by the higher (just governance as image of cosmic order) but is secondary to contemplation.
- **Universal Orientation**: All reality strives back (even matter indirectly desires form); the One is the object of eros (desire/love) at every level. No one is excluded; the path is open through reason and practice.

The reference file signals the treatises on the soul's nature, descent, and return (Ennead IV especially; also I, V–VI).

## From Wallis's *Neoplatonism* (1972)

- **Why contemplation can suffice (for Plotinus)**: a level of the soul never descends ([[Concepts/The Undescended Soul (Plotinus vs Iamblichus)]]); the return is a redirection of attention to what we already are. Virtue and dialectic are inseparable — "without dialectic virtue is imperfect; without virtue true philosophy is impossible" (I.3.6).
- **The final step**: union requires "divesting ourselves of everything" (V.3.17.38), putting away "all otherness"; the mystical state is an undifferentiated unity beyond subject and object, better figured as *touch* than vision; contact occurs through the "centre of the soul." The One does not turn toward us or love its products — eros runs upward only.
- **The school's great dispute**: Iamblichus denied the undescended soul, so contemplation alone cannot complete the return — ritual and divine grace are required ([[Concepts/Theurgy (Iamblichus)]]); Proclus added faith–truth–love as theurgic virtues and a providential *descending* love. See [[Contradictions/Plotinus vs Iamblichus - Contemplation vs Theurgy]] — Augustine's insufficiency-of-the-Platonists argument (already noted below) is the Christian analogue of Iamblichus's position.
- The Athenian School generalized *epistrophē* into the cosmic law of [[Concepts/Procession, Remaining, and Return (Neoplatonic Triad)]]: "All things pray except the First" (Theodorus of Asine).

- **Classical Synthesis and Contrasts**:
  - Vs. Plato: Deepens the Cave allegory, Phaedo soul-immortality, Republic Good, and Timaeus return to order — adds systematic hierarchy and explicit mystical union.
  - Vs. Aristotle: Eudaimonia as activity of the *intellect* in accordance with *its* virtue over a complete life; contemplation as highest (Nicomachean Ethics X is Plotinian in spirit).
  - Vs. Stoics: Both value "likeness to the divine," equanimity, and the examined life, but Plotinus orients toward transcendent return/contemplation while Stoics emphasize immanent acceptance of role, endurance, and duty within the world (dichotomy of control as preparation, not the whole path).
  - Vs. Epicurus: Return via contemplation/union vs. ataraxia via prudent withdrawal and simple pleasures in this life; hierarchy and eros for the One vs. atomist materialism and contractarian "Garden" community (both anti-superstitious and accessible, but different teloi).
  - Vs. Thucydides: The "real" life is the intelligible ascent, not immersion in the flux of power, empire, and rhetorical justification (though the philosopher may still act justly in the image-world).

## Augustine's Pilgrimage of Grace as Christian Transposition

Augustine's *Confessions* (especially Books 1–9 narrative + Book 10 memory search) provides the great personal, Christian version of the return (*epistrophe*). The "pilgrimage of grace" is the soul's descent into multiplicity (sin, Manichean materialism, divided will, incontinence) and its graced return to rest in the One (God) via purification, interiority, and the Mediator.

- "Restless is our heart until it comes to rest in you" = the eros for the Good driving the return.
- Book 7: explicit conversion to Neo-Platonism (Plotinus) as intellectual ascent and vision of the One — real but insufficient (ecstasy fades; will remains divided).
- Garden conversion ("tolle, lege") and baptism: grace effects the decisive turn; memory (Book 10) becomes the ongoing practice of return ("plumb the depths of his memory").
- Time as distention of the soul (Book 11) explains the temporal character of the pilgrimage; creation and return are ordered to Sabbath rest in the eternal.
- Contrasts with pure Plotinian return: requires the incarnate Word (not just contemplation); body and history are not merely to be escaped (hylomorphism and resurrection in Aquinas develop this); grace transforms rather than simply purifies the natural.

This directly enriches [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] (Augustine as the pivotal Christian reader who both uses and transcends the Enneads), [[Concepts/Restless Heart - Rest in God (Augustine)]], [[Concepts/Memory - Interiority and Self-Knowledge (Augustine)]], and the Aquinas pages (grace perfects nature; beatific vision as the fulfilled return). The vault's own process (scattered raw sources → integrated, cross-referenced wiki) is a secularized, practical *epistrophe* — with "grace" as user direction + LLM maintenance perfecting the "nature" of the sources and tools.
