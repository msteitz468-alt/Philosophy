# Res Cogitans and Res Extensa (Descartes)

> The two fundamental substances in Descartes's metaphysics: *res cogitans* (thinking thing, mind/soul: whose essence is thought—doubting, understanding, affirming, willing, imagining, sensing) and *res extensa* (extended thing, body/matter: whose essence is extension in length, breadth, and depth, with its modes of shape, size, position, and motion). They are *really distinct* (can be conceived and produced by God separately) yet in the human being are closely conjoined and fused into a single composite entity. Established primarily in the *Second* and *Sixth Meditations*.

## Core Claims

**From the Second Meditation (nature of mind)**: After the cogito, Descartes strips away everything dubitable. The 'I' is not body, not "rational animal," not subtle vapor. What remains is thought. "I am therefore, speaking precisely, only a thinking thing (*res cogitans*), that is, a mind, or a soul, or an intellect, or a reason." The wax example shows that even bodies are known by the intellect (extension, flexibility, mutability) rather than the senses—reinforcing that mind is known more clearly than body.

**From the Sixth Meditation (real distinction + existence of bodies + union)**:

- **Real distinction**: "I have a clear and distinct idea of myself, in so far as I am a thinking and not an extended thing, and, on the other, a distinct idea of the body, in so far as it is only an extended and not a thinking thing." Since God can produce separately whatever I can clearly and distinctly understand separately, mind and body are really distinct substances. The mind can exist without the body.

- **Existence of bodies**: Imagination (a mode requiring effort and directed toward a body) and sensation (passive faculty of receiving ideas that often occur against the will) are best explained if bodies exist. Given God's non-deceptiveness and the strong propensity he gave me to believe ideas come from bodies, it would be deception if they did not. Bodies exist at least with all the properties I clearly and distinctly understand (extension and its modes—the object of pure mathematics).

- **Union, not mere pilot-in-ship**: "Nature also teaches me... that I am not present in my body only as a pilot is present in a ship, but that I am very closely conjoined to it and, so to speak, fused with it, so as to form a single entity with it." This explains why pain, hunger, etc., are *confused* modes (not pure intellection) and why they guide the composite toward preservation. Sensations and appetites arise from the union.

- **Primary vs. secondary qualities**: In bodies, only extension, shape, size, position, motion, number, duration etc. are clearly and distinctly known. Colors, sounds, tastes, heat/cold as felt are obscure and confused; they do not resemble anything exactly in the bodies themselves but serve (via the composite's "nature") to signal benefit or harm.

## Key Distinctions and Tensions

- > [!WARNING]
  > Conflicts with [[Concepts/Subjective Idealism (Berkeley)|Subjective Idealism (Berkeley)]] on the existence of *res extensa* (material substance) — see [[Contradictions/Subjective Idealism vs. Physical Realism]].

- **Substance dualism**: Two really distinct substances (complete in themselves, needing only God's concurrence to exist). Not property dualism or hylomorphism (soul as form of body).
- **Interaction problem**: How do two substances with no common attribute interact? (A problem Descartes acknowledges in correspondence, especially with Princess Elizabeth; the *Passions of the Soul* addresses the union via the pineal gland as seat, but the *Meditations* leave the "how" largely open.)
- **Animals**: No minds/souls (or only mechanical souls); they are automata. Discussed in Objections (esp. 6th).
- **Human being**: A composite substance (mind + body united), not merely a mind using a body.

The Replies (esp. to Arnauld on the real distinction, to Hobbes on whether thinking implies body) clarify substance, objective reality, and that the cogito does not presuppose knowledge of what substance is.
