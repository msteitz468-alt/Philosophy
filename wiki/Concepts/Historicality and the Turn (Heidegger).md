# Historicality and the Turn (Heidegger)
> "History is not dead and gone, history is what we are."

## Summary

Historicality (*Geschichtlichkeit*) and the Turn (*Kehre*) represent, respectively, the final layer of *Being and Time*'s analysis and the transition to the later Heidegger. Together they show how [[Concepts/Dasein (Heidegger)|Dasein]]'s temporal structure extends into the communal and historical, and how Heidegger's own thought moved from Dasein-centred questioning to a receptive openness to Being's self-disclosure.

### Historicality (Geschichtlichkeit)

Heidegger rejects three prior solutions to the problem of historical relativism:
1. **Spengler's** acceptance: cultures are self-contained organisms with growth and decay; no transhistorical standpoint is possible.
2. **Platonism's** extraction: eternal truths transcend history.
3. **Simmel's** compromise: values are relatively stable amid historical change.

Heidegger insists that history (*Geschichte* — history as happening, distinct from *Historie*, historiography) is not something Dasein merely *studies* but something Dasein *is*. Dasein "stretches itself out between its birth and its death"; authentic historicality requires running ahead to death, choosing a possibility (one's **fate**, *Schicksal*), and adhering to it in self-constancy. **Destiny** (*Geschick*) is the communal counterpart: "the historizing of the community, of a people," orchestrating individual fates.

Dasein must "choose its hero" from the past and engage in **repetition/retrieval** (*Wiederholung*) — not reproducing a past possibility but conducting a "conversation" or "rejoinder" with it. [[Thinkers/Nietzsche]]'s three types of historiology (monumental, antiquarian, critical) map onto Dasein's three temporal ecstases; Heidegger claims "Nietzsche understood more than he has made known to us."

Inauthentic historicality is dispersed in the present: it yields world-history focused on objects rather than Dasein's own possibilities.

### The Incompleteness of Being and Time

*Being and Time* was left incomplete. The projected third section — moving from the analytic of Dasein to "being in general" — never appeared. Heidegger could not make the transition without either a Dasein-independent vantage point (which his own philosophy blocks) or reducing being to what Dasein makes of it (which would be subjectivism). This structural impasse motivated the Turn.

### The Turn (Kehre)

The Turn is the shift from *Being and Time*'s Dasein-centred analysis to the later Heidegger's emphasis on Being, truth, art, and language as impersonal forces that *constitute* Dasein rather than being constituted by it. In *BT*, Dasein discloses Being; after the Turn, Being discloses *itself* — through art, poetry, language, and thinking. Dasein's role shifts from active questioner to receptive listener.

Key expressions of the later position:
- "Language speaks, not man. Man only speaks when he fatefully answers to language" (PR 96).
- Art is not Dasein's creation but "the truth of beings setting itself to work" — truth happens *through* art, not *by* Dasein.

The Turn is debated: is it a break, a deepening, or a self-correction? Inwood treats it as continuous with the tensions already present in *BT*.

### World-Time and Now-Time

Between ecstatic temporality and "vulgar" clock-time, Heidegger identifies **world-time** — time as datable ("now, when the lecture begins"), spanned ("from dawn to dusk"), public ("it is time for…"), and significant ("too late"). Clock-time and **now-time** (*Jetzt-zeit*) are derivative, "levelled off" abstractions that strip away these features. Now-time appears infinite because of Dasein's "fleeing in the face of death."

[[Thinkers/Hegel]] is treated as "the last and greatest of the Cartesians" who accepted [[Thinkers/Aristotle]]'s conception of time and did not go beyond it.

## Key Claims / Positions

### How Different Thinkers Approach This

- **[[Thinkers/Hegel]]**: The *Phenomenology of Spirit* is a developmental history of consciousness. Heidegger parallels this structure but insists Hegel remains within the Cartesian-Aristotelian framework (subjectivism + now-time). Heidegger confronts Hegel explicitly at the end of BT.
- **[[Thinkers/Nietzsche]]**: *Untimely Meditations* II on history provides Heidegger's model for the three modes of historiology. The later Heidegger's Nietzsche lectures (1936–44) treat Nietzsche as the "last metaphysician" — the culmination, not the overcoming, of the tradition.
- **Dilthey**: Tried to do for history what [[Thinkers/Kant]] did for natural science. Heidegger credits Dilthey (and his friend Yorck von Wartenburg) for raising the question of Dasein's historicality but claims they remained within the framework of life-philosophy.
- **Husserl**: "Hardly interested in history" and held to a "banal platonism" distinguishing factual from ideal validity. Heidegger rejects any timeless realm.
- **Kierkegaard / Jaspers**: Sources for *Existenz*, *Angst*, and *Augenblick* that Heidegger secularises and ontologises.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] The concept of "choosing one's hero" and the language of *Volk*, fate, and destiny have obvious resonances with the political movements of the 1930s. Whether this language is essential to historicality or a contingent and dangerous expression of it is unresolved.
- The Turn raises the question of whether the later Heidegger can still be called a phenomenologist, since phenomenology in Husserl's sense requires a constituting subject.
- The structural impossibility of completing BT (the impasse between subjectivism and Dasein-independence) may be Heidegger's most honest philosophical admission.
