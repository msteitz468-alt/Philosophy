# Intersubjectivity and the Life-World (Husserl)
> Objects are constituted as "there for everyone" through empathy and intersubjectivity; the life-world is the pre-scientific, everyday world presupposed by all knowledge, whose alienation from modern science constitutes the "Crisis."

## Summary

Two of Husserl's most important contributions to philosophy — developed extensively in unpublished manuscripts (*Ideas II*, *Cartesian Meditations*, *Crisis*) — are his analyses of **intersubjectivity** (how other minds are experienced and how a shared world is constituted) and the **life-world** (Lebenswelt: the pre-theoretical everyday world that underlies all scientific knowledge).

## Intersubjectivity: Empathy and the Other

The phenomenological reduction appears to trap the phenomenologist in solipsism — only my own experience is given. Husserl's response: the experience of the *other* as a fellow conscious subject is itself a distinctive intentional structure that can be phenomenologically described.

**Empathy (Einfühlung)**: The primary mode of experiencing another person. Empathy is not an inference from analogy nor a hypothesis; it is an intuitive *transfer* of the sense "I" to the other — I experience the other's body as an animate, bodily-psychic being like myself. The other appears as another self, another center of experience.

Structure of empathy-experience:
1. I perceive the other's *Leib* (lived body) as a bodily-animate being, not merely a physical *Körper* (inanimate body).
2. Through *analogical apperception*, the other's body is co-constituted as a second center of orientation with its own kinesthetic experience.
3. The unified object "man" (person with body and psychic life) is constituted through this double constitution. Husserl (*Ideas II*, §46): "It is only with empathy and the constant orientation toward the psychic life … appresented along with the other's living body … that the closed unity, man, is constituted."

**Objectivity via intersubjectivity**: Physical objects are constituted as "there for everyone" — not just for me but for any possible experiencer — through the intersubjective constitution of a shared world. Objectivity is not a feature of individual experience but of *intersubjective* experience.

**The solipsism problem**: Husserl's *Cartesian Meditations* (Fifth Meditation) tries to reconstruct intersubjectivity from within the transcendental attitude. Smith acknowledges that Husserl was never fully satisfied with this argument. The challenge: after the epoché brackets all worldly commitments, how can we reconstitute *other* minds as genuinely other?

**Edith Stein** (Husserl's student) deepened the analysis of empathy in her 1916 dissertation *On the Problem of Empathy*.

## The Life-World (Lebenswelt)

The **Lebenswelt** is the world as constituted in everyday experience prior to and presupposed by scientific theorisation. It is not a separate domain of objects but a structure of meaning — the always-already-operative background horizon within which all experience, including scientific experience, takes place.

Features of the life-world:
- **Pre-scientific**: Constituted before and independently of mathematical physics.
- **Value-laden**: Not a mere fact-world but a "value-world, goods-world, practical world" (*Ideas I*, §27). Values, uses, and practices are as much features of the life-world as spatial extension.
- **Intersubjective**: Shared among members of a community; constituted through empathy and cultural transmission.
- **Historically sedimented**: Beliefs and meanings passed down from prior generations — the "sedimented" layer of all knowing. Most of what any individual "knows" consists of culturally transmitted judgments constituting the bulk of the life-world's meaning-structure.
- **Anti-skeptical ground**: Even the skeptic's questioning presupposes the life-world. The life-world cannot be genuinely suspended — any epoché leaves it operative as the horizon of every intentional experience.

**Epistemic role**: All scientific knowledge — from everyday perceptual judgment to mathematical physics — presupposes the life-world as its ground. Forgetting this ground creates alienation.

## The Crisis of European Sciences

In the *Crisis* (1935–38), Husserl diagnoses a *Krisis* in European culture: modern natural science, since Galileo's "mathematization of nature," has become alienated from the life-world. Mathematical idealizations are *taken for* the things themselves. The "garb of ideas" (Newton's laws, differential equations) is "draped over" lived experience and mistaken for the underlying reality.

"A kind of alienation has set in, whereby we view the essence of space-time and natural things and ultimately ourselves and our humanity in idealized mathematical terms that do not seem to connect with our everyday life."

Husserl connects this crisis to the rise of irrationalism (including Nazism): when science loses touch with the life-world of human values and purposes, it cannot answer the question of how to live.

**Transcendental phenomenology as the remedy**: By grounding scientific idealizations in the structures of lived experience, phenomenology can reconnect science to the life-world without abolishing science. The *Crisis* argues that this reconnection is the proper task of European philosophy.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]]**: "Being-in-the-world" is Heidegger's structural parallel to Husserl's life-world — Dasein is always already practically absorbed in a world of equipment and significance. Heidegger's concept is existential-ontological; Husserl's life-world is transcendental-phenomenological. Key difference: for Husserl, the life-world can be made the *object* of a phenomenological analysis that grounds science; for Heidegger, there is no standpoint "above" the world from which to survey it.
- **[[Thinkers/Jean-Paul Sartre]]**: Sartre's "being-with-others" and "the Look" describe intersubjectivity from an existential (and conflictual) angle. Where Husserl grounds the other in empathy (a sympathetic, constituting directedness), Sartre grounds the other in the Look (an objectifying, freedom-negating encounter). See [[Concepts/The Look and the Other (Sartre)]].
- **[[Thinkers/Ludwig Wittgenstein]]**: Wittgenstein's "forms of life" (Lebensformen) in the *Philosophical Investigations* are structural parallels to the life-world — the irreducible, pre-theoretical background of shared practice that makes language and meaning possible. Neither can be "grounded" from outside; both are where justification comes to an end. See [[Concepts/Forms of Life (Wittgenstein)]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] The solipsism problem: After the phenomenological reduction, how can we access the genuinely *other* mind? Husserl's Fifth Meditation solution via analogical apperception has been widely criticised as insufficient, and Husserl himself regarded it as incomplete.
- > [!warning] The "crisis" diagnosis: Is Galilean mathematization merely a methodological abstraction (correctable by phenomenological grounding) or does it reflect something deep about the structure of nature that cannot be life-world-grounded? The natural-scientific image of the world may be simply incommensurable with the life-world, not reducible to it.
