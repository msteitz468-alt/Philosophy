# The Three Disciplines — Perception, Action, and Will (Hadot)

> The architecture of Stoic practice as reconstructed by Pierre Hadot from Epictetus's **three *topoi*** (fields of exercise) and Marcus Aurelius's *Meditations*, and popularized by Ryan Holiday in *The Obstacle Is the Way* as **Perception → Action → Will**. Each discipline governs one relation of the self to the world: how we *judge* what happens (perception), how we *act* toward others and our goals (action), and how we *accept* what is beyond our power (will). They are "interdependent, interconnected, and fluidly contingent" — practiced in sequence but feeding back into one another.

## The Three Fields

Epictetus (*Discourses* III.2) names three *topoi* the student must train in; Hadot, in *The Inner Citadel*, shows the *Meditations* is organized around them. Holiday renames and sequences them for a modern reader. For the primary text itself — Epictetus's own statement of the three fields, their fixed order (desire first), and the *askēsis* that works them — see [[Concepts/The Three Fields of Training - Desire, Action, and Assent (Epictetus)]].

| Discipline | Epictetan *topos* | Governs | Holiday's gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Perception** | assent to impressions (*sunkatathesis*) | judgment / the mind | See things objectively, "neither good nor bad"; strip the added "I." |
| **Action** | impulse to act (*hormē*) | doing / the body | Directed, bold, persistent, creative action toward the good and toward others. |
| **Will** | desire and aversion (*orexis*) | acceptance / the heart & soul | Endure and love what cannot be changed; the "inner citadel." |

Holiday's summary formula: *"First, see clearly. Next, act correctly. Finally, endure and accept the world as it is."* Or, compressed: *"See things for what they are. Do what we can. Endure and bear what we must."*

> Note: Holiday reorders Epictetus's canonical sequence (which puts desire/aversion *first* as the most fundamental) for narrative and motivational reasons, opening with perception and reserving acceptance for the climactic third act. The philosophical content is Hadot's; the ordering is Holiday's.

## How the Disciplines Interlock

- **Perception precedes action**: "Where the head goes, the body follows." Only after judgment is cleansed of fear and the added self can action be directed rather than reactive.
- **Action supports will**: "We prove and support our will with our actions"; having *tried* everything within our power is what makes acceptance of failure non-cowardly.
- **Will backstops both**: perception and action can be "thwarted or inhibited" by the world; the will alone is "the one thing we control completely, always" — the fallback when agency disappears.

This maps onto the [[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)]]: perception and will are almost entirely "up to us"; action is the contested zone where our effort meets an indifferent world.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Epictetus** — originates the three *topoi* as a curriculum: master desire/aversion first (to secure equanimity), then impulse (to act with justice toward others), then assent (to judge truly). See [[Thinkers/Epictetus]], [[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)]].
- **Marcus Aurelius** — the *Meditations* enacts all three under the pressure of empire: guarding the ruling faculty ([[Concepts/Ruling Faculty and the Daemon (Marcus Aurelius)]]), acting "for the common good... as a limb of the whole," and cheerfully accepting the assigned portion and death. The epigraph Holiday builds the book on — "Objective judgment... Unselfish action... Willing acceptance of all external events. That's all you need" — is itself a one-line statement of the three disciplines.
- **Pierre Hadot** — recovers the disciplines as **spiritual exercises** (*askēsis*), arguing ancient philosophy was a way of life, not a theory; his *Inner Citadel* is the scholarly source Holiday leans on. See [[Concepts/The Inner Citadel (Hadot)]].
- **Ryan Holiday** — turns the triad into a self-help program illustrated by historical vignettes; see [[Thinkers/Ryan Holiday]] and [[Concepts/Turning Obstacles Upside Down (Holiday)]].

## Relevance to Vault

- Provides the unifying **framework page** the vault previously lacked: the scattered Marcus/Epictetus concepts (dichotomy of control, ruling faculty, view from above, every act as if the last, *amor fati*, *premeditatio malorum*, *memento mori*) all slot into one of the three disciplines.
