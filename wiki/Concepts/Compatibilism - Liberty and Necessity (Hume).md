# Compatibilism - Liberty and Necessity (Hume)

> Hume's "reconciling project" (*Enquiry* Section VIII): the ancient dispute between liberty and necessity is "merely verbal," and once the terms are properly defined, *all mankind have always agreed in both*. **Necessity** is nothing more than the constant conjunction of similar objects plus the mind's inference from one to the other — and these two circumstances demonstrably hold of human action. **Liberty** is "a power of acting or not acting, according to the determinations of the will" — not an absence of causes (which would be mere chance), which belongs to everyone "who is not a prisoner and in chains." Far from conflicting, both are *essential to morality*. The classic statement of compatibilism.

## Redefining Necessity (and Finding It in Human Action)

Hume's account of [[Concepts/Necessary Connexion and the Two Definitions of Cause (Hume)|causal necessity]] does the work. Since "necessity" in matter is *only* constant conjunction + inferential transition (we never observe a deeper tie), the question becomes: do these two circumstances obtain in human conduct? Manifestly:

- **Constant conjunction — the uniformity of human nature**: "Mankind are so much the same, in all times and places, that history informs us of nothing new or strange." The same motives (ambition, avarice, friendship, public spirit) produce the same actions; study the French and English and you understand the Greeks and Romans. History's "chief use" is to reveal "the constant and universal principles of human nature." A traveller reporting men with no avarice or ambition would be disbelieved like one telling of "centaurs and dragons."
- **Inference** — we constantly and confidently infer actions from motives and characters: the whole of social life, commerce, politics, history, and criticism presupposes it. Apparent irregularities are like a stopped watch — not a failure of necessity but the "secret operation of contrary causes." Crucially, "natural" and "moral" evidence form *one chain*: the prisoner foresees his death as certainly from "the constancy and fidelity of his guards" as from "the operation of the axe."

So everyone has always *tacitly* held the doctrine of necessity as applied to the will — they only "reluctantly acknowledge it in words," because, finding no felt connexion in their own volitions, they wrongly suppose matter has a deeper necessity that mind lacks. Correct the error about matter and the dispute dissolves.

## Redefining Liberty

By **liberty** we cannot mean that actions have *no* connexion with motives — that would be the denial of the obvious. Nor can it mean uncaused action: "chance… is universally allowed to have no existence," and "liberty, when opposed to necessity, not to constraint, is the same thing with chance." Properly, liberty is **hypothetical liberty**: "if we choose to remain at rest, we may; if we choose to move, we also may" — a power of acting *according to the determinations of the will*, opposed not to *causation* but to *constraint* (chains, coercion). This belongs to "every one who is not a prisoner."

## Both Are Essential to Morality

Hume reverses the usual fear that determinism destroys responsibility:

- **Necessity is required**: rewards, punishments, praise, and blame work only because motives "have a regular and uniform influence on the mind." Blame attaches to actions only as "proofs of criminal principles in the mind" — i.e., as flowing from durable character. "Actions are, by their very nature, temporary and perishing; and where they proceed not from some cause in the character… they can neither redound to his honour, if good, nor infamy, if evil." Repentance "wipes off every crime" precisely because it alters the enduring principles. Without the action-character connexion (necessity), there is no ground for responsibility at all.
- **Liberty is required**: actions are objects of moral sentiment "so far only as they are indications of the internal character"; if derived "altogether from external violence" (constraint), they bear no moral quality.

Hume concedes one hard residue: if the whole causal chain traces back to God as "ultimate Author," then either human actions are not criminal or God is the author of sin. He answers the first horn (the Stoic "all is good in the whole" consolation is "weak and ineffectual" against the natural moral sentiments) but admits the second is a "mystery" beyond "natural and unassisted reason" — returning philosophy to "her true and proper province, the examination of common life."

## Relevance to Vault

- **Free-will cluster**: Hume's hypothetical liberty + uniformity is the *modern default* on free will, and a distinctive third position among the vault's options. Contrast **Spinoza** (harder necessitarianism: freedom is acting from one's own nature via adequate ideas, no contingency at all — [[Concepts/Freedom and Blessedness - Intellectual Love of God (Spinoza)]]); **the Stoics** (fate + freedom in assent — [[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)]]); **Descartes** (a robust, near-libertarian freedom of the will to affirm/withhold assent); **Epicurus/Lucretius** (the *swerve* introduced precisely to secure libertarian freedom — [[Concepts/Epicurean Atomism (Swerve)]], which Hume's compatibilism makes unnecessary).
- **Method / character**: the "uniformity of human nature" thesis underwrites the vault's practical-judgment threads — it is the working premise of [[Concepts/Persuasion & Sales Science]], negotiation, prospecting, and the reading of character (inferring conduct from motive and situation). Responsibility-as-flowing-from-durable-character also informs how the vault evaluates patterns of action over time.
- **AI / agentic**: a deterministic *system* can still be meaningfully "free" in the compatibilist sense (acting per its own deliberation/policy, unconstrained) and a fit subject of praise/blame/correction (reward and punishment work *because* motives reliably shape action). Useful framing for agent alignment, accountability, and the relation between determinism and responsibility.

**Open questions**:
- Is "hypothetical liberty" (acting per one's will) *enough* for moral responsibility, or does it leave the will itself untouched by genuine alternatives (the "could have done otherwise" worry)?
- Hume's own unresolved horn: how to free the ultimate cause (God/world) from authorship of sin — a "mystery" he declines.
- Does the uniformity thesis overstate cross-cultural sameness (a tension with later historicism and his own *History*)?
