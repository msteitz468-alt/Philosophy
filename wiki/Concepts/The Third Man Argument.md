# The Third Man Argument

> An infinite-regress objection to the Theory of Forms: if a single Form is posited to explain what many things have in common, the Form plus those things form a new "many" needing yet another Form, and so on without end — so there is never *one* Form but "an unlimited multitude." The name (τρίτος ἄνθρωπος, "third man") comes from the standard example — over many men and the Form Man, a "third man" must be posited.

## The Argument

Given a set of large things, we posit **one** Form, Largeness, by which they are all large (the *One-over-Many* premise). But Largeness itself is large (*self-predication*), and it is distinct from the large things (*non-identity*). So Largeness together with the original large things is a new plurality of large items, requiring a **further** Largeness to explain *their* common largeness — and that regress never stops:

> "So another Form of 'largeness' will appear again alongside the 'largeness' that has come to be and the things that partake in it… So each of your Forms will no longer be one, but unlimited in multitude." — *Parmenides* 132a–b

A second, structurally identical regress follows the **paradigm** model (132d–133a): if things resemble the Form as copies resemble a pattern, the pattern is like the copy, requiring a Form of their shared likeness, endlessly.

The regress is driven by the tacit premises **self-predication** (the Form of F is itself F) and **non-identity / non-self-partaking** (the Form of F is not identical to, and does not partake of, the very Form that explains F-ness). Modern reconstructions (Vlastos) show the two premises are jointly inconsistent with One-over-Many.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Plato (origin):** The argument is stated *by Plato himself*, in Parmenides' mouth, against young Socrates — a **self-critique**, not an external attack. It sits within the broader [[Concepts/Participation Problems and the Critique of the Forms (Plato Parmenides)|participation critique]]. Plato does not there resolve it; he redirects to the dialectical gymnastic of Part II.
- **Aristotle:** repurposes it as a decisive weapon against separate Forms in the *Metaphysics* (I.9, VII.13) — see [[Concepts/Critique of Platonic Forms (Aristotle)]]. That the objection is Plato's own is the sharp point: [[Contradictions/Plato vs Aristotle on the Forms]] begins inside Platonism.
- **Later logic:** a paradigm case of a **vicious infinite regress** generated by a universal that must instantiate itself; recurs in debates on universals, self-reference, and (loosely) in [[Thinkers/Douglas Hofstadter]]'s strange loops as a benign vs. vicious contrast.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] Is self-predication ("Largeness is large") a genuine Platonic commitment or a confusion between the Form and its instances? If Forms are not self-predicating, the regress may not bite — a standing interpretive dispute.
