# Critique of Ostensive Definition (Wittgenstein)
> Pointing and saying "that is called X" cannot be the foundation of meaning, because the pointing can always be interpreted in many ways — it teaches only when a place for the word in language is already prepared.

## Summary

The *Philosophical Investigations* opens (§1) with a quotation from [[Thinkers/Augustine|Augustine]]'s *Confessions* depicting how he learned language: elders named objects by pointing, and he grasped that the sounds stood for the things. [[Thinkers/Ludwig Wittgenstein|Wittgenstein]] takes this homely picture as the crystallization of a deep and pervasive theory — that every word names an object, and that **ostensive definition** ("*that* is called 'red'," pointed) is the bedrock connecting word to world. Much of §§1–64 is a sustained demonstration that ostension cannot play this foundational role.

The core objection: "an ostensive definition can be variously interpreted in *every* case" (§28). If I point at two nuts and say "that is called 'two'," the learner might take "two" to name the colour, the shape, the kind of nut, or that very pair. Pointing fixes nothing by itself; it succeeds only against a background grasp of what *kind* of word is being explained — "one has already to know (or be able to do) something in order to be capable of asking a thing's name" (§30).

## Core Mechanics

### 1. The "place" must already be prepared
"The ostensive definition explains the use—the meaning—of the word when the overall role of the word in the language is clear" (§30). To point at a chess piece and say "this is the king" teaches a beginner only if he already knows that this is a game and that pieces have moves — "the place is already prepared" (§31). Ostension does not create meaning from nothing; it fills a slot that the surrounding [[Concepts/Language-Games (Wittgenstein)|language-game]] has already cut.

### 2. Pointing does not interpret itself
The gesture of pointing, and the act of "directing one's attention," are themselves variously interpretable (§§33–35). There is no special inner experience of "meaning *the shape*" rather than the colour that accompanies and disambiguates the pointing. What disambiguates is the technique of use the learner has, or lacks.

### 3. Naming is not yet a move
Even where ostension works, naming "is so far not a move in the language-game—any more than putting a piece in its place on the board is a move in chess. We may say: *nothing has so far been done, when a thing has been named*" (§49). Naming is a *preparation* for using a word (§26). This severs the Augustinian assumption that to attach a name to a thing is already to have said something.

### 4. The standard metre
The famous example: "There is one thing of which one can say neither that it is one metre long, nor that it is not one metre long, and that is the standard metre in Paris" (§50). The paradigm by which we *define* a measure is not itself something measured by it — names and samples are *instruments* of the language, not items the language is about. This dissolves the *Tractatus*'s demand for indestructible "simple objects" as the ultimate bearers of names (§§46–48).

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. [[Thinkers/Augustine|Augustine]] and the naming tradition:** Augustine's picture (and with it much of empiricist theories of concept-acquisition, e.g. [[Thinkers/Locke|Locke]]) treats word-learning as the labelling of antecedently given objects/ideas. Wittgenstein shows the labelling already presupposes a mastered practice.
- **vs. the early Wittgenstein:** The *Tractatus* required simple names correlated by their meaning with simple objects. The critique of ostension and of "simples" (§§46–48, §§60–64 — the broom and its broomstick) undermines that semantic atomism. See [[Contradictions/Early vs Later Wittgenstein]].
- **Feeds [[Concepts/Meaning as Use (Wittgenstein)|Meaning as Use]]:** Since attaching a name accomplishes nothing until the word is *used*, meaning must lie in use, not in the name–object link.
