# Multiple Drafts Model (Dennett)

> Dennett's central positive theory of consciousness in *Consciousness Explained* (1991): all conscious experience consists of parallel, distributed editorial processes running continuously in the brain, with no single location or moment that constitutes "the" finish line where consciousness occurs. There is no canonical "final draft" — only an ongoing process of content-fixation, revision, and re-revision. This is the direct replacement for the [[Concepts/Cartesian Theater (Dennett)|Cartesian Theater]].

## The Core Claim

The brain does not produce a single authoritative representation that is "presented" to a central observer (the Cartesian Theater). Instead, many parallel streams of content-processing — probes, edits, revisions — run simultaneously in different neural circuits. At any given moment, what can be elicited (by verbal report, button-press, or later recall) is a function of *when* you tap into the process, not a readout of a fixed inner showing.

**Convergence with Minsky**: The Multiple Drafts model is strongly convergent with Marvin Minsky's "Society of Mind" (1986) theory of consciousness as the product of interactions among many simple "agents" with no central self or executive. Minsky's B-brains (higher-level agencies that monitor and influence lower ones) and emphasis on limited, interference-prone self-examination provide mechanistic support for Dennett's claim that there is no privileged "finish line" and that introspection is itself just more content fixation subject to revision. Both treat the apparent unity of consciousness and self as emergent from distributed processes rather than a given. See [[Sources/The Society of Mind - Marvin Minsky (1986)]] and [[Thinkers/Marvin Minsky]].

Key features:
1. **No finish line**: There is no place in the brain where "it all comes together." Feature detections do not have to be re-presented to a master observer.
2. **Continuous revision**: Content is under editorial revision until it is used (in report, action, or further processing). "Editing" is not a metaphor — drafts are genuinely overwritten and re-represented.
3. **No canonical order**: Events that seem temporally ordered in experience may not have been processed in that order. The apparent sequence is a narrative reconstruction, not a replay of a fixed inner film.
4. **No absolute timing**: Asking "exactly when did I become conscious of X?" is a category error — there is no single "time of experience" because there is no Cartesian Theater finish line against which to clock it.

## Color Phi Phenomenon (Key Evidence)

Kolers and Goodman's experiment: two spots of light appear in rapid succession at different screen positions — first red, second green. Subjects report seeing the *first* spot change color *mid-trajectory* before the second spot is perceived.

The brain cannot precognize. Therefore, the color-change experience must have been generated *retroactively* — the arrival of the second (green) spot caused the first spot's representation to be revised (or the two spots' representations were fused into a single motion-with-color-change trajectory). Under the Cartesian Theater model, there would have to be a moment when the first spot was "consciously red" before any revision — but no such moment is identifiable. Under Multiple Drafts, there is no fact of the matter about whether the color change was "really" experienced at the time of the first spot or attributed to it later.

## Orwellian vs. Stalinesque Revisions

At macroscopic timescales, one can distinguish two kinds of cognitive distortion:
- **Orwellian revision**: a representation was formed but then memory was retroactively rewritten (like Orwell's *1984* — history altered after the fact).
- **Stalinesque fabrication**: the representation was never formed accurately; a "show trial" version was all that was ever present.

At macroscopic scales this distinction matters and is measurable. But at the millisecond timescales of neural processing that underlie experience, the distinction *collapses*: there is no fact of the matter about which type of revision occurred, because there is no Cartesian Theater checkpoint against which to judge.

**British Empire / Battle of New Orleans analogy**: The War of 1812 ended January 8, 1815 (Treaty of Ghent, December 24, 1814) — but the Battle of New Orleans was fought on January 8, 1815, because news of the peace hadn't reached the commanders. Was the battle fought "during the war" or "after the war"? No fact of the matter, because information propagation takes time. Similarly, neural events do not have a single moment of "entering consciousness."

## Libet's Backwards Referral (Misinterpretation Resolved)

Benjamin Libet's experiments showed apparent backwards referral of experience (subjects report stimuli as earlier than electrophysiology predicts). Dennett's interpretation: these experiments confuse "time of representing" with "time represented." There is no absolute timing of experience because there is no Cartesian Theater; what we report as "when I felt it" is itself a content of the narrative, not a clock-reading of an inner theater.

Grey Walter's precognitive carousel: patients with motor cortex electrodes found slides appeared to "anticipate" their decisions to advance them. Dennett: the decision-process sets up timing expectations that are themselves neural events registered before the "decision" surfaces in the Joycean narrative — not backward causation or a fixed moment of will.

## Relation to the Joycean Machine

The Multiple Drafts model describes the underlying neural reality; the [[Concepts/Joycean Machine and Memes (Dennett)|Joycean Machine]] describes what emerges at the virtual-machine level. The parallel pandemonium (Selfridge) of competing editorial processes is the substrate; the more-or-less serial stream-of-consciousness is the culturally-installed virtual machine running on top. The serial Joycean narrative is real (it has causal effects) but it is implemented by, not identical to, the parallel Multiple Drafts process.

## Relation to Heterophenomenology

[[Concepts/Heterophenomenology (Dennett)|Heterophenomenology]] is the *method* for studying what subjects report as their experience; Multiple Drafts is the *theory* of what is actually going on in the brain that produces those reports. The heterophenomenological world (the theorist's fiction of what subjects seem to experience) is explained, at the mechanistic level, by the ongoing multiple-drafts editorial process.
