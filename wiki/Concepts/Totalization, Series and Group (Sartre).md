# Totalization, Series and Group (Sartre)
> The social philosophy of the *Critique of Dialectical Reason*: the lone individual is an abstraction; the human totalises itself through history, against the inertia of matter and scarcity, and a *series* of isolated people becomes a *group* only by binding itself in fraternity-terror.

## Summary

In the *Critique de la raison dialectique* (1960), which he considered his masterwork, Sartre moves from the lone individual of *Being and Nothingness* to **humanity (humanité)** in history. The grounding question is *how to make active what is passive*: the child's becoming is largely shaped by others (parents, school, norms) — that is passive; *active* becoming is when one becomes oneself *in answer to* the Other, understanding oneself from freedom rather than determination. This is the Genet structure (see [[Concepts/Existential Psychoanalysis and the Original Choice (Sartre)]]) writ social.

**Dialectic as critical reason.** Against orthodox Marxism, Sartre denies that *reality itself* is dialectical (a dogmatic view that forgets the human stands *at a distance* from reality); rather, our *knowing* is dialectical. As in [[Thinkers/Kant]], reason is "critical" — it asks under what conditions knowledge is possible, instead of throwing a theory like a net over the world.

**Project → totalisation.** What *Being and Nothingness* called the *project* (the human is never what it is, oriented to a future) becomes, at the level of humanity, **totalisation (totalisation)** — never a given but the ongoing process by which humans understand themselves in relation to the world. Totalisation must be sharply distinguished from "totality": a fully known totality would be finished, factual, and would destroy the open, dialectical structure of human knowing. History exists only for humans; the present selects and cultivates what mattered in the past; historical change happens through *negation* (every great revolution negates the past).

**The "single" individual does not exist.** No one lives purely as an individual; even a self-employed worker is embedded in an economy and totalised by the market. Sartre's window scene: looking down, I see a gardener on one side of a wall and a road-mender on the other; each is part of the economy but, separated by the wall, unaware of each other. Only I, a **third (le tiers)**, oversee their relation — totalisation requires mediation by a third and is in principle unknowable from either worker's position. This abandons the individualist standpoint of *Being and Nothingness*.

**The inert, praxis, scarcity.** Humans are needy, material beings, so they relate to the **inert** — what "will not budge" (the boulder on the path is an obstacle only *relative to* my free project). When I instrumentalise inert matter for human ends (making a chair from rough wood), that is **praxis** — possible only from freedom ("anti-physis"). Social structures, norms, and traditions can equally appear as inert. The whole dialectic is driven by **scarcity (rareté)**: there is not enough for everyone, so one person's sufficiency means another's lack — a source of violence, exploitation, and colonialism. In the *Critique* the pour-soi/en-soi pair returns as **"practical organism"** vs **"practical-inert field."** The assembly-line worker (Chaplin's *Modern Times*) is absorbed by the machine — a *passive activity* in which freedom is negated; he must first attain *class consciousness* (become aware he is reduced to a thing) before that negation can itself be negated.

**Series → group → fraternity-terror.** People queuing for a bus form a **series**: a plurality of individuals with nothing in common but a goal mediated by an external inert object (the bus); if someone is sick, all the better — more room. A series becomes a **group** when individuals share a goal unattainable alone (a demonstration); in the group the individual realises his individuality *more* fully than alone. But the group is fragile (police can break it up), so to persist it demands unconditional loyalty: the individual swears an **oath (le serment)**. This binds the group as a **fraternity held together by internal terror (fraternité-terreur)** — the terror of the French Revolution. Terror is not a corruption of an original bond; it *guarantees* brotherhood: "brotherhood is the most immediate and most constant form of the Terror." Only an oath-bound brotherhood can make revolution. Like Marx, Sartre refuses to picture the good society (that would be dogmatic and deny freedom): "we do not know what a good society is, but we know very well that society as it is, is unacceptable" — *On a raison de se révolter*.

## Key Claims / Positions
- The dialectic is in our *knowing*, not in reality itself (against materialist dialectic).
- *Totalisation* is the open process of self-understanding; distinct from a finished *totality*.
- The isolated individual is an abstraction; relations require mediation by a *third*.
- *Praxis* instrumentalises the *inert*; *scarcity* drives the whole social dialectic and violence.
- pour-soi/en-soi → "practical organism"/"practical-inert field" (continuity with early Sartre).
- *Series* (external mediation) can transform into a *group* (shared goal), held by oath into a *fraternity-terror*; only such a brotherhood can make revolution.

### How Sartre Differs From Prior Thinkers in the Wiki
- **vs [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]**: keeps scarcity, alienation, class consciousness, and the eleventh Feuerbach thesis, but rejects the *materialist* dialectic and "scientific" Marxism; the dialectic is subjective/critical.
- **vs [[Thinkers/Kant]]**: borrows the sense of "critical" reason (conditions of knowing) for the dialectic.
- **vs the public-choice/methodological-individualist tradition** ([[Thinkers/James M. Buchanan]], [[Thinkers/Mancur Olson]]): the exact inverse — for Sartre the lone, calculating individual is an *abstraction*; the group is where individuality is realised, bound by oath rather than by self-interested exchange.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] *Fraternity-terror* makes revolutionary terror constitutive of solidarity rather than its corruption — a deliberately uncomfortable claim that puts Sartre at odds with liberal-democratic and rights-based politics (he denies foundational rights entirely; see [[Concepts/Engaged Literature - Prose vs Poetry (Sartre)]]).
