# Categorical Imperative - Universal Law Formulation (Kant)

> Since I have robbed the will of every impulse that could have arisen from the obedience to any law, there is nothing left over except the universal lawfulness of the action in general which alone is to serve the will as its principle, i.e., **I ought never to conduct myself except so that I could also will that my maxim become a universal law**. Here it is mere lawfulness in general (without grounding it on any law determining certain actions) that serves the will as its principle, and also must so serve it, if duty is not to be everywhere an empty delusion and a chimerical concept; common human reason, indeed, agrees perfectly with this in its practical judgment, and has the principle just cited always before its eyes.

## Development in the *Groundwork* (First Section)

After establishing that the good will's moral worth lies in its principle (from duty/respect for the law, independent of material ends or effects), Kant derives the supreme principle: the will, stripped of all inclination-based determining grounds, can only be determined by *universal lawfulness in general*.

**Formulation**: "I ought never to conduct myself except so that I could also will that my maxim become a universal law." (Commonly: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law.")

A *maxim* is the subjective principle of the volition (the rule the agent gives himself for a particular action or type of action). The test is whether one can consistently will that this maxim hold as a *universal law* (for all rational beings, or at least for all in relevantly similar circumstances), without self-contradiction or destruction of the practice the maxim presupposes.

**Example (false promise)**: In a tight spot/embarrassment, may I make a promise with the intention of not keeping it? One might will the *particular lie* to escape, but can one will the *maxim* ("Make an untruthful promise when in embarrassment I cannot otherwise escape") as a universal law? No: if everyone followed this, promises would lose all credibility and point (no one would believe avowals about future actions, or would repay in kind). The institution of promising would be destroyed, and the maxim would undermine itself. Hence it cannot be willed as a universal law; the action is contrary to duty. (One can will the lie in the isolated case, but not the universal permission.)

Common human reason already operates with this principle (though not always formulated in abstract universal terms) and uses it as its practical compass for distinguishing what conforms to duty from what does not. It does not need philosophy to apply it in concrete cases, but philosophy clarifies and secures it.

This is the first (and most famous) formulation of the **Categorical Imperative**—categorical because it commands unconditionally (no "if" of inclination or end), in contrast to hypothetical imperatives (technical: if you will the end, will the necessary means; pragmatic: if you will happiness, act in such-and-such way). The moral law binds the will *as such*, through respect, independently of any desired effect.

## Relevance to Vault

- **The supreme principle**: The a priori, formal, universalizable law that grounds all genuine moral obligation and the good will's worth. It is the "supreme principle of morality" whose search is the aim of the *Groundwork*.
- **Contrast with empiricist threads**: Vs. Hume (no a priori moral "ought"; distinctions of vice/virtue from sentiment/custom, not reason's universal law) and Locke (moral ideas ultimately from experience or revelation, not pure reason's formal law independent of anthropology).
- **Vs. Aristotle**: Universal law (testable by consistency for all rational beings) vs. context-sensitive phronesis, the mean, and eudaimonia in the polis.
- **With Spinoza**: Autonomy under self-given universal law (freedom as determination by reason) vs. determination by conatus/adequate ideas within deterministic Nature.
- **AI/agents**: The universal law test as a core reasoning scaffold and constitutional principle for ethical AI: before adopting a policy or maxim, ask whether it can be consistently willed as binding on all (relevant) agents without self-undermining the system (e.g., deception, free-riding on others' compliance, or actions that would destroy trust/cooperation if universalized). "Categorical" constraints (non-negotiable rules) vs. hypothetical (instrumental to goals/rewards). Relevant to scalable oversight, corrigibility, and avoiding wireheading or goal misgeneralization.
- **Wiki/personal**: "I ought never to [maintain/edit/synthesize] except so that I could also will that my maxim become a universal practice for all maintainers." E.g., "Always trace claims to sources and note uncertainties" can be universalized; "Omit inconvenient counter-evidence when it complicates the narrative" cannot (it would destroy the reliability of the wiki). Duty to the schema (GROK.md) and to accuracy as the "law" eliciting respect, even when inclination (speed, elegance, personal sympathy) pulls otherwise. Universal law as a lint heuristic for consistency across pages/clusters.
- **Ethics cluster**: The rational a priori law that tensions and completes the vault's other poles (sentiment/custom in Hume, experience in Locke, flourishing in Aristotle, control in Stoics, natural participation in Aquinas, grace-enabled will in Augustine).
