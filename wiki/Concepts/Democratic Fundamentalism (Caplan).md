# Democratic Fundamentalism (Caplan)
> The quasi-religious commitment to democracy that places it beyond critique from any external standard — the secular counterpart of religious faith.

## Summary

Caplan coins "democratic fundamentalism" in *The Myth of the Rational Voter* (2007) to label a widespread but rarely named ideological stance: the near-religious belief that democracy is immune to root-and-branch critique, that no standard external to democratic process can legitimately evaluate democratic outcomes, and that all problems with democracy are cured by *more* democracy.

The concept is his rebuttal to the "market fundamentalism" charge routinely leveled at economists who favor free markets. Caplan argues the charge misdescribes the mainstream — only the extreme Mises/Rothbard fringe holds markets as invariably superior regardless of evidence — while a far more absolute and evidence-resistant commitment to democracy pervades modern discourse and is treated as intellectually respectable.

## The Religion Analogy

Caplan draws an extended analogy between democracy-worship and religious belief:
- Democratic fundamentalists treat doubt about democracy as heresy, not as an empirical question.
- Criticism of democracy's outcomes is deflected not with counter-evidence but with accusations of elitism, anti-democratic sentiment, or slippery-slope warnings about dictatorship.
- The slogan attributed to Al Smith captures the doctrine in pure form: "All the ills of democracy can be cured by more democracy."
- Just as religious fundamentalists insulate their beliefs from falsification by stipulation ("God's ways are not our ways"), democratic fundamentalists insulate democracy by defining any non-democratic alternative as illegitimate by definition.

The epistemological parallel: both religious fundamentalism and democratic fundamentalism treat their central claim not as a hypothesis open to evidence but as an axiom that organizes evidence.

## The Market Fundamentalism Charge — Rebutted

The "market fundamentalism" charge (associated with George Soros, Robert Kuttner, Joseph Stiglitz) claims that economists have an almost religious faith in markets regardless of empirical outcomes. Caplan's response:
1. Mainstream economists have extensively documented market failures (externalities, public goods, information asymmetries, monopoly). The mainstream is not market-fundamentalist.
2. The fringe that holds categorical pro-market positions (Rothbard, the extreme Austrian school) is explicitly marginal within economics.
3. Milton Friedman — typically held up as the archetype market fundamentalist — supports significant government intervention in monetary policy, education vouchers, and negative income tax.
4. The term therefore misdescribes the mainstream while accurately describing the fringe — but the fringe is not the target of the charge.

By contrast, democratic fundamentalism is mainstream, respectable, and rarely named as such.

## Implications

Democratic fundamentalism, Caplan argues, explains why reform proposals that would reduce democratic scope (expert-insulated agencies, prediction markets for policy, higher bars for voter participation) are routinely dismissed not on empirical grounds but as anti-democratic and therefore beyond consideration. The Policy Analysis Market (PAM) — a DoD-funded prediction market for national-security questions that was canceled after one day of political outcry in 2003 — is his case study: the cancellation was not based on evidence about prediction markets' reliability but on democratic-fundamentalist intuitions that betting on security events is "grotesque."

## Related Concept: Democracy vs. Markets

Caplan argues the main alternative to democratic majority rule is not dictatorship but *markets*. When democratic fundamentalists lament "the weakening of democracy," they point to markets facing less government oversight. The heretical thought they suppress: if democracy produces bad outcomes, expanding markets rather than democracy may be the appropriate response.
