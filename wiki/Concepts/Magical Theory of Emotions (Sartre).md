# Magical Theory of Emotions (Sartre)
> Emotion is not a feeling that befalls a passive subject; it is an active, irreflexive *behaviour* by which consciousness magically transforms a world it cannot otherwise master.

## Summary

In *Sketch for a Theory of the Emotions* (1938, *Magie en emotie*) Sartre turns his phenomenology against the psychologists who treat emotion as a feeling that "happens to" the subject and can be catalogued as an objective fact with causes. To say "my fear of the dog comes from the fact that I am afraid of dogs" is, for Sartre, like saying "the fire is caused by flame." In fear, I do not *perceive* my fear; the fear *is* the mode of consciousness — "as the fire is wholly flame."

Emotion is therefore not an expression of an inner state but a *way of grasping the world* — and that way is a *behaviour*. The world appears to consciousness as an obstacle course of difficulties; when consciousness sees no adequate practical way through, it tries to **transform the world magically**. Hearing terrible news, I cover my face; threatened by a rottweiler I faint — fainting is a flight that "magically" abolishes the situation, a conjuring trick. The child who screams and stamps is not "expressing" anger but attempting, through the emotional behaviour itself, to transform the situation. Joy, too: I dance because I take an advance on a love whose real approach is full of frustrations I wish to evade.

Crucially the emotion *is* its behaviour, not its cause: "anger or jealousy are behaviours, not causes of behaviour." The more I flee, the more afraid I become; the harder I laugh, the merrier. Emotion has no external cause that "disturbs" consciousness; in emotion consciousness falls into its own magical world. It is thus an irreflexive **choice** — unreasoned, but a choice nonetheless. This already contains, in miniature, the later doctrines of freedom and bad faith: even what seems to merely happen to us is something consciousness *does*.

## Key Claims / Positions
- Emotion is a mode of consciousness, not a perceived inner object.
- It is a behaviour that tries to *magically transform* an unmasterable world.
- The emotion is identical with its behaviour; it has no external cause.
- It is an irreflexive (unreasoned) choice — a precursor of radical freedom.

### How Sartre Differs
- **vs empirical/positivist psychology**: rejects causal explanation of feelings; emotions are not facts but meanings. Continuous with his [[Concepts/Consciousness as Intentionality and Néantisation (Sartre)|critique of psychologism]].
- **vs Freud**: no libidinal or unconscious cause underlies emotion; it is fully conscious behaviour. See [[Contradictions/Sartre vs Freud - Lucidity vs the Unconscious]].
