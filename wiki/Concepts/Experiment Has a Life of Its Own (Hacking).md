# Experiment Has a Life of Its Own (Hacking)

> Theory and experiment do not stand in any fixed, universal relationship. "There is no right answer to the question: Which comes first, experiment, theory, invention, technology...?" Experiment, instrumentation, and technology often develop with substantial independence from high theory; they interact in multiple, historically specific ways.

## Core Thesis

Philosophers of science have over-focused on theories and representation. Experimental practice has its own history, skills, constraints, and rationality that cannot be reduced to "testing" or "applying" a theory. Illustrations are drawn from optics, thermodynamics, solid-state physics, radio astronomy, and detailed modern cases (microscopes, electron beams, phenomenon creation).

- Experiments can be curiosity-driven, technology-driven, or theory-stimulated — all three occur.
- "Happy meetings" between theory and experiment happen, but are not the whole story.
- Instrumentation and apparatus have their own developmental logic (improving resolution, stability, purity).
- The experimenter often trusts results because multiple physically independent routes converge, not because a single underlying theory is fully believed.

## Key Illustrations (Hacking)

- Which comes first? Varies: sometimes experiment precedes and stimulates theory; sometimes theory guides what to build; often invention/technology runs ahead.
- Microscopes: belief in what is "seen" rests on agreement across instruments relying on different physical principles (ordinary light, phase contrast, electron, acoustic). The convergence is practical evidence, not deduction from one theory.
- "Meaningless phenomena": some experimental setups initially produce effects whose theoretical meaning is unclear or absent; they become meaningful later or remain useful as tools anyway.
- Theory-history vs. experiment-history: the two narratives can diverge; philosophers and historians often privilege the theoretical story.

## Philosophical Consequences

- Undermines both strong theory-first (rationalist) and strong observation-first (positivist) pictures.
- Supports entity realism: the entities that function reliably inside experimental systems earn their ontological keep through use, even when the high theory around them is contested or instrumental.
- Provides a partial answer to incommensurability worries: experimental skills and instruments can be transferred or compared across theory changes in ways that pure representations cannot.
- "The experimenter must be a realist" (about the entities used as tools) even if the speculator or theorist need not be.

## Relation to Other Vault Content

- Direct counterpoint to strong readings of theory-ladenness and incommensurability in [[Concepts/Incommensurability (Kuhn)]] and Feyerabend's natural interpretations: while granting much interaction, Hacking documents substantial practical independence and cross-checks.
- Engages Lakatos on crucial experiments and auxiliary hypotheses (Hacking's Bacon chapter uses historical cases to show that piling on auxiliaries has practical limits; also criticizes some of Lakatos's historical reports).
- Complements Popper's emphasis on testing: testing is real, but it is only one of the many roles experiment plays, and the entities used in testing are already granted reality by the experimenter.
- With positivism (Ayer cluster): challenges the reduction of legitimate science to what can be directly observed without theoretical entities or causes.

## Vault Meta / AI

Agentic systems and tools often develop "a life of their own." A capability (retrieval method, verifier, image model, code interpreter) can be refined, combined, and trusted through use and cross-checks long before (or independently of) any overarching "theory" of the whole system. "Which comes first, the prompt theory or the working prototype?" — both directions occur; stable, intervenable tools earn their status by what they let us do, not by how completely they are theoretically justified. The wiki itself grows through experimental edits that create new stable cross-references and phenomena.
