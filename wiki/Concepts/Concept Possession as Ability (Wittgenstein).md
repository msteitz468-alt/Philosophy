# Concept Possession as Ability (Wittgenstein)
> The view that possessing a concept or understanding a word is not an occurrent mental state or neurological mechanism, but a technique, capacity, or ability to use words in accordance with rules within social practices.

## Concept Overview
Wittgenstein challenges the Cartesian and mentalist view that understanding a word or possessing a concept consists in having a specific mental image or "logical germ" before the mind. In *Philosophical Investigations* §150, he writes:
> "The grammar of the word 'knows' is evidently closely related to that of 'can', 'is able to'. But also closely related to that of 'understands'. ('Mastery' of a technique.)"

This means that possessing a concept is a *potentiality* rather than an occurrent mental state or a physical mechanism in the brain. When we say someone has the concept of "addition," we mean they have the ability or technique to execute addition under ordinary circumstances.

## Theoretical Debates

### Glock's Taxonomy: Abilities vs. Techniques
Hans-Johann Glock (2010) identifies and critiques Wittgenstein's positive account of concepts:
- **Concept-Identity**: Wittgenstein sometimes equates concepts with techniques or rules of classification. Glock argues this is too strong: concepts are not identical to techniques but are rather *expressed* by them.
- **Possession as Ability**: Understanding is an ability, and specifically a **two-way power** (in the Aristotelian sense). An agent with a two-way power can choose whether or not to exercise it (e.g., a speaker can choose to use a word or remain silent), whereas physical states (like a computer displaying text) are one-way causal mechanisms.

### McDowell's Defense of Understanding-States
In the same volume, John McDowell addresses a common misreading (often associated with Warren Goldfarb) which claims that Wittgenstein rejects the idea that understanding is a "state of mind" altogether. McDowell clarifies:
- **Definite States**: Understanding *is* a definite state, but it is not an "occult" state in a hidden mental medium that magically contains all future applications.
- **Normative Inertness**: Formulas, mental images, or brain states are "normatively inert"—they cannot on their own determine what counts as correct rule-following.
- **Primitive Propensities**: What makes understanding a state is the agent's shared, primitive propensity to find certain applications of a rule natural. Grasping a rule means being trained into a practice where we go on in the same way without requiring a "regress of interpretations."
