# Distributional Coalitions (Olson)
> Special-interest cartels and lobbies focused on redistributing existing income rather than increasing social output.

## Summary

In *The Rise and Decline of Nations* (1982), Mancur Olson introduces the term **distributional coalitions** to describe special-interest organizations, cartels, professional associations, and lobbies that engage in what the economic literature calls **rent-seeking**.

### The Logic of Redistribution vs. Production

Olson distinguishes between two ways a group can increase its income:
1. **Production**: Working to make the society as a whole more productive, so that the group's share of the larger national pie increases.
2. **Redistribution**: Working to secure a larger slice of the existing pie, even if the process of cutting the pie reduces its overall size.

For a typical interest group, which represents only a small fraction of the society (e.g., a specific guild of dentists, a cartel of steel manufacturers, or a narrow union of railway workers), the second strategy is far more rational. If a group representing 1% of the population spends its energy increasing national productivity, it bears 100% of the cost but receives only 1% of the benefit. If it instead spends its energy lobbying for a monopoly tariff or subsidy worth $1 million, it captures 100% of the benefit, while the social cost (e.g., a $1.5 million loss in overall economic efficiency) is dispersed across the other 99% of the population.

Consequently, **distributional coalitions are structurally indifferent to the social losses (the "public bads") their actions cause**. They operate as "distributional" coalitions because their primary activity is redistributive rather than productive.

### Characteristics of Distributional Coalitions

Based on the implications derived from collective-action logic, Olson outlines several key features of these coalitions:

* **Price-Fixing over Quantity-Fixing**: Coalitions prefer to control prices (or wages) rather than quantities. Fixing prices allows them to hide the redistribution as a "market price" and makes policing cartel defection easier.
* **Slow Decision-Making**: Because they rely on consensual bargaining among members or complex internal constitutional procedures, coalitions make decisions much slower than individual firms or competitive markets. They crowd their agendas and suffer from institutional inertia.
* **Exclusivity**: Successful coalitions are exclusive. To maintain their supracompetitive prices or wages, they must restrict entry, limit membership diversity, and prevent non-members from competing.
* **Perverse Incentives**: Coalitions foster group prejudice (racial, ethnic, or occupational) to enforce endogamy, strengthen selective incentives, and justify the exclusion of cheaper competitors.

## Key Claims / Positions

### How Different Thinkers Approach This

- **[[Thinkers/Adam Smith]]**: In *The Wealth of Nations*, Smith famously warned that "people of the same trade seldom meet together, even for merriment and diversion, but the conversation ends in a conspiracy against the public, or in some contrivance to raise prices." Olson formalizes Smith's warning, showing that mercantilism is the predictable result of merchant lobbies exploiting the public's rational ignorance.
- **Rent-Seeking Theorists (Gordon Tullock, Anne Krueger, Jagdish Bhagwati)**: Tullock and Krueger pioneered the analysis of how resources spent to obtain government monopolies (tariffs, licenses) represent a deadweight loss to society. Olson integrates rent-seeking into a broader historical theory of institutional change, showing how the accumulation of these coalitions shapes the fate of nations.
- **Harberger Monopolist Loss Model**: Arnold Harberger argued that the allocative losses from monopoly in the U.S. were tiny (~0.1% of GNP). Bhagwati countered that when we include the resources wasted in rent-seeking to *obtain* those monopolies, the social losses are vastly larger. Olson agrees with Bhagwati, showing that the accumulation of coalitions silts up the entire economy.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] The boundary between a "distributional coalition" (which reduces efficiency) and a legitimate civil society organization (which protects rights) is blurry. For example, is a professional medical association a cartel restricting the supply of doctors, or a necessary guarantor of public health standards? Olson's model focuses on the cartelistic function, often ignoring the positive externalities of professional standards.
- While narrow coalitions reduce efficiency, some inclusive or "encompassing" groups actually support growth. The threshold at which a coalition transitions from narrow rent-seeking to encompassing social responsibility is a key policy question. See [[Concepts/Encompassing Organizations (Olson)]].
