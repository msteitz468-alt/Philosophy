# Live Unnoticed (Lathe Biosas)

> Epicurean ethical maxim (from Epicurus' fragments and letters): "Pass your life making sure no one notices you" (lathe biosas). Avoid public ambition, political turmoil, fame-seeking, and the disturbances they bring. Instead, cultivate a private, simple life of modest natural pleasures, friendship, prudent self-sufficiency, and inner tranquility (ataraxia). This is not antisocial withdrawal but a strategic choice for peace of mind — society as contract for security, not natural sociability (contra Aristotle); the wise person retreats to the "Garden" (literal or metaphorical) to live unnoticed, free from unnecessary fears, desires, and conflicts. Consistent with atomist physics (blind chance, no divine spotlight) and empiricism (focus on accessible, personal experience over grand narratives).

## Core Elements from the Source
- **Rationale**: Public life brings ambition, envy, fear of loss/failure, and entanglement in others' (often irrational) affairs. "Live unnoticed" protects ataraxia by limiting exposure to vain/unnecessary disturbances. Aligns with classifying desires (pursue only natural/necessary) and "death is nothing" (no need for posthumous fame or legacy).
- **Not Isolation**: The Garden was a community of mutual aid, trust, and friendship (highest natural pleasure). "Unnoticed" means avoiding the spotlight of power/politics/spectacle, not renouncing all social bonds. Contractarian view: humans form society for security; once secured, the wise withdraw to enjoy it privately.
- **Link to Physics/Ethics**: Blind atomic collisions (no purposeful cosmic audience) make grand public striving pointless. Focus on personal, verifiable experience (empiricism) over myth or status. Prudence teaches when to engage vs. withdraw.
- **Critics' View**: Often attacked as cowardly, selfish, or elitist (hiding from civic duty). Epicurus counters that it is the rational path to universal happiness — accessible, anti-elitist, and consistent with nature's goal of tranquility.
