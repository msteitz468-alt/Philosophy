# Picture Theory of Language (Wittgenstein)

> The theory that language represents reality by mirroring its logical structure, acting as a logical picture of facts.

## Summary

The **Picture Theory of Language** is the core semantic doctrine of [[Thinkers/Ludwig Wittgenstein|Ludwig Wittgenstein]]'s *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* (1922). It asserts that propositions are logical pictures (*Bilder*) of reality. 

Under this theory, a proposition is meaningful if and only if its elements (words) correspond to simple objects in the world, and its structure shares the same **logical form** as the state of affairs (*Sachverhalt*) it represents. If a sentence cannot be decomposed into a logical picture of possible empirical facts, it is literally nonsensical (*unsinnig*).

---

## Core Mechanics

### 1. The Isomorphism between Language and World
Wittgenstein establishes a strict parallel between the structure of language and the structure of reality:
* **The World** consists of **Facts**, which are composed of atomic **States of Affairs**, which are combinations of simple **Objects**.
* **Language** consists of **Propositions**, which are composed of **Elementary Propositions**, which are combinations of simple **Names**.

A proposition is a model of reality. Just as a physical scale model of a traffic accident represents the cars and road, the elements of a proposition represent objects, and their logical arrangement represents the physical arrangement of those objects.

### 2. Logical Form and the Unsayable
For a picture to represent a state of affairs, it must share a common structure with it: **logical form** (*logische Form*). 
* A picture can represent anything in the world except its own pictorial form; it can only **show** it (*Tractatus* 2.172: *"The picture cannot, however, represent its pictorial form; it shows it forth"*).
* Consequently, language can represent the world of facts, but it cannot represent the logical structure that makes representation possible. Logic is transcendent; it shows itself but cannot be spoken of (*saying vs. showing*).

### 3. The Limits of Meaningful Language
Under the picture theory, a proposition only has sense (*Sinn*) if it represents a possible state of affairs in the world. This yields three classes of sentences:
1. **Sensible Propositions** (*sinnvoll*): Empirical statements of science that picture possible facts (e.g., "The cat is on the mat"). They can be true or false.
2. **Tautologies and Contradictions** (*sinnlos*): Logical truths (p or not-p) and contradictions (p and not-p). They have no pictorial content (say nothing about the world) but are not nonsensical; they outline the boundaries of logical space.
3. **Nonsensical Propositions** (*unsinnig*): Statements that attempt to speak of things that cannot be pictured. This includes all of traditional metaphysics, ethics, aesthetics, and religion. Because we cannot construct a logical picture of "the Good" or "the Absolute," sentences about them are meaningless.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

> [!warning] Repudiated by the later Wittgenstein himself. The *Philosophical Investigations* (1953) abandons the single logical form and object-and-name semantics defended here in favour of [[Concepts/Meaning as Use (Wittgenstein)|meaning as use]] within [[Concepts/Language-Games (Wittgenstein)|language-games]]. See [[Contradictions/Early vs Later Wittgenstein]].

### Crux of Self-Application (The Ladder)
* The most famous tension in the *Tractatus* is its own self-application. The propositions of the *Tractatus* are not empirical statements picturing facts, nor are they mere tautologies. Therefore, by its own criteria, the *Tractatus* is nonsensical.
* Wittgenstein famously embraces this contradiction in *Tractatus* 6.54: *"My propositions elucidate in this way: he who understands me finally recognizes them as senseless, when he has climbed out through them, on them, over them. (He must so to speak throw away the ladder, after he has climbed up on it.)"*

### Connection to Vault
* **Vienna Circle and Ayer**: The logical positivists (like [[Thinkers/A.J. Ayer|A.J. Ayer]]) adopted the picture theory's demarcation line to formulate the [[Concepts/Verification Principle (Ayer)|Verification Principle]], arguing that any non-analytic, non-empirical sentence is meaningless.
* **Isomorphism and Hofstadter**: Wittgenstein's claim that meaning arises through a structural isomorphism between symbols and the world is a direct precursor to [[Thinkers/Douglas Hofstadter|Douglas Hofstadter]]'s analysis of meaning in *Gödel, Escher, Bach*. However, Hofstadter rejects Wittgenstein's strict limits, showing that self-referential systems can generate meaning through "strange loops."
