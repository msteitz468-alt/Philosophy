# Memory and Recollection (Aristotle)

> Memory is a seal-like impression of a past perception, contemplated *as a likeness* of the absent original; recollection is a distinct, effortful search through associated "movements" — linked by similarity, contrast, or contiguity — found only in animals capable of deliberation. The founding text of Western **associationist** psychology.

## Summary

Memory is essentially of the **past** — not of the future (mere expectation) or the present (mere perception) — and therefore requires perceiving time-elapsed, which is why only time-perceiving animals remember, using the same organ (the common sense) that perceives time. Memory is grounded in a *phantasma* functioning like a seal-impression stamped in the soul by the original perception ("some such thing as a picture"); poor memory in the very young and very old is explained by their receiving surfaces being respectively too soft ("running water") or too hard/frayed ("old walls") to hold or take an impression. The philosophical puzzle — how contemplating a present mental image counts as remembering an absent thing — is solved by the **picture/likeness distinction**: the same image can be regarded either in itself (mere thought) or as a likeness of something else (a mnemonic token); it is the latter mode of regard that constitutes memory, analogous to viewing a portrait either as a painted surface or as a likeness of Koriskos. Recollection (*anamnesis*) is then sharply separated from memory: not its "recovery" but an active search through a network of movements connected by natural or habituated succession, executed by moving from a starting-point (similar, contrary, or contiguous to the target) through the chain until the sought item is reached — "a sort of inference." Because it requires deliberative search, recollection belongs to man alone among the animals Aristotle knows, while memory is shared with many non-human animals.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Memory belongs directly to the **primary faculty of sense-perception** (which perceives time) and only incidentally to intellect — hence widely shared with animals, unlike recollection.
- **Wax/seal analogy**: perception stamps an impression on a receptive substrate; age and emotional agitation affect the quality of the impression and hence memory's reliability.
- **Picture/likeness solution**: the mnemonic image is one thing considered two ways; regarding it as a likeness of an absent original (rather than as a bare presentation) is what makes a mental state a memory. Confusing a non-likeness for a likeness produces pathological false memory (the case of Antipheron of Oreus).
- **Good memory and quick recollection are distinct traits** found in different types of people (the slow have good memory; the quick-witted recollect well) — an early functional dissociation of storage from retrieval.
- **Associative principle**: recollective search proceeds via **similarity, contrast, or contiguity** of successive "movements"; orderly material (like geometric proofs) is recollected easily, disordered material with difficulty; repetition entrenches sequences until "custom assumes the role of nature."
- **Recollection is exclusive to deliberative animals** — "a mode of inference," hence a rational, investigative act, not a passive re-presentation.
- Recollection is **corporeal**: it is a "searching for an image in a corporeal substrate," evidenced by the physical discomfort of failed recollective effort (especially in melancholics) and the involuntary, momentum-like character of the search once triggered.

## How Aristotle Uses This

Builds on *phantasia* as developed in *De Anima* and *On Dreams* (see [[Concepts/Imagination and Common Sense (Aristotle)]], [[Concepts/Dreams as Residual Sense-Movements (Aristotle)]]) — memory is simply the imaginative "movement" contemplated under the aspect of pastness/likeness, and recollection is the deliberate manipulation of chains of such movements.

## How Later Thinkers Use This

- **Associationism (Hume and the British empiricists)**: the recollection-by-similarity/contrast/contiguity triad is the direct classical ancestor of Hume's laws of association of ideas (resemblance, contiguity, cause-and-effect) — a genealogical link worth deepening once Hume's *Treatise* is directly ingested into the vault (currently the vault's Hume material centers on the problem of induction and Hume's Fork; the associationist psychology itself is not yet primary-sourced).
- **Medieval faculty psychology**: alongside the common sense and imagination (see [[Concepts/Imagination and Common Sense (Aristotle)]]), memory becomes one of the fixed internal senses of scholastic psychology.
