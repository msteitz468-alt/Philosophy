# Theory of Perception (Aristotle)

> A sense is "what has the power of receiving into itself the sensible forms of things without the matter... in the way in which a piece of wax takes on the impress of a signet-ring without the iron or gold." Each sense has a proper object no other sense can perceive (color, sound, flavor...), while common sensibles (motion, number, figure, magnitude) are perceived by more than one sense; nearly every sense requires an external medium.

## Summary

*De Anima* Book II, chs. 5–12 develops perception as a species of the soul's general capacity to receive form without matter (continuing [[Concepts/Soul as Form of the Body (Hylomorphism, Aristotle)]]). Sensation is a qualitative change in which "what acts and what is acted upon are like" only after the change has occurred — the sense organ, before perceiving, is unlike its object, and becomes like it through the process. Aristotle distinguishes the proper or "special" object of each sense — "that which cannot be perceived by any other sense," e.g. color for sight, sound for hearing, flavor for taste — from the common sensibles ("movement, rest, number, figure, magnitude"), which are perceived by more than one sense and require no dedicated organ. Each sense (except, with qualification, touch) requires an external medium: sight requires the transparent actualized as light, since color "has in it the power to set in movement what is actually transparent," and "if what has colour is placed in immediate contact with the eye, it cannot be seen" — the same logic extends to hearing (air) and smell (air or water), while touch and taste work through internal media (flesh, the tongue). Touch is treated as the most fundamental and indispensable sense, "the primary form of sense," belonging to all animals and, alone among the senses, capable of existing without the others — its loss is fatal in a way the loss of no other single sense is. Each sense organ functions as "a mean between any two opposite qualities which determine the field of that sense," which is what enables it to discriminate rather than merely register.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Perception is literally the reception of sensible *form* without matter — a direct extension of the hylomorphic framework from metaphysics into cognitive theory, not a separate ad hoc account.
- The proper/common sensible distinction gives a principled taxonomy of what each sense can and cannot detect, and explains why certain objects (motion, number) are cross-modally accessible while others (color) are not.
- Nearly all perception is mediated — direct contact between object and organ (as with color against the eye) actually prevents perception, a counterintuitive but load-bearing claim.
- Touch is foundational: it is necessary for animal life as such, unlike any of the other four senses, and this asymmetry structures Aristotle's account of which capacities are essential to being an animal at all.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Downstream — medieval and scholastic epistemology**: the "reception of form without matter" formula becomes a central plank of scholastic epistemology (Aquinas's species-in-medio theory of perception is a direct descendant), and underlies the broader medieval principle that "whatever is received is received according to the mode of the receiver."
- **Contrast with early modern representationalism**: Aristotle's direct-realist framing (the sense organ takes on the object's actual form, not merely a causally-produced idea or image of it) differs sharply from Descartes's and Locke's representational theories of perception, where the mind perceives its own ideas rather than external form as such — an implicit but sharp epistemological fault line between the classical and early modern traditions.
- **Continuous with [[Concepts/Imagination and Common Sense (Aristotle)]]**: the common-sensibles doctrine here is the direct antecedent of Book III's "common sense" (a unified discriminating capacity across the five senses), and the wax-seal model of sensation is explicitly reused for imagination as a residual "movement" from actual sensation.
