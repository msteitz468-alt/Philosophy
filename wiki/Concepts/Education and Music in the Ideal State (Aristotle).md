# Education and Music in the Ideal State (Aristotle)

> Education must be uniform and publicly controlled, aimed at the constitution's end rather than private advantage, and must include the "useless" liberal subject of music, whose rhythms and modes actually imitate and shape moral character — not merely for utility, but because leisure, not occupation, is the true end of life.

## Summary

*Politics* Book VIII (the treatise's unfinished final book) argues education cannot be left private because "the care of each part is inseparable from the care of the whole" and "no one of the citizens belongs to himself... they all belong to the state" — citizens must be "molded to suit the form of government under which he lives." Aristotle divides occupations and subjects into liberal and illiberal (*banausic*): illiberal pursuits "deform the body" or are paid employments that "absorb and degrade the mind," and the same act can be liberal or servile depending on whether it is done for oneself or for another's sake. Of the four customary educational subjects (reading/writing, gymnastics, music, sometimes drawing), music receives Aristotle's most developed defense: it belongs in education "not on the ground either of its necessity or utility" but because "the first principle of all action is leisure," and music supplies the "intellectual enjoyment" proper to leisure rather than to work or war. Against the purely athletic Spartan model, which brutalizes rather than cultivates true courage, Aristotle insists music has genuine ethical, imitative power — melodies and rhythms are not mere signs but "imitations" of anger, gentleness, courage, and temperance, so that "in listening to such strains our souls undergo a change." Different musical modes produce different characters: the Mixolydian is sad and grave, relaxed modes enfeeble, the Dorian produces "a moderate and settled temper," and the Phrygian "inspires enthusiasm" — on this basis he criticizes Plato's *Republic* for retaining the Phrygian mode. Children should be taught to actually perform music, not merely listen, since only performers become competent judges of it, but training should stop short of professional virtuosity, "which vulgarizes." The flute is rejected for education specifically as "too exciting," impeding use of the voice and associated with vulgar professional performance rather than moral character; the Dorian mode is endorsed as education's proper "mean." Music's three uses — education, purgation (*katharsis*, explicitly deferred to the *Poetics*), and leisure enjoyment — are distinguished, with the educational use favoring "the most ethical modes." The text breaks off mid-sentence at "education should be based upon three principles — the mean, the..." — the well-attested unfinished state of the *Politics*.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Education is a public, constitution-directed enterprise, not a private family matter — citizens "belong to the state."
- Liberal (leisure-oriented, self-directed) education is sharply distinguished from illiberal/banausic (paid, other-directed, body-deforming) training; the same activity's moral status depends on its purpose.
- Music has genuine mimetic-ethical power over character, not merely aesthetic or recreational value — a substantive philosophy of art's moral effect, not just a pedagogical convenience.
- Specific musical modes have determinate ethical valences (Dorian = settled/moderate; Phrygian = enthusiastic/frenzied), grounding concrete curricular judgments (endorse Dorian, limit Phrygian, reject the flute).
- Leisure, not utility or occupation, is the ultimate end that justifies including music in education at all — directly continuous with Book VII's leisure-centered account of the good life ([[Concepts/The Best State and the Good Life (Aristotle)]]).
- The *Politics* is textually incomplete: it ends mid-sentence, corroborating the traditional scholarly view that the work as transmitted is an unfinished lecture-course compilation.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Vs. [[Thinkers/Plato]]**: Aristotle directly criticizes the *Republic*'s retention of the Phrygian mode in the guardians' education, given his own analysis of its enthusiasm-inducing (rather than settled/moderate) ethical effect — a concrete musical disagreement within their broader shared premise that art shapes character and must therefore be regulated by the state.
- **Continuous with [[Concepts/The Best State and the Good Life (Aristotle)]]**: leisure (*scholē*) is the load-bearing concept linking Book VII's citizen-class design to Book VIII's curricular argument — both converge on leisure, not utility, as the mark of properly human (as opposed to merely useful or servile) activity.
- **Anticipates aesthetics**: the mimetic theory of music's ethical power (melody/rhythm as *imitations* of character-states, not mere signs) is a companion piece to the theory of *mimesis* and *katharsis* developed for tragedy in the *Poetics*, explicitly cross-referenced here but not yet ingested into the vault.
