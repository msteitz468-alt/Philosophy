# Becoming (Kelly)

> One of Kevin Kelly's 12 inevitable technological forces (2016). Everything—tangible and intangible—is in constant flux, requiring ongoing energy, order, and maintenance to exist. Products become services and processes; fixed nouns give way to fluid verbs. Constant upgrading (increasingly automatic) is the new hygiene; everyone is a "perpetual newbie" because new technologies are invented faster than we master them and obsolescence cycles accelerate. Existence is chiefly maintenance. This is the pivot of protopia (incremental improvement amid new problems) and the "Beginning" of the planetary holos. The shift is already underway and will intensify for decades as a strong bias of digital technology.

## Summary (from the source)

Kelly's epiphany: "Everything, without exception, requires additional energy and order to maintain itself." This is the second law of thermodynamics made personal and cultural. Even "inanimate" things (stone, iron, paper) and especially the intangible (websites, software, code, apps, bits) degrade without attention. "Brand-new computers will ossify. Apps weaken with use. Code corrodes."

The digital landscape itself assaults stability: when everything around you upgrades, you must upgrade or fall behind. Neglecting minor upgrades leads to traumatic big ones. Kelly now treats upgrading as hygiene—regular, necessary, and increasingly automatic (OSes and apps update behind the scenes, "slowly changing their features over time").

Consequences:
- Everyone becomes (and remains) a newbie. New tech is invented faster than mastery; upgrades displace competence before it solidifies; the average lifespan of a phone app was ~30 days at the time of writing. "Endless Newbie is the new default for everyone, no matter your age or experience."
- Products become services/processes. An automobile becomes a continuously updated transportation service (sensors, over-the-air updates, usage-based adaptation, connections to other systems). A shoe becomes "shoeing"—disposable covers, morphing treads, floors that act as shoes. "In the intangible digital realm, nothing is static or fixed. Everything is becoming."
- This ceaseless change is the "pivotal axis of the modern world." Processes trump products. The scientific method itself is the greatest example: a process that generates ongoing benefits rather than any single invention.
- The "holes in our heart": new technologies create new longings and itches we didn't know we had (smartphones, always-on networks). This is painful but also the trigger for ingenuity and growth. We stretch the container of our identity. Discontent is the source of expansion.
- Protopia: the realistic state. Things are a little better today than yesterday, but only a little, and new problems are generated as fast as (or faster than) old ones are solved. The net positive compounds over decades into civilization, but it is "hard to cheer for" because incremental and always accompanied by discomfort. Utopias have no problems (and thus no opportunities); dystopias are unsustainable.

Kelly celebrates the "never-ending discontentment that technology brings." We are not content merely to survive; we invent new desires. This is what makes us different from our animal ancestors and what drives the technium.

The force is already visible and will continue: endless upgrades, morphing interfaces, everything as a service or process, perpetual newbieness as the human condition in a technological civilization.

## Relation to Other Vault Content

- **Core to Kelly's map**: Becoming is the unifying frame for the other 11 forces (cognifying, flowing, screening, accessing, etc., are all forms of becoming). See [[Concepts/The Inevitable (Kelly)]].
- **Foundation in technium thesis**: The force of Becoming is one expression of the deeper "wants" of the technium (seventh kingdom of life with evolutionary dynamics, exotropy as cosmic trajectory of increasing order/mind, convergence on forms, human co-evolution). See [[Sources/What Technology Wants - Kevin Kelly (2010)]] and [[Concepts/Technium (Kelly)]], [[Concepts/Exotropy (Kelly)]], [[Concepts/Convergence (Kelly)]].
- **Cross to acceleration/optimism (Kurzweil)**: Shared sense of relentless change and new abundance, but Kelly emphasizes the maintenance burden, perpetual newbie status, and protopian (not rupture) character.
- **Cross to risks/control (Bostrom, Wiener)**: The same becoming that creates new value also creates new problems and new dependencies (including on AI systems that are themselves becoming). Steering the particulars matters precisely because the general direction is strong.
- **Cross to limits/hype (Mitchell, Hofstadter)**: Constant becoming and upgrading can be experienced as hype cycles and brittleness. The "holes in the heart" and perpetual newbie status are real psychological/cultural costs that Mitchell's corrective on understanding vs. pattern-matching helps illuminate.
- **Cross to mind and emergence (Hofstadter, Minsky, Dennett)**: Becoming/strange loops/tangled hierarchies/society of agents are mechanisms by which mind and the holos emerge from flux. No fixed self or finished product.
- **Cross to History of Ideas (Lovejoy/Chain)**: The static, finished, hierarchical "diagram" of pre-modern thought (Great Chain) is precisely what relentless becoming displaces. Technology as the accelerant of the process Lovejoy traced to its "negative outcome" (the hypothesis of complete rational intelligibility fails; the world is contingent and in flux).
- **Vault meta**: The wiki itself is an instance of becoming—perpetual new pages, targeted upgrades (edits), maintenance of links/index/log, everyone (including the agent) a newbie to the latest sources, remixing of raw material into concepts, filtering of abundance, questioning via lint/ingest. The index and log are flows rather than fixed products.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **Maintenance burden vs. liberation**: Constant becoming frees us from finished, static limitations but imposes endless attention debt ("black hole for attention"). Who pays, and how is it distributed?
- **Perpetual newbie as humility or alienation**: Humbling and generative (per Kelly) or a source of status anxiety, deskilling, and loss of mastery (counter-narratives in labor and education critiques)?
- **Protopia realism**: Does incremental improvement with new problems actually compound positively over time, or do the problems (esp. at planetary scale with cognifying) accumulate faster than the benefits? Ties to existing vault debates on progress, risk, and control.
- **"Holes in the heart"**: Is the creation of new desires a feature (ingenuity, growth) or a bug (manufactured discontent, consumerism)?
