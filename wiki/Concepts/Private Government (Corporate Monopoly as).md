# Private Government (Corporate Monopoly as)
> The thesis that dominant corporations are not merely large market actors but a new form of quasi-governmental power — regulating, taxing, surveilling, and punishing without democratic accountability.

## Summary

Zephyr Teachout's central philosophical contribution is to reframe monopoly from an *economic* category to a *political* one. A monopolist is, by definition, an entity with enough power to set the terms of an interaction — pricing, employment conditions, data use, information flow — unilaterally. This is not market exchange; it is rule-making. The monopolist does not bargain; it governs.

Teachout draws on Justice William O. Douglas's observation that "all power tends to develop into a government in itself." Amazon, Google, Facebook, Monsanto, AT&T, Pfizer — on this view — are not analogous to Standard Oil (an old-fashioned profit-maximizer). They are a 21st-century form of centralized, authoritarian government. They:
- **Regulate**: Facebook determines what speech is permissible for hundreds of millions of people; Monsanto determines which crop varieties are available to farmers
- **Tax**: Platform fees, app store commissions, and supplier margins function as private taxation
- **Extract**: Wage theft, data harvesting, predatory pricing extract value without consent
- **Surveill**: Workplace monitoring, app data, consumer tracking — without the constitutional limits on state surveillance
- **Punish**: Deplatforming, algorithmic demotion, contract termination — extrajudicial and opaque
- **Lobby**: Citizens United (2010) enabled unlimited corporate political spending; monopolists shape the laws that govern them

## How Different Thinkers Use This

**Zephyr Teachout:** Private government is the primary threat to democracy in the 21st century. The solution is structural — break up the power — not behavioral (fines, consent decrees). A corporation broken into ten competing entities cannot govern.

**Hobbes parallel (implicit):** [[Thinkers/Thomas Hobbes]] argued that the only solution to the war of all against all is a Leviathan — a sovereign with monopoly on legitimate force. Teachout's argument can be read as: we have allowed private Leviathans to arise, sovereign within their domains, without the social contract justification Hobbes provided. They are tyrants, not sovereigns.

**Aristotle parallel:** [[Thinkers/Aristotle]] distinguished legitimate rule (in the interest of the governed) from tyranny (in the ruler's interest). Monopolists govern in their own interest; they are tyrants in the Aristotelian sense.

**Madison parallel:** Federalist No. 10 warned that factions — groups united by a common interest adverse to other citizens — would destroy republican government if not checked. Teachout updates this: the modern faction is the monopoly corporation, which uses Citizens United to do what Madison's constitutional design tried to prevent.

## The Citizen-Consumer Collapse

Teachout diagnoses a conceptual error embedded in the consumer welfare standard: it treats people primarily as *consumers* (buyers of goods) rather than as *citizens* (members of a self-governing polity). The ethical consumerism response — "vote with your wallet," boycott Amazon — accepts this framing and reinforces corporate authority. The political response — demand legislation, build movements — reasserts the citizen's role.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Conflicts with libertarian / public choice view that corporations *cannot* be governments because they lack monopoly on legitimate coercion and people can (in principle) exit. Teachout's response: exit is illusory when markets are dominated by one or two firms; the "vote with your feet" option disappears under monopoly.
- > [!warning] Tension with [[Thinkers/Michel Foucault]]'s analysis of power: Foucault's *biopower* and *governmentality* also describe diffuse, non-state forms of governance — corporate surveillance capitalism fits this framework. Neither Foucault nor Teachout is cited against the other, but the concepts overlap productively.
