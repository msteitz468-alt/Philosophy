# Atlantis

> The legendary island empire beyond the Pillars of Heracles (Strait of Gibraltar), described in Plato's Critias (and summarized in Timaeus) as a powerful, advanced civilization that waged war on ancient Athens ~9,000 years before Solon and then sank in a cataclysm "in a single day and night of misfortune."

## The Account in Plato (sole ancient source)
- **Transmission**: Egyptian priests at Sais preserve records; Solon (Athenian lawgiver, relative of Critias' ancestors) hears the tale ~590 BC and brings it back. Passed down in Critias' family.
- **Ancient Context**: Occurs in the "heroic" era after a great deluge that left only illiterate mountain survivors. Athens at the time had an ideal constitution (echoing the one Socrates described "yesterday" in the dialogue frame, with affinities to Republic).
- **Atlantis Description** (the extant portion of Critias):
  - Island "larger than Libya and Asia [Minor] combined," located in the Atlantic "opposite the strait."
  - Capital: concentric alternating rings of sea and land (canals, bridges, harbors) around a central small island/acropolis with royal palace and temple of Poseidon + Cleito (his mortal consort/mother of the 10 kings).
  - Temple: enormous, clad in precious metals including orichalcum (fabled "mountain copper"), with golden roof, ivory, etc. Large enough for the kings and 10,000+ to gather.
  - Government: 10 kings (descendants of Poseidon) with absolute power in their regions but bound by strict laws inscribed on orichalcum pillar in Poseidon's temple; periodic grand bull-hunts and ritual sacrifices (bulls captured bare-handed, then sacrificed with prayers/wine libations; blood in mixing bowl for oath of justice).
  - Wealth & Engineering: vast plain with irrigation channels, docks for triremes, walls, aqueducts, abundant resources (metals, timber, elephants, fragrant plants).
  - Decline: over generations, divine portion in the kings' nature "became faint" through mixing with mortal; they became greedy, hubristic, "filled with lawless ambition and power."
- **War & End**: Atlantis attacks Europe/Asia; Athens (leading smaller virtuous alliance) defeats them. Then "violent earthquakes and floods... the island of Atlantis... disappeared into the sea." The text of Critias breaks off just as Zeus is convening the gods to discuss punishing the Atlanteans.
- **No Hermocrates**: Planned trilogy (Timaeus → Critias → Hermocrates) never completed.

## Status as History vs Myth/Allegory (per 2008 intro/notes)
- **No factual basis**: No sunken city or geological evidence (volcanoes, mud shoals) at the described Atlantic location and timescale. Plato is our only ancient literary source; no independent Egyptian/Greek records or earlier traditions support the tale.
- **Possible inspirations debated**: Minoan Crete + Thera eruption (parallels in culture/decline but chronology and location don't match well). Political allegory (Atlantis as stand-in for 5th-c. imperial Athens or Persia; ancient Athens as idealized ancestral/Spartan constitution). Moral tale about hubris, naval power, and the superiority of balanced agrarian/landed virtue over maritime empire.
- **Plato's framing**: Presented by Critias as "absolutely true" with Solon's authority, yet the whole Timaeus/Critias frame is "likely account" territory. Modern scholarship treats it as largely fictional invention by Plato for philosophical/political purposes.

## Cultural Legacy
- Origin point of the entire Atlantis mythos in Western (and global pop) culture: lost advanced civilizations, cataclysms, utopian/pre-flood societies.
- Fueled Renaissance speculations, 19th-20th c. theosophy/pseudoarchaeology (Donnelly, etc.), Nazi occultism, New Age, comics, films (Disney's Atlantis, etc.).
- In serious scholarship: primarily a window into Plato's political thought, use of myth, and late cosmology rather than history.
- "Atlantis" as archetype for any vanished golden age or cautionary imperial tale.
