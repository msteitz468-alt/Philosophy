# Surveillance Capitalism and Platform Power
> The business model of dominant tech platforms — Facebook, Google, Amazon — in which surveillance of users, workers, and competitors is simultaneously the means of profit extraction and of political domination.

## Summary

Zephyr Teachout's Chapter 2 presents the big three tech companies not as innovative firms but as private governments with explicit governmental ambitions. Three features define platform power:

1. **Surveillance as infrastructure.** Google's public kiosks, drones, education tools, and cloud services embed surveillance into essential public infrastructure. Amazon's Alexa, fulfillment data, and HQ2 beauty contest extracted intimate knowledge of American cities and commerce. Facebook's business model is fundamentally the conversion of social behavior into targeting data. Surveillance is not a side effect; it is the product.

2. **Private regulation.** Platforms are no longer participants in markets; they *are* the market. Amazon controls which products consumers see (the buy box), which publishers thrive (Hachette, Macmillan), which cities receive investment. Google controls which ideas are surfaced, which companies succeed (via search ranking), and which academic opinions get published (funding + pressure on think tanks). Facebook controls the terms of public discourse for billions.

3. **Governmental ambition without accountability.** Zuckerberg has explicitly described Facebook as a government — with "referendums," judicial oversight boards, and a proposed global currency (Libra). Bezos repurposes military airfields, contracts with the U.S. government for 62% of highest-level cloud authorizations, and harvests data from city governments. Google embeds itself in law schools, think tanks, and school systems. These are not accidental effects of commercial success — they are the point.

## Facebook as Private Government

The 2018 Zuckerberg Senate hearing is Teachout's key illustration. Senators addressed Zuckerberg as if he were the governor of a foreign country: they "supported" his legislation, praised his "enacting reforms," asked for his "help." When asked if Facebook was a monopoly, he said he didn't "feel like it" was — the response of a monarch, not a market participant. Julia Angwin: "Mark Zuckerberg isn't going to run for President, he is already President."

Zuckerberg: "In a lot of ways Facebook is more like a government than a traditional company." This is not a metaphor Teachout is imposing; it is Zuckerberg's own self-description.

## Amazon as Quasi-State

Jeff Bezos explicitly describes his goal as becoming the "underlying infrastructure that commerce runs on." Amazon Marketplace operates as a quasi-state for its 6 million sellers: they pay fees (taxes), obey rules (regulation), and live in terror of termination (punishment) — with no democratic input into any of it. Amazon's buy box determines which products exist commercially; refusal to use Amazon's fulfillment service risks burial in search results. Stacy Mitchell: Bezos "built Amazon to replace markets."

## The Targeted Ad Business Model as Democracy Threat

The targeted ad model requires maximum surveillance and maximum addiction — the two features most corrosive to democratic public life. Facebook's algorithms are explicitly programmed to exploit shame, anxiety, and fear of missing out to maximize time on platform. This is Tristan Harris's "race to the bottom of the brainstem." The business model makes it structurally impossible for Facebook to prioritize user well-being or public discourse, regardless of Zuckerberg's stated intentions.

## Comparison: Mafia and Fascism

Teachout invokes two analogies (partly following Tim Wu):
- **Mafia:** Coexists with democratic government; divides territory among "families"; buys off politicians; allows competitors only as tribute-payers; uses force (deplatforming, algorithmic demotion, predatory pricing) rather than legal authority.
- **Fascism (Tim Wu):** Centralized control, unified ideology, strong-leader model, corporate-state fusion — but *without* the state, and *without* the democratic legitimacy that even authoritarian states claim.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Connects to [[Thinkers/Michel Foucault]]'s concept of *biopower* and *governmentality*: Foucault describes how power operates through surveillance, normalization, and the production of subjectivity — not just overt coercion. Platform surveillance is a perfect Foucauldian apparatus. Teachout does not cite Foucault, but the structural analysis is convergent.
- > [!warning] Conflicts with libertarian defense: platforms are private; users consent; exit is available. Teachout's counter: consent under monopoly conditions is not free consent; exit is illusory (Facebook bought every competitor); users have no meaningful alternative.
- > [!warning] The "mafia" analogy has limits: the Mafia uses violence; tech platforms use algorithmic manipulation and network effects. The power mechanisms are different even if the structural position is similar.
