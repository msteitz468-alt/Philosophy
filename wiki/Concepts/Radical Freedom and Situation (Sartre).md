# Radical Freedom and Situation (Sartre)
> Because consciousness *is not* anything, the human is not free to choose among options but *is* freedom itself — a freedom that realises itself only in and through the particular situation it must take up as its own.

## Summary

Since the pour-soi *is not* (see [[Concepts/Being-for-itself and Being-in-itself (Sartre)]]), the human can only be thought as **free**. Freedom, for Sartre, is not a property one *has* — only an en-soi has properties — and it is emphatically *not* "freedom of choice" among objects (like choosing soft drinks in a shop). The debate over free will misses the point twice over: we cannot *have* a free will as a property, and the very question already presupposes freedom (a rock could never ask it). Freedom is an ungrounded *starting point*: "the being we are not, that we are ourselves."

**Situation, not determination.** The world does not determine me; it places me in a *situation* — always particular, always mine, never absorbable into a universal philosophy. A pandemic, an illness, a war are objective facts, but it is my free subjectivity that relates to them, and different people take them up differently. A situation "demands behaviour," and behaviour implies that I am not already something (a stone does not "behave" as a stone). Even natural law (I fall from a window under gravity) does not abolish freedom: unlike the stone, I *relate* to the law — I can fear the fall, jump, over- or under-estimate the danger. Hence "there are no situations that determine my life as gravity determines the falling of the stone."

**Anguish.** Sartre distinguishes *fear* (irreflexive, directed at a determinate object — my death, the war's consequences) from **anguish (angoisse)**, which is reflexive and is the very *experience of my freedom*. Fear is being afraid of sliding into the abyss; anguish is the vertiginous disclosure that nothing prevents me from *throwing myself* in. Anguish breaks through the everyday order in which I live "as if everything were logical and fixed," and reveals that all my footholds are a game.

**Project.** Freedom is realised as a **project (projet)**: the pour-soi seen from a goal that lies in the future but is chosen so as to be, in the present, who I am. I do not design my life from a drawing board or pick it off a menu; I become myself *in* the situations I fall into by engaging myself with them. There is no "I" prior to the situation. To recognise the situation as mine — neither passively "accepting" it (refusing, fighting, is also a choice) nor disowning it as fate — is what Sartre calls **engagement**. Sartre's "ethics," Welten stresses, contains nothing more than this: *not disowning freedom*. Ethics is unavoidable but can never be *founded*, since any foundation would immediately betray freedom.

## Key Claims / Positions
- The human does not *have* freedom; the pour-soi *is* freedom.
- Freedom is not choice among objects but the ungrounded way I take up my situation.
- A situation determines nothing; it is taken up freely (the relation, not the situation, is free).
- *Anguish* (reflexive) is the experience of freedom; *fear* (irreflexive) is directed at an object.
- Freedom is realised as a future-oriented *project*; recognising the situation as mine = engagement.
- Ethics cannot be founded; "Sartrean ethics" = the refusal to disown freedom.

### How Sartre Differs From Prior Thinkers in the Wiki
- **vs [[Thinkers/Kant]]**: like Kant, ethics presupposes freedom and is not derivable from natural law; *unlike* Kant, there is no universal moral law or rational ground — values are arbitrary.
- **vs compatibilist/determinist debates** (e.g. [[Thinkers/Hume]], [[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]): Sartre sidesteps the free-will/determinism question as malformed; causality does not apply to existence.
- **vs [[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]]**: resonates with thrownness/resoluteness, but Sartre foregrounds an almost limitless freedom of self-definition.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] The doctrine of "absolute" freedom-in-situation is widely charged with underweighting material/social constraint — a charge Sartre himself answers in the *Critique* by re-locating freedom within the *practical-inert* and scarcity.
