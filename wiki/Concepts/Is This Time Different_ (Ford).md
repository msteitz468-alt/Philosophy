# Is This Time Different? (Ford)

> Martin Ford's (2015) central historical and empirical question: whether the current wave of automation driven by information technology, machine learning, and robotics represents a qualitative break from previous technological disruptions. Historically, economists and policymakers have dismissed fears of permanent technological unemployment as a "Luddite fallacy" — new jobs and opportunities have always emerged to absorb displaced workers, often at higher skill and wage levels. Ford argues that the data since the 1970s (and accelerating in the 21st century) shows the postwar "Golden Age" symbiosis between technological progress and broad-based prosperity has ended, and that the characteristics of today's general-purpose IT make this time different in ways that conventional retraining or sectoral transition stories do not adequately address.

## Summary (from the source)

Ford opens Chapter 2 with the famous Milton Friedman "shovels vs. spoons" anecdote and the 1964 Ad Hoc Committee on the Triple Revolution report (which warned of "cybernation" leading to massive unemployment and proposed a guaranteed income). He traces earlier concerns (Norbert Wiener's 1949 cybernation warning, Kurt Vonnegut's *Player Piano* (1952), the 1960s National Commission on Technology, Automation, and Economic Progress) and notes that the dire predictions did not materialize in the postwar decades — unemployment stayed low, wages rose with productivity, and new jobs appeared.

The "Golden Age" (roughly 1945–1973) was characterized by:
- Rapid technological progress raising worker productivity.
- Productivity gains translating into higher real wages for ordinary workers.
- Rising incomes driving mass consumption, which created more jobs and investment.
- A virtuous feedback loop that benefited a broad middle class.

Ford documents that this pattern broke in the 1970s. Key evidence ("Seven Deadly Trends"):
- Stagnant/declining real wages for production and nonsupervisory workers since 1973 (~13% decline after inflation) despite 107% productivity growth.
- Median household income growth lagging far behind per-capita GDP (much of the gain from women entering the workforce rather than broad wage growth).
- The 2000s: zero net new jobs created in the U.S. (first such decade since the Great Depression), despite the economy needing ~1 million jobs per year just to keep pace with population/workforce growth.
- "Jobless recoveries" becoming the norm.
- Sharp decline in labor's share of national income; capital and business owners capture the gains.
- Soaring income and consumption inequality (top 5% of households ~40% of spending and rising).
- New industries and companies achieving enormous scale and market value with minimal employment (Google, Facebook, WhatsApp examples).

The chapter title "Is This Time Different?" directly confronts the mainstream economic view that technology always creates more jobs than it destroys in the long run. Ford does not claim technology is "bad"; he claims the *relationship* between technological progress and the economic fortunes of ordinary workers has changed in structural ways.

Why different now?
- **General-purpose technology**: IT and software can be applied across virtually every industry and task, unlike sector-specific machines (e.g., agricultural mechanization or early factory automation).
- **"Predictable" vs. merely "routine" jobs**: The relevant criterion is whether a task can be learned from a detailed record of past performance or improved by repeating prior examples. Big data + machine learning makes this possible for many white-collar and professional roles (radiology image analysis, legal discovery, journalism, programming, customer service, office work) that were previously thought safe.
- **New industries born automated**: Emerging sectors incorporate powerful labor-saving technology from the start; they do not follow the historical pattern of labor-intensive growth followed by later automation.
- **Speed and breadth**: The exponential improvement in computing power (Moore's Law and beyond) and the network effects of digital platforms compress timelines and amplify impacts.

Ford acknowledges that past automation fears were often overstated in their timing or specifics. He argues the data since the 1970s, combined with the qualitative nature of current technologies, justifies reopening the question seriously rather than dismissing it by historical analogy.

## Key Claims / How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Ford**: The postwar Golden Age was a temporary "Goldilocks period," not the normal state of a technological economy. The decoupling of productivity and wages, the characteristics of general-purpose IT, and the "predictable jobs" vulnerability mean we cannot assume automatic broad-based prosperity or easy sectoral transitions. Policy must adapt (see basic income).
- **Kelly (forces as the difference)**: The "cognifying" and "becoming" forces — cheap ubiquitous intelligence added to everything, constant flux from products to services/processes, perpetual newbies and upgrading — are exactly what make the current period different from earlier industrial revolutions. Kelly sees these as strong biases producing long-term protopian gains if embraced and managed; Ford sees them producing the structural break in labor demand and consumption. Cross to [[Sources/The Inevitable - Kevin Kelly (2016)]], [[Concepts/Cognifying (Kelly)]], [[Concepts/Becoming (Kelly)]], [[Concepts/Technium (Kelly)]] (technium "wants" driving exotropic acceleration).
- **Bostrom and superintelligence**: The same accelerating technology that Ford analyzes for near-term job displacement is the substrate for the intelligence explosion and superintelligence paths Bostrom examines. "Is this time different?" at the economic level is a precursor question to "are we prepared for transformative AI?" Cross to [[Sources/Superintelligence - Nick Bostrom (2014)]].
- **Kurzweil acceleration**: Ford's analysis of exponential progress (the car-doubling-speed analogy for Moore's Law) aligns with Kurzweil's Law of Accelerating Returns, but Ford focuses on the distributional and demand-side consequences during the transition rather than the post-Singularity abundance. The "different" part is whether institutions keep up. Cross to [[Sources/The Singularity Is Near - Ray Kurzweil (2005)]], [[Concepts/Law of Accelerating Returns (Kurzweil)]].
- **Mitchell limits**: Ford points to rapid practical advances (Watson, deep learning, robotics) as evidence the displacement is already underway for predictable work. Mitchell's emphasis on the remaining gaps (brittleness, lack of common sense/analogy) suggests the transition may be uneven — some jobs automated quickly, others resistant longer — but does not eliminate the economic risk from the automatable slice. Cross to [[Sources/Artificial Intelligence - Melanie Mitchell (2019)]].
- **Wiener and historical warnings**: Ford explicitly positions his argument as a data-updated revival of Norbert Wiener's early cybernation concerns and the 1960s Triple Revolution report. The "this time different" question was asked before; Ford says the data now supports taking it seriously. Cross to [[Sources/The Human Use of Human Beings - Norbert Wiener (1950)]], [[Concepts/Second Industrial Revolution (Wiener)]].
- **History of Ideas / progress**: Challenges the assumption (common in postwar economics) that the Golden Age was the natural, permanent outcome of technological progress. Parallels vault treatments of how presuppositions shape what counts as "normal" or "progress" (Lovejoy static Chain vs. becoming; Butterfield/Kuhn on mental shifts and what gets seen as revolutionary vs. normal science).

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- **Historical analogy vs. structural break**: Are we in another (painful but temporary) transition like agriculture-to-industry or manufacturing-to-services, or has general-purpose IT + AI + capital intensity changed the rules so that net job creation at living wages is no longer automatic? Ford's data vs. the long record of past fears being "wrong."
- **With Kelly protopia**: Kelly presents the forces as ultimately generative and manageable for incremental improvement. Ford accepts the forces but argues their labor-market and demand effects may require more than "managing particulars" — possibly a new social contract.
- **Speed and political response**: Even if the long-run outcome is abundance, the transition costs (unemployment, inequality, demand collapse, social instability) could be severe if institutions lag. "Is this time different?" is partly a question of whether political and economic systems can adapt faster than the technology.
- **Measurement and definition**: What counts as "technological unemployment" vs. cyclical, frictional, or structural unemployment from other causes (globalization, demographics, policy)? Ford uses multiple indicators (wage-productivity gap, jobless recoveries, labor share, new-industry employment intensity) to make the case.
- **Interaction with other trends**: Automation does not occur in isolation. Aging populations, climate change, resource constraints, and rising costs in education/health care (sectors resistant to productivity gains) interact with and can amplify the effects.
