# Political Animal and the Natural Polis (Aristotle)

> "Man is by nature a political animal" (*zōon politikon*). The polis is not a convention but the natural, self-sufficient end-point of a series of communities (household → village → polis) each existing for the sake of some good, and it is prior in nature to the individual and the household because the whole is prior to the part.

## Summary

*Politics* Book I opens by resolving the polis into its natural elements: the household (male/female pairing for reproduction, ruler/ruled pairing for preservation) grows into the village, and villages combine into the self-sufficient polis, "the end of the other associations," at which point "the state is a creation of nature." Aristotle applies his general definition of nature — "the nature of a thing is its end," what it becomes when its growth is complete — to the city itself. Because man alone among animals possesses *logos* (reasoned speech, not mere voice signaling pleasure/pain), he alone can communicate the just and unjust, the good and evil, which "is the association of living beings who have this sense" that makes a household and a state. Anyone who could live outside the polis "must be either a beast or a god" — self-sufficiency of that kind is not human. From this Aristotle draws his most famous structural claim: the state is prior in nature to the individual, "as the whole is of necessity prior to the part" — a hand severed from the body is a hand in name only, since its function (and hence its being) depends on the whole. Justice, finally, is "the bond of men in states," the ordering principle without which man is "the most unholy and the most savage of animals."

## Key Claims / Positions

- Natural teleology applied to society: communities are natural because they arise necessarily and serve an end (survival, then the good life).
- *Zōon politikon*: political community is not a late convention layered on solitary individuals but constitutive of human nature.
- *Logos* (reasoned speech about justice/injustice) — not mere signaling — is the differentia that makes man political in a way no herd animal is.
- Organic priority: the whole (state) is ontologically prior to the part (individual/household), since the part's function/identity depends on the whole.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Versus [[Thinkers/Plato]]**: Plato's *Republic* also treats the city as natural (arising from mutual need, Book II), but grounds its unity on a functional division of labor overseen by philosopher-guardians who abolish private household and property for the ruling class in the name of maximal unity. Aristotle's own Book II critique (see [[Concepts/Critique of Plato's Republic (Aristotle)]]) argues Plato pursues too much unity, treating the state as if it should become a single household or individual, when a state is by nature a plurality (*zōon politikon* does not mean the citizens dissolve into an organism, only that each depends functionally on the polis).
- **Downstream contrast with [[Thinkers/Thomas Hobbes]]**: Hobbes inverts the thesis — the state is an artificial construction (the Leviathan, a "mortal god") created by contract to escape the state of nature, not a natural growth from the household; there is no natural sociability, only fear of violent death. This is a direct and productive contradiction with Aristotle's naturalism about political community.
- **Downstream contrast with [[Thinkers/John Stuart Mill]]**: Mill's liberal individualism (in *On Liberty*) treats the individual and their liberty as the starting normative unit against which the state's authority must be justified — the reverse priority ordering from Aristotle's whole-prior-to-part.
