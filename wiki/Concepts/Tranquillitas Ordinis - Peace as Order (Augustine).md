# Tranquillitas Ordinis — Peace as Order (Augustine)

> Augustine's master definition of peace: *pax omnium rerum tranquillitas ordinis* — "the peace of all things is the tranquillity of order" (*City of God* XIX.13). Peace is not the absence of conflict but the right arrangement of every part to its proper place — from the body's organs to the heavenly fellowship in God.

## Summary

Book XIX of the *City of God* is the great philosophical summit of the work: Augustine engages the entire Western tradition on the supreme good, rejects all philosophical attempts to locate happiness within this life, and proposes that the City of God's supreme good is *peace in life everlasting*. At the center of this argument is his universal definition of peace: ordered arrangement, at every scale of existence.

**The definition** (XIX.13):  
*"The peace of the body is a tempering of the component parts in duly ordered proportion; the peace of the irrational soul is a duly ordered repose of the appetites; the peace of the rational soul is the duly ordered agreement of cognition and action. The peace of body and soul is the duly ordered life and health of a living creature; peace between mortal man and God is an ordered obedience, in faith, in subjection to an everlasting law; peace between men is an ordered agreement of mind with mind; the peace of a home is the ordered agreement among those who live together about giving and obeying orders; the peace of the Heavenly City is a perfectly ordered and perfectly harmonious fellowship in the enjoyment of God, and a mutual fellowship in God; the peace of the whole universe is the tranquillity of order — and order is the arrangement of things equal and unequal in a pattern which assigns to each its proper position."*

## Peace at Every Level

Augustine traces peace through a nested hierarchy — every created thing seeks its own appropriate peace:

| Level | Peace consists in |
|-------|------------------|
| **Body** | Right proportion of organs and parts |
| **Irrational soul** | Ordered repose of appetites |
| **Rational soul** | Ordered agreement of cognition and action |
| **Body + soul (living creature)** | Duly ordered life and health |
| **Man and God** | Ordered obedience, faith, subjection to eternal law |
| **Men with men** | Ordered agreement of minds |
| **Household** | Ordered authority and obedience among family members |
| **City** | Ordered concord of citizens in authority and obedience |
| **Earthly city** | Temporal peace — partial, imperfect, imposed by domination |
| **Heavenly City** | Perfect fellowship in the enjoyment of God; mutual peace in God |
| **The whole universe** | *Tranquillitas ordinis* — all things in their proper position |

Even the wretched are not outside the scope of order: they suffer a disordered peace, a punishment fitting their defection. "There is no peace that is not some form of peace, however wrong or imperfect." Even the robber, the tyrant, even the monstrous Cacus — all seek some form of peace for themselves. "Pride is a perverted imitation of God": it rejects the fellowship of equality under God in order to impose its own domination — but it still seeks *some* peace, only a false one.

## Peace as the Aim of Even War

Augustine's most famous political insight: *even wars are waged in pursuit of peace.*

> "Anyone who joins me in an examination, however slight, of human affairs… recognizes that just as there is no man who does not wish for joy, so there is no man who does not wish for peace… Even wars, then, are waged with peace as their object, even when they are waged by those who are concerned to exercise their warlike prowess, either in command or in the actual fighting." (XIX.12)

The robber who ambushes travelers still maintains peace with his gang. The tyrant who conquers nations wants those nations to become his own people, obeying his own peace. Even the individual sinner seeks bodily peace and the repose of satisfied desire. This universality of the desire for peace is the evidence that peace is natural — ordered peace is the good of every being, as its nature intends.

## Just War

Augustine did not systematically develop just war theory, but laid the foundations:

- A just war must be caused by the injustice of the opposing side; it is never desired for its own sake.
- "The wise man will wage just wars. But if he remembers that he is a human being, he will rather lament the fact that he is faced with the necessity of waging just wars; for if they were not just, he would not have to engage in them."
- Just war is always a tragedy — the injustice that makes it necessary is itself an evil to be mourned. The wise man does not celebrate war; he accepts it with grief.
- This contrasts sharply with the Roman glorification of conquest and the idea that war brings honor. For Augustine, even the victor in a just war mourns the necessity.

Later tradition (Aquinas, Grotius, Vitoria) developed the formal *jus ad bellum* criteria (just cause, right intention, proper authority, last resort, proportionality) from Augustine's foundation. See [[Concepts/Natural Law (Aquinas)]].

## The Supreme Good = Peace in Life Everlasting

In Book XIX, Augustine systematically refutes all philosophical positions that locate the supreme good within this life. His argument:

1. All philosophers agree the supreme good must bring *happiness*.
2. Happiness requires stability, security, and freedom from reversal.
3. Every natural good in this life (health, friendship, wisdom, virtue) can be and is overturned by disease, betrayal, madness, or death.
4. Even the Stoic "wise man," who must kill himself to escape unbearable ills, proves that this life cannot be the locus of true happiness.
5. Therefore: "We are saved in hope, but when hope is seen fulfilled it is hope no longer. If we are hoping for something we do not yet see, then it is with steadfast endurance that we await it." (XIX.4, quoting Romans 8)

The true Supreme Good = *"peace in life everlasting"* or *"life everlasting in peace."* The City of God's end is **eternal peace** — the tranquillitas ordinis at its maximum, the perfectly harmonious fellowship in the enjoyment of God, where no adversity disturbs, no enemy intrudes, no will rebels.

> "So great is the peace which will be there that nothing better can be found, nothing more enjoyable can be imagined." (XIX.27)

## Relationship to Other Traditions

**vs. Eudaimonia (Aristotle)**: Eudaimonia is activity of the soul in accordance with virtue over a complete life. Augustine accepts that human nature has natural goods and that virtue is excellent — but insists that no amount of virtue or natural flourishing within this life achieves the stability and completeness that true happiness requires. Natural happiness is real but imperfect; it awaits its completion in the supernatural peace of God. See [[Concepts/Eudaimonia]], [[Concepts/Beatific Vision - Last End (Aquinas)]].

**vs. Ataraxia (Epicurean/Stoic/Pyrrhonian)**: The various Hellenistic versions of inner peace (prudent pleasure, control of what is "up to us," suspension of judgment) are genuine goods and genuine achievements of philosophical wisdom — but they cannot withstand the extremity of human ills. The Stoic sage who must die to preserve his freedom proves ataraxia's fragility. Augustine's peace transcends inner equilibrium because it depends not on the individual's wisdom or will but on God's grace and the eschatological fulfillment of all creation. See [[Concepts/Ataraxia]], [[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)]], [[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]].

**vs. The One (Plotinus)**: The return of the soul to the One (henosis) is the Neoplatonic analogue — but it is an individual, interior, and temporary achievement (the soul must return to embodiment). Augustine's peace is communal (the City of God = a fellowship), bodily (resurrection), permanent (eternal Sabbath), and given by grace rather than achieved by contemplation alone. See [[Concepts/Return of the Soul (Epistrophe)]], [[Concepts/The One (To Hen)]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Augustine's just war framework is foundational but incomplete. Aquinas formalized the criteria; later tradition debated whether Augustine's foundational pessimism about earthly peace makes the bar for "just war" too low (almost any war could be labeled "necessary") or too high (the grief requirement means war can never be undertaken with good conscience in the glory Augustine's Rome celebrated).
- The relationship between temporal peace and the Church's role is contested: does Augustine authorize theocratic coercion (use temporal force to bring heretics to unity) or not? His anti-Donatist writings suggest yes in practice; his theoretical framework suggests the earthly city has legitimate but limited authority.
