# Federalism and Compound Republic (Federalist 51)

## Overview

In *Federalist No. 51*, James Madison describes the American system as a "compound republic" that provides "double security" to the rights of the people through a particular division of power.

" In the compound republic of America, the power surrendered by the people is first divided between two distinct governments, and then the portion allotted to each subdivided among distinct and separate departments. Hence a double security arises to the rights of the people. The different governments will control each other, at the same time that each will be controlled by itself."

This is the essence of federalism as designed in the Constitution: neither a pure confederacy (states sovereign, general government only agent of states) nor a pure consolidation (states abolished or purely administrative), but a compound in which states remain "constituent parts of the national sovereignty" (direct representation in Senate, retention of important sovereign powers) while the general government operates directly on individuals in its sphere.

## Key Arguments (from No. 51 and supporting essays)

- **First division (federal vs. state governments)**: Power is divided between two distinct governments. Each has its sphere; they control each other. States are not eliminated; they retain "certain exclusive and very important portions of sovereign power" and have direct representation in the Senate. The general government has direct operation on citizens for national objects.
- **Second division (within each government)**: Separation of powers into distinct departments with checks (see separate concept).
- **Double security**: The two levels check each other; within each level, departments check each other. "The different governments will control each other, at the same time that each will be controlled by itself."
- **Multiplicity and extent**: The compound structure, combined with the large extent of the Union and the resulting multiplicity of interests and sects, protects against majority faction and oppression of minorities (cross-reference Faction No. 10).
- **Not consolidation**: "The proposed Constitution, so far from implying an abolition of the State governments, makes them constituent parts of the national sovereignty, by allowing them a direct representation in the Senate, and leaves in their possession certain exclusive and very important portions of sovereign power. This fully corresponds, in every rational import of the terms, with the idea of a federal government."
- **Practical balance**: Energetic general government for union objects (defense, commerce, uniform law, treaties) + preservation of states for local purposes, experimentation, and as additional checks.

Supporting essays (e.g., No. 39) elaborate the mixed federal/national character depending on the object.
