# Verisimilitude (Truthlikeness) (Popper)

> From *Conjectures and Refutations* ch. 10 ("Truth, Rationality, and the Growth of Scientific Knowledge"). **Verisimilitude** is Popper's measure of how *near to the truth* a theory is — its **truth-content minus its falsity-content**. It lets a fallibilist say that one false theory can be *better*, i.e. correspond more closely to the facts, than another, and so makes sense of the idea that science *progresses toward truth* without ever attaining certainty.

## The Idea

- Popper accepts **Tarski's** rehabilitation of the objective (**correspondence**) theory of truth: a statement is true if and only if it corresponds to the facts. Truth is a **regulative idea** — the standard we may fall short of. Without it, talk of *error* and of *learning from mistakes* would make no sense.

- But mere truth/falsity is too coarse to describe scientific progress, since *every* universal theory is strictly speaking probably false, and a refuted theory (Newton) can still be a magnificent advance over its predecessor (Kepler/Galileo). We need a comparative notion: not just *true or false*, but *closer to or farther from the truth*.

- **Definitions**:
  - The **content** of a statement *a* = the class of all its logical consequences.
  - Its **truth-content** = the class of its *true* consequences (German *Wahrheitsgehalt*).
  - Its **falsity-content** = the class of its *false* consequences.
  - A true statement has only true consequences; a false statement has *both* true and false consequences (Popper's example: "It always rains on Sundays" is false, but entails the true "it rained last Sunday" if it did).

- **Comparative verisimilitude**: theory *t₂* is closer to the truth than *t₁* iff either (a) the truth-content but *not* the falsity-content of *t₂* exceeds that of *t₁*, or (b) the falsity-content of *t₁* but *not* its truth-content exceeds that of *t₂*. Informally: *more truth and no more falsehood*, or *less falsehood and no less truth*.

- **Verisimilitude vs. probability** (the crux): the two are *opposites*. High **probability** in the calculus of probability is high *logical weakness / low content* — a tautology has probability 1 and says nothing. Science values **high content** (bold, improbable, prohibitive theories), because content is what can be tested and what carries truth. So the aim of science is **not** high probability (as inductivists assume) but **high verisimilitude**: a bold theory that, even if eventually false, has large truth-content and small falsity-content. "We prefer an interesting, bold conjecture even if soon refuted to a recital of irrelevant truisms — because in finding it false we learn much about the truth."

## Role in the Growth of Knowledge

Verisimilitude is the **regulative aim** that [[Concepts/Corroboration (Popper)|corroboration]] is meant to track: a well-corroborated *bold* theory is our best current guess at higher truthlikeness, though corroboration never *proves* higher verisimilitude. It completes the picture: [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)|falsifiability]] (what makes a theory scientific) + severe testing → corroboration (the report of survival) → verisimilitude (what we hope is thereby increasing). Tied to the **three requirements for the growth of knowledge**: a good new theory has a *simple unifying idea*, is *independently testable* (new predictions), and *passes new severe tests* — each of which is an attempt to push truth-content up while keeping falsity-content down.

## Open Question / Technical Caveat

Popper's *formal definition* of verisimilitude (the truth-content/falsity-content comparison) was shown in **1974 (Tichý and Miller)** to fail: on this definition no false theory can be more verisimilar than another false theory, which collapses exactly the cases it was meant to handle. The **intuition** — that science aims at increasing truth-content and decreasing falsity-content, that some false theories are nearer the truth than others — is widely retained, and successor definitions have been proposed; but Popper's own measure does not work. (Post-dates the 1963 text; flagged here so the page is not read as endorsing the broken measure.)

## Relation to Other Vault Content

- **With [[Concepts/Correspondence Theory of Truth (Russell)]]**: Popper and Russell share the objective correspondence theory of truth and the insistence that minds *discover*, not *create*, truth. Verisimilitude is Popper's graded extension of it.

- **Vs. [[Concepts/Pragmatic Theory of Truth (James)]]**: sharp opposition. For James truth "happens" to ideas and is "the expedient"; for Popper truth is correspondence and is a fixed standard we approach — usefulness is irrelevant to truth. (Popper, following Russell, ties truth-as-usefulness to authoritarian thinking — see [[Concepts/No Authoritative Sources of Knowledge (Popper)]].)

- **Vs. inductivist probabilism**: extends [[Concepts/Corroboration (Popper)]]'s rejection of "degree of confirmation = probability"; verisimilitude is the *positive* aim that probability cannot supply.

- **With [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]]**: verisimilitude is how Popper makes *progress* intelligible after granting Hume that induction gives no justification — we approach truth by error-elimination, not by confirmation.

- **Vs. [[Thinkers/Thomas Kuhn]]** (direct conflict): Kuhn denies any goal "set by nature in advance" — science is "evolution *from* primitive beginnings," **not** "evolution *toward*" a fixed truth, so there is no truth for theories to get *nearer* to. This is the deepest clash with Popper's verisimilitude; see [[Concepts/Scientific Revolutions and Paradigm Shifts (Kuhn)]] and [[Contradictions/Popper vs Kuhn - Falsification vs Paradigms]]. (Kuhn does use "verisimilitude" approvingly, but only for the realism of his own two-stage account, not for approach-to-truth.)

## Vault Meta / AI

A page or synthesis is to be judged not by how "probable"/safe/hedged it is, but by its **content and truthlikeness**: a bold, specific, high-content summary that is mostly right and partly wrong is more valuable than a vague, unfalsifiable one that is trivially "true." Successive ingests aim to raise truth-content and cut falsity-content (correcting errors, adding correct detail) — the wiki's analogue of growing verisimilitude. For AI: optimize for informative, testable correctness, not for the hedged high-probability blandness that maximizes "safety" at the cost of content.
