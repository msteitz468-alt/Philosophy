# Metempsychosis (Transmigration of Souls)
> The doctrine that the soul is immortal and passes, after death, into a succession of bodies — human, animal, even plant — a teaching attached to Pythagoras in the *earliest and best-attested* layer of the tradition.

## Summary
Metempsychosis is, for Walter Burkert in [[Sources/Lore and Science in Ancient Pythagoreanism - Walter Burkert (1972)]], the one doctrine securely traceable to [[Thinkers/Pythagoras]] himself. Where the mathematical and astronomical achievements dissolve under source-criticism, the teaching of the transmigrating soul is fixed by the *oldest* witnesses — making it the historical bedrock of [[Schools/Pythagoreanism]] and the strongest evidence that Pythagoras was first of all a **religious** figure.

## The Evidence (why this one survives criticism)
- **Xenophanes** (a near-contemporary, hostile) mocks Pythagoras for stopping a man beating a puppy, claiming to recognize in its yelp the voice of a dead friend. A contemporary's satire is the gold standard of early attestation.
- **Aristotle** refers offhandedly to "the tales of the Pythagoreans" in which "just any soul" can "clothe itself" in "just any body" — treating metempsychosis as a well-known Pythagorean *myth*, and criticizing its indifference to which kind of body receives which soul.
- **Dicaearchus** (via Porphyry) reports that what became publicly known of Pythagoras' teaching was: the soul is immortal; it migrates into other kinds of animals; events recur cyclically; all living things are kin — while stressing that the *inner* teaching was guarded by exceptional secrecy (σιωπή).

## Burkert's Interpretation: the "Shaman," not the Scientist
Burkert reads the metempsychosis complex against **shamanism** — the figure who claims to know "more" than ordinary men, travels between worlds, and remembers former lives. (In later editions he softens the "shamanism" label, but the core point stands: the *Wundermann*/wonder-worker, not the natural scientist, is what the early evidence shows.) The mark of the shaman is the claim to *know beyond ordinary limits*; the mark of the scientist is *inference from evidence* (τεκμαίρεσθαι, as in Alcmaeon). On every early testimony, Pythagoras is the former. See [[Contradictions/Pythagoras the Scientist vs the Religious Charismatic]].

## Zhmud's Revisions (Zhmud 2012)
[[Sources/Pythagoras and the Early Pythagoreans - Leonid Zhmud (2012)]] *accepts* that Pythagoras genuinely taught transmigration — "there is no doubt that Pythagoras and many of his followers believed in the transmigration of souls" — but reverses or qualifies several Burkertian points:
- **No shamanism.** Zhmud rejects the "Greek shaman" model wholesale: shamanism is a historically *late* Asian complex (diffusing *to* Siberia in roughly the first millennium AD), almost certainly absent from Archaic Greece; the Meuli–Dodds "Scythian intermediary" rests "on sand" (two data points — the hemp steam-bath and the Enarees — neither shamanic). Dowden's quip on the steam-bath: "*the only problem: no shaman.*" The *katabasis* legends are late and comic (Hieronymus, Hermippus, 3rd c.), not early cult evidence.
- **Borrowed *from* Orphism, not invented by Pythagoras.** Against Burkert (Greeks owe metempsychosis to Pythagoras; Orphism borrowed from him), Zhmud holds Pythagoras "was probably the first but not the only Greek philosopher to take from Orphism the doctrine of reincarnation." Xenophanes (B7) already treats metempsychosis as familiar, not as Pythagoras' novelty. No Egyptian or Indian import (Egypt had no metempsychosis).
- **Stripped of the religion of salvation.** Pythagorean metempsychosis "lacks a primary context" — no Titan-anthropogony, no original sin, no body-as-tomb (*sōma-sēma*) or body-as-prison (those are *Orphic*, per Plato/Aristotle, **not** Pythagorean — correcting Burkert's cautious attribution). The soul's cycle is part of the *natural order* (*kyklos anankēs*), with **no final liberation** and no morally progressive series of rebirths.
- **Not universal, and vegetarianism inconsistent.** Metempsychosis "is not attested in the case of a single [named] Pythagorean known to us"; Pythagoras was likely **not** a strict vegetarian (Aristoxenus, Heraclides, the athlete Milo) — the elegant fix being that souls do not enter *sacrificial* animals, so their meat is edible (letting politically active Pythagoreans join civic sacrifice). The bean-taboo, better attested, was not originally tied to transmigration (cf. favism).
- **Orphism is real** (against the Wilamowitz/West deconstruction): the Olbia graffiti, gold tablets, and Derveni papyrus confirm a genuine movement — but a *Bacchic/Dionysian* one whose circles "nowhere intersect" with the Pythagorean; there were no "Orphico-Pythagoreans."

## The Huffman Volume (2014): Pythagorean, Not Essentially Orphic
Gábor Betegh's chapter in [[Sources/A History of Pythagoreanism - Carl A. Huffman (ed.) (2014)]] sharpens the relationship to Orphism (see [[Concepts/Pythagoreanism and Orphism]]):
- Metempsychosis is among the **best-documented tenets of *Pythagoras* himself**, whereas the evidence for it in *early Orphism* is "scarce and mostly indirect" — which complicates the simple "borrowed from Orphism" story (Zhmud) as much as the "invented by Pythagoras" story (Burkert).
- It is **not a shared *essential* core** linking the two movements: neither metempsychosis nor strict vegetarianism is the defining feature of either (see [[Concepts/Pythagorean Way of Life and Ethics]]). Porphyry's *Life* §19 ("the soul is immortal; it changes into other kinds of living beings... all animate things should be regarded as of the same family") is the oldest concise doctrinal summary — but it comes through the admiring Neopythagorean filter (see [[Concepts/The Three Ancient Lives of Pythagoras]]).
- **Reception**: the doctrine became the chief *embarrassment* of the Pythagoras revival — Christians rejected it (John of Salisbury: "even Plato must be refuted"), and Ficino strained to distinguish a respectable *metensomatosis* from animal *metempsychosis* (see [[Concepts/Pythagoras in Medieval and Renaissance Thought]]). It is also conspicuously *absent* from the [[Concepts/Pseudo-Pythagorean Writings]], confirming those are later school-philosophy, not archaic religion.

## How Different Thinkers Use This
- **Pythagoras / early Pythagoreans**: transmigration grounds a *way of life* — vegetarianism, ritual purity, and kinship of all living things follow from it.
- **[[Thinkers/Empedocles]]**: inherits and versifies the doctrine (the *daimōn* exiled through a cycle of births); cited by ancient commentators to explain the Pythagorean view.
- **[[Thinkers/Plato]]**: transforms metempsychosis into a philosophical instrument — the *Phaedo*, *Republic* (Myth of Er), and *Phaedrus* tie the cycle of rebirth to *katharsis* and *anamnēsis* (recollection). Burkert cautions that Plato's linkage of purification to *science* is bound up with his own theory of Forms (*chōrismos*) and should **not** be read back into early Pythagoreanism, where there is "not a trace of the idea of science as an escape from the world."
- **[[Thinkers/Plotinus]]** and Neoplatonism absorb the transmigrating, ascending soul into a metaphysics of return (cf. [[Concepts/Return of the Soul (Epistrophe)]]).

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] Whether the soul that transmigrates is morally *judged* between lives (implying it is *not* "just any" soul into "just any" body, as Aristotle charges) is disputed — the later moralized cycle may be a Platonic refinement of a cruder early belief.
