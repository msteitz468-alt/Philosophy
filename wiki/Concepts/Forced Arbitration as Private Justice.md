# Forced Arbitration as Private Justice
> The replacement of public courts by corporate-controlled arbitration — a private justice system in which the judge is paid by one party, there is no jury, no appeal, no public record, and no precedent.

## Summary

Forced arbitration is Teachout's case study for the mechanism by which monopoly corporations have dismantled the institutional infrastructure of democratic equality — the open court system. Since the Supreme Court's *Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp.* (1991) decision (written by Scalia), corporations have been permitted to require all employees and consumers to waive their right to a jury trial, class action, and appeal as a condition of employment or service — even for statutory civil rights claims.

The result: nearly two-thirds of large U.S. employers (companies over 1,000 employees) now use mandatory arbitration, and class action waivers are in close to 100% of major consumer contracts (banks, telecoms, tech platforms). A consumer contract with Wells Fargo, AT&T, Amazon, or Uber requires agreeing that any future dispute will be resolved in private arbitration — individually, never collectively.

## How Arbitration Works (and Fails)

- **Arbitrators are paid by the corporation**, not the state. Repeat-player arbitrators (those hired by the same employer repeatedly) favor the employer in roughly 80% of cases — compared to workers winning ~33% in public courts.
- **No jury, no appeal, no rules of evidence.** Arbitrators decide admissibility case-by-case. Discovery is minimal. Oral hearings can be waived. Reasons need not be given.
- **Class action ban.** The arbitration clause forbids collective action. So if Amazon defrauds 10 million customers of $30 each, no class action is possible. As Judge Posner noted: "The realistic alternative to a class action is not 17 million individual suits, but zero individual suits, as only a lunatic or a fanatic sues for $30."
- **"Loser pays" clauses** — the newest innovation — make even small suits prohibitively risky. Workers effectively have no legal recourse.
- **Secret proceedings** prevent precedent from forming and hide corporate misbehavior from public view. Civil rights violations, wage theft, and safety violations vanish.

## The Historical Mechanism

The 1925 Federal Arbitration Act was passed to resolve business-to-business commercial disputes between equals. The Supreme Court in *Alexander v. Gardner-Denver* (1974) unanimously held it could not override civil rights statutes — individual rights could not be prospectively waived. The Center for Public Resources (CPR), funded by 200 major corporations, spent the following decade lobbying and litigating to reverse this. *Gilmer* (1991) effectively overturned *Alexander*, holding that an "experienced businessman" who signed an arbitration clause had freely chosen to waive his federal rights.

Teachout reads Scalia's "fantasy of choice" — the neoliberal assumption that any signed contract reflects free individual choice — as the philosophical error enabling the entire system. In a monopolized economy, "choice" not to sign an arbitration clause is illusory: all companies in a sector use the same clauses, so refusing means forgoing employment or essential services altogether.

## Monopsony Enabling Arbitration

Arbitration is not merely a parallel problem to monopoly — it is *enabled by* monopoly. When there is only one wireless carrier, one bank, one ride-share platform in a region, employees and consumers have no leverage to reject arbitration clauses. The alternative court, the alternative employer, the alternative service — the option set that would give "choice" meaning — has been eliminated by monopoly. Arbitration and monopolization "reinforce each other": monopoly eliminates the exit option that would make arbitration refusal credible; arbitration immunizes monopolists from legal challenge.

## The Democratic Stakes

Forced arbitration does not merely harm individual plaintiffs — it destroys the public character of law:
- Cases are not brought, so violations are never documented
- Precedent does not form, so law does not evolve
- Trials are not public, so accountability disappears
- Civil rights statutes become dead letters without enforcement mechanisms

As Charlotte Garden (arbitration law expert) observes: the violations are "not scuttled away into private courts — they are vanished."

## Contradictions / Open Questions
