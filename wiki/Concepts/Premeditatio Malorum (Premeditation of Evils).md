# Premeditatio Malorum (Premeditation of Evils)

> The Stoic spiritual exercise of deliberately rehearsing, in advance, everything that could go wrong — loss, failure, illness, betrayal, death — so as to rob misfortune of its power to surprise and shatter us. Also called **negative visualization**. Ryan Holiday treats it as the opening move of the discipline of the **Will** ("Anticipation / Thinking Negatively") and identifies its modern management descendant, the **premortem**, as the same technique reinvented.

## The Exercise

Seneca's model (paraphrased by Holiday): before a journey, "rehearse your plans" and then go over, in your head or in writing, everything that could prevent it — *a storm could arise, the captain could fall ill, the ship could be attacked by pirates.* The result is a mind that meets reality already fitted for it:

> *"Nothing happens to the wise man against his expectation... nor do all things turn out for him as he wished but as he reckoned — and above all he reckoned that something could block his plans."* — Seneca

The structure is a simple loop: *What if... → Then I will... / Instead I'll... / No problem, we can always...* And where nothing can be done, the exercise shades into **managing expectations**: sometimes the only answer to "What if..." is *"It will suck, but we'll be okay."*

## Why It Works

- The worst thing is never the event itself but "the event *and* being caught by surprise." Anticipation removes the second, avoidable harm.
- It converts the Stoic doctrine of the [[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)]] into a forward-looking habit: since "the only guarantee, ever, is that things will go wrong," and the only variable we fully control is ourselves, we prepare the self.
- Holiday's runner metaphor: those who rehearse adversity are like runners who train on hills or at altitude, so bad luck becomes a chance to "make up time" on competitors who expected a flat course.

## The Premortem (Modern Descendant)

Holiday credits psychologist **Gary Klein** with the *premortem*: before a project launches, the team imagines it has already failed spectacularly and asks *why*. This is a *postmortem* run in advance. Now standard in start-ups and the *Harvard Business Review*, it is, Holiday insists, "actually nothing new — the credit goes to the Stoics, [who] even had a better name." Mike Tyson supplies the folk version: *"If you're not humble, life will visit humbleness upon you."*

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Seneca** — the fullest ancient source; *praemeditatio futurorum malorum* recurs across the *Letters* and *On the Shortness of Life*. Paired with his daily "rehearse death" (see [[Concepts/Memento Mori (Stoic Practice)]]). See [[Thinkers/Seneca]], [[Sources/Seneca - Letters from a Stoic]].
- **Epictetus / Marcus Aurelius** — Marcus's morning preparation ("I shall meet with the busy-body, the ungrateful, the arrogant...") is a premeditation of the day's evils, so that no one's behavior can disturb the ruling faculty. See [[Concepts/Ruling Faculty and the Daemon (Marcus Aurelius)]].
- **Ryan Holiday** — modernizes it as "thinking negatively" and links it to the premortem and expectation-management. See [[Thinkers/Ryan Holiday]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

> [!warning] Superficial tension with the Stoic (and Holiday's own) emphasis on [[Concepts/Every Act as if It Were the Last (Marcus Aurelius)|living in the present]]: does rehearsing future evils not pull the mind out of the present moment? The Stoic resolution is that *premeditatio malorum* is a bounded exercise done *in order to* free the present of anxiety — you visualize the loss so that, having priced it in, you can act now without fear. It is preparation, not rumination; the failure mode (anxious catastrophizing) is precisely what the disciplined version avoids.
