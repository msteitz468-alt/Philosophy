# The Look (Le Regard) and the Other (Sartre)
> The Other is disclosed not by inferring "they too have a consciousness" but by my *being-seen*: under the Look I am turned from a free nothing into a fixed object — "Hell is other people."

## Summary

How can the other person appear as a *consciousness* rather than as one object among objects? To assert that "the other also *has* a consciousness" is to ascribe a property to an object, which destroys the very subjectivity one wants to affirm — the threat of **solipsism**. Sartre's solution is to reverse the direction. He begins from **shame**: in shame I am ashamed of *what I am* in the eyes of the Other; shame (and pride) necessarily imply that I am *something* for another. I cannot ground the Other's subjectivity from my side, but I can *derive* it from the fact that I am an object *in his eyes*.

His scene: I sit in a park, the world arranged around *my* consciousness. A man appears on a nearby bench, also looking at the park. Something vertiginous happens — the park now appears from a perspective that is not mine, a perspective I cannot reach; the universe reorganises around the Other's intentionality, and *I*, within that park, become an object among objects. **The Look (le regard)** does not take away my consciousness; it takes away my *freedom*. I become an en-soi, a thing. "By the look I become aware of my object-being." Sartre concludes not "the Other also has a consciousness" (a metaphysical assumption, a relapse into solipsism) but that **"being-seen-by-the-Other is the truth of seeing-the-Other."** My self-consciousness makes a detour through the Other's look.

This makes the relation to the Other primarily **conflict**. The other is a subject, which inexorably means I am an object — or vice versa, but never both at once. "Hell is other people" (*Huis clos*). It is not an ethical claim but a structural one. **Love** is no exception: I love the Other primarily because they recognise me, and to love is to want to be *loved* — to want to be the privileged object of the Other's freedom. But the lover demands the impossible: to possess the Other *as freedom*. Love is therefore impossible *without* the conflict that seems to oppose it.

**The body** is what makes the Look possible: I can be seen only because I have a body, whose being is of the order of the en-soi. I am pour-soi *or* en-soi, never both: when I catch the Other's look I do not see his eyes as things, and vice versa. The body has three ontological dimensions — the body I *am* (as consciousness/transcendence), the body as en-soi *for the Other*, and the decisive third: my being mediated by the Other's look. Hence no body is "ugly," "beautiful," or "attractive" except in the Other's look; "self-consciousness" is always consciousness that views itself *via* the Other.

**Sexuality** radicalises this. The pour-soi, lacking being, wants to *be* a body, and to be desired by the Other; desire is to want the Other's body *and* to be a body for the Other. Against Freud's "libido," sexual desire for Sartre is not something hidden behind consciousness — it *is* consciousness, an **incarnation theory**: desire is a freedom that desires the freedom of the Other, situated in flesh ("one does not desire because of an erection; one gets an erection because one desires"). The sadist wants to subject the Other's *freedom* (not a mere body-object, which would kill the pleasure); the masochist wants to *experience* being an object — both confirming that consciousness is pour-soi *or* en-soi, never both. Sexuality is thus "the drama of consciousness and objectification."

## Key Claims / Positions
- The Other is disclosed through *being-seen*, not by inference; this overcomes solipsism.
- The Look turns the pour-soi into an object, stealing not my consciousness but my freedom.
- Shame/pride prove I am something for another; "being-seen is the truth of seeing."
- The relation to the Other is structurally *conflict*; "Hell is other people."
- Love wants to possess the Other *as freedom* — impossible, so love requires conflict.
- The body is en-soi for the Other; aesthetic/erotic meaning exists only in the Look.
- Sexuality is incarnation: desire is a freedom desiring the Other's freedom, not a libido.

### How Sartre Differs From Prior Thinkers in the Wiki
- **vs the traditional "problem of other minds"**: refuses to *infer* other minds; recasts the question as the phenomenology of being-an-object-for-another.
- **vs Freud**: rejects the libido/unconscious account of sexuality for an intentional, incarnational one — see [[Contradictions/Sartre vs Freud - Lucidity vs the Unconscious]].
- **vs [[Thinkers/Hegel]]**: develops the master/slave struggle for recognition into an ontological, never-resolved conflict.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- The Other both *threatens* my freedom (objectifying me) and is *required* for me to "be" anything at all — a permanent ambivalence Sartre never dissolves; it becomes, in the *Critique*, the problem of the [[Concepts/Totalization, Series and Group (Sartre)|group]].
