# Collective Action and Free Riding (Olson)
> Large groups composed of rational individuals will not act in their common or group interest unless there is coercion or selective incentives.

## Summary

The theory of collective action, formulated by Mancur Olson in *The Logic of Collective Action* (1965) and summarized in *The Rise and Decline of Nations* (1982), critiques the traditional assumption that groups with a shared interest will naturally organize to achieve that interest. Olson shows that if we assume individuals are rational and self-interested, large-group organization is highly improbable.

### The Public Goods Problem

The outputs of collective action — such as a labor union's higher wages, a lobby's tax loophole, or clean air — are **collective goods** (public goods). By definition, once provided, they cannot be withheld from any member of the group, regardless of whether that member contributed to the cost of provision. Because the benefit is non-excludable, each individual has a rational incentive to **free-ride**: to enjoy the benefit while letting others bear the cost. If all members of a group reason this way, the collective good will not be provided.

### The Role of Group Size

Olson identifies group size as the critical determinant of whether collective action can occur:

1. **Large/Latent Groups**: In a large group, the individual's share of the gains from collective action is vanishingly small. At the same time, the organizational and bargaining costs of coordinating thousands or millions of individuals are extremely high. Thus, the cost of action to any individual exceeds their personal share of the benefit. Large groups cannot organize voluntarily.
2. **Small/Privileged Groups**: In a small group (such as an oligopoly of a few firms), the individual benefit share is large. At least one member may find that their personal share of the collective good exceeds the entire cost of providing it. In this case, the good can be provided unilaterally or through simple, low-cost consensual bargaining. Small groups possess a disproportionate organizational advantage over large groups.

### Selective Incentives

To overcome the free-rider problem, large collective-action organizations must employ **selective incentives**. These are extra inducements applied selectively to individuals depending on whether they contribute to the collective good:

* **Negative Selective Incentives**: Coercion, tax penalties, or social sanctions (e.g., union shops where membership is mandatory, or social ostracism of non-contributors).
* **Positive Selective Incentives**: Private goods or services restricted exclusively to contributors (e.g., low-cost insurance, technical journals, or social status offered by professional associations to their members).

### Rational Ignorance

A major political implication of collective-action logic is the concept of **rational ignorance**. Because a typical citizen's vote has a vanishingly small probability of changing the outcome of a national election, the personal return on investing time and resources to become well-informed about public affairs is virtually zero. The citizen remains rationally ignorant. 

Conversely, small, organized interest groups (like a specific industry cartel) stand to gain concentrated, massive benefits from targeted policies. They invest heavily in information and lobbying, easily outmaneuvering the unorganized, rationally ignorant public.

## Key Claims / Positions

### How Different Thinkers Approach This

- **[[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]**: Marxist theory assumes that social classes naturally act in their collective interest (once they overcome "false consciousness"). Olson's logic shows that large classes face the most severe free-rider barriers. A worker may want the proletariat to succeed, but their rational individual choice is to avoid the personal risks of revolution while hoping others fight.
- **Pluralist Political Theory (e.g., David Truman)**: Traditional pluralism held that the political system represents a balanced vector of competing interest groups, assuming any shared interest will naturally generate an organization. Olson refutes this, showing the system is systematically biased in favor of small, concentrated interests (capital, select industries) over large, dispersed interests (consumers, taxpayers).
- **[[Thinkers/John Stuart Mill]]**: Mill recognized that certain public improvements (like a shorter workday) require legislation because voluntary agreements will fail if individuals can defect. Olson formalizes this intuition into a choice-theoretic model of public goods.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] The theory struggles to explain large-scale voluntary collective actions that lack obvious selective incentives, such as voter turnout, mass environmental movements, or volunteer charity. Critics argue Olson's purely materialist rational-actor model ignores moral, ideological, and expressive incentives.
- The social selective incentives that work in small, homogeneous groups (companionship, social pressure) are hard to scale or formalize in larger, diverse modern societies.
