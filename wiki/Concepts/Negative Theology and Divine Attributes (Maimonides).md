# Negative Theology and Divine Attributes (Maimonides)

> The doctrinal core of *Guide* Part I: God is an absolutely simple, incorporeal unity of whom **no positive essential attribute can be predicated**. We approach knowledge of Him only by *negations* and by attributes of His *action*. Every affirmative predicate either collapses into His essence (and so says nothing) or adds an accident to it (and so destroys His unity). The result is a rigorous *via negativa* secured by reading all scriptural God-language as homonymous and figurative.

## From Anthropomorphism to Incorporeality

Maimonides' first move is hermeneutic. Terms applied to God in Scripture — "image," "place," "sitting," "seeing," even "existence" — are **homonyms**: one word bearing wholly distinct senses, and the corporeal sense is never God's. "The adherence to the literal sense of the text of Holy Writ is the source of all this error." The governing rabbinic principle is "the Torah speaketh in the language of man" — anthropomorphic language accommodates the multitude, not a description of God. Corporeality and passivity are denied outright: "if God could be affected in any way whatever, another being beside Him would act on Him and cause change in Him."

This is enforced by the esoteric method of the whole *Guide*: truths "should be at one time apparent, and at another time concealed," scattered like the gold glimpsed through the silver filigree of Proverbs' "apples of gold in silver filigree." The book conceals as it reveals, written "to the one man" — the [[Sources/The Guide for the Perplexed - Maimonides|perplexed]] believer — not the multitude.

## The Argument Against Positive Attributes

God is "one simple substance, without any composition or plurality of elements." Any essential attribute would be either (a) identical to the essence — making the predicate empty — or (b) something superadded — an accident implying composition and plurality, hence "many eternal beings." Therefore:

> "God exists without possessing the attribute of existence. Similarly He lives, without possessing the attribute of life; knows, without possessing the attribute of knowledge; is omnipotent without possessing the attribute of omnipotence; is wise, without possessing the attribute of wisdom: all this reduces itself to one and the same entity."

Terms like Existence, Wisdom, Power, Life apply to God and to creatures "by way of perfect homonymity, admitting of no comparison whatever" — there is no *degree* of likeness between divine and creaturely wisdom; they share only a name.

## Two Legitimate Substitutes

What survives are:
1. **Attributes of action.** Scripture's many divine names denote God's varied *effects*, not qualities in Him — as fire by the single quality of heat can bleach, blacken, melt, and harden. The thirteen *middot* shown to Moses "were nothing but His actions."
2. **Negations.** "Even that knowledge of God which is accessible to man cannot be attained except by negations." Each demonstrated negation brings one nearer to God; "with every additional positive assertion you follow your imagination and recede from the true knowledge of God." The natural terminus is reverent silence: "Silence is praise to Thee" — heaping up epithets is like praising a king who owns gold for owning silver.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]:** Aquinas read Maimonides ("Rabbi Moses") closely and accepts much — divine simplicity, the rejection of accidents in God — but *softens* the pure negative theology into the **analogy of being**: "wise" said of God and creatures is neither univocal (Maimonides' worry) nor purely equivocal/homonymous (Maimonides' result) but *analogical*, so positive attributes can be affirmed of God's essence proportionally. This is the single most important medieval divergence on God-talk.
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Al-Ghazali]]:** both deny that God is composite, but Ghazali (against the *falasifa*) insists God has **real attributes** — knowledge, will, power, life — additional to the essence, precisely to keep God a living personal agent ([[Concepts/God's Knowledge of Particulars (Ghazali)]]). Maimonides' via negativa is closer to the philosophers' "simplicity" that Ghazali attacks as emptying God out. A pointed cross-tradition tension.
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] / Neoplatonism:** the negative approach to the One (the [[Concepts/Emanation (Proodos)|source beyond being and predication]]) is Maimonides' deep background; he philosophizes the biblical God toward the ineffable One.
- **vs. [[Sources/Ethics - Spinoza (Cambridge, Kisner ed. 2018)|Spinoza]]:** raised on Maimonides, Spinoza inverts him — instead of a God beyond all positive predication, *Deus sive Natura* is the one substance of which thought and extension are positively known attributes. Negative theology's God-beyond-the-world becomes immanent substance.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Does pure negation leave any content to worship? Aquinas thought not, hence analogy. Maimonides answers: negation plus attributes of action suffice, and the rest is silence.
- Is "perfect homonymity" of "existence" coherent — can we even assert God *exists* if the word shares only a sound with creaturely existence?
