# Systematic Economic Biases (Caplan)
> Lay voters hold four directional, non-random biases about economics that persistently push democratic policy toward protection, restriction, and pessimism.

## Summary

In *The Myth of the Rational Voter* (2007), Bryan Caplan identifies four families of systematic bias in lay beliefs about economics, documented empirically through the 1996 Survey of Americans and Economists on the Economy (SAEE). These biases are the empirical backbone of his argument that the [[Concepts/Miracle of Aggregation]] fails: voter errors are not random but directionally predictable, so they amplify rather than cancel in the aggregate.

The benchmark for "correct" belief is the consensus of PhD economists — not because economists are infallible, but because they have spent careers studying the questions at issue, and because similar benchmark-comparison methods are standard in cognitive psychology (Kahneman-Tversky). Caplan controls for self-interest and ideology, finding that at most 20% of the lay-expert gap can be attributed to economists' higher incomes or conservative bias; the remaining 80% is attributed to economic knowledge.

## The Four Biases

### 1. Antimarket Bias
The tendency to underestimate the economic benefits of market mechanisms. Lay voters focus on the *motives* of profit-seeking business (greed) while neglecting the *discipline* imposed by competition. They treat prices as set by corporate intent rather than supply and demand, view profits as a social subtraction rather than an efficiency signal, and distrust middlemen, traders, and financial intermediaries as parasites.

Classic expressions: attributing gas price increases to corporate greed rather than supply and demand; viewing price gouging laws as beneficial; hostility to "speculation."

Historical antecedents: Frédéric Bastiat documented antimarket bias in 19th-century France ("economic sophisms"). Ludwig von Mises attributed it to the cultural legacy of feudal suspicion of commerce.

### 2. Antiforeign Bias
The tendency to underestimate the economic benefits of interaction with foreigners, treating international exchange as zero-sum. Lay voters believe that importing goods destroys domestic wealth, that hiring foreign workers takes jobs from natives, and that foreign investment is a form of exploitation.

Classic expressions: support for tariffs, opposition to free trade agreements, hostility to immigration, skepticism of outsourcing.

Caplan notes this is among the oldest and most persistent economic errors — the mercantile fallacy that Adam Smith spent most of *The Wealth of Nations* refuting.

### 3. Make-Work Bias
The tendency to equate economic prosperity with the *quantity of labor employed* rather than the *output produced per unit of effort*. Lay voters see labor-saving technology, automation, and downsizing as harmful even when they increase real output and living standards, because they conflate destroying jobs with destroying wealth.

Classic expressions: opposition to automation; support for "job-saving" import restrictions; treating efficiency improvements as a social cost.

Bastiat called this "Sisyphism" — the belief that labor is valuable in itself and that making work easier is a loss. Economists from Smith onward have argued the opposite: the goal is production, not employment.

### 4. Pessimistic Bias
The systematic tendency to overestimate the severity of current economic problems and underestimate past, present, and future economic performance. Lay voters believe the economy is deteriorating even during periods of growth, that living standards are falling, and that economic conditions are worse than recorded data indicate.

Classic expressions: nostalgia for an imagined economic golden age; skepticism about GDP growth figures; overestimation of poverty, inequality, and unemployment trends.

Caplan attributes this partly to the media's negativity bias and partly to an evolved heuristic that treats economic danger signals as more attention-worthy than signs of stability.

## Empirical Evidence: The SAEE

The Survey of Americans and Economists on the Economy (SAEE), conducted by the Washington Post, Kaiser Foundation, and Harvard (1996), asked 1,510 randomly selected members of the public and 250 PhD economists the same 37 questions about economic conditions and policy simultaneously. It is the direct empirical test of whether lay-expert gaps exist and in which direction they run.

Findings:
- Large, statistically significant gaps on nearly every question.
- Lay respondents are systematically more pessimistic, more anti-market, more anti-foreign, and more make-work-oriented than economists.
- After constructing an "Enlightened Public" controlling for income, ideology, party, age, gender, race, and job security, the adjusted public ("what would the public believe if they had an economics PhD?") tracks economists' views much more closely than the raw public.
- Education in general (not just economic training) moves beliefs toward economists' positions on 19 of 37 questions.
- Economists are not consistently right-wing: they are more optimistic about downsizing than very conservative Republicans and more optimistic about immigration than very liberal Democrats.

## What These Biases Do

Each bias generates political demand for specific bad policies:
- **Antimarket bias** → price controls, antitrust overreach, anti-profiteering laws
- **Antiforeign bias** → tariffs, immigration restrictions, sanctions, buy-local mandates
- **Make-work bias** → opposition to automation, support for featherbedding regulations
- **Pessimistic bias** → stimulus demands during non-recessions, support for protectionist "emergency" measures

Together, they constitute the demand side of democratic policy failure. Politicians who supply protectionism, restriction, and interventionism are not betraying voters — they are faithfully executing irrational mandates.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Caplan's benchmark (economist consensus) is contested: critics (including some economists) argue that mainstream economic consensus is itself ideologically tilted toward free markets, making the lay-expert gap partly a *political* rather than *epistemic* gap. See [[Sources/The Myth of the Rational Voter (Caplan)]].
- > [!warning] The four biases overlap with but do not map perfectly onto standard cognitive psychology heuristics (availability heuristic, loss aversion, etc.). The relationship between evolved cognitive architecture and culturally transmitted economic errors is not fully specified.
