# Intelligence Explosion (Bostrom)

> The hypothesized rapid, recursive self-improvement of an AI system once it reaches a certain threshold of capability in AI research, software engineering, and related domains. Nick Bostrom (2014) analyzes the kinetics: whether the takeoff is fast (days/weeks/months) or slow (years/decades), depending on the recalcitrance (difficulty) of further intelligence gains versus the optimization power (computational and algorithmic resources) being applied. A fast intelligence explosion would give humanity little time to react or correct course.

## Summary

Building on I.J. Good's 1965 speculation ("an ultraintelligent machine could design even better machines; there would then unquestionably be an 'intelligence explosion'..."), Bostrom examines the conditions under which an intelligence explosion occurs and its speed.

Key variables:
- **Optimization power**: The resources (hardware, researcher effort, algorithmic improvements) devoted to making the AI smarter.
- **Recalcitrance**: How hard it is to achieve further gains in intelligence at a given level (software bottlenecks, hardware limits, fundamental theoretical barriers).
- Takeoff is fast when optimization power is high relative to recalcitrance (easy further gains once a seed exists).

The book discusses different paths (pure AI vs. whole brain emulation) having different recalcitrance profiles. Emulation might have a more "continuous" takeoff; de novo AI might jump discontinuously once key insights are reached.

A fast takeoff amplifies the control problem: there may be little time for testing, iteration, or international coordination once the process begins.

## Key Claims

- Intelligence explosion is not science fiction; it follows from the possibility of recursive self-improvement in the relevant domains.
- The speed of the explosion has major strategic implications (decisive first-mover advantage more likely in fast takeoff).
- We should not assume a slow, observable, controllable transition.

## Relation to Vault

- **Turing (1950)**: Turing's prediction that machines would reach and surpass human intelligence, combined with his learning-machine programme, is an early root of the intelligence explosion idea. Bostrom provides the quantitative and strategic analysis.
- **Wiener**: Warnings about learning machines that improve themselves in unintended directions scale to explosion scenarios.
- **Minsky/Dennett/Hofstadter**: Models of mind and learning inform what "self-improvement" would actually consist of (agent recruitment, new administrative layers, new drafts/loops, better isomorphisms). A society-of-mind architecture might change the recalcitrance curve.
