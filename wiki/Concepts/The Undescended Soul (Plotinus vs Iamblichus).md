# The Undescended Soul (Plotinus vs Iamblichus)

> Plotinus's most controversial innovation: a level of every human soul that never descends, remaining eternally in contemplation of Intelligence (Enn. IV.8.8) — rejected by Iamblichus, whose fully descended soul needs theurgy and divine grace to return.

## Summary

For [[Thinkers/Plotinus]], the soul contains all the divine Hypostases within her yet is normally unaware of them; "who we are" has a dynamic answer (we are the level our attention occupies) and a static one (the rational vs. the purified true self). Its highest phase is "unfallen": it never leaves the Intelligible world, which is why contemplation alone can complete the soul's return — the mystic need only wake up to what he already is. The descent itself is doubly characterized: a biological necessity (expression of the Universal Logos) and an irrational audacity (*tolma*), the soul falling by identifying with her own image in matter (III.9.3; IV.3.12). Related is the impassibility (*apatheia*) of the pure soul — emotion and error belong to the animated body and the lower soul's inattention — though Plotinus then struggles to say why moral discipline is needed at all (III.6.5).

[[Thinkers/Iamblichus]] rejected the doctrine outright: the soul descends wholly, is a distinct hypostasis below Intelligence, and therefore cannot save itself by thought; salvation requires the gods' freely given power channeled through ritual — see [[Concepts/Theurgy (Iamblichus)]]. [[Thinkers/Proclus]] mediated the anthropology with his two vehicles (*ochēmata*) of the soul, one immortal and one perishable.

Wallis lists the tension among the problems Plotinus bequeathed: if every soul contains the whole Intelligible world and divine souls eternally enjoy identity with Intelligence, is the distinction between Soul and Intelligence as hypostases abolished? Porphyry's telescoping said, in effect, yes; Iamblichus's stratification was the corrective.
