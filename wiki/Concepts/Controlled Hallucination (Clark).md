# Controlled Hallucination (Clark)
> All perception is a form of hallucination — brain-generated guessing — that happens to be *controlled* by being continuously checked against sensory prediction-error signals.

## Summary

"Controlled hallucination" is Andy Clark's (and Anil Seth's) vivid formulation of what perception *is* under [[Concepts/Predictive Processing (Clark)]]. The brain "is actively trying to hallucinate the world but checking that hallucination against the evidence coming in via the senses." Perception is "controlled" precisely because the inner guessing remains sensitive to sensory error signals; when that control lapses, the very same process yields ordinary hallucination.

Clark's guiding image: "the brain is constantly painting a picture, and the role of the sensory information is mostly to nudge the brushstrokes when they fail to match up with the incoming evidence." Perception "is more like painting than some kind of point-and-shoot photography — it is an act of creation that draws upon our own needs and history." All experience is therefore "part phantom": we can no more perceive prediction-free than "surf without a wave."

## Key Claims / Positions

- **The continuity thesis.** Hallucination, illusion, and veridical perception are not different in kind — they differ only in how well the brain's guessing is being corrected by evidence. Clark's opening example is his own *phantom vibration syndrome*: a habitual expectation of a buzzing phone, plus stress, generated a clear felt buzz "out of whole cloth."
- **Demonstrations of top-down construction.** The Dress (Pascal Wallisch's 13,000-subject study, "larks vs. owls" priors about lighting), the Hollow Mask and Ponzo illusions, Mooney images, sine-wave speech (Haskins Labs; Chris Darwin's demos), and the Maastricht "White Christmas" experiment all show priors and expectations building or reshaping conscious experience.
- **"No single way of getting things right."** Because perception is constructive, there is no raw, prior-free "correct" experience to compare against; the discipline that keeps perception truthful is "test then flexibly update," not passive reception.
- **Bias is intrinsic, not incidental.** The same prior-driven construction that makes perception fast and efficient also injects bias directly into the sensory realm — see [[Concepts/Precision-Weighting (Clark)]] on "wishful seeing," affective realism, and shooter bias.

## How This Relates to Prior Thinkers

- **[[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]**: controlled hallucination is a natural partner to the **Multiple Drafts** model — both deny a passive inner screen and a Cartesian Theater where a finished image is displayed. Clark supplies a generative mechanism (predictive construction) for Dennett's editorial architecture.
- **[[Thinkers/George Berkeley]]**: echoes "to be is to be perceived," but Clark's constructivism is empirically constrained — the hallucination is *controlled*, anchored to a mind-independent world via error correction, blocking the slide to idealism.
- **[[Concepts/Clear and Distinct Perception (Descartes)]]**: directly opposed. Where Descartes seeks an indubitable perceptual bedrock, Clark holds that no perception is a transparent window; all are constructed guesses.
- **[[Concepts/Simulation Hypothesis (Virk)]]**: the "rendered world" intuition, relocated inside the skull.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] The claim that veridical perception and hallucination differ only in degree of control sharpens, rather than resolves, the realism question raised against [[Concepts/Simulation Hypothesis (Virk)]] and Berkeleyan idealism: what secures contact with the world if all experience is brain-generated? Clark's answer is prediction-error correction — a naturalist reply that the idealist tradition would say begs the question.
