# Apeiron (Anaximander)

> The Boundless, Indefinite, or Infinite; the primary substance (*arche*) proposed by Anaximander to be the source of all things.

## Summary

The **Apeiron** (ἄπειρον) represents the first highly abstract concept in Western metaphysics. Unlike his teacher Thales, who identified a specific visible substance (water) as the primary principle, Anaximander argued that the ultimate source of reality must be indeterminate and infinite. It is "boundless" in two senses: spatially infinite/unlimited, and qualitatively indefinite (lacking the specific qualities of any one element).

---

## Key Claims and Arguments

**The Argument from Opposites**:
- Anaximander observed that the elements of the physical world are in a state of mutual hostility (opposites like hot/cold, wet/dry).
- If one of these elements were infinite (e.g., if the primary substance were water), it would overwhelm and obliterate its opposites (fire).
- Therefore, the source of these opposites must be a neutral, indeterminate substance that underlies them all, allowing them to remain in a dynamic balance (cosmic justice).

**Eternal Motion and Separation**:
- The Apeiron is in eternal motion. Through this motion, the opposites (the hot and the cold, the dry and the wet) "separate out" from it to form the worlds.
- The Apeiron is described as "eternal," "ageless," and "deathless," encompassing and steering all the infinite worlds that are generated and dissolved.

---

## Relevance to Later Philosophy

- **Precursor to Matter**: The Apeiron is the direct historical precursor to Aristotle's concept of *prime matter* (pure potentiality without form) and Locke's "something I know not what" underlying substance.
- **Introduction of the Infinite**: Established the infinite (*apeiron*) as a load-bearing metaphysical concept, which would later be critiqued by the Pythagoreans (who saw the infinite as bad and the limited as good) and Aristotle (who rejected actual infinities).
