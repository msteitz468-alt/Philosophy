# Bad Faith (Mauvaise Foi) (Sartre)
> Lying to oneself "against better knowledge": the lucid flight from one's own freedom, in which the pour-soi pretends to be a fixed thing (en-soi) in order to escape the burden of being responsible for itself.

## Summary

To be free is to *have to be* rather than to *be* (baker, guilty, husband). But we constantly disown this. When I claim "I had no choice" — "my back was against the wall," "if I hadn't, someone else would have," "people are just like that," "that's just how I am" — I try to regard myself as an *en-soi*, a thing with fixed properties, and thereby deny my freedom. Sartre calls this **mauvaise foi**. It is a *double negation*: the denial of my freedom, which is itself a non-being.

Welten insists on translating *mauvaise foi* not as "bad faith"/"kwade trouw" but as acting **"against better knowledge,"** because the phenomenon presupposes a wholly *lucid* consciousness. There is, for Sartre, no Freudian *unconscious* in play: I can only lie to myself about what I already clearly know (as a lie presupposes knowing the truth I conceal). Bad faith is *camouflage* — hiding behind essences such as identities or character traits — while a clear knowing is concealed beneath it.

Sartre's emblem is the **café waiter** who plays at being a waiter — a little too upright, too eager — because one can never *be* a waiter as a stone is a stone; one can only *play the role*. But, crucially, this is *not* a moral verdict: Sartre does not mean the waiter ought to behave "authentically." His whole work is shot through with the **impossibility of being authentic**: there is no moment at which we are truly "ourselves." There are only endless moments of *in*authenticity. "Authenticity" can figure only as a *project* (a striving) against this negative background, never as an achieved state — which is why claims like "I'm just being myself" or "do what your heart tells you" are, for Sartre, themselves bad faith. Even "good faith" is a double bad faith, since to claim to be in good faith is already to make the pour-soi into an en-soi with the property of sincerity.

The flight has clear payoffs: if everything is due to a "factual" situation that is "not mine," I need not hold myself responsible. But "life is not something I undergo: it is mine." The lucidity of consciousness demands that I live my life as my choice — which is also my choice to create *shadows* in that unbearable clarity. For Sartre the great social ally of bad faith is **bourgeois morality**, which "knows what is normal," takes everything seriously except its own freedom, and hides behind fixed norms and traditions.

## Key Claims / Positions
- Bad faith = lying to oneself, treating the free pour-soi as a fixed en-soi.
- It is a *double negation* (denying a freedom that is itself a non-being).
- It presupposes full *lucidity* — there is no unconscious to hide in.
- The café waiter "plays" the role; this is *not* a moral failing but a structural condition.
- Authenticity is impossible as a state; only inauthenticity is ever fully present.
- "Good faith," "being yourself," "follow your heart" are themselves bad faith.
- Bourgeois morality is bad faith's chief social form.
