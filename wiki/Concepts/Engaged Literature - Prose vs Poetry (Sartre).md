# Engaged Literature: Prose vs Poetry (Sartre)
> Prose is an *act* — words used to change the world — and only such committed prose can be engaged; poetry, like painting and music, is immanent and self-referential, and cannot do the work of engagement.

## Summary

Formed under the Occupation, Sartre's idea of **engagement** asks whether literature can not merely describe and interpret a situation but *live it through from freedom and change it*. *Qu'est-ce que la littérature?* (*What is Literature?*, 1948) answers by analysing literature as a **handeling/act** — a human intention that changes something in the world (how we understand things). Engaged literature is thus *instrumental*: words are used to achieve something, as in a **speech act** ("Shut the door"). Sartre reserves the term **prose** for such engaged language and opposes it to **poetry**.

The distinction is not one of genre or style but of how language *works*. Prose has the structure of the **pour-soi** — pure transcendence, reaching beyond itself toward the world, of the order of imagination (always imagination *of* something). Poetry has the immanence of the **en-soi**: the poet "refuses to use language," treating words as *things* rather than as transparent signs; poetry belongs in the same domain as painting and music, where the "meaning" of a melody is the melody itself. Tintoretto's yellow sky over Golgotha does not *signify* anguish the way a word signifies; one does not "read" a painting. Hence action — the praxis of ethics and politics — requires imagination/transcendence, because it must sketch a society other than the factual one.

This yields some sharp consequences. Engaged art is **not** the same as taking a political stand or depicting horrors: a "political position" always shows up objectively, as an en-soi, and Sartre even asks whether Picasso's *Guernica* "won a single soul for the Spanish cause." He is cool toward literary realism (Balzac, Zola, Dickens): it describes social ills but compels no real change. Genet's work is formally prose yet *works* poetically — he refuses to communicate. Sartre does not banish poetry, painting, or music (he is not [[Thinkers/Plato]] expelling the poets); they simply belong to the *aesthetic* domain, while literature and theatre belong to the *ethical/political* domain that must drive genuine social change.

In **"Materialism and Revolution"** Sartre extends this: materialism is a hidden, self-refuting metaphysics that reduces the world to a factual en-soi and denies the freedom from which materialist thinking is itself possible; **revolution** is therefore never a politically *objective* event but a freely engaged realisation. There are, for Sartre, **no foundational/natural rights**: "if the angel's voice tells me what to do, it is still I who decide it is the angel's voice." The single individual's fully chosen act binds all humanity — which is why a vegetarian who says "everyone must decide for themselves" acts in bad faith. Finally, in **"Plaidoyer pour les intellectuels"** (1965), the **intellectual** is defined as "someone who meddles in what is not their business," who puts their learning at risk, questions the ruling class's self-evident "truths," and is necessarily hated for it — "the human is the future of the human."

## Key Claims / Positions
- Prose is an act/speech-act (pour-soi, transcendence); poetry is immanent (en-soi), like music/painting.
- Only committed prose can be *engaged*; the distinction is about how language works, not genre.
- Engaged art ≠ political statement or depiction of horrors; realism describes but does not change.
- Materialism is a covert metaphysics that denies the freedom enabling it.
- Revolution is subjective/engaged, not objective; there are no foundational or natural rights.
- The individual's chosen act binds all humanity; the *intellectual* contests ruling "truths."

### How Sartre Differs From Prior Thinkers in the Wiki
- **vs [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]**: endorses Marx's eleventh Feuerbach thesis ("change the world") but rejects *materialist*/"scientific" Marxism; engagement is grounded in freedom, not material law.
- **vs [[Thinkers/Plato]]**: refuses to expel the poets — poetry is valid but belongs to a different (aesthetic) domain.
- **vs [[Thinkers/Ludwig Wittgenstein]]**: independently arrives at language-as-action ("speech act") but harnesses it to political engagement rather than therapeutic description; cf. [[Concepts/Meaning as Use (Wittgenstein)]].
