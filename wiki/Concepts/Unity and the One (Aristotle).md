# Unity and the One (Aristotle)

> What is it for something to be *one*? Aristotle's answer (Book X / Iota): "one" is said in many ways but means fundamentally **the first measure of a kind**, and — crucially against the Platonists and Pythagoreans — neither unity nor being is itself a *substance*. The One is a predicate true of everything, not a thing standing apart.

## Summary

In Book X Aristotle inventories the senses of **"one" (*hen*)**: the *continuous* (especially what is continuous by nature, with one indivisible motion), the *whole with a form* (a complete shape, "not unified by glue or nails"), the *individual indivisible in number*, and the *universal indivisible in account*. All trace back to **indivisibility of movement or of definition**. The primary sense, though, is the **one as measure**: "to be one is to be the first measure of a kind." Number is known by the unit; length by a unit length, weight by a unit weight. The measure must be homogeneous with what it measures and, ideally, indivisible to perception. (Here Aristotle rejects Protagoras: knowledge and perception are *measured by* things, not the measure of them — cf. [[Concepts/Principle of Non-Contradiction (Aristotle)]].)

The decisive metaphysical claim: **neither "one" nor "being" is the substance of things.** Since "no universal can be a substance" ([[Concepts/Substance (Ousia) (Aristotle)]]) and "one" has as many senses as the categories (just as "being" does, [[Concepts/Being qua Being (Aristotle)]]), unity is not a genus and not a separate entity over the many. Yet "in a sense unity means the same as being," for both are predicated of everything and are *convertible*: "to be one is just to be a particular thing."

From the One flow **identity, likeness, equality**; from plurality, **otherness, unlikeness, inequality**. Aristotle distinguishes mere **otherness** (anything not the same) from **difference**, which requires a shared genus within which things diverge. The greatest, complete difference is **contrariety** (*enantiōsis*): "contrariety is complete difference," each genus has one fundamental contrariety, a thing has exactly **one** contrary, and the primary contrariety is between a positive state and its **privation** (*sterēsis*). The "one" and the "many" are opposed not as contraries but as *measure to measured* (relative opposites) — which is why not everything one is a *number* (indivisibles are one but not numbers). A closing argument: the **perishable and the imperishable differ in kind** (perishability belongs of necessity, not by accident), which by itself blocks the Platonic move of treating Forms and their sensible namesakes as one in form.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **"One" said in many ways**, unified by *indivisibility*; primary sense = **measure** of a kind.
- **Unity and being are not substances or genera**, but universal, convertible predicates.
- **Identity/likeness/equality** belong to the one; **otherness/unlikeness/inequality** to plurality.
- **Contrariety = complete difference**; one contrary per thing; rooted in **state vs. privation**.
- The **one and the many** are *relative* opposites (measure/measured), not contraries.
- **Perishable ≠ imperishable in kind** — a weapon against Platonic Forms.

## How This Sits Among Thinkers

- **Versus [[Thinkers/Parmenides]] and the Eleatics**: Parmenidean Being-is-One ([[Concepts/Parmenidean Being and the One (Parmenides)]]) treated unity as the single substance, forcing monism. Aristotle's "one is not a substance but a measure/predicate" dissolves the Eleatic collapse of plurality.
- **Versus the [[Thinkers/Pythagoras|Pythagoreans]] and [[Thinkers/Plato]]**: both make the One a first principle/substance and reduce things to number ([[Concepts/Number as the Essence of Reality (Pythagoras)]]); Books XIII–XIV ([[Concepts/Critique of Platonic Forms (Aristotle)]]) press exactly this point — the One cannot be both an element and the Good and the substance of all.
- **Downstream — Neoplatonism**: [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] does the opposite, exalting **[[Concepts/The One (To Hen)]]** above being as the transcendent source ([[Concepts/Emanation (Proodos)]]). Aristotle's deflationary One and Plotinus's hyper-transcendent One are the two great ancient options the later tradition chooses between.
- **Identity/difference** analysis feeds the perennial logic of identity (cf. Leibniz's identity of indiscernibles, [[Concepts/Monad (Leibniz)]]).
