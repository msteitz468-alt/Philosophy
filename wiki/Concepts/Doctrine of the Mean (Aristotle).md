# Doctrine of the Mean (Aristotle)

> Moral virtue is a stable, choice-governing disposition (*hexis*) lying in a mean between excess and deficiency of passion and action — not a mathematical midpoint but "the mean relative to us," determined by the rational principle (*orthos logos*) by which the practically wise person would determine it. Acquired by habituation ("we become just by doing just acts"), not by nature or teaching alone.

## Summary

Book I establishes that all inquiry and action aim at some good, that goods are hierarchically ordered under a supreme, self-sufficient final good (*eudaimonia*), and — via the function (*ergon*) argument — that this good is "activity of soul in accordance with virtue" over a complete life, requiring but not reducible to external goods (fortune, friends, health). Book II turns to how such virtue is acquired: not by nature (we are only "naturally adapted to receive" virtues, then "made perfect by habit," exactly as we learn crafts by practicing them) and not by teaching alone, but by repeated right action from youth. Virtue of character is therefore neither a passion (which arises independent of choice) nor a mere capacity (possessed by nature) but a *hexis* — a durable disposition of choice. Its defining structure is the mean: too much and too little destroy excellence, while the intermediate preserves it, and this intermediate is not an "equidistant" objective midpoint (like six between two and ten) but "relative to us," varying by person and circumstance, correctly gauged only by *phronesis* (practical wisdom) exercising judgment case by case — "to the right person, to the right extent, at the right time, with the right motive, and in the right way, that is not for every one, nor is it easy." Because virtue is thus a narrow target and vice a wide one ("men are good in but one way, but bad in many"), moral excellence is rare and praiseworthy. Some acts (adultery, theft, murder, envy) admit no mean at all — they are simply wrong in every instance, by name.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Eudaimonia as activity, not state**: the function argument identifies the human good with the exercise, not mere possession, of virtue — "activity of soul in accordance with virtue," and if there are several virtues, "in accordance with the best and most complete."
- **External goods are necessary but not constitutive**: happiness requires favorable circumstances (birth, friends, health, wealth) as instruments/conditions, but its substance is virtuous activity, which can survive (though be diminished by) great misfortune.
- **Virtue is acquired by habituation, not nature or teaching**: "the things we have to learn before we can do them, we learn by doing them... we become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts, brave by doing brave acts."
- **Virtue is a *hexis* (state of character concerned with choice)**, distinct from passions (which arise without choice) and mere faculties (natural capacities) — the durable middle term between raw disposition and deliberate action.
- **The mean is relative, not absolute**: two senses of "intermediate" are distinguished — the objectively equidistant intermediate (same for everyone) vs. the intermediate "relative to us" (varies by individual, circumstance, and is fixed only by the judgment a practically wise person would make).
- **Virtue is a narrow target, vice a wide one**: "men are good in but one way, but bad in many" — hence moral achievement is rare and praiseworthy, echoing the Pythagorean association of the good with the limited/determinate and evil with the unlimited/indeterminate.
- **Some acts admit no mean**: certain names (adultery, theft, murder, spite, envy, shamelessness) already imply badness — there is no right amount, time, or manner of these; they are wrong by definition.
- **Pleasure and pain as diagnostic and formative**: habituation to feel delight/pain correctly toward the right objects is both evidence of virtue and the mechanism by which it is built — "the whole concern of both virtue and political science is with pleasures and pains."
- **Practical wisdom (*phronēsis*) is required to locate the mean in any actual case** — the mean cannot be specified by a rule in advance, only judged by the person of practical wisdom attending to the concrete particulars.

## How Aristotle Uses This

Sets the structural template that governs the entire treatise: every particular virtue examined in Books III–IV (courage, temperance, liberality, magnificence, magnanimity, good temper, friendliness, truthfulness, wit) is analyzed as a mean between a named excess and a named deficiency (see [[Concepts/Moral Virtues as Means (Aristotle)]]), and justice (Book V) is explicitly reframed as a mean of a different, relational kind (see [[Concepts/Justice - Universal, Particular, and Equity (Aristotle)]]). The claim that *phronēsis* alone can locate the mean anticipates and is fully developed in Book VI's account of practical wisdom (see [[Concepts/Practical Wisdom - Phronesis (Aristotle)]]).
