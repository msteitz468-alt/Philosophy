# Stoic Freedom (Eleutheria) (Epictetus)

> Freedom is not a legal or political status but a condition of the will — to desire only what is in one's power, so that nothing can compel, hinder, or frustrate you. From an ex-slave, this is the *Discourses'* culminating theme (IV.1, "Of Freedom").

## Summary

Discourses **IV.1**, the longest single discourse, redefines freedom (*eleutheria*) away from Roman legal status toward an inner condition:

> "The man who is unrestrained, who has all things in his power as he wills, is free; but he who may be restrained, or compelled, or hindered, or thrown into any condition against his will, is a slave." (IV.1)

By this test the emperor may be a slave (to fear, flattery, appetite) and the manumitted slave still unfree if he now craves office. Real freedom is achieved only by confining desire and aversion to the [[Concepts/Prohairesis (Moral Purpose)|prohairesis]] — the one thing no one can take. Everything external must be held loosely, "surrendered to the divine administration," so that no tyrant has leverage:

> "No one has authority over me. God hath made me free; I know his commands; after this no one can enslave me." (IV.7)

The exemplars are **Socrates** (who kept his post and valued the just soul over "this pitiful body," IV.1) and **Diogenes** (unmasterable because he wanted nothing an enemy could withhold — "you can no more enslave the Athenians than you can fish," IV.1). Freedom coincides with piety: the free man is the one who *wills what happens*, aligning his choice with Zeus's (the Cleanthes hymn closes IV.1). It is bought "at a great price" and kept only by unremitting practice (IV.12, "Of Taking Pains").

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Freedom is internal, slavery is internal**: neither tracks social rank; slavery is subjection of the *will* to externals (IV.1).
- **The mechanism**: want only what is up to you → nothing can be taken from you against your will → you are unconquerable.
- **Detachment must be total**: even the desire for tranquillity, leisure, or to be well-thought-of is a chain (IV.4, IV.6). "No man is hurt but by himself" / by his own principles (IV.6).
- **Freedom = willing what happens**: the paradoxical Stoic identity of the deepest freedom with glad consent to necessity — the seed of [[Concepts/Amor Fati (Love of Fate)]].
- **Costly and perpetual**: purchased by relinquishing externals and preserved only by continual vigilance (IV.12).

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Epictetus** gives the doctrine unique authority as a former slave; freedom is the *telos* toward which the three fields of training aim.
- **[[Thinkers/Kant]]**: the closest modern parallel and a sharp contrast. Both make freedom the property of a rational will independent of external causes; but Kantian freedom is *autonomy* — the will legislating the moral law to itself — whereas Stoic freedom is *accord with cosmic nature/Zeus*, closer to what Kant would call heteronomy. See [[Concepts/Freedom as the Key to Autonomy (Kant)]], [[Concepts/Autonomy and Heteronomy of the Will (Kant)]].
- **[[Thinkers/Nietzsche]]**: rejects "willing what happens" as life-denying resignation dressed as strength; his freedom is self-overcoming and creation of values, not consent to fate — yet [[Concepts/Amor Fati (Love of Fate)]] ironically re-appropriates the Stoic formula. 
- **[[Thinkers/Hegel]]**: the [[Concepts/Master-Slave Dialectic (Hegel)]] famously names Stoicism as the freedom of self-consciousness that withdraws into thought while indifferent to its actual bondage — a critique of exactly Epictetus's move.
- **[[Concepts/Compatibilism - Liberty and Necessity (Hume)]]**: Epictetus is an ancient compatibilist — free will genuine, cosmos determined.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] Freedom of the will within a providentially determined cosmos — see [[Contradictions/Stoic Determinism vs Aristotelian Contingency]].
- Hegel's charge: is "inner freedom" while remaining in chains a genuine liberation or a consoling withdrawal that leaves the world's domination intact?
