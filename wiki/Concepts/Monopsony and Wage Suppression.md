# Monopsony and Wage Suppression
> Monopsony — monopoly on the buying side — is Teachout's solution to the "wage mystery": why wages stagnated despite rising productivity, falling unemployment, and increased education. When few employers dominate a labor market, they can suppress wages without competing, just as monopoly sellers can raise prices without competing.

## Summary

From 1940–1980, U.S. productivity rose 98% and wages rose 95%. Since 1980, productivity rose 75% but wages rose only 5%. This divergence — the "wage mystery" or "wage puzzle" — baffled mainstream economists who predicted that low unemployment would force wages up. The orthodox explanation (technology, globalization) fails because prices rose while wages fell.

Teachout follows a 2018 wave of economic literature identifying the cause: **monopsony** — concentrated employer power in local labor markets. When a few companies dominate regional employment (often 2–4 employers across sectors), they need not compete for workers. They can informally coordinate (wink-and-nod collusion) to keep wages suppressed, since no firm benefits from offering higher wages that competitors will simply match without the workers' wage advantage persisting.

## The Mechanism

- In competitive labor markets, employers compete for workers, driving wages toward the marginal product of labor.
- In monopsonistic markets, the dominant employer is a "wage setter" not a "wage taker": it can suppress wages below competitive levels without losing workers to competitors (because competitors pay equally low wages or don't exist).
- Economist Simcha Barkai estimated that excess market power costs workers $14,000/year per worker in lost compensation.
- The 2018 study (Steinbaum et al.) found that in most U.S. labor markets, workers have few realistic job options within their industry due to consolidation — and that this local concentration significantly suppresses posted wages.

## The Stock Buyback Connection

Mainstream economists assumed that when the "labor share" of income fell, it was offset by increased capital investment. Barkai's research disproved this: both labor and capital investors were losing. The surplus was going into stock buybacks (illegal before 1982, now the largest use of corporate revenue), inflating share prices — not into productive investment. The wage mystery thus connects monopsony with financialization: concentrated employers extract from workers, then redistribute to shareholders rather than invest productively.

## "Before-the-Fact Redistribution of Power"

Teachout's political philosophy argument: progressive redistribution strategies (minimum wage, wealth taxes) operate *after* monopoly power has already extracted surplus from workers. They are necessary but insufficient. The more fundamental reform is "before-the-fact redistribution of power" — breaking up employer monopolies so that workers bargain in competitive markets from a position of genuine choice. Anti-monopoly is the structural prerequisite for redistribution to work.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Teachout is partly endorsing neoclassical competitive market logic (more competition → higher wages → workers get marginal product), while rejecting it for its failure to account for power. The tension: can a structural anti-monopoly argument be made without accepting that competitive markets would produce fair wages? [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]] would argue even competitive capitalism exploits workers.
- > [!warning] Connects to [[Concepts/Chickenization of Labor]]: chickenization is the lived, structural version of monopsony — the specific mechanisms (isolation, surveillance, arbitrage of information) by which employer power is exercised.
