# Rule of Law vs Royal Art of Statesmanship (Plato Statesman)

> The Platonic political doctrine that distinguishes between the ideal rule of a wise, scientific ruler (the Royal Art) who stands above the law, and the realistic "second-best" rule of written law (the Rule of Law) necessary when no such ruler is available.

## Summary

In the *Statesman*, Plato refines his political theory, moving away from the utopianism of the *Republic* toward a more realistic and practical model. The dialogue argues that while the ideal form of government is the unhindered rule of a wise individual possessing the **Royal Art** (statesmanship as a science), the rarity of such individuals makes the **Rule of Law** an absolute, non-negotiable necessity for actual human cities.

---

## The Core Distinction

### 1. The Royal Art (*Basilike Techne*)
- **Definition**: True statesmanship is a science or art of governance, not a matter of heredity, wealth, or democratic consent.
- **Superiority to Law**: The wise statesman should not be bound by written laws:
  - Written laws are inherently rigid: *"Law can never issue an injunction which is at once simple and applicable to all, because human affairs are too complex and fluid."*
  - Just as a doctor or sea-captain must adjust their instructions to save the patient or ship, the wise ruler must be free to adjust his decrees to the changing circumstances of the city.
  - The statesman acts as a **weaver**, weaving together the courageous (warp) and moderate (weft) temperaments of the citizens through education and laws.

### 2. The Rule of Law (The "Second-Best" Path)
- **Definition**: The strict, unyielding adherence to written laws and ancestral customs.
- **The Rarity of Wisdom**: Because a perfectly wise statesman is rarely found, actual human rulers are prone to ignorance, passion, and corruption. If given absolute power above the law, they will inevitably become tyrants.
- **The Safeguard**: Therefore, actual cities must enforce the **Rule of Law** as their "second-best voyage" (*deuteros plous*).
  - Laws represent the accumulated, tested wisdom of past generations.
  - No citizen or ruler is permitted to violate the written law under any circumstances, because doing so without the royal art leads directly to anarchy and tyranny.

## Classification of Regimes

The dialogue classifies the six actual regimes based on whether they adhere to the **Rule of Law**:

```
                       [ Actual Regimes ]
                               |
         +---------------------+---------------------+
         | (Law-Abiding)                             | (Lawless)
         |                                           |
   1. Monarchy (Best)                          4. Tyranny (Worst)
   2. Aristocracy                              5. Oligarchy
   3. Constitutional Democracy (Worst)         6. Extreme Democracy (Best)
```

- **Law-Abiding Regimes**: Monarchy is best (since a single ruler can act most decisively for good), followed by Aristocracy, with Democracy being the worst (since power is diluted).
- **Lawless Regimes**: Extreme Democracy is the best (since its dilution of power makes it least capable of doing great harm), followed by Oligarchy, with Tyranny being the absolute worst.

---

## Relevance to Later Philosophy

- **Foundations of Constitutionalism**: Plato's defense of the Rule of Law as a necessary safeguard directly influenced Aristotle's *Politics* (which argued for the rule of laws over the rule of men) and the development of modern constitutionalism and the separation of powers.
- **Transition to the Laws**: Prefigures the political philosophy of the *Laws* ([[Sources/Classical Philosophy - Stoics, Additional Plato Dialogues, and Notes 2026]]), where Plato abandons the philosopher-king model completely to outline a highly detailed, law-abiding second-best state (Magnesia).
