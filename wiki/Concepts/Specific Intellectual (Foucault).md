# Specific Intellectual (Foucault)
> Against the "universal intellectual" who speaks in the name of universal humanity (Sartre's model), Foucault proposes the "specific intellectual" who speaks from concrete, expert knowledge of a particular institution — from inside the power-knowledge nexus they are contesting.

## Summary

The concept emerged directly from Foucault's GIP (Groupe d'Information sur les Prisons) activism (1971–) and was developed in conversation with Deleuze in the 1972 interview "Intellectuals and Power." It represents Foucault's political self-understanding and his critique of the Sartrean model of intellectual engagement.

The **universal intellectual** (exemplified by Sartre, Zola, and the French tradition of the *intellectuel*) claims to speak in the name of abstract universal values — justice, truth, humanity. For Foucault, this model is both politically ineffective and epistemically dishonest: it claims a position outside power that is unavailable, and it silences the voices of those actually experiencing oppression by substituting the intellectual's voice.

The **specific intellectual** speaks from what they actually know — their concrete, expert, institutional position. Dr. Edith Rose, the prison psychiatrist at Toul who publicly exposed systematic abuse, was Foucault's exemplar: she spoke as a professional with insider knowledge, not as a universal moral spokesperson. The physicist (rather than the literary intellectual) becomes the model citizen — one whose expertise is specific to a technical domain and whose political intervention is grounded in that domain.

The GIP itself embodied this principle: rather than speaking *for* prisoners, its project was to enable prisoners to speak for themselves, providing the infrastructure (questionnaires, publications, connections) without substituting its voice.

## Key Claims

- Intellectuals do not represent the oppressed — they merely reproduce the power relations of representation.
- Effective political intervention works through specific knowledge within specific institutions — not through general consciousness-raising.
- "Speaking on behalf of others" is shameful, not heroic — it displaces the subject of experience with the universal subject of moral discourse.
- The specific intellectual's power-knowledge *is* a form of power and must be used strategically, not disavowed.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Foucault was himself the unambiguous public face and de facto leader of the GIP, effectively speaking *for* it while insisting on collective and prisoner-led discourse — a personal enactment of the contradiction his concept identifies.
- The "specific intellectual" risks collapsing into mere technocracy: experts speaking from within institutions may serve those institutions' normalizing functions rather than contesting them.

> [!warning] Compare [[Thinkers/Jean-Paul Sartre]]'s universal intellectual model — the two represent rival conceptions of what philosophical-political engagement looks like.
