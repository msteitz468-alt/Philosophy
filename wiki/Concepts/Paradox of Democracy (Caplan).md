# Paradox of Democracy (Caplan)
> If democratic rule aligns rulers with the ruled, why do democracies persistently adopt policies harmful to most of their citizens?

## Summary

Caplan opens *The Myth of the Rational Voter* (2007) with the Paradox of Democracy: the expectation that democratic government, by giving "power to the people," should prevent socially harmful policies — but the empirical record shows democracies routinely adopt and maintain such policies. Protectionism is the paradigm case: economists across the political spectrum have condemned it for centuries, yet almost every democracy restricts imports.

## Three Standard Responses (and Caplan's Assessment)

**1. Agency failure (Special interests hijack politicians)**
Elected officials shirk voter preferences and serve donors, lobbies, or their own agendas. This is the Classical Public Choice answer (Buchanan, Tullock, Olson). Caplan credits it partial validity but argues it cannot explain the full pattern: the empirical literature shows voters vote *sociotropically* (for what they believe benefits the nation), and politicians who openly contradict voter beliefs typically lose. Special interests can only operate where voter indifference leaves slack.

**2. Voter ignorance**
Voters don't know enough to punish bad policies. The rational ignorance argument (Downs). Caplan's objection: ignorance alone cannot produce *systematic* policy distortion if the [[Concepts/Miracle of Aggregation]] holds. Ignorant but unbiased voters cancel out; only biased voters systematically distort outcomes.

**3. Democracy works fine (Wittman)**
Bad policies are a myth; what looks like failure is actually efficient compromise. Donald Wittman's position. Caplan's reply: the empirical evidence on systematic economic beliefs refutes this. Policies that economists uniformly consider harmful (e.g., agricultural price supports, trade restrictions) persist across decades and political parties.

**Caplan's resolution:** Voters are not ignorant in the passive sense — they actively hold systematically biased beliefs about how the economy works, believe those policies are beneficial, and demand them. Politicians supply the bad policies not because they betray voters but because they faithfully represent irrational voter beliefs. "Democracy fails because it does what voters want." See [[Concepts/Rational Irrationality (Caplan)]] and [[Concepts/Systematic Economic Biases (Caplan)]].

## The Externality of Irrational Voting

Caplan recasts the paradox using the language of externalities: each irrational voter bears essentially zero private cost for their false beliefs (since their single vote rarely determines outcomes), while the resulting bad policy harms everyone. The social cost of widespread voter irrationality is not borne by the individual voters who produce it — a textbook externality generating tragedy-of-the-commons dynamics.
