# Ontological Argument (Descartes)

> In the *Fifth Meditation*, a proof of God's existence from the idea of a supremely perfect being: existence belongs to God's essence just as having three angles equal to two right angles belongs to the essence of a triangle, or having a valley belongs to the essence of a mountain. One cannot clearly and distinctly conceive God without existence; therefore God exists. The argument is presented as having the same status of certainty as mathematical truths and as independent of (though complementary to) the causal proof in the *Third Meditation*.

## Core Argument

"I find the idea of him, that is, of a supremely perfect being, in myself, just as much as I find the idea of any shape or number. And I clearly and distinctly understand that eternal existence belongs to his nature—just as clearly and distinctly as I understand that the properties I can demonstrate of some shape or number belong in fact to the nature of that shape or number."

"So that, even if not all the conclusions I have come to in my meditations over the past few days were true, I would still have to ascribe the same degree of certainty to the existence of God that I up to now have ascribed to mathematical truths."

Addressing the objection that one can separate existence from essence in other things (and thus imagine a God who does not exist): "existence can no more be separated from the essence of God than we can separate from the essence of a triangle that the sum of its three angles adds up to two right angles, or than we can separate the idea of a mountain from the idea of a valley."

The thought of a supremely perfect being who lacks existence (a perfection) is contradictory. "It is not that my thought brings his existence about, or that it imposes any necessity on anything, but, on the contrary, that the necessity of the thing itself, namely the existence of God, determines me to think it."

Further reply to "I can imagine a winged horse, so why not a God without existence?": When one supposes a supremely perfect being, one *must* attribute all perfections, including existence; one is not free to withhold it (unlike the arbitrary addition of wings to a horse). "Whenever I choose to think about the first and supreme being, and bring forth the idea of him... from the treasury of my mind, I must necessarily credit him with all perfections."

The idea of God is not fictitious; it is the image of a true and immutable nature (multiple marks: only God has existence in essence; cannot conceive multiple such beings; if one exists now it must have existed eternally and will continue; many other inseparable perfections).

## Relation to the Causal Proof and to Prior Tradition

The *Fifth* presents this as a "new argument" (in addition to the principal causal proof of the *Third*). Both are needed; the ontological reinforces the status of mathematical certainty itself (which depends on God).

Descartes was aware of the Anselmian version (via Caterus in the *First Objections*, who notes Aquinas's critique of Anselm). He distinguishes his version: it relies on the clear and distinct idea of a supremely perfect being (not on the premise that "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" exists in the mind) and on the inseparability of existence from that essence.

## Relevance to Vault

The ontological argument models a form of certainty grounded in the analysis of essences/ideas rather than empirical causation or external authority.

- **Essences and immutable natures**: Parallels the vault's treatment of core concepts (e.g., [[Concepts/Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)]], [[Concepts/Natural Law (Aquinas)]], [[Concepts/Ataraxia]]) as having stable, analyzable structures with necessary implications once clearly grasped. Properties "follow" from the clear idea.
- **Existence as perfection / necessity**: In vault terms, certain things (the schema itself, the value of compounding via wiki maintenance, the discipline of provenance and cross-reference) have a kind of "existence in essence"—they are required by the very idea of a persistent, reliable knowledge base. One cannot coherently conceive the project without them.
- **Freedom and necessity in thought**: One is not free to think of the supreme being without existence (or the vault project without its core disciplines) once the idea is clearly formed. This is a model for "schema-locked" reasoning: once the GROK.md criteria are clearly and distinctly in view, certain conclusions (update index and log, maintain wikilinks, flag contradictions) are necessitated.
- **Contrast with other vault God/ultimate arguments**: Vs. Aquinas's Five Ways or beatific vision (causal, participatory, grace-elevated); vs. Augustine's restless heart finding rest (existential, confessional); vs. Plotinus's One (emanative, beyond being). Descartes's version is idea-based, a priori, and tied to the clarity of the meditator's own thought.
- **Objections as lint**: The "sophism" objections (Caterus, Gassendi, and Descartes's own anticipations) and replies are a model for the vault's own critical practice: surface separation of existence/essence seems possible until the specific nature of *this* idea (supremely perfect) is clearly grasped.

**Open questions**:
- Does the argument prove *actual* existence or only that *if* a supremely perfect being is possible, it exists necessarily? (Kantian-style critique; Descartes would say the clear idea rules out the "merely possible" framing.)
