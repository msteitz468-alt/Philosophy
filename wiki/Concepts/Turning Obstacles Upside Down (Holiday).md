# Turning Obstacles Upside Down (Holiday)

> The signature thesis of Ryan Holiday's *The Obstacle Is the Way*: every impediment can be converted into an advantage, so that *"what stands in the way becomes the way."* The claim rests on a single line of Marcus Aurelius (*Meditations* 5.20): *"The impediment to action advances action. The mind adapts and converts to its own purposes the obstacle to our acting."* An obstacle is never simply bad; for the person who perceives, acts, and endures rightly, it is raw material — at worst, the occasion to practice a virtue.

## The Argument

1. **Nothing is good or bad in itself.** Following the Stoic doctrine of impressions (and Hamlet: "there is nothing either good or bad, but thinking makes it so"), an event is neutral; the *story we tell about it* supplies the value. "There is the event itself and the story we tell ourselves about what it means."
2. **Therefore the value is (partly) ours to assign.** Not through "gushing, hazy optimism" or denial, but through a "complete flip" — seeing "through the negative, past its underside, and into its corollary: the positive." Not *"This is not so bad"* but *"I can make this good."*
3. **The conversion is enacted by the three disciplines.** Perception isolates and de-fangs the obstacle; Action dismantles or goes around it; Will accepts and finds meaning in whatever remains. See [[Concepts/The Three Disciplines - Perception, Action, and Will (Hadot)]].
4. **The residual case still works.** When an obstacle truly cannot be overcome, it becomes "an opportunity to practice some other virtue or skill" — forgiveness, acceptance, humility, service to others. "It's an infinitely elastic formula."

Holiday's framing device is the Zen parable of the boulder in the road, under which a peasant who bothers to move it finds gold and a note: *"The obstacle in the path becomes the path."*

## Supporting Ideas

- **Adversarial / post-traumatic growth** — Holiday cites sports psychology on injured elite athletes who emerge with greater perspective and strength in exactly the areas the injury threatened: "That which doesn't kill me makes me stronger" treated as fact, not cliché. "The struggle against an obstacle inevitably propels the fighter to a new level of functioning."
- **Andy Grove's maxim** — "Bad companies are destroyed by crisis. Good companies survive them. Great companies are improved by them." Holiday generalizes this from firms to persons.
- **Eisenhower at the Battle of the Bulge** — the paradigm case: the enemy's overwhelming thrust "carried its own destruction within itself"; the obstacle, properly seen, was a trap for the one who created it.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Marcus Aurelius** — the source text (5.20) and its enacted example: the revolt of Avidius Cassius became, after the rebel's death, an "opportunity to practice forgiveness on a significantly larger scale"; "the fire... rises higher by means of this very material." See [[Thinkers/Marcus Aurelius]].
- **Nietzsche** — supplies the "stronger" formula and, via [[Concepts/Amor Fati (Love of Fate)]], the affirmative extreme; but his transvaluation is creative self-overcoming, not Stoic providence (see caveat below).
- **Ryan Holiday** — systematizes the flip as a repeatable method illustrated by ~50 historical vignettes. See [[Thinkers/Ryan Holiday]] and [[Sources/The Obstacle Is the Way - Ryan Holiday (2014)]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

> [!warning] The thesis flirts with a subtle over-claim. Orthodox Stoicism holds externals are *indifferents* — neither good nor bad and *not* to be sought — whereas Holiday's "I can make this *good*" often means turning adversity into worldly advantage (fortune, victory, ambition realized). This instrumentalizes virtue toward external success, which the Stoics denied is the good at all. Read strictly, an obstacle's only guaranteed "advantage" is the chance to act virtuously; the further promise that obstacles reliably yield success is motivational rhetoric, not Stoic doctrine. Compare [[Concepts/Amor Fati (Love of Fate)]] and Nietzsche's critique in [[Sources/Beyond Good and Evil - Nietzsche (AmazonClassics Edition)]].
