# The Three Gods — Father, Creator, and Creation (Numenius)

> [[Thinkers/Numenius of Apamea]]'s graded triad of divinities — a First God (the Good / uncreating Intellect), a Second God (the Demiurge who orders matter), and a Third God (the cosmos itself) — the clearest named ancestor of Plotinus's One–Intellect–Soul.

## The Doctrine

"Numenius, who teaches three Gods, calls the First *Father*, the Second *Creator*, and the Third *Creature*; for according to his opinion the world is the Third God" (fr. 36).

- **First God (Father).** Absolutely simple, "in himself," "in no way divisible" (fr. 26). He is pure Intellect and "the Good-in-itself," but *uncreating*: "the First God may not undertake creation, and therefore must be considered the Father of the Creating Divinity" (fr. 27a). He is "free from all labor, inasmuch as he is King" — the "Standing One," at rest, occupied only with the Intelligible (fr. 27a, 30). Reached not by inference from sensible things but by withdrawal — "far from the visible world, communing with the Good, the alone with the alone" (fr. 10).
- **Second God (Creator / Demiurge).** "Double": by nature intelligible, but because he "gazes on Matter and cares for it," he "forgets himself," comes into contact with the perceptible, and becomes the ordering Creator (fr. 11, 26). He "rules in that he passes through the heaven" and is "in motion" (fr. 27a, 30). The famous image: the Demiurge is a **pilot** who steers the ship of ordered matter across the sea of chaos while looking upward to the God on high, taking his critical (discerning) power from contemplation and his impulse from desire for matter (fr. 32).
- **Third God (Creature).** The ordered cosmos itself. Numenius calls the Demiurge "double" — First and Second God together — "and the Third is the Created" (fr. 36).

The Second God is good only *derivatively*, "by participation in the First"; the First is the Good-in-itself, "the Idea of the Creator" — as men are formed after the Idea of Man, the Demiurge is good after the First Good (fr. 33–34).

## Why It Matters: Ancestor of the Hypostases

Numenius's triad is the most explicit pre-Plotinian model for the [[Concepts/The One (To Hen)|One]] → Intellect → Soul hierarchy, and his "undiminished giving" (knowledge passed like flame from candle to candle without loss, fr. 29) is a mechanism [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] made central to [[Concepts/Emanation (Proodos)]]. Numenius's treatises were read in Plotinus's school, and Plotinus's followers had to rebut the charge that he simply repeated Numenius (Porphyry, *V. Plot.* 17).

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Numenius:** three ranked *gods*; the First is Intellect (nous) and the Good; the Second is internally divided (contemplative + demiurgic); the Third is the *world*.
- **[[Thinkers/Plotinus]]:** three *hypostases*, but the highest — the [[Concepts/The One (To Hen)|One]] — is *beyond* Intellect and being, not itself Intellect; the third is the *Soul* (World-Soul + individual souls), not the cosmos; and emanation is a single undiminished overflow, not a god who "forgets himself" by turning to matter. Plotinus keeps the levels distinct where Numenius blurs the Second.
- **[[Thinkers/Porphyry]]:** "telescopes" the Hypostases (blurring their distinctness) — in a sense returning toward Numenius's more collapsed scheme.
- **[[Thinkers/Iamblichus]] / Proclus:** multiply and stratify the divine ranks far beyond three (henads, triads within triads).
- Contrast [[Thinkers/Plato]]'s single Demiurge (*Timaeus*) who is *good* and looks to the Forms: Numenius splits that figure into a transcendent Good and a subordinate world-ordering Creator.
