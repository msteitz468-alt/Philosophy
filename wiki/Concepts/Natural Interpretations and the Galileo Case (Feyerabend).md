# Natural Interpretations and the Galileo Case (Feyerabend)

> **Natural interpretations** are "mental operations which follow so closely upon the senses" that we mistake them for the raw data — theory-laden assumptions so deeply built into perception and language that observing them requires "a special effort." Feyerabend's central case study, **Galileo's defence of Copernicus**, shows that scientific progress proceeds not by reading off neutral facts but by *replacing* natural interpretations — often by rhetoric, disguise, and moves that violate every empiricist rule.

## Natural Interpretations

Facts are not given; they are constituted. Between a bare sensation and an observation statement lies a layer of "mental operations… firmly connected with [the senses'] reactions" — the **natural interpretations**. Historically these were seen either as Kantian a priori presuppositions or as Baconian *prejudices* to be peeled away. Feyerabend rejects the Baconian programme of stripping observation down to a neutral "sensory core": natural interpretations are not added to a pre-existing field of sensations, they **constitute** that field — "eliminate all natural interpretations, and you also eliminate the ability to think and to perceive." A person with none would be "completely disoriented." Nor can the cluster be unravelled term by term without circularity (we use the concept under investigation to identify the very percepts that are supposed to test it). The only exit is an **external measure of comparison** — a *contradicting* alternative theory (hence [[Concepts/Counterinduction (Feyerabend)|counterinduction]]).

## The Galileo Case Study

**The tower argument** (Aristotelians against Earth's motion): a stone dropped from a tower falls straight to its base; if the Earth moved, it would be "left behind." This refutes Copernicus *only if* the "observed" straight fall is a **real** (absolute-space) motion. That identification — "apparent motion = real motion," a naïve *operative realism* — is a hidden natural interpretation, learned from childhood and embedded in 17th-century language. From within it, "the argument is impeccable and quite forceful."

Galileo's response (as Feyerabend reads him):
- He does **not** strip away interpretations (Bacon) nor retreat to pure reason (rationalists); he *replaces* the offending natural interpretation with a new one — the **relativity of motion** ("motion among things which share it in common is non-operative… insensible, imperceptible, and without any effect whatever") plus **circular inertia** — thereby introducing a **new observation language**.
- He disguises the change as **anamnesis ("reminiscence")**, making it seem that "nothing has changed" and we still speak in the old familiar way — a rhetorical sleight, not a neutral demonstration.
- For the heavens he "**changes sensations**" with the **telescope**, yet "offers no theoretical reasons why the telescope should be expected to give a true picture of the sky." The first telescopic images were "indistinct, indeterminate, contradictory and in conflict with what everyone can see"; the optical theory that might have validated them was itself refuted by simple tests. Some telescopic phenomena were genuinely Copernican, but Galileo presented "one refuted view (Copernicanism)" supported by phenomena from "another refuted view (that telescopic images faithfully depict the sky)."

**Moral**: Galileo won by **propaganda, style, ad hoc hypotheses, and counter-inductive defiance of the evidence** — "irrational" methods that were *needed* because of the "uneven development" of the parts of science. A strict empiricism or [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)|falsificationism]] applied at the time would have killed Copernicanism. (Galileo's circular law even "drastically reduces the content of dynamics" relative to Aristotle's — a *loss*, by Popperian lights.)

## Vault Meta / AI

The deepest lesson for knowledge work: **"facts" arrive pre-interpreted.** A source's "data" carries sedimented theory; an ingest that treats them as neutral inherits hidden commitments. To detect a natural interpretation, deploy a *contradicting* frame and watch where it feels "absurd" — that friction marks the buried assumption. For AI: an agent's perception is constituted by its current frame/representation; only rival framings reveal what the default one silently builds in. (And note Galileo's telescope: trusting a powerful new instrument/tool before there is any theory of *why* it is reliable is a recurring, and risky, pattern.)
