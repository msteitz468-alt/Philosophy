# Simulation Hypothesis (Chalmers)
> Chalmers's precise formulation — "We are and always have been in an artificially designed computer simulation of a world" — with a full taxonomy of simulation types and a defense of its meaningfulness against verificationism.

## Summary

In *Reality+* Chapters 1–2, Chalmers frames the book around three ancient questions given modern form by virtual worlds: the **Knowledge Question** ("Can we know whether or not we're in a virtual world?" — his answer: no), the **Reality Question** ("Are virtual worlds real or illusory?" — real), and the **Value Question** ("Can you lead a good life in a virtual world?" — yes). Zhuangzi's butterfly dream, Narada's transformation by Vishnu, and Plato's cave respectively prefigure the three.

His formulation refines Bostrom's "We are living in a computer simulation" by making it explicitly **lifelong and designed**. Being "in" a simulation means having one's sensory inputs from and motor outputs to it — Neo's brain is spatially outside the Matrix but "in" it in the sense that matters. There is a key asymmetry: we could in principle confirm we ARE simulated (simulators reveal themselves, source code, a bridge turned upside down), but never prove we're not — "any evidence of ordinary reality... could presumably be simulated."

## Taxonomy

- **Sims**: **biosims** (biological brains hooked up, as in the Matrix) vs. **pure sims** (fully simulated beings); correspondingly **impure / pure / mixed simulations**.
- **Global vs. local**: local simulations must flexibly fill in detail (the failure dramatized in *The Thirteenth Floor*).
- **Temporary/permanent, perfect/imperfect, pre-programmed/open-ended.** "A perfect simulation can be defined as one that precisely mirrors the world it's simulating."
- **Imperfect** simulation hypotheses are testable in principle (Beane, Davoudi & Savage 2012 on lattice-spacing signatures in cosmic rays), hence scientific; the **perfect** simulation hypothesis is untestable yet meaningful — "a philosophical hypothesis about the nature of our world." Popper's falsifiability criterion is thereby rejected as "too strong."
- The simulation hypothesis is equivalent to the **virtual-world hypothesis**: "I am in a (fully immersive) virtual world."

## How This Compares to Other Thinkers

- **[[Thinkers/Nick Bostrom]]** ([[Arguments/Simulation Argument (Bostrom)]]): Chalmers accepts the statistical structure but broadens ancestor simulations to "humanlike sims" and expands the trilemma with further sim blockers.
- **[[Thinkers/Rizwan Virk]]** ([[Concepts/Simulation Hypothesis (Virk)]]): Virk's RPG/NPC framing treats simulation as revelation that reality is game-like and seeks physical [[Concepts/Evidence of Computation (Virk)]]; Chalmers's interest is the reverse — showing that simulation would *not* undermine the reality of the ordinary world ([[Concepts/Simulation Realism (Chalmers)]]).
- **[[Thinkers/Karl Popper]]**: the perfect simulation hypothesis is a direct counterexample Chalmers wields against falsifiability as a meaning criterion — cf. [[Sources/The Logic of Scientific Discovery - Karl Popper (Routledge Classics)]] and [[Sources/Language, Truth and Logic - A.J. Ayer (1936)]] (verificationism, rejected in ch. 4).
- **Baudrillard**: his four phases of simulation (representation → simulacrum) are adapted to classify computer simulations.
- **Zhuangzi / Plato / Descartes**: the hypothesis is the computational heir of the butterfly dream, the cave, and the evil demon — see [[Arguments/Cartesian Skepticism and the Simulation Riposte (Chalmers)]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Whether pure sims can be conscious (needed for the hypothesis to include us) is argued yes in ch. 15 via fading qualia ([[Concepts/Machine Consciousness and Mind Uploading (Chalmers)]]) — against biological-substrate views (Koch–Tononi, Searle's [[Contradictions/Hofstadter vs Searle - Strange Loops vs Chinese Room]]).
