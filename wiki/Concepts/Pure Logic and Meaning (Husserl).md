# Pure Logic and Meaning (Husserl)
> Pure logic is the "theory of theories" — an a priori philosophical account of what any unified scientific theory is, grounded in the objectivity of ideal meanings rather than psychological acts.

## Summary

Husserl's **pure logic** (developed in *Logical Investigations*, 1900–01, building on Bernard Bolzano) is not a symbolic calculus or a psychological account of reasoning. It is a philosophical theory of what any unified, rigorous theory *is*: an ideal system of propositions about a domain, governed by formal categories of meaning and formal categories of objects. Logic is the "theory of theories," or **metatheory** — it applies to all sciences including itself.

The destruction of **psychologism** — the reduction of logical laws to empirical facts about how humans think — is Husserl's starting point. The construction of a theory of ideal meanings is his positive contribution.

## Anti-Psychologism

**Psychologism** (Mill, Erdmann): Logical laws are empirical generalizations about human psychological processes. Consequence: logic is contingent (if our psychology were different, the laws of logic would be different) and relative (different species might have different logics).

Husserl's critique: (1) Logical laws are not empirical generalizations but a priori necessities — they could not be otherwise. (2) Psychologism leads to relativism and ultimately to self-refutation (the very claim "all logical laws are psychological facts" is asserted as a logical/necessary truth, not a contingent psychological observation). (3) Logical objects (propositions, meanings) are ideal — they are not mental events.

**Every normative discipline (logic) rests on a theoretical discipline.** We ought to reason validly not for pragmatic reasons but because certain inferences are objectively valid by virtue of their logical form. Normativity presupposes theoretical grounding.

## Pure Logic: Three Tasks

1. **Categories of meaning**: The a priori forms of meaningful expression — Concept (individual vs. general), Proposition (complete judgment), Connective (logical operators). Grammar is not arbitrary but constrained by these ideal forms.

2. **Laws and theories**: The formal laws governing inferences among propositions, and the structure of theories as systems of such propositions. Includes consequence-logic (what follows from what) and truth-logic (what is true).

3. **Theory of manifolds**: A manifold is the abstract form of a field of objects — the objective correlate of a theory's form. A "definite manifold" is one that can be exhaustively characterized by a complete axiomatic system. This is Husserl's anticipation of model-theoretic semantics; the same formal structure can be "realized" in different domains (integers, corporate hierarchies).

## The Four-Level Correlation Schema

> **Sentences** → **Linguistic meanings** (Bedeutungen) → **Intentional senses** (Sinne) → **States of affairs** (Sachverhalte) in the world

- **Sentences**: Physical or mental acts of uttering/writing.
- **Linguistic meanings**: The meanings of expressions as "stamped" by language — public, shareable.
- **Intentional senses**: The underlying content of the judgment-act, prior to linguistic expression. Language "stamps" sense, modifying it rather than transparently expressing it.
- **States of affairs**: What a true proposition represents in the world — objective, mind-independent configurations of objects and properties.

This schema makes philosophy of language continuous with phenomenology (via intentional sense) and ontology (via states of affairs).

## Meanings as Ideal Species

Meanings (senses, propositions) are **ideal species** — universals that can be instanced in many acts of consciousness without being reducible to any one act. A proposition is not a token mental event but an ideal meaning expressible by any grammatically complete sentence expressing that thought.

Early Husserl (*Logical Investigations*): meanings are ideal species of act-types.
Later Husserl (*Ideas I*): meanings are renamed **noemata** and distinguished from species — they represent rather than instantiate. This shift is partly a refinement, partly a revision whose consistency is debated.

## Formal Logic and Formal Ontology

Pure logic requires **two parallel sets of categories**:
- **Meaning-categories** (Concept, Proposition, Connective): the forms of what can be meaningfully said.
- **Object-categories** (Individual, Property/Relation, State of Affairs, Unity, Plurality, Number): the forms of what can exist.

**Semantic rules** articulate the correlation: a singular term (meaning-category) refers to an individual (object-category); a predicate (meaning-category) refers to a property (object-category); a complete proposition (meaning-category) represents a state of affairs (object-category).

**Formal ontology** and **formal logic** are the objective and subjective sides of the same pure a priori domain.

## Connection to Frege

Husserl's Sinn/Sachverhalt parallels Frege's Sinn/Bedeutung (sense/reference):
- Both posit ideal senses that determine reference without being reducible to mental acts.
- Both hold that the same object can be referred to under different senses ("the victor at Jena" and "the vanquished at Waterloo").
- **Key difference**: Frege treats senses as free-standing abstract objects with no special tie to consciousness. Husserl grounds semantics in intentionality (transcendental phenomenology) — senses are "in" the space of consciousness as intentional contents, grounded transcendentally.
- **Demonstratives**: Frege struggled with "I," "here," "now." Husserl had a prior solution: these are "essentially occasional expressions" whose sense is keyed to the perceptual act and the lived body of the speaker.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Gottlob Frege]]**: Independent parallel development. Frege's review of Husserl's *Philosophy of Arithmetic* (charging psychologism) catalysed the *Prolegomena*. Both agree on the objectivity of senses; they diverge on whether semantics requires transcendental phenomenological grounding.
- **[[Thinkers/Ludwig Wittgenstein]] (early)**: The *Tractatus*'s "picturing" theory of meaning has structural parallels to Husserl's correlation schema — propositions "show" the structure of states of affairs. But Wittgenstein grounds meaning in the logical form of propositions, not in intentional acts of consciousness.
- **[[Thinkers/W.V.O. Quine]]**: Quine's rejection of the analytic/synthetic distinction targets the logical positivist version, but his holism and naturalized epistemology conflict with Husserl's a priori ideal meanings and his transcendental method. See [[Concepts/Analytic-Synthetic Distinction (Quine)]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Language as transparent vs. transformative: Husserl holds that language "expresses" intentional sense, but also that it "stamps" and modifies sense. How much distortion language introduces into the underlying judgment is never fully resolved.
- > [!warning] Whether pure logic is autonomous from phenomenology (Frege's implicit view) or necessarily grounded in it (Husserl's claim) remains contested. Post-Gödel logic has largely proceeded without phenomenological grounding.
