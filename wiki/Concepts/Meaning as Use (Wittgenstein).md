# Meaning as Use (Wittgenstein)
> The meaning of a word is not an object it names nor an idea it labels, but its use in the practice of language — "a sign is dead; in use it is alive."

## Summary

"For a large class of cases—though not for all—in which we employ the word 'meaning' it can be defined thus: the meaning of a word is its use in the language" (§43). This is the most quoted sentence of [[Thinkers/Ludwig Wittgenstein|Wittgenstein]]'s *Philosophical Investigations* and the positive doctrine that replaces the referential and mentalist theories he attacks. To know what a word means is not to have located the entity it stands for (the [[Concepts/Picture Theory of Language (Wittgenstein)|picture theory]]'s object) nor to be undergoing some inner experience; it is to be able to deploy the word correctly across the [[Concepts/Language-Games (Wittgenstein)|language-games]] in which it lives.

Wittgenstein guards the thesis carefully. He distinguishes the **meaning** of a name from its **bearer**: "When Mr. N.N. dies one says that the bearer of the name dies, not that the meaning dies" (§40). Meaning cannot be the object, because the object can vanish while the word goes on meaning. Nor is it a mental picture that, grasped "in a flash," somehow contains all its future applications in advance (§§138–141) — that picture, however vivid, still has to be *used*, and the same picture can be applied in different ways.

## Core Mechanics

### 1. The sign is dead; use gives it life
"Every sign *by itself* seems dead. *What* gives it life?—In *use* it is alive" (§432). A signpost, a word, an arrow — none points anywhere by its own intrinsic nature; "the arrow points only in the application that a living being makes of it" (§454). Meaning is therefore not lodged inside the sign or in a private accompaniment; it is constituted by the regular employment of the sign in a shared practice.

### 2. "Meaning is a physiognomy"
Words wear their use on their face. "Meaning is a physiognomy" (§568): the sense of a word is something one reads off how it functions among us, like the expression of a face, not an object hidden behind it. Correspondingly, "Essence is expressed by grammar" (§371) — what a thing *is* shows in how its concept-word is used.

### 3. Against meaning as a mental act
"Nothing is more wrong-headed than calling meaning a mental activity!" (§693). To mean someone by a word is not to perform an inner act of pointing-in-thought running alongside the words; intention and meaning are "embedded in a situation, in human customs and institutions" (§337). This connects meaning-as-use to the [[Concepts/Rule-Following (Wittgenstein)|rule-following]] argument: understanding a word is mastery of a technique, not possession of an interpretation in the mind.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. the early Wittgenstein & [[Thinkers/Bertrand Russell|Russell]]:** Referential/atomist semantics held that a name means by standing for an object and a proposition means by picturing a fact. Meaning-as-use abandons the object-correlate entirely. See [[Contradictions/Early vs Later Wittgenstein]].
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Locke|Locke]] (ideas as meanings):** For Locke words are signs of *ideas* in the mind. Wittgenstein's private-language argument ([[Concepts/Private Language Argument (Wittgenstein)]]) and the use-theory together deny that an inner idea could fix public meaning at all.
- **vs. [[Thinkers/A.J. Ayer|Ayer]]:** The [[Concepts/Verification Principle (Ayer)|verification principle]] made *method of verification* the meaning. Wittgenstein's broader "use" includes far more than verification — commanding, joking, thanking, praying — so it neither privileges nor excludes empirical talk by a single rule.
- **Toward AI / [[Thinkers/Douglas Hofstadter|distributional semantics]]:** "Meaning is use" is often cited as a philosophical ancestor of usage-based and distributional theories of meaning in linguistics and machine learning, where a word's meaning is recovered from the company it keeps rather than from a labelled referent.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
> [!warning] Replaces the object-and-name semantics of [[Concepts/Picture Theory of Language (Wittgenstein)]] — see [[Contradictions/Early vs Later Wittgenstein]].

- The hedge "for a large class of cases—though not for all" leaves open which uses of "meaning" the slogan does *not* cover, a much-debated interpretive question.
