# Parmenidean Being and the One (Parmenides)

> The metaphysical doctrine that reality is a single, eternal, indivisible, and completely changeless sphere of Being, and that all change and multiplicity are illusions of the senses.

## Summary

The core of Eleatic monism is the logical deduction that **"what is, is; and what is not, cannot be."** From this apparently simple tautology, Parmenides deduced that empty space (void), change, motion, and plurality are logically impossible, since they all require the existence of Non-Being (what is not). Real existence (Being) must therefore be a single, uniform, and eternal unity.

---

## Core Logical Deductions

**1. The Impossibility of Non-Being**:
- To think of something is to think of something that *is*. 
- We cannot think of "nothing" or "what is not," because the moment we think of it, it becomes an object of thought (it exists in the mind).
- Therefore, Non-Being is inconceivable and impossible.

**2. The Rejection of Generation and Decay**:
- If Being were generated, it must have come either from Being or from Non-Being.
- It cannot come from Non-Being, because Non-Being does not exist.
- It cannot come from Being, because Being already exists.
- Therefore, Being is ungenerated, eternal, and indestructible.

**3. The Rejection of Motion and Multiplicity**:
- Motion requires empty space (void) for a body to move into.
- Void is defined as the absence of Being (Non-Being).
- Since Non-Being cannot exist, there is no empty space.
- Without empty space, there can be no motion, and no separation between distinct objects.
- Therefore, reality is a single, continuous, homogeneous block of Being (often described as a well-rounded sphere, finished on all sides).

---

## Historical Impact and Significance

- **The Deductive Standard**: Parmenides shifted metaphysics from speculative analogies (water, air, fire) to strict logical deduction. If a premise is accepted, the thinker must accept all logical consequences, even if they contradict sensory experience.
- **The Pluralist Response**: Parmenides' denial of empty space and change forced later thinkers to redefine the nature of matter:
  - **Empedocles** and **Anaxagoras** argued that elements are eternal and unchanging (like Parmenidean Being), but they can mix and separate, creating the appearance of change.
  - **Democritus** accepted that atoms are internally indivisible and changeless (Parmenidean Being), but boldly asserted that **Non-Being (the void) exists** as a physical reality, allowing atoms to move and rearrange.
