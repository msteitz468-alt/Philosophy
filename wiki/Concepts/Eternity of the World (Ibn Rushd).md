# Eternity of the World (Ibn Rushd)

> The thesis (primarily Aristotelian, defended by Ibn Rushd / Averroes against Ghazali) that the ordered cosmos has no absolute temporal beginning: time, motion, and the world are eternal, with God as their eternal, simultaneous, and necessary cause rather than a temporal creator who “decides” at a particular moment to bring the world from non-existence into existence.

## Core Claims (from Tahafut al-Tahafut, First Discussion)

**Philosophers’ (Aristotelian/Avicennan) Position (defended by Averroes)**:
- Aristotle was the first to affirm that the world (the ordered whole, cosmos) is eternal. Prior thinkers assumed a beginning from primitive matter or previous worlds.
- Time and motion are eternal; there is no “before” the world in empty time that could motivate a beginning.
- An eternal God can be the cause of an eternal effect without “new will” or change in the divine (will in God is not like human will with choice between contraries or temporal deliberation).
- Potentiality and actuality: the world is the eternal actualization of eternal possibilities grounded in matter (itself eternal). No absolute becoming from pure nothing.
- Infinite causal series in time are possible in one sense (accidental, horizontal father-son or revolution sequences) while essential causation traces to the First Mover.
- Criticisms of Ghazali’s “eternal will” argument: it equivocates on “will” and “determinant”; treats God’s willing like a human decision that can be delayed; assumes new determinations are possible in the eternal.

**Ghazali’s (Ash‘arite/Theological) Attack**:
- The temporal cannot proceed from the absolutely eternal without a new determinant (otherwise it would have always existed or never). This leads either to infinite regress or an eternal God in whom new determinations arise (impossible).
- If the world had a beginning, there must have been a prior state of pure possibility with no sufficient reason to actualize at one moment rather than another.
- Infinite past revolutions (e.g., of spheres) that have “ended” at the present moment are impossible.
- God’s will is completely undetermined and can create distinctions itself; creation is not bound by “necessity” or prior possibility in the same way.
- Distinguishes God’s eternal willing *that* the world exist at a certain time from the world existing from eternity.

**Averroes’ Refutations and Nuances**:
- Many of Ghazali’s arguments are dialectical, not demonstrative; rely on equivocal uses of “possible,” “will,” “eternal,” “agent.”
- The “delay after will” problem is real for voluntary agents only if will is conceived anthropomorphically. For the Perfect Being there is no need, no choice between contraries, no “new impulse.”
- Aristotle himself contains tensions (eternal motion/time + requirement that every series of movers be finite; defense of potentiality while his own logic seems to exclude intermediaries between being and non-being).
- The philosophers’ view is that God’s priority to the world is causal/simultaneous, not temporal. True causal relation between eternal and eternal is possible.
- Ghazali’s alternative (arbitrary will establishing distinctions without reason) risks making God neither wise nor good.

The editor’s introduction highlights that both sides struggle with the relation between an unchanging eternal God and a changing world, and with whether “creation” language can coherently apply to something co-eternal.

## Relation to Other Vault Sources

- **Aristotle**: Direct defense of the Stagirite’s position (see *Physics* VIII, *De Caelo*, *Metaphysics* Lambda). Complements the vault’s Organon (modal/essentialist logic), Nicomachean Ethics (eudaimonia as activity in a complete life — which presupposes a stable natural order), and Rhetoric.
- **Thomas Aquinas / Summa**: Aquinas accepts *creatio ex nihilo* and temporal beginning as taught by faith/revelation, but develops sophisticated accounts of God’s eternal knowledge and will (e.g., “God willed from eternity that the world should be, but not that it should be from eternity”). He uses Aristotelian causation and hylomorphism extensively while correcting Averroist interpretations (especially on the soul). “Grace does not destroy nature but perfects it” and natural law presuppose a stable created nature with real causal powers.
- **Augustine (Confessions, City of God)**: Time as *distentio animi* (distention of the soul); creation and time co-originate; God abides in simple eternity (“today”). Strong personal/experiential and theological counter to purely philosophical eternity. The two-cities framework judges even the “best of the pagan world” (Marcus) as ultimately insufficient.
- **Plotinus / Neoplatonism**: Eternal emanation (*proodos*) from the One without loss or temporal beginning; soul’s *epistrophe*. The *Tahafut* critiques and refines emanationist elements in the Islamic *falasifa*.
- **Plato (Timaeus)**: The demiurge imposes order on pre-existing chaos/receptacle in time; the generated world is “a moving image of eternity.” Provides an alternative ancient creation narrative (craftsman, not pure will or pure necessity).
- **Lucretius / Epicurus**: Atomist eternal worlds (infinite collisions, swerve); no creation, no demiurge, no final causes. Direct materialist counterpoint.
- **Sextus Empiricus**: Skeptical Modes target causal claims, infinite regress, disagreement among Dogmatists (including Peripatetics) on first principles and the eternity/temporality question.

## Vault Implications and Open Questions

- **Causation and Natural Law**: The eternity debate is inseparable from the later natural-sciences discussion on whether there are real, necessary connections between causes and effects in the world (see sibling concept [[Concepts/Occasionalism vs Natural Causation (Ghazali and Ibn Rushd)]]). Occasionalism undermines the knowable natures required for natural law and scientific demonstration.
- **Last End and Beatitude**: An eternal world raises questions about the “complete life” required for eudaimonia and whether a temporal creature’s happiness can be fully realized within an eternal cosmic frame (Aquinas elevates this to supernatural beatific vision).
- **Creation, Time, and Grace**: Strong parallels and contrasts with Augustine’s analysis of time, memory, and the “restless heart” seeking rest in the eternal God; Aquinas’ synthesis of Aristotelian nature + Christian creation and grace.
- **Method**: Exemplifies rigorous point-by-point engagement with an opponent while preserving the integrity of both demonstrative philosophy and religious principles — a model for the wiki’s own “opposition before integration” (cf. Pyrrhonian equipollence) and lint passes.
- **Open Tensions**: Does affirming eternity diminish divine freedom or make God “impotent” (as Averroes himself notes paradoxically)? Can “creation” language survive without a temporal beginning? How do the philosophers’ concessions to religious imagery for the masses (resurrection, etc.) avoid a strict “double truth”?
