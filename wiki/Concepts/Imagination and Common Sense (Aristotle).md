# Imagination and Common Sense (Aristotle)

> No sixth sense exists beyond the five; a general/common sensibility, already latent across them, perceives the common sensibles and makes us aware that we are perceiving at all. Imagination (*phantasia*) is a distinct third capacity — "a movement resulting from an actual exercise of a power of sense" — that can persist, and err, after the sensation that produced it has ceased.

## Summary

*De Anima* Book III opens by closing off the possibility of further senses: every tangible quality is covered by touch, and every other sensible quality is transmitted through a medium (air or water) already assigned to one of the five senses. The common sensibles (motion, rest, number, figure, magnitude, unity) require no dedicated organ of their own; "there is already in us a general sensibility which enables us to perceive them directly." This same general capacity explains our awareness that we are seeing or hearing — either sight perceives itself, or a single unified perceptive power discriminates across the senses, which is also what allows us to judge between disparate sensibles like white and sweet at one and the same time, avoiding an infinite regress of monitoring senses. Aristotle then sharply distinguishes imagination (*phantasia*, etymologically linked to *phaos*, light) from both perception and belief/knowledge: it can occur in the complete absence of its object (as in dreams), it can be false where perception of a proper sensible is not, and it is not present in all perceiving animals. Positively, imagination "must be a movement resulting from an actual exercise of a power of sense" — dependent on and derived from sensation, but capable of outlasting and diverging from it. This capacity, distinguished into more passive/sensitive and more active/deliberative forms across different animals, becomes essential to Aristotle's later account of animal motion: an animal capable of appetite must possess imagination, since desire needs some object represented to it even in the physical absence of that object.

## Key Claims / Positions

- Perception, imagination, and thought/belief are three genuinely distinct cognitive capacities, not degrees of the same thing — imagination in particular is neither infallible perception nor fallible-but-object-independent belief.
- Self-awareness of perceiving (knowing that one sees, not just seeing) is explained by the same unifying "common" capacity that perceives common sensibles, rather than by a separate higher-order faculty — an economical solution avoiding infinite regress.
- Imagination is derivative of, but not reducible to, actual sensation: a "movement" it leaves behind, capable of persisting, decaying, or erring independently of any current sensory input.
- Imagination is a necessary condition for animal desire and therefore for animal self-motion (developed further via *orexis*, desire) — cognition and motivation are integrated rather than separate systems.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Downstream — medieval faculty psychology**: the common sense (*sensus communis*) and imagination as distinct internal faculties become fixtures of medieval and scholastic psychology (five external senses + internal senses: common sense, imagination, memory, estimation), directly inherited from this text.
- **Contrast with early modern empiricism**: Hume's and Locke's treatment of "ideas" as faded copies of "impressions" functionally resembles Aristotle's imagination-as-residual-movement-from-sensation, though without Aristotle's teleological integration of imagination into animal desire and motion.
- **Anticipates modern cognitive science**: the common-sensibles/binding problem (how do we perceive a single object as having color, shape, and motion together, given separate sense modalities?) is a genuine ancient anticipation of the contemporary "binding problem" in perception research.
