# Harmony of the Spheres
> The doctrine that the heavenly bodies, moving in mathematically ordered orbits, produce an inaudible cosmic music — joined in Pythagorean cosmology to the system of the *central fire* and to the soul's astral ascent.

## Summary
"Harmony of the spheres" names two distinct things that the tradition fused: (1) a **cosmology** — the planetary system attributed to [[Thinkers/Philolaus]], with the Earth a moving body around a central fire — and (2) a **metaphysical-religious** claim that the cosmos sounds a continuous concord. In [[Sources/Lore and Science in Ancient Pythagoreanism - Walter Burkert (1972)]], Burkert keeps the two apart and shows that neither establishes Pythagoras as an astronomer-scientist: the cosmology is fifth-century *physiologia* (Philolaic, late), and the celestial music is number-mysticism that does not even fit the Philolaic system.

## The System of the Central Fire (Philolaus)
Aristotle and the doxographers report a system in which **ten** bodies revolve about a **central fire** ("the hearth of the universe"): the counter-earth (ἀντίχθων), Earth, Moon, Sun, the five planets, and the sphere of fixed stars.
- **Not Copernican.** The Earth-as-planet "seems to anticipate Copernicus," but Burkert insists this is misleading: the centre is occupied by the *fire*, not the Sun, and the central fire (like the counter-earth) is *permanently invisible* because the Earth keeps its uninhabited face turned toward it.
- **Explains almost nothing.** Since one revolution of the Earth about the central fire just *is* the day–night cycle (Aristotle, *Cael.* 293a22), the system cannot account for planetary retrogradations the way heliocentrism does; the Pythagoreans simply denied appreciable parallax. Burkert rejects Erich Frank's claim that a "true Copernican" system was the original and the central fire a later mythic overlay.
- **The counter-earth** has no satisfactory astronomical work to do; Aristotle's gibe — that it was added merely to bring the count of bodies up to the perfect number **ten** — is given weight. Number-symbolism, not observation, drives it.
- Every bold feature has Ionian parallels (the Sun as reflector/burning-glass in Empedocles and Diogenes of Apollonia; a moving Earth in Leucippus/Democritus): "it is impossible to separate 'Ionian' and 'Pythagorean' astronomy." Philolaus even assigns geometrical angles to gods — among the first traces of **astrology** in Greece.

## The Celestial Music and Astral Immortality
The audible-cosmic-harmony doctrine (the planets as Sirens, the concord we cannot hear because we have heard it since birth) is attested for "the Pythagoreans" by Plato and Aristotle — but Burkert notes it "has no organic connection with the Philolaic system": it presupposes **seven** planets matched to the seven-stringed lyre, not the **ten** bodies of the central-fire cosmos. It belongs with **astral immortality** — the soul's ascent to a heavenly home — i.e. with the religious core ([[Concepts/Metempsychosis (Transmigration of Souls)]]), not with technical astronomy.

## Zhmud's Counter-Reading: Real Early Astronomy (Zhmud 2012)
[[Sources/Pythagoras and the Early Pythagoreans - Leonid Zhmud (2012)]] reads this material as *genuine early Pythagorean science*, not number-mysticism — and detaches the celestial music from Philolaus' system:
- **The order of the planets is a real, pre-Philolaus discovery.** Eudemus (fr. 146) credits "the Pythagoreans" with being *first to find the order* of the planets (moon–sun–Venus–Mercury–Mars–Jupiter–Saturn–fixed stars), based on sidereal period and brightness — and these Pythagoreans lived in the *first half of the 5th c.*, before Philolaus. The order owes nothing to Babylon (against Burkert, who pushed Philolaus' planet-knowledge onto a Babylonian source); Zhmud, following Neugebauer, finds Near-Eastern influence on early Greek astronomy minimal. **[[Thinkers/Alcmaeon of Croton]]** already knew the planets' independent west-to-east motion along the ecliptic.
- **The "dogs of Persephone" symbolism is late** (3rd-c.-AD Clement/Porphyry), not the pre-scientific core Burkert took it for.
- **The harmony of the spheres is a *physical* model, and older than Philolaus.** Its five principles (from Aristotle, *De Caelo* 290b: moving bodies make sound; loudness ∝ speed/size; speeds ∝ distances in concord-ratios; hence a cosmic concord; unheard because constant from birth) form Zhmud's clearest reconstructable early-Pythagorean system. Because Archytas and Philolaus each give *different*, non-Aristotelian explanations, the doctrine pre-dates them both.
- **Detach it from Philolaus** (against Burkert *and* Huffman): Aristotle never linked the celestial harmony to Philolaus, and the doctrine presupposes a *geocentric* model — a moving Earth around a central fire would destroy the music. The link to Philolaus is **Alexander of Aphrodisias' insertion**, not Aristotle's.
- **The counter-earth is astronomy, not arithmology.** Zhmud argues it was posited to explain the *frequency of lunar eclipses* (cf. Philip of Opus), not merely to pad the cosmos to the perfect number ten — and notes Aristotle's body-count is itself inconsistent. The decad-as-perfect-number is an *Academic* idea; the older Pythagorean "complete" number was three. See [[Concepts/Number as the Essence of Reality (Pythagoras)]].
- **Plato and Kepler.** The *logos* behind the Myth of Er (*Republic*) derives from this Pythagorean theory (its Sirens/Ananke trappings do not); and **Kepler's *Harmonice mundi*** (and his third law) is the doctrine's eventual scientific fulfilment.

## The Huffman Volume (2014): Harmonics and the Medieval Afterlife
- **The science behind it.** Andrew Barker's harmonics chapter grounds the cosmic-concord idea in the genuine ratio-theory of the *kanonikoi* — pitch as a quantitative attribute of speed/frequency, the concords as 2:1, 3:2, 4:3. Nicomachus' later *planetary scale* maps these onto the heavens. See [[Concepts/Pythagorean Harmonics]].
- **The transmission that made it canonical.** Andrew Hicks' chapter traces how the harmony of the spheres reached the Latin West through **Calcidius, Macrobius, and Boethius**: it "flowed from the World-Soul's numerical structure," and Boethius' *De institutione musica* fixed the threefold *musica mundana / humana / instrumentalis* for a millennium. Aristotle's *De Caelo* critique then reduced it to a "*Pythagoricum figmentum*" (Grocheio) — dormant until **Ficino's** Renaissance revival and, ultimately, Kepler. See [[Concepts/Pythagoras in Medieval and Renaissance Thought]].

## Relevance to the Vault
- A textbook case of the book's thesis: a doctrine later hailed as proto-scientific (and as anticipating [[Thinkers/Plato|Plato's]] *Timaeus* and even Copernicus) is, on analysis, fifth-century natural philosophy plus number-mysticism. See [[Contradictions/Pythagoras the Scientist vs the Religious Charismatic]].
- The early-modern "rebirth of Pythagorean science" (Copernicus, Galileo, Kepler) read this cosmology as their own ancestor — part of why the traditional portrait of Pythagoras proved so durable.
