# If You Can Spray Them Then They Are Real (Hacking)

> Hacking's emblematic and pragmatic criterion for entity realism, from the anecdote that converted him: in real laboratory practice, entities like electrons are accepted as real because we *use* them as tools — we "spray" positrons or electrons to charge a niobium ball in a Millikan-style experiment hunting fractional charges. "So far as I'm concerned, if you can spray them then they are real."

## The Anecdote (the conversion moment)

Hacking was not initially exercised by scientific realism. A friend described an ongoing Stanford experiment (LaRue, Fairbank, Hebard) to detect free quarks (fractional charges of ±1/3 e) using a modern variant of Millikan's oil-drop method: a small superconducting niobium ball (much larger than Millikan's drops) levitated in a magnetic field.

To change the charge on the ball for the measurement, "we spray it with positrons to increase the charge or with electrons to decrease the charge."

From that day, Hacking was a scientific realist — not primarily because of the (still controversial) quarks, but because electrons and positrons had become *standard, reliable tools* whose effects, causes, and manipulations were understood well enough to be used to find out something else. Skeptical doubts that might have applied in 1908 (intermolecular forces on the drops? is Millikan really measuring charge on electrons or something else?) had been answered in practice by the existence of dependable emitters and the ability to use them causally.

## The Criterion

We count as real those entities we can use to intervene in the world to affect something else, or that the world uses to affect us. Reality as *intervention* (manipulation, use as probe or tool) provides a ground that reality as *representation* (picturing, literal description) could not securely deliver.

This is not naive "I can kick it" realism; it is tied to understanding causes and effects well enough to deploy the entity technologically within the experiment.

## Scope and Limits (per Hacking)

- Applies paradigmatically to entities that have become "tools of the trade" (electrons, positrons, various particles and fields in modern physics).
- Does *not* automatically extend to every theoretical posit (e.g., the quarks in the story remained controversial; alternative explanations were still live).
- The experimenter can remain anti-realist about the overarching theories while being realist about the entities used inside them.
- "The speculator, the calculator, and the model-builder can be anti-realist; the experimenter must be a realist."

## Relation to Other Vault Content

- Grounds the distinction in [[Concepts/Entity Realism vs Theory Realism (Hacking)]].
- Provides a practical, intervention-based answer to worries raised by [[Concepts/Incommensurability (Kuhn)]] and theory-ladenness: even if representations and meanings shift, the ability to use an entity to produce further effects across different setups stabilizes its reality.
- Contrasts with positivist anti-entity stances (Ayer et al.) and with purely representational pragmatist or internal-realist views (James, Putnam as discussed by Hacking).
- Complements Popper's realism (conjecturalism, verisimilitude) by offering a different, experimental-practice route to the same broad attitude.

## Vault Meta / AI

" If you can usefully spray it (call a tool, invoke a capability, manipulate a parameter that reliably affects downstream outputs), then for practical purposes in this system it is real." The criterion favors entities we can actually *use* over those we can only represent or theorize about. Favors building with stable, intervenable components (skills, MCP tools, verifiers) whose causal profiles are understood through use.
