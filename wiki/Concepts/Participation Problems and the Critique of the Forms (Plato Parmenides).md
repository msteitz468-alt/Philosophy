# Participation Problems and the Critique of the Forms (Plato Parmenides)

> In Part I of Plato's *Parmenides* (126a–137c), the aged Parmenides interrogates young Socrates' [[Concepts/Theory of Forms|Theory of Forms]] and exposes that the central relation of the theory — *participation* (μέθεξις), by which sensible things "partake of" a Form — cannot be coherently stated. This is Plato putting his own signature doctrine on trial, and it is the primary-text origin of objections usually met later in [[Concepts/Critique of Platonic Forms (Aristotle)|Aristotle's critique]].

## The Setup

Socrates posits Forms "themselves by themselves" — a Form of Likeness, Largeness, Beauty, Justice — in which the many sensible things participate and after which they are named (129–130). He is confident about Forms for mathematical and value predicates, hesitant about Forms of Human/Fire/Water, and recoils from Forms of Hair, Mud, and Dirt — a squeamishness Parmenides marks as a symptom of philosophical immaturity (130c–e).

## The Objections

1. **The whole/part dilemma of participation (131a–e).** Does a thing partake of the **whole** Form or a **part** of it?
   - *Whole*: then the one Form is present entire in each of many separate things at once, and so is "separate from itself" (Socrates' "one day / one sail over many" analogy fails — a sail covers each person only part by part).
   - *Part*: then the Form is divisible; absurdities follow — a large thing is large by a part of Largeness that is *smaller* than Largeness itself; a thing equal by less than the Equal; the Small made large. One cannot apply physical (spatial, quantitative) criteria to abstracts.
2. **The Third Man regress (132a–b).** Positing one Form of Largeness over many large things yields a new plurality (Largeness + the large things) requiring *another* Largeness, and so on without end. Each Form becomes "unlimited in multitude." See [[Concepts/The Third Man Argument]].
3. **Forms as thoughts (132b–c).** Socrates' retreat — a Form is merely a thought in minds — collapses: a thought is a thought *of* something that is; that object is the Form again.
4. **The paradigm/likeness regress (132d–133a).** Forms as *patterns* (paradeigmata) that things resemble regresses too: if the copy is like the pattern, the pattern is like the copy, so a further Form of their shared likeness is required, endlessly. Hence "nothing can be like the Form, nor the Form like anything else."
5. **The Greatest Difficulty — radical separation (133a–134e).** If the Forms are strictly "themselves by themselves," Forms have their being only *in relation to one another* (Mastery to Slavery, Knowledge to Truth), and things "amongst us" only in relation to *each other*. Consequences:
   - **Knowledge itself** is knowledge of **Truth itself** (a Form); so *human* knowledge, being of things amongst us, cannot reach the Forms — **the Forms are unknowable to us**.
   - Worse: a god possessing exact Knowledge itself could know only the Forms, hence **could not know human affairs** — and could not be our master. Complete *chorismos* (separation) makes the Forms both unknowable and useless.

## The Resolution Parmenides Points To

The objections do **not** license abandoning Forms: "if someone will not concede that there are Forms… he will completely destroy the power of discourse" (135b–c). The fault is Socrates' attempting to "define prematurely… before you are properly trained." The remedy is a **dialectical gymnastic**: for any hypothesis, trace the consequences **both** "if it is" **and** "if it is not," for the subject in relation to itself and to the others (135e–136c). Part II runs this on the One — see [[Concepts/The Eight Deductions - Hypotheses about the One (Plato Parmenides)]]. On Hermann's reading, Part II's "combining" arguments (II, III, V, VII) and "separating" arguments (I, IV, VI, VIII) are precisely the demonstration of combining-and-separating the Forms that Socrates asked for at 129e–130a — so the Second Part *answers* the First.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Plato (self-critique):** The objections are Plato's own, put in Parmenides' mouth. Whether they refute the middle-period Forms (Developmentalist reading) or only a naive version to be repaired (Unitarian/Hermann reading) is unsettled — logged in [[Sources/Plato's Parmenides - Text, Translation and Introductory Essay (Hermann, 2010)]].
- **Aristotle:** inherits the **Third Man** and the "separation" and "causal inertness" charges as external weapons against Plato — see [[Concepts/Critique of Platonic Forms (Aristotle)]]. The vault's [[Contradictions/Plato vs Aristotle on the Forms]] thus begins *inside* Plato.
- **Neoplatonists:** rather than a refutation, Plotinus and Proclus read the fallout — especially the unknowable, relationless One of Argument I — as the doorway to the **One beyond Being** and negative theology. See [[Concepts/The One (To Hen)]].
