# Miracle of Aggregation
> If voter errors are random, they cancel in large electorates, giving any small informed minority the power to determine democratic outcomes.

## Summary

The Miracle of Aggregation is the statistical argument that democratic outcomes can be epistemically reliable even with a massively ignorant electorate. The logic: if individual voters err randomly around the correct answer, errors will cancel out by the law of large numbers, leaving the *average* vote as an accurate signal. A small minority of informed voters therefore effectively controls democratic outcomes, since the uninformed masses' errors wash out.

The concept draws on James Surowiecki's *The Wisdom of Crowds* (2004) and earlier work by Benjamin Page and Robert Shapiro on the rationality of collective public opinion. Caplan in *The Myth of the Rational Voter* (2007) grants that the miracle is *real* but argues it rests on a condition — **random error** — that is empirically false for political beliefs about economics.

## The Condition It Requires

The Miracle works if and only if voter errors are *random* — symmetrically distributed around the truth, with no systematic directional bias. If errors are systematic (most voters err in the same direction), aggregation amplifies the error rather than canceling it.

Caplan's empirical work (especially the 1996 Survey of Americans and Economists on the Economy, SAEE) demonstrates that lay beliefs about economics are systematically biased in four directions: anti-market, anti-foreign, make-work, and pessimistic. These biases are not random — they are predictably directional. The Miracle therefore fails for economics, which is at the heart of most modern policy disputes.

## How Rational Ignorance Meets the Miracle

Classical Public Choice theorists (Downs, Tullock) argued that voter ignorance causes democratic failure. But Caplan points out that rational ignorance alone cannot produce systematic policy distortion *if* the Miracle of Aggregation holds: ignorant but unbiased voters cancel out, leaving informed voters decisive. The rational ignorance model therefore paradoxically *defends* democracy against its critics, since the informed minority should steer outcomes correctly.

Caplan's move: show that the errors are not random (the Miracle fails) and that this is due to *active* irrational demand for false beliefs ([[Concepts/Rational Irrationality (Caplan)]]), not mere ignorance.

## Relationship to Wittman's Defense of Democracy

Donald Wittman used the Miracle as part of his case that democracy works well ("The Myth of Democratic Failure," 1989; *The Myth of Democratic Failure*, 1995). Caplan credits Wittman's critique of Classical Public Choice but argues Wittman never demonstrated that voter errors are actually random — he assumed it. The SAEE data show they are not.
