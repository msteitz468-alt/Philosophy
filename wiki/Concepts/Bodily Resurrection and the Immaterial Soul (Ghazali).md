# Bodily Resurrection and the Immaterial Soul (Ghazali)

> Discussions 18–20 of the *Tahafut*, on the human soul and the afterlife — culminating in the third doctrine for which al-Ghazali condemns the philosophers as unbelievers: their **denial of bodily resurrection**. His strategy is layered: (18) the philosophers cannot *demonstrate* the soul is an immaterial substance; (19) nor that it is necessarily immortal; (20) but **even if** their immaterial soul is granted, bodily resurrection — explicitly and literally promised in the Qur'an — remains possible and must be affirmed.

## Disc. 18 — The Soul's Immateriality Is Undemonstrated

Al-Ghazali does *not* deny that the soul is an immaterial substance; he even allows the religious law may support it. He denies only that **reason alone** proves it, against Avicenna's ten proofs: "We deny... their claim that reason alone indicates this and that there is no need in it for the religious law." His recurring move is to grant the philosophers their own psychology (the inner senses, the estimative faculty) and turn it against them, exposing an over-hasty leap from particular cases to an unrestricted universal:

- **The ewe and the wolf.** Proof 1 says knowledge is indivisible, so its receptacle must be indivisible and hence non-bodily. But the ewe's estimative faculty apprehends the wolf's *enmity* — an indivisible, non-spatial "meaning" — and the philosophers themselves locate that faculty in a *bodily* organ that perishes. So an indivisible apprehension *can* reside in a body; "one of the two things has been contradicted."
- **The mind as another sense.** Against the proof that the intellect knows itself and its own organ (which no bodily sense does), he suggests the mind may simply be a further bodily sense with unusual reach — "as the crocodile stands in relation to the rest of the animals."
- **Bodily renewal.** Against the proof from the body's total material turnover (the self persists though the matter is replaced), he answers that some original matter always remains, "just as it is said 'this tree is that tree.'"

The verdict on each is the same epistemic point: such arguments "bequeath opinion" but do "not yield reliable certitude... in which error is not allowed."

## Disc. 19 — The Soul Is Not Provably Immortal

Even if the soul exists as a substance, the philosophers cannot prove it *cannot* perish. Their argument (the soul has no bodily receptacle, no contrary, and power cannot produce non-existence) fails because the soul, though perhaps not *indwelling* the body, has a hidden **connection** or "special affinity" with it: "it is not improbable that this unknown relation is of a mode that renders the soul in need of the body for its existence so that, if [the body] is corrupted, [the soul] is corrupted." If an unknown relation conditions the soul's *creation*, it may equally condition its *durability*. And in any case God's power can annihilate it: "the whole, then, could be annihilated so that nothing would remain save God."

## Disc. 20 — Bodily Resurrection (the Condemned Denial)

The decisive section. Al-Ghazali's defense:

1. **The "even if" concession.** He takes the philosophers' own third option — the soul survives death as a self-subsisting substance and is *returned to a body*, whether from its original matter, other matter, or matter created anew. "For [an individual] is what he is by virtue of his soul, not his body," so the reuniting "lies within God's power and would be a true return of that soul... This, then, becomes true resurrection." The immateriality he refused to *demonstrate* he is willing to *grant* dialectically, because even granting it costs the philosophers nothing he needs.
2. **Scripture is explicit and literal here.** Unlike anthropomorphic verses (which rational proof requires reading metaphorically), the descriptions of paradise and hellfire "attained a degree [of explicit statement] that does not [render it] subject to metaphorical interpretation," and resurrection is *not* rationally impossible — so by his [[Concepts/Criterion of Demonstrability (Ghazali)|demonstrability criterion]] "one must follow the apparent [literal] meaning."
3. **The pleasures and pains are bodily.** Against the philosophers' purely intellectual afterlife, he affirms a *combination* of spiritual and bodily felicity, since bodily pleasure and pain require the bodily instrument restored at the resurrection.
4. **Dismissing the objections.** "Souls infinite, matter finite" rests on the world's eternity, which he denies (finite souls), and anyway God creates ex nihilo. To the charge that returning a soul to a body is *transmigration*: "there is no need to squabble about terms... We only deny transmigration *in this world*."

## The Verdict (Conclusion)

Al-Ghazali names the **three doctrines that necessitate takfir** (the charge of unbelief): "the world's pre-eternity... that God's knowledge does not encompass the temporal particulars... and their denial of the resurrection of bodies." "These three doctrines do not agree with Islam in any respect... This is manifest infidelity which none of the Islamic sects have believed." The other seventeen errors he frames as merely heretical innovation (*bid'a*), shared with one or another recognized sect — pointedly declining to settle whether innovators in general are infidels, "lest the discourse should stray from the objective of this book." See [[Concepts/Eternal Will and Temporal Creation (Ghazali)]] and [[Concepts/God's Knowledge of Particulars (Ghazali)]] for the other two.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]:** Averroes holds the philosophers *do* affirm an afterlife — but as a doctrine necessary for the moral order of the masses, with the exact mechanism (identical body vs. simulacrum) left open; he charges al-Ghazali with crude literalism and with conceding too much by granting the immaterial soul. The "even if" concession is exactly the seam Averroes pries at.
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]:** Aquinas threads the needle al-Ghazali leaves dangling — the rational soul is *both* immaterial *and* the substantial form of *this* body, so personal bodily resurrection is metaphysically natural to the human composite, not a sheer act of divine power on a separable substance. Where al-Ghazali grants immateriality only for argument's sake, Aquinas builds his whole eschatology on it (and rejects the Averroist unicity of intellect to keep "Socrates" the one who is raised).
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Locke|Locke]]:** The identity worry al-Ghazali waves off ("this tree is that tree") becomes Locke's central problem — personal identity through material change tracked by *consciousness* rather than substance ([[Concepts/Association of Ideas (Locke)]] cluster), a wholly different solvent for the same puzzle.
- **vs. ancient mortalism:** Direct antithesis to the Epicurean–Lucretian denial of any survival; al-Ghazali's "bodily pleasures of paradise" is exactly what Lucretius' therapy against the fear of death aims to dissolve.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- If a soul can be reunited with *new* matter and still be "the same person," what does the original body contribute — and how is this not the transmigration al-Ghazali rejects "in this world"?
- Is granting immateriality "for the sake of argument" stable, given Disc. 18 left the soul's nature undecided? Averroes thinks not.

> [!warning] The philosophers (per [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]) affirm a primarily spiritual/intellectual afterlife and deny resurrection of *bodies*; al-Ghazali condemns this as unbelief — see [[Sources/Incoherence of the Incoherence - Ibn Rushd]]
