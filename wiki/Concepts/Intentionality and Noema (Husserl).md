# Intentionality and Noema (Husserl)
> Consciousness is always consciousness *of* something; the "noema" is the ideal sense through which an act is directed toward its object.

## Summary

Intentionality — the directedness of consciousness toward objects — is, for Husserl, "the central structure in the essence of consciousness." Every act of consciousness (perception, judgment, memory, imagination, desire) is a consciousness *of* something. This structural property was introduced to Husserl by [[Thinkers/Franz Brentano]], who drew on the medieval concept of intentional inexistence. Husserl radicalised and systematised it into the core of phenomenology.

The **noema** (pl. noemata) is Husserl's mature answer to the question: *how* is consciousness directed toward an object? The noema is the ideal content — the "sense" (Sinn) — through which an act is directed. It is neither the act itself (a real, temporal event in consciousness) nor the object (worldly or abstract), but a third, ideal entity mediating the two.

## The Act–Noema–Object Structure

The full structure of an intentional act:

> *Background → Subject — act (noesis) — noematic sense ("the object as intended") → object ) horizon*

- **Noesis**: The "real" (reell), temporal intentional part of the act — the sense-giving, animating layer; an act of perceiving, judging, wishing.
- **Noema**: The "non-real" (nichtreell), ideal, non-temporal content correlated with the noesis. It is a *moment* (dependent part) of the experience — its esse consists in its percipi, but not in a Berkeleyan sense: the noema is not a real component of experience but an ideal one.
- **Object**: What the act is *about* — potentially existent or non-existent, possibly reached through the noema when intuition is fulfilled.
- **Horizon**: The range of possibilities *predelineated* by noematic sense for the same object — what the object may look like from other sides, further possible properties, temporal and spatial continuation. "The 'and so forth' is an absolutely indispensable moment in the thing-noema" (*Ideas I*, §149).

The noema does NOT function as a veil between consciousness and the world. In successful perception (intuitive fulfillment), consciousness reaches the object *through* the noema, not a representation *of* it.

## Structure of the Noema

The noematic nucleus has two layers:
1. **The "pure X"** (determinable subject): The identity-component across varying perspectives — the same object intended under different predicates. This accounts for tracking identity through perceptual change.
2. **Predicate-senses**: Properties attributed to X within the act (e.g., ⟨red⟩, ⟨round⟩, ⟨in front of me⟩).

Additionally, the noema has a **thetic character** — the positing mode of the act (perceiving-as-real, judging, wishing, imagining, remembering). This accounts for the difference between perceiving and imagining the same object.

Example: "The victor at Jena" and "the vanquished at Waterloo" are different noematic senses directed toward the same object (Napoleon). This parallels Frege's Sinn/Bedeutung distinction.

## Hyle and Noesis

The **hyle** (hyletic data, sensuous material) is the non-intentional, raw sensory matter of an experience — the sensations that get "animated" by the noesis into a directed act. Hyle is real (reell) like the noesis. The noesis "informs" the hyle, making it intentional. This is Husserl's attempt to avoid both pure intellectualism (no role for sensation) and pure empiricism (sensation without intentional form).

## The Four Readings of the Noema

Scholarly debate has produced four interpretations:
1. **Intentional-object reading**: The noema just *is* the object-as-intended — no mediating entity.
2. **Neo-phenomenalist reading**: The noema is a "thick" sense, including perceptual appearance.
3. **Mediating-sense / "West Coast" reading** (Føllesdal, Dreyfus, Smith/McIntyre): Noema as a Fregean abstract sense mediating reference to the object.
4. **Bracketed-object / "East Coast" reading** (Sokolowski, Drummond): The noema is the object as encountered within the epoché.

Smith endorses a variant of the West Coast reading while integrating the phenomenal dimension: the noema is simultaneously (i) an ideal logical meaning (semantic), (ii) a phenomenal mode of presentation (how the object appears), and (iii) a historically constituted artifact (its genesis is cultural).

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Franz Brentano]]**: Introduced "intentional inexistence" — mental acts contain their objects as immanent. Husserl replaced immanence with ideal noematic sense; the object need not be "in" consciousness.
- **[[Thinkers/Gottlob Frege]]**: Frege's Sinn (sense) determines Bedeutung (reference). Husserl's Sinn/noema parallels this but grounds semantics in intentionality and transcendental phenomenology, which Frege does not.
- **[[Thinkers/Jean-Paul Sartre]]**: Accepts Husserlian intentionality but radicalises it: if consciousness is *purely* directedness, there is no prior ego doing the directing. The "I" is only a transcendent object of reflection. Consciousness is *néant* (nothingness). See [[Concepts/Consciousness as Intentionality and Néantisation (Sartre)]].
- **[[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]]**: Retains intentionality but replaces the transcendental subject with Dasein's "being-in-the-world." Heidegger objects that Husserl's focus on consciousness-directed-at-objects is epistemological and derivative; Dasein is primordially absorbed in practical engagement, not a spectating consciousness.
- **[[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]**: [[Concepts/Heterophenomenology (Dennett)]] takes subjects' reports about their intentional states as data without granting them privileged authority. Dennett rejects the idea of a phenomenal "given" — there is no intrinsic intentional content independent of third-person functional description.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] The noema is described as both "ideal" (non-temporal, abstract) and a "moment" (dependent part) of a concrete temporal act. How an atemporal entity can be ontologically dependent on a temporal one is a genuine puzzle Husserl resolves by analogy to how an ideal species is "in" a concrete instance — but the analogy is contested.
- > [!warning] Frege vs. Husserl on the grounding of sense: Frege's senses are free-standing abstract objects; Husserl's are intentional contents of acts, grounded transcendentally. Whether pure logic is autonomous from phenomenology (Frege's implicit position) or necessarily grounded in it (Husserl's claim) remains a live dispute.
