# Problem of Induction (Hume)

> Hume's "sceptical doubt" (*Enquiry* Section IV, Part II): all our reasoning about matters of fact beyond present sense and memory rests on cause and effect; our knowledge of cause and effect comes entirely from experience; and *every* inference from experience presupposes that **the future will resemble the past** (that "the course of nature continues uniformly the same"). But this Uniformity Principle can be justified by neither kind of reasoning Hume's Fork allows. Therefore inductive inference "is not founded on reasoning, or any process of the understanding." It is the most influential single argument in the theory of knowledge.

## The Argument

1. All factual inference beyond the senses works through **cause and effect** ("his friend is in France" because of a received letter; a watch on a desert island implies prior inhabitants).
2. Causal relations are known **only by experience**, never a priori: no examination of an object's sensible qualities reveals its effects. Adam could not infer drowning from water's fluidity; the motion communicated to the second billiard ball is "a quite distinct event" from the first, and a hundred other outcomes are equally conceivable. (Cf. [[Concepts/Hume's Fork (Relations of Ideas vs Matters of Fact)]]: the contrary of any predicted effect implies no contradiction.)
3. Experience informs us only of the **past and the observed** — "that precise period of time which fell under its cognizance." To reach the *future and the unobserved*, we add a hidden premise: **the future will resemble the past** / like sensible qualities will be attended with like secret powers.
4. This Uniformity Principle cannot be established:
   - **Not by demonstration** (relations of ideas): "it implies no contradiction that the course of nature may change," that snow might taste of salt or fire feel cold. A change in nature is perfectly conceivable, hence not demonstrably false.
   - **Not by experience** (matters of fact): any such argument would itself be a causal inference from past to future, and so would *presuppose the very principle in question*. "To endeavour the proof of this last supposition by probable arguments… must be evidently going in a circle, and taking that for granted which is the very point in question."
5. Therefore the step from "I have always found A conjoined with B" to "this A will be conjoined with B" is **not made by reason**. "I cannot find, I cannot imagine any such reasoning."

The "peasants, infants, nay even brute beasts" make this inference and learn from experience; if it required a subtle argument, they could not. So whatever drives induction is "obvious to the capacity of a mere infant" and *not* ratiocination (see [[Thinkers/Hume]] Section IX on animal reason).

## What It Is *Not*

Hume is not advising us to *stop* inducing. "None but a fool or madman will ever pretend to dispute the authority of experience, or to reject that great guide of human life." As an *agent* he is "quite satisfied"; only as a *philosopher* curious about foundations does he find the inference unjustified by reason. The doubt is theoretical, not practical — which is why Section V supplies a **"sceptical solution"**: the inference is the work of **custom**, not argument. See [[Concepts/Custom and Belief (Hume)]].

## Legacy

The defining problem of modern epistemology of science. **Kant** took it as the spur to his whole critical project. **Popper** accepted Hume's negative result in full and supplied a methodological bypass (see dedicated subsection below). **Goodman**'s "new riddle of induction" (grue) showed the problem is even deeper — *which* regularities to project is underdetermined. It frames every philosophy of confidence, evidence, and learning.

## Popper's Methodological Response (from *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*)

Popper accepts Hume's entire negative argument: no rational foundation exists for the inference from observed conjunctions to unobserved cases; any principle of induction leads to infinite regress or must be taken as synthetic a priori; probabilistic weakenings inherit the same difficulties. He does *not*, however, accept Hume's "sceptical solution" (custom/habit as the non-rational engine that makes induction work in practice) as an adequate *logic* of science, nor does he follow Russell (*Problems of Philosophy*) in positing an a priori inductive principle to assign rising probabilities.

Instead, Popper drops the requirement (shared by Hume's critics and the positivists) that empirical/scientific statements must be "conclusively decidable" — both verifiable *and* falsifiable. By admitting statements that are unilaterally decidable (falsifiable but not verifiable), the apparent contradiction between the "fundamental thesis of empiricism" (experience decides truth or falsity) and Hume's critique disappears.

- Science proceeds by **bold conjectures** (theories, hypotheses) advanced to solve problems.
- From these, **testable predictions** are deduced (especially risky ones that conflict with background expectations or rival theories).
- Predictions are tested against **basic statements** (accepted singular reports of observable states).
- A negative outcome **falsifies** (via modus tollens). A positive outcome provides only temporary **corroboration** ("the theory has proved its mettle" by surviving severe tests so far).
- No inductive step from "verified instances" to the truth or probability of the universal ever occurs or is needed.

The **criterion of demarcation** that emerges is **falsifiability** (see [[Concepts/Falsifiability as Criterion of Demarcation (Popper)]] and [[Concepts/Problem of Demarcation (Kant and Popper)]]). A system is scientific if it is possible in principle to deduce basic statements that contradict it. This also yields methodological rules: auxiliary hypotheses must increase (not dilute) falsifiability; the aim is to expose theories to the fiercest critical struggle rather than to protect them.

**Corroboration** (see dedicated concept) is the positive, non-probabilistic counterpart: a report of past survival under detailed and severe testing, always relative to the riskiness of the tests and current background knowledge. It is not "verification," not a degree of probability, and not a guarantee for the future.

This is the core of **critical rationalism**: rational progress occurs through conjecture and (attempted) refutation, not through inductive justification. The vault's own practices — source-driven updates, contradiction pages, lint, explicit provenance, and refusal to treat any summary as final — instantiate a small-scale version of the same discipline.

See primary source: [[Sources/The Logic of Scientific Discovery - Karl Popper (Routledge Classics)]] and the new [[Thinkers/Karl Popper]] page. Companion volume *Conjectures and Refutations* (1963) present in `raw/` (pending).

**Quine's holist radicalization (from "Two Dogmas of Empiricism")**: Even Popper's methodological bypass assumes that individual statements or theories can be assigned relatively localized empirical content against which they can be tested. Quine (1951) argues that the second dogma (reductionism) is false: our statements about the world face experience not individually but only as a corporate body — the whole web of belief (science, logic, ontology included). Any single contrary experience can be accommodated by readjustments anywhere in the interior (auxiliaries, meanings, even logical laws), subject only to pragmatic constraints of overall simplicity and conservatism. There is no clean "empirical content" for an isolated prediction or law. This deepens Hume's problem into underdetermination: the web as a whole is underdetermined by its boundary conditions (experience). See [[Concepts/Confirmation Holism (Duhem-Quine Thesis)]], [[Sources/Two Dogmas of Empiricism - W.V.O. Quine (1951)]], and [[Thinkers/W.V.O. Quine]]. Productive tension with Popper: holism does not make criticism impossible, but it makes the unit of criticism the web (or large sub-webs) rather than the single conjecture.

## Relevance to Vault

- **AI / machine learning — the foundational caveat**: this *is* the problem of generalization. Every model induces a hypothesis from finite past data and applies it to unseen cases on the unstated assumption that the world stays uniform (i.i.d., no distribution shift). Hume's circularity is the "no free lunch" intuition avant la lettre: there is no purely rational guarantee that past performance predicts future performance. Distribution shift, adversarial inputs, and overfitting are Humean "changes in the course of nature." The vault should treat induced patterns as *custom-grounded expectations*, not demonstrations — useful, indispensable, but not certain.
- **Evidence discipline**: pairs with [[Concepts/Of Miracles (Hume)]] ("proportion belief to evidence") — induction grounds the *baseline* expectations against which surprising testimony is weighed.
- **Skeptical cluster**: a sharper, narrower scepticism than Pyrrhonian suspension ([[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]]) — Hume isolates *exactly* where reason fails (the inductive step) rather than suspending on everything; and unlike Cartesian doubt ([[Concepts/Method of Hyperbolic Doubt (Descartes)]]) it finds *no* rational foundation to rebuild on, only a natural one.
- **Vs. rationalism**: the direct refutation of Spinoza's a priori intelligibility of nature ([[Concepts/Geometrical Method - Rationalism (Spinoza)]]) and of any Principle of Sufficient Reason that promises insight into why causes must produce their effects.
- **Method / humility**: models why the vault flags, dates, and sources claims rather than treating accumulated regularities as eternal truths — and why "mitigated scepticism" (modesty about projection) is the right maintainer temperament.

**Open questions**:
- Does the "sceptical solution" (custom) *answer* the problem or merely *describe* the belief reason cannot justify — leaving the normative question (is induction *reliable*?) open?
- Is there a non-circular vindication (pragmatic, probabilistic, evolutionary) of induction, or only Humean naturalization?
- Does Popper's methodological solution (falsifiability + corroboration) fully escape the problem, or does it relocate similar difficulties into the acceptance of basic statements and the assessment of "severity" of tests? (See [[Concepts/Basic Statements and the Empirical Basis (Popper)]] and [[Concepts/Corroboration (Popper)]].)
- How does the methodology interact with actual historical practice (Kuhnian paradigms, research programmes that resist falsification)?
