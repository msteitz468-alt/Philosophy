# History of Sexuality (Foucault)
> The "repressive hypothesis" — that modernity silenced sexuality — is false; modernity produced an unprecedented proliferation of sexual discourse, creating sexual identities ("the homosexual") from confessional technologies of power-knowledge.

## Summary

*La Volonté de savoir* (1976; English: *The History of Sexuality, Vol. 1: An Introduction*) opens by demolishing what Foucault calls the **repressive hypothesis**: the popular-radical view that Victorian society repressed sexuality, and that our liberation consists in speaking about sex. For Foucault, this narrative is itself an effect of power — and a particularly insidious one, because it recruits us as agents of our own normalization through confession.

The truth is the opposite: modernity produced a **proliferation** of discourses on sexuality — medical, psychiatric, pedagogical, demographic, legal. The effect was not silence but the production of *specific sexual identities* as objects of knowledge and intervention. The invention of "the homosexual" as a *type of person* (not merely a type of act) in the 19th century is Foucault's key example: once sodomy was a practice, now "the homosexual" is a species with a psychology, an etiology, a developmental history, and a social danger profile.

**Scientia sexualis vs. ars erotica:** Western modernity created a *scientia sexualis* — a science of sex that extracts truth from confession (medical, psychological, pastoral). This is contrasted with the *ars erotica* of other cultures (classical Rome, Japan, China, India) — traditions of erotic knowledge oriented not to truth-extraction but to the intensification of pleasure itself. Foucault aligned his own later ethics of the self with the ars erotica tradition.

The planned History of Sexuality was originally to be six volumes; Foucault radically rewrote it before his death. *L'Usage des plaisirs* and *Le Souci de soi* (both 1984) shifted from modern sexuality to Greco-Roman practices of self-governance. See [[Concepts/Ethics of the Self and Care of the Self (Foucault)]].

## Key Claims

- Liberation discourse — speaking about sex, revealing the truth of one's sexuality — is not liberation but the extension of confessional power-knowledge into everyday life.
- Sexual identity is a historical product, not a natural kind: "the homosexual" as a person-type was produced by 19th-century medicine, not discovered.
- Biopower invests sexuality because reproduction is the most direct interface between individual bodies and the management of populations.
- Foucault personally rejected "sexual liberation" as a framework, preferring the language of *pleasures* over *desire* and aligning with an aesthetics of existence rather than a politics of identity.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Foucault's indifference to feminism is glaring: neither *Surveiller et punir* nor *La Volonté de savoir* engages seriously with gender or the specific technologies of power applied to women's bodies. He was criticized for this by contemporaries.
- He dismissed early AIDS reports as "implausible" in the early 1980s — a tragic irony given his theoretical work on how medicine constructs pathological identities.

> [!warning] The repressive hypothesis demolition sits in tension with [[Thinkers/Karl Marx]]'s understanding of ideology as suppression of truth by ruling-class interests. For Foucault, power does not *hide* the truth of sexuality — it *produces* it.
