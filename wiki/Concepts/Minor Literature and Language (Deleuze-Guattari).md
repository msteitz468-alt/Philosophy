# Minor Literature and Language (Deleuze-Guattari)
> Minor literature is not the literature of a minor language but the revolutionary use of a major language by a minority — characterized by deterritorialization, immediate political charge, and collective enunciation.

## Summary

Drawing on Kafka (a Czech Jew writing in German, a deterritorialized major language), Deleuze and Guattari define minor literature by three characteristics:

1. **High deterritorialization coefficient:** The language is used in a way that causes it to stammer, vary, to undermine its own major constants. Kafka's German is already deterritorialized (Prague German, isolated from its territory) — and he pushes it further rather than reterritorializing it.
2. **Everything is political:** In a minor literature, individual intrigues connect immediately to political arrangements — the family triangle opens onto the commercial, bureaucratic, legal apparatus. There is no private affair.
3. **Everything has collective value:** Because there is no established major literature to absorb expression, the minor writer produces collective enunciation. "There isn't a subject; there are only collective assemblages of enunciation."

**Major vs. minor language** (Essay 17): The distinction is not one of size but of treatment. Major language extracts constants and invariants (phonological, syntactic, morphological) that serve as standard norms. Minor language operates by **continuous variation** — it never reaches a stable equilibrium. "You will never find a homogeneous system that is not still or already affected by a regulated, continuous, immanent process of variation."

**The minor as becoming-minoritarian:** A language becomes minor not by being spoken by few people, but by being put into continuous variation. Becoming-minoritarian is the universal condition of becoming as such — there is no becoming-major. Even the largest group can only become-minoritarian with respect to its own constant.

**One Manifesto Less** (Essay 25, on Carmelo Bene's theater): Minor theater operates by *subtraction* rather than representation — amputating elements of power (the king, the master) to release potentialities in minor characters (Mercutio, the slave). This non-representational theater is the theatrical equivalent of minor literature.

## Key Claims

- "There isn't a subject; there are only collective assemblages of enunciation, and literature expresses these acts insofar as they're not imposed from without and insofar as they exist only as diabolical powers to come or revolutionary forces to be constructed."
- "You will never find a homogeneous system that is not still or already affected by a regulated, continuous, immanent process of variation."
- Minor authors are "the untimely ones" — they do not interpret their time, they communicate across times through the middle, not through origin or end.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Wittgenstein]] (Language-Games, Forms of Life):** Wittgenstein also rejects a single homogeneous language in favor of multiple language-games embedded in forms of life. But Wittgenstein is descriptive (how language *is* used in practices); Deleuze/Guattari are political-constructive (how language *can be* made to stammer, vary, deterritorialize).
- **[[Concepts/Picture Theory of Language (Wittgenstein)]]:** The early Wittgenstein seeks a perfect logical form underlying language. Deleuze/Guattari's minor language is the anti-image: language as continuous variation resisting any constant form.
