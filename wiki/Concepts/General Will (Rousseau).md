# General Will (Rousseau)

> The collective interest and rational will of the sovereign citizenry, directed exclusively toward the common good and public interest.

## Summary

In *The Social Contract* (1762), [[Thinkers/Jean-Jacques Rousseau|Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] introduces the **General Will** (*volonté générale*) as the moral and legal foundation of a legitimate state. It is not the sum of private interests, but the shared interest of all citizens when they think and vote as public-spirited members of the community. 

For Rousseau, law is only legitimate when it expresses the General Will, and citizens are only truly free when they obey the laws they have collectively made. 

---

## Key Features

### 1. General Will vs. Will of All
Rousseau makes a crucial distinction between:
* **The General Will** (*volonté générale*): Focuses strictly on the common interest and public good. It is universal in its object and its essence.
* **The Will of All** (*volonté de tous*): The mere sum of individual, private wills and self-interests. It looks only to private interest and is only a sum of particular wills.

If citizens vote based on their private factions or personal gain, the result is the "will of all." The General Will is discovered when citizens vote in assembly, setting aside personal biases to consider what is best for the entire society.

### 2. Inalienable and Indivisible Sovereignty
Because the General Will is the collective will of the people, sovereignty belongs entirely to the citizenry as a body.
* **Inalienable**: Sovereignty cannot be transferred or represented. Rousseau famously rejected representative assemblies (like the British Parliament), stating that a people represented is no longer free.
* **Indivisible**: The sovereign body cannot be split into different branches of power. The legislative authority must remain unified in the hands of the citizens.

### 3. "Forced to Be Free"
Rousseau famously wrote:
> *"Whoever refuses to obey the general will shall be compelled to do so by the whole body. This means nothing less than that he will be forced to be free."*

To modern ears, this sounds authoritarian. For Rousseau, however, true freedom is **moral liberty**—obedience to the law one has prescribed for oneself. Since the citizen participated in forming the General Will, disobeying it is a contradiction of their own rational will. Forcing them to obey the law is restoring them to their own self-legislated freedom.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

> [!WARNING]
> The General Will stands in tension with liberal theories of individual autonomy, such as [[Thinkers/John Stuart Mill|John Stuart Mill]]'s [[Concepts/Harm Principle (Mill)|Harm Principle]], which warns against the "tyranny of the majority" and seeks to protect private liberty against collective coercion.

### Critiques
* **Totalitarian Potential**: Critics (like Isaiah Berlin) argue that by identifying freedom with obedience to a collective will, Rousseau's theory provides a dangerous justification for state totalitarianism and the suppression of dissent.
* **Practicality**: The General Will requires a small, culturally homogeneous state (like ancient Sparta or Geneva) where direct democracy is logistically feasible. In large, modern, diverse nation-states, finding a unified "General Will" is highly problematic.
