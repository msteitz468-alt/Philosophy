# Being-for-itself and Being-in-itself (pour-soi / en-soi) (Sartre)
> The two "regions of being" of *Being and Nothingness*: the inert *en-soi* that simply is what it is, and the conscious *pour-soi* that is *not* what it is — a hole in being around which existence whirls.

## Summary

In *L'être et le néant* (*Being and Nothingness*, 1943) Sartre names two ontological regions. The **être-en-soi** ("being-in-itself") is the mode of being of the stone, the tree, the object: massive, opaque, full positivity — it *is what it is*, with its attributes clinging to it as facts. The **être-pour-soi** ("being-for-itself") is the mode of being of consciousness: it is *nothing but* a relation to something, and so it *is not* — it has no positive ontological status or essence of its own. Borrowing Hegel's "for-itself," Sartre says only this "for" (as in "re-*pre*-sentation") makes self-consciousness possible; a consciousness that coincided with itself could not reflect at all. The pour-soi is therefore a *néant*, a *néantiser* (a "nihilating"): "I am a hole in being, a nothing, like the eye in the storm of the hurricane, which is itself nothing, but around which the whole of existence whirls with violence."

The distinction is **dialectical, not dualist**. Sartre is not reviving a body/mind or two-worlds doctrine. The en-soi is not a sphere *alongside* the pour-soi; the pour-soi is simply the *perspective from which* the en-soi appears as massive, as being-what-it-is. There is no point in speaking of the en-soi without the pour-soi. This is why phenomenology, for Sartre, *implies* an ontology — a vocabulary he takes from [[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]] even as he inverts its starting point: Sartre departs not from Being but from the *lack* of being.

The whole drama of the pour-soi is that, as a nothing, it is pure unrest and "desires to be" an en-soi — to coincide with itself, to have a fixed identity, to rest. But this is structurally impossible: were it to succeed, consciousness would cease. The human being is thus, in Hegel's terms, the **"unhappy consciousness,"** and in Sartre's, a **"useless passion" (*passion inutile*)** — the perpetual, doomed longing of a nothing to become something. This longing is the root of [[Concepts/Bad Faith (Mauvaise Foi) (Sartre)|bad faith]] and the engine of the analysis of [[Concepts/The Look and the Other (Sartre)|the Other]].

In the later *Critique of Dialectical Reason* the same structure recurs under new names: the pour-soi becomes the **"practical organism"** and the en-soi the **"practical-inert field"** — evidence, Welten argues, that early and late Sartre are one project. See [[Concepts/Totalization, Series and Group (Sartre)]].

## Key Claims / Positions
- **En-soi**: full, opaque positivity; *is what it is*; has an essence.
- **Pour-soi**: consciousness as nothing, pure relation/transcendence; *is not what it is*.
- The relation is dialectical (perspective), not a dualism of two substances.
- The pour-soi vainly desires to become an en-soi → "useless passion," "unhappy consciousness."
- Phenomenology implies ontology; Sartre starts from the *néant*, not (as Heidegger) from Being.

### How Sartre Differs From Prior Thinkers in the Wiki
- **vs [[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger]]**: shares ontological aim but begins from non-being, not Being.
- **vs [[Thinkers/Hegel]]**: adopts for-itself/unhappy consciousness and the dialectic (via Kojève) but without the consummating synthesis — the coincidence is permanently impossible.
- **vs [[Thinkers/Descartes]]**: against the *res cogitans* as a thinking thing; the pour-soi is precisely *not* a thing with properties.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- The pour-soi's craving to be an en-soi is, by Sartre's own account, never realisable — making the human condition structurally tragic; see [[Concepts/Bad Faith (Mauvaise Foi) (Sartre)]].
