# Correspondence Theory of Truth (Russell)

> One-sentence hook: Russell's realist theory that truth is the **correspondence of a belief to a fact** — "minds do not *create* truth or falsehood; what makes a belief true is a *fact*" — explicitly rejecting **coherence** as the *definition* of truth (while keeping it as a *test*), and standing as the vault's direct realist counter to the pragmatic theory of truth.

## Overview

Chapter XII of *The Problems of Philosophy* (1912). Russell asks the *preliminary* question — not "*which* beliefs are true?" but "*what do we mean* by truth and falsehood?" — and lays down three requisites any theory must meet:

1. **Truth must admit an opposite (falsehood).** Many theories "have constructed theories according to which all our thinking ought to have been true, and have then had the greatest difficulty in finding a place for falsehood."
2. **Truth and falsehood are properties of *beliefs* (and statements).** "A world of mere matter… would contain no truth or falsehood." (Facts exist without minds; *truths* require beliefs.)
3. **But truth depends on something *outside* the belief.** "If I believe that Charles I died on the scaffold, I believe truly… because of an historical event which happened two and a half centuries ago," not "because of any intrinsic quality of my belief."

The third requisite forces **correspondence**: "truth consists in some form of correspondence between belief and fact."

## Key Arguments

- **Rejection of coherence as the *definition* of truth.** Two objections: (1) "there is no reason to suppose that *only one* coherent body of beliefs is possible" — a novelist could invent a coherent false past; rival scientific/philosophical hypotheses can each fit all known facts. (2) Coherence "presupposes the truth of the laws of logic" (two propositions cohere only if both *can* be true — which requires the law of non-contradiction); so coherence can't *define* truth, since "the laws of logic supply the skeleton… within which the test of coherence applies." Verdict: "coherence cannot be accepted as giving the *meaning* of truth, **though it is often a most important *test*** of truth after a certain amount of truth has become known."
- **The multiple-relation theory of judgment** (Russell's 1912 mechanism, later abandoned): belief is *not* a two-term relation between a mind and a single object (else, like acquaintance, it could never be false — there is no object "Desdemona's love for Cassio" when she does not love him). Instead judgement is a *many-termed* relation knitting the mind together with several objects in an *order* (a "sense"/"direction"): "Othello believes that Desdemona loves Cassio" relates Othello, Desdemona, loving, Cassio. Belief is the "cement" uniting the constituents.
- **Truth = correspondence to an associated complex.** "A belief is *true* when there is a corresponding *fact*, and is *false* when there is no corresponding fact." If the object-terms, in the order the belief gives them, are actually united by the object-relation into a complex (e.g., the real "Desdemona's love for Cassio"), the belief is true; otherwise false.
- **Minds don't make truth.** "Minds do not *create* truth or falsehood. They create *beliefs*, but… cannot make them true or false" (except the special case of beliefs about one's own future actions). "What makes a belief true is a *fact*, and this fact does not… involve the mind of the person who has the belief." Hence beliefs "(a) depend on minds for their *existence*, (b) do **not** depend on minds for their *truth*."

## The realist line against pragmatism (the key cross-link)

Russell's correspondence theory is the vault's direct counter to [[Concepts/Pragmatic Theory of Truth (James)]] and [[Concepts/Humanism - Reality as Malleable (Schiller and James)]]. Where James says "truth *happens* to an idea… is *made* true by verification" and Schiller says "the world is malleable… man engenders truths upon it," Russell insists truth is fixed by mind-independent fact. He says so twice in *Problems*: ch. XII ("minds do not create truth") and the closing ch. XV, which rejects "the view which tells us that Man is the measure of all things, that **truth is man-made**… This view, if our previous discussions were correct, is untrue." In the 1945 *History* the same realism returns as the **"cosmic impiety"** warning (see [[Concepts/Philosophy of Logical Analysis (Russell)]]). So Russell's anti-pragmatism was lifelong and is now doubly sourced in the vault.

## Contrasts / Lineage

- **Vs. classical/medieval adequation** (Aristotle, [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]; [[Concepts/Knowledge as Perception of Agreement or Disagreement of Ideas (Locke)]]): Russell's correspondence is the modern analytic refinement of *adaequatio rei et intellectus* — truth as a relation of belief to fact, made precise with relations/order.
- **Vs. [[Concepts/Clear and Distinct Perception (Descartes)]]**: Descartes makes the *criterion* of truth internal (clarity/distinctness, God-guaranteed); Russell makes the *nature* of truth external (correspondence) and the *test* fallible (ch. XIII: self-evidence in degrees; knowledge ≠ mere true belief).
- **Vs. coherence/idealism** (Hegel, Bradley, the Absolute): Russell's two objections target exactly the idealist coherence theory the *History* also opposes.
- **Anticipates** the analytic truth debates (Tarski, deflationism) and the "justified true belief" / Gettier problem (ch. XIII already denies that true belief = knowledge).
