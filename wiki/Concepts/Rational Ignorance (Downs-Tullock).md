# Rational Ignorance (Downs-Tullock)
> Because one vote almost never determines an election, the rational citizen does not invest in political information — the expected return is too low to justify the cost.

## Summary

Rational ignorance is the public choice explanation for why voters are poorly informed about politics. Anthony Downs formalized the argument in *An Economic Theory of Democracy* (1957): acquiring political knowledge takes time and effort (costs), but the probability that any single vote determines an election outcome is negligibly small (near-zero benefit). The rational expected-value calculation yields: do not acquire political information.

Gordon Tullock named the phenomenon "rational ignorance." James Buchanan and the broader public choice tradition built on it. The concept became the cornerstone of Classical Public Choice explanations of democratic failure.

## The Logic

1. The probability that one vote is pivotal in a large election ≈ 0.
2. Therefore the expected benefit of holding correct political beliefs (since one's vote almost certainly won't change the outcome) ≈ 0.
3. Acquiring accurate political information takes time and effort (real costs).
4. Rational agents do not invest resources when expected return is negligible.
5. Conclusion: voters rationally remain ignorant.

Rational ignorance is not a criticism of voters' intelligence but a prediction about their incentives. A very smart person, faced with the same payoff structure, would also rationally remain uninformed.

## Relationship to the Miracle of Aggregation

Caplan notes a tension in the Classical Public Choice tradition: rational ignorance was meant to explain democratic failure, but it generates a paradox. If ignorant voters err *randomly*, the [[Concepts/Miracle of Aggregation]] kicks in and errors cancel out, making ignorance harmless for democratic outcomes. Classical Public Choice thus undermines its own critique of democracy.

This paradox dissolves only if voter errors are *systematic* rather than random — which Caplan argues is the case via [[Concepts/Systematic Economic Biases (Caplan)]]. But then the explanation is not ignorance but *irrationality*, and the correct framework is [[Concepts/Rational Irrationality (Caplan)]], not rational ignorance.

## Distinction from Rational Irrationality

| | Rational Ignorance | Rational Irrationality |
|---|---|---|
| Voter's problem | Cost of acquiring information > benefit | Demand for false but comforting beliefs |
| Epistemic state | Uninformed (blank) | Actively misinformed |
| Error type | Random (no directional bias) | Systematic (directionally biased) |
| Policy implication | Errors cancel out in aggregate | Errors amplify in aggregate |
| Remedy | Better voter information | Reduce scope of collective decisions |
