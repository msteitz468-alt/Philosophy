# Eternal Will and Temporal Creation (Ghazali)

> Al-Ghazali's positive answer (in the First Discussion of the *Tahafut*) to the philosophers' proofs for an eternal world: the world was created in time by an **eternal will** that, from eternity, decreed the world's existence *at the moment it began*. Will is precisely the power "to differentiate a thing from its similar" — so it can specify one moment over an indistinguishable other without requiring any *new* determinant arising in God. This is the constructive doctrine that the wider book ([[Concepts/Criterion of Demonstrability (Ghazali)|officially "only refuting"]]) nonetheless leans on, and the first of the three doctrines for which al-Ghazali condemns the philosophers as unbelievers.

## The Problem (the Philosophers' Strongest Proof)

The Avicennan argument al-Ghazali calls the strongest: a temporal effect cannot proceed from an absolutely eternal cause whose states never change. If God's will to create is eternal and unchanging, then either the world always existed (it never *began*) or it never exists; for the world to *begin* at a particular moment, something new — a "giver of preponderance" (*murajjih*) — must tip the balance, and that novelty would itself need an originator, regressing infinitely. Pointedly: **"Why was the world not created before its creation?"** No new will, instrument, purpose, or nature can arise in the Eternal to explain the chosen moment.

## Al-Ghazali's Counter-Doctrine

His core reply: "The world was temporally created by an eternal will that decreed its existence at the time in which it came to be; that [the preceding] nonexistence continued to the point at which [the world] began; that existence prior to this was not willed and for this reason did not occur." The eternal will does not *change* when the world begins; it eternally contains the specification of *that* moment.

The conceptual key is his definition of will: **"Will stands as an expression for an attribute whose function — nay, its essence — is to differentiate a thing from its similar."** Because "the relation of power to two contraries is the same," power alone cannot select one of two equal options; a distinct attribute — will — is exactly the faculty of selecting between indiscernibles *without a further reason*. So the demand for a *murajjih* begs the question: it assumes selection always needs an external determinant, which is precisely what will denies.

He blocks the auxiliary objections:
- **"Why not sooner?" (the equal-times argument).** Two moments before creation are perfectly alike, so (the philosophers say) there can be no reason to prefer one. Al-Ghazali: this presses a false analogy to *human* deliberation; God's will, like His knowledge, is unlike ours, and its whole nature is to specify one of two similars. He illustrates with the thirsty man choosing between two identical glasses, and two equally good dates — selection happens.
- **"How long did God refrain before creating?"** A pseudo-question: "According to us, duration and time are both created." There was no empty time in which God "waited."
- **The "before the world" intuition** (Proof 2, that time must precede the world) he diagnoses as an estimative illusion, exactly parallel to imagining empty space "beyond" the finite cosmos — the imagination manufactures a void the intellect has no warrant for.

## Post-Eternity (Second Discussion)

The world is created in time but **need not end**: "it is not a necessary requirement for that which is temporally originated that it should have an end, whereas it is a necessary requirement of the act to be temporally originated and have a beginning." Whether the world endures forever or is annihilated "is only known through the revealed law" — a creation that is asymmetric in time (a beginning without a necessary end).

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. the *falasifa* / [[Thinkers/Ibn Rushd]]:** Averroes' central rebuttal is that "eternal will" is an **equivocation** — it borrows the word "will" from voluntary temporal agents while denying everything that makes will *will* (deliberation, change, choice between live contraries). An eternal undetermined will that arbitrarily fixes a moment makes God neither wise nor good; the Aristotelian holds instead that an eternal cause has an eternal effect ([[Concepts/Eternity of the World (Ibn Rushd)]]).
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]:** Aquinas reaches a strikingly similar formula — "God willed from eternity that the world should be, but not that it should be from eternity" — preserving an eternal will with a temporally finite effect, though he grounds the beginning in revelation rather than claiming to demonstrate it.
- **vs. [[Thinkers/Augustine]]:** Augustine's *distentio animi* dissolves the "before creation" worry by making time itself a creature ([[Concepts/Time and Eternity - Distentio Animi (Augustine)]]) — the same move as al-Ghazali's "duration and time are both created," reached through interior phenomenology rather than the logic of will.
- **vs. Kant:** The dispute is a medieval instance of the **First Antinomy** (whether the world has a beginning in time), where Kant will argue *both* sides generate equally valid proofs — vindicating al-Ghazali's narrower claim that the philosophers never *demonstrated* eternity, while denying his positive thesis equal standing.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

> [!warning] Directly conflicts with the Aristotelian eternal-world thesis defended in [[Concepts/Eternity of the World (Ibn Rushd)]] — see [[Contradictions/Temporal Creation vs Eternal World]]

- Does "an attribute that differentiates without a reason" preserve God's wisdom, or does it make creation arbitrary (Averroes' charge)?
- Is the asymmetry — necessary beginning, contingent end — coherent, or does whatever licenses an endless future also license a beginningless past?
