# Foreknowledge and the Use of Spies (Sun Tzu)

> One-sentence hook: Sun Tzu's culminating doctrine (Chapter XIII) — what lets the wise commander "achieve things beyond the reach of ordinary men" is **foreknowledge**, which "cannot be elicited from spirits… nor obtained inductively from experience… but only from other men" — so intelligence (the five classes of spies) is the cheapest and most decisive investment in war.

## Overview

Chapter XIII ("The Use of Spies") is the climax of the treatise: all the prior doctrine (calculation, knowing the enemy, striking weakness, deception) *depends on accurate information about this specific adversary*, and that information has a single source — human intelligence.

> "What enables the wise sovereign and the good general to strike and conquer, and achieve things beyond the reach of ordinary men, is **foreknowledge**. Now this foreknowledge cannot be elicited from spirits; it cannot be obtained inductively from experience, nor by any deductive calculation. **Knowledge of the enemy's dispositions can only be obtained from other men.**"

The argument is partly *economic*: armies cost "a thousand ounces of silver a day" and campaigns last years, yet commanders who "begrudge the outlay of a hundred ounces of silver" for spies are guilty of "the height of inhumanity" — a tiny intelligence budget decides the vast one. "Spies are a most important element in war, because on them depends an army's ability to move."

## Key Arguments

- **Foreknowledge is irreducibly empirical-but-particular.** Strikingly, Sun Tzu *rules out* three sources: the supernatural ("spirits"), **induction from past experience**, and **deductive calculation** — leaving only direct human report about the present enemy. Strategic knowledge is of *particulars now*, not general laws.
- **The five classes of spies** ("the divine manipulation of the threads… the sovereign's most precious faculty"):
  1. **Local spies** — inhabitants of the enemy's district.
  2. **Inward spies** — the enemy's own officials, turned.
  3. **Converted spies** — the enemy's spies, detected and turned back ("getting hold of the enemy's spies and using them for our own purposes") — Sun Tzu's **most valuable** class, the hinge of the whole system (all other intelligence flows through knowing the enemy via his own turned agents).
  4. **Doomed spies** — fed false information and *meant* to be caught, so they carry the lie to the enemy (a deception delivery system).
  5. **Surviving spies** — those who return with news.
  When all five are at work and "none can discover the secret system," it is "the sovereign's most precious faculty."
- **Intelligence requires the best people and total discretion.** "None should be more liberally rewarded… more secret operations" — spies need "benevolence and straightforwardness" to manage, "subtle ingenuity of mind" to use; "if news of a secret is divulged by a spy before the time is ripe, he must be put to death."
- **Closes the loop with deception.** Foreknowledge (Ch. XIII) and [[Concepts/All Warfare Is Based on Deception (Sun Tzu)|deception]] (Ch. I) are mirror images: you *gather* true information about the enemy while *feeding* him false information (the doomed/converted spy). Both rest on managing the flow of belief.

## Contrasts / Lineage

- **The epistemology twist — vs. [[Concepts/Problem of Induction (Hume)]] and [[Concepts/Knowledge by Acquaintance and Knowledge by Description (Russell)]]**: Sun Tzu's claim that foreknowledge "cannot be obtained inductively from experience… [but] only from other men" is a remarkable convergence with the empiricists *and* a twist on them. Like Hume, he denies that general past regularities settle a particular future case; like [[Thinkers/Bertrand Russell|Russell]], he holds that knowledge of *this* particular requires something like *acquaintance* (here, human report) rather than deduction from universals. Intelligence is knowledge-by-description grounded in someone's acquaintance.
- **Vs. [[Concepts/Of Miracles (Hume)|Hume on testimony]]**: Sun Tzu *relies* on human testimony as the sole route to foreknowledge — but the converted/doomed spy is exactly why testimony must be *weighted and cross-checked* (the enemy is feeding you doomed spies too). Hume's "proportion belief to the evidence" and the problem of deceptive testimony are the defensive complement.
- **Vs. [[Concepts/Power, Empire, and Revolt (Thucydides)]]**: Thucydides repeatedly shows intelligence failures and disinformation (the Sicilian expedition launched on bad information); Sun Tzu prescribes the intelligence discipline whose absence Thucydides chronicles.

## Vault / AI / Personal Relevance

- **"Foreknowledge ≈ context-gathering before action"**: the strongest vault-meta reading — the cheap-but-decisive investment is *reconnaissance*. For the wiki: read the index, log, and related pages (the "intelligence") before ingesting/editing; for an agent: gather and verify context/tool-outputs before committing to a plan. "Begrudging the outlay" for intelligence ≈ skipping context-gathering to save effort, then losing the campaign.
- **AI/agentic & security**: the five spies map onto modern information operations — reconnaissance, insider sources, **counter-intelligence (converted spies)**, **disinformation (doomed spies)**, and exfiltration. The *converted spy as most valuable* ≈ turning an adversary's own signals to your advantage; the defensive mirror is **robustness to poisoned/deceptive inputs** (don't trust unverified "intelligence"; cross-check sources — cf. the vault's provenance + verify discipline).
- **"Only from other men" + verification**: a caution against over-trusting either pure induction (stale generalization) *or* a single human source (which may be a converted/doomed spy). Triangulate.
- **Career/competition**: market and competitive intelligence as a high-ROI, low-cost investment; relationships and field knowledge as "local/inward spies"; healthy skepticism toward planted information.
