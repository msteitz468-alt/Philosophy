# Aspect Seeing (Wittgenstein)
> When the duck-rabbit "switches," the picture on the page does not change, yet I see it differently — "seeing-as" is neither pure perception nor pure interpretation, but "the echo of a thought in sight."

## Summary

In Part II §xi of the *Philosophical Investigations* — its longest and most studied section — [[Thinkers/Ludwig Wittgenstein|Wittgenstein]] examines **aspect perception**, or "seeing-as." He distinguishes two uses of the word "see": seeing an object (I could draw a copy of what is before me), and seeing an *aspect* or likeness — as when "I contemplate a face, and then suddenly notice its likeness to another. I see that it has not changed; and yet I see it differently. I call this experience 'noticing an aspect.'" The puzzle is that nothing in the object alters, yet something genuinely perceptual seems to change.

The famous illustration is **the duck-rabbit** (borrowed from the psychologist Jastrow): a figure that can be seen as a duck's head or a rabbit's head. The "dawning" of the rabbit-aspect where one had seen only a duck is the central phenomenon. Wittgenstein's interest is not psychological-causal but conceptual: what is the place of this concept "among the concepts of experience"?

---

## Core Mechanics

### 1. Continuous seeing vs. the dawning of an aspect
"I must distinguish between the 'continuous *seeing*' of an aspect and the '*dawning*' of an aspect." We do not normally say "now I am seeing it as a rabbit" while steadily looking at a rabbit-picture; the "as" enters only where a switch is possible, where one *could* see it otherwise.

### 2. Half-perception, half-thought
"'Seeing as ...' is not part of perception. And for that reason it is like seeing and again not like." The dawning of an aspect "seems half visual experience, half thought" — Wittgenstein calls it "the echo of a thought in sight." What is perceived in the change "is not a property of the object, but an internal *relation* between it and other objects." Hence the report of an aspect-change "is the expression of a new perception and at the same time of the perception's being unchanged."

### 3. Aspect-seeing presupposes mastery of a technique
"The substratum of this experience is the mastery of a technique." One can see the duck-rabbit's two aspects only "if you are already conversant with the shapes of those two animals." Aspect-perception is therefore *internally tied to concepts and skills* — it is not a brute given of the senses but is shaped by what one has learned. This links it to [[Concepts/Forms of Life (Wittgenstein)|forms of life]] and to the rejection of a pure, concept-free sensory given.

### 4. Aspect-blindness
Wittgenstein entertains the fiction of an "aspect-blind" person, unable to "see something *as* something" — who would have "an altogether different relationship to pictures," comparable to lacking "a 'musical ear.'" This connects to "experiencing the meaning of a word" (the way "Wednesday" can feel "fat" and "Tuesday" "lean") — a secondary, aspect-like dimension of language and perception.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **vs. empiricist sense-data:** If even simple seeing is shot through with concept and technique, there is no neutral, theory-free given to serve as the foundation of empirical knowledge. This converges with [[Concepts/Paradigm (Kuhn)|Kuhn]]'s "theory-ladenness of observation" and the Gestalt psychologists' figure/ground switches.
- **Continuous with the [[Concepts/Private Language Argument (Wittgenstein)|inner/outer]] theme:** the criteria for "now I see it as a rabbit" are again partly behavioural and expressive, not the report of a private inner picture. "An 'inner process' stands in need of outward criteria."
- **For aesthetics and AI/perception:** aspect-seeing is a touchstone for theories of interpretation in art and for debates over whether machine "perception" (pattern recognition) involves anything like seeing-*as*. Compare [[Thinkers/Martin Heidegger|Heidegger]]'s account of the world disclosing itself under an interpretive "as."
- **Epistemic logic and deduction**: Arif Ahmed (2010) extends aspect-seeing to the philosophy of logic, arguing that recognizing the validity of a novel deductive rule (N3-rule) is a perceptual achievement of aspect-dawning, where one suddenly sees a concrete, visible sentence as an instance of a valid inference schema.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- Is aspect-dawning a genuinely *perceptual* state, a judgment, or a third thing? Wittgenstein deliberately resists classifying it, which later philosophers of perception have found either profound or evasive.
