# Tabula Rasa - Blank Slate or Empty Cabinet (Locke)

> The mind at birth is like "white paper, void of all characters" or an "empty cabinet" — without any innate ideas, principles, or characters stamped upon it by nature or God. All ideas, all materials of knowledge and belief, are acquired subsequently through experience: sensation (from external objects via the senses) and reflection (the mind's own operations upon its ideas).

## Core Statement from the *Essay*

In the Introduction Locke describes the mind's initial state in the course of outlining his historical plain method: the senses at first let in "PARTICULAR ideas, and furnish the yet empty cabinet."

The full doctrine is developed negatively in Book I (no innate ideas or principles are present from the beginning) and positively in Book II (the actual origin and composition of ideas from sensation and reflection). The phrase "tabula rasa" (scraped tablet / blank slate) is the later Latin shorthand for the view Locke articulates with the English metaphors of white paper and empty cabinet.

## Key Supporting Arguments (Book I, this ingest chunk)

- Children and "idiots" give no evidence of any pre-existing stock of ideas or maxims. They acquire particular ideas of sensible qualities (sweet, bitter, white, black, etc.) through repeated experience long before they possess the general abstract ideas presupposed by supposedly innate speculative or practical principles.
- If ideas or principles were innate/imprinted, they would have to be perceived or known from the first; "imprinting" without the mind's taking notice of it is unintelligible.
- The component ideas of the candidate innate maxims (identity, impossibility, whole/part, God, worship, justice) are themselves late-acquired, often obscure even in adults, and absent or variable across cultures and developmental stages.
- The positive route to all our ideas is experience. Once particular ideas are in the cabinet, the mind can retain them, abstract, compare, compound, and reflect on its own operations — thereby generating the entire stock of human knowledge and belief without any need for original inscriptions.

## Contrasts and Tensions in the Vault

- **Vs. Descartes**: The innate "idea of God" as a trademark or objective reality requiring a cause at least as great as itself is the direct target. Clear and distinct perception as an internal criterion independent of sensory experience is likewise rejected in favor of ideas whose origin and content can be traced to sensation or reflection.
- **Vs. Plato and recollection/Forms**: No pre-natal or innate knowledge to be "recollected"; all content enters through the senses or the mind's reflection on what the senses have furnished.
- **Vs. Aristotelian essentialism / innate principles of demonstration**: While Locke retains substance-language and speaks of real essences in places, the route to general ideas and maxims is empirical abstraction and observation, not direct intellectual grasp of forms or essences independent of experience.
- **Predecessor to Hume**: Hume's Copy Principle (every simple idea is a copy of a prior simple impression) is the sharpened, more sceptical version of Locke's claim that the materials of thought come from experience. Hume accepts the blank-slate starting point and the experiential origin but denies that we ever receive impressions of power, necessary connexion, or an enduring self — moving Locke’s empiricism to its limit.
- **Lucretius / Epicurean atomism**: Both emphasize that secondary qualities (color, taste, etc.) are not in the atoms/things themselves but arise from arrangements and interactions with perceivers. Locke’s primary/secondary distinction (Book II, future chunk) is a later, more systematic version of a similar insight.
