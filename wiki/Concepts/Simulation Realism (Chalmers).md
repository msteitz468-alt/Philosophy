# Simulation Realism (Chalmers)
> "If we're in a simulation, the objects around us are real and not an illusion" — the load-bearing thesis of *Reality+*, argued via the equivalence of the simulation hypothesis with it-from-bit creation.

## Summary

The centerpiece of *Reality+* (chs. 6 and 9). Against the "standard line" (Ready Player One's "Reality is the only thing that's real") and David Deutsch's partial realism (VR "kicks back" but "the simulated aircraft and its surroundings do not really exist"), Chalmers argues that if we are in a simulation, most of our ordinary beliefs remain true: there are real trees, real cars, a real New York — they are digital objects, "structures of binary information, or bits."

**The reality checklist** — five criteria of realness (ch. 6):
1. **Existence**
2. **Causal power** — the Eleatic dictum from Plato's *Sophist*: "everything which possesses any power of any kind… has real being"
3. **Mind-independence** — Philip K. Dick's dictum: "Reality is that which, when you stop believing in it, doesn't go away"
4. **Non-illusoriness** — things are roughly as we believe them to be
5. **Genuineness** — Austin's "is it a real X?"

In a perfect, permanent simulation, objects satisfy **all five**. Excluded criteria (fundamentality, originality) are marginal: Dolly the cloned lamb is neither "but she is perfectly real." Being digital is like being quantum mechanical — a discovery about underlying nature, not a debunking.

**The master argument** (ch. 9): the simulation hypothesis is *equivalent* to the **it-from-bit creation hypothesis** ("Both hypotheses describe a being that creates a reality by setting bits in motion"); if it-from-bit creation is true, most ordinary beliefs are true; therefore simulation realism. The crucial step is **structuralism about physics**: "Photons are whatever play the photon role" — if digital entities play that role, "simulated photons have been genuine photons all along." Semantics cooperates (ch. 20): Kripke/Putnam externalism anchors a lifelong sim's "hurricane" to virtual hurricanes — Dennett's slogan "A simulated hurricane doesn't make you wet" is "at best half-true"; simulations are "bigger on the inside." But "ultimately, it's structuralism, rather than externalism, that drives the simulation realism that drives my response to skepticism."

## Predecessors of the No-Illusion View

Chalmers finds only three, each resting on a doctrine he rejects: Bouwsma 1949 (via verificationism), Zhai 1998 (via idealism), Putnam 1981 (via strong externalism). His structuralist route is new.

## How This Relates to Prior Thinkers

- **[[Thinkers/George Berkeley]]**: *esse is percipi* is "too strong, but a good heuristic"; it-from-bit-from-consciousness (Berkeley-style) remains "on the table."
- **[[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]]**: the hurricane slogan disputed; but Dennett's "Where Am I?" is adopted for the hard two-brain scenarios.
- **[[Thinkers/Descartes]]**: simulation realism converts his skeptical scenario into a metaphysical one — see [[Arguments/Cartesian Skepticism and the Simulation Riposte (Chalmers)]].
- **[[Concepts/Simulation Hypothesis (Virk)]]**: Virk hunts for evidence that reality is computational ([[Concepts/Evidence of Computation (Virk)]]); Chalmers argues that even if it is, nothing ordinary is lost. Same hypothesis, opposite existential valence.
- **[[Concepts/Controlled Hallucination (Clark)]]**: Clark's predictive-processing "controlled hallucination" raises a realism question this page answers structurally — perception tracks real (possibly digital) structure.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] Consciousness resists structuralization (Mary's room, ch. 22) — the one place Chalmers's structuralism stops, against [[Thinkers/Daniel Dennett]] and [[Concepts/Predictive Processing and the Hard Problem (Clark)]] deflationism.
