# Essence and Definition (Aristotle)

> *To ti ēn einai* — literally "the what-it-was-to-be," Aristotle's term for the essence: what a thing is *in virtue of itself* (*kath' hauto*), captured by its real definition. Essence belongs primarily to substance, and a primary substance is *identical* with its essence. *Metaphysics* VII (Zeta).

## Summary

The essence of a thing is "what it is said to be *propter se* (in virtue of itself)" — not what it is accidentally. Being pale is no part of your essence (you are pale only as a musician is musical, accidentally); being a certain kind of living thing is. A **definition** (*horismos*) is the formula (*logos*) that states the essence, and Aristotle imposes a strict condition: the formula must express the thing *without naming the thing itself* — it gives the meaning while the term is absent. Definition in the strict sense belongs **primarily to substance**; other categories have essence and definition only in a secondary, dependent way (just as they are beings only by reference to substance, [[Concepts/Being qua Being (Aristotle)]]).

The pivotal thesis: **each primary thing is one and the same as its essence.** "Each thing and its essence are one and the same in no merely accidental way." For self-subsistent, primary things, the identity must hold — otherwise an infinite regress opens (the essence of the essence of the essence…) and knowledge becomes impossible, since "knowledge is of the essence." "The good must be one with the essence of good, the beautiful with the essence of beauty." This identity-of-thing-and-essence is, at the same time, Aristotle's deepest weapon against [[Concepts/Critique of Platonic Forms (Aristotle)]]: if the Form *Good* were a separate thing distinct from the essence of good, it would be unknowable and superfluous.

Essence is thus the **formal cause** ([[Concepts/The Four Causes (Aristotle)]]) and the primary sense of substance ([[Concepts/Substance (Ousia) (Aristotle)]]): it is the *form* that makes a composite the kind of thing it is. Because productions and demonstrations alike "start from the what-it-is," essence is the principle of both being and knowing.

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Essence = the *kath' hauto* "what it is,"** stated by a non-circular real definition.
- **Definition belongs primarily to substance**; accidents have only derivative essence.
- **A thing is identical with its essence** (for primary substances) — denial yields regress and destroys knowledge.
- Essence is **form**, the formal cause, and is what is grasped in scientific *apodeixis* (cf. [[Sources/Aristotle - Modal Logic (Organon, Patterson)]]: *per se* / essential predication grounds demonstration).
- The essence (form) is ungenerated and imperishable (VIII); only composites come to be.

## How This Sits Among Thinkers

- **Versus [[Thinkers/Plato]]**: shares Socrates' search for universal definitions of the virtues, but — per Book I and Zeta — refuses to "make the universals or the definitions exist apart." Aristotle keeps the definable essence *immanent* in the individual.
- **Versus [[Thinkers/Locke]]**: [[Concepts/Nominal and Real Essences (Locke)]] is the empiricist response — we can know only the *nominal* essence (the abstract idea/name); the Aristotelian *real essence* (inner constitution) is hidden from us. This deflates the confidence of Aristotelian definition.
- **[[Thinkers/Spinoza]]**: definition by *genus* and essence is geometrized — to define a thing is to give its cause ([[Concepts/Geometrical Method - Rationalism (Spinoza)]]).
- **Modal logic**: the essence/accident split is the metaphysical ground of Aristotle's modal copula and the strong (essential) reading of necessity — see [[Concepts/Modal Copula (Aristotelian Modality)]].
