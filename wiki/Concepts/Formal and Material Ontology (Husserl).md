# Formal and Material Ontology (Husserl)
> Husserl's two-tier ontology: formal ontology studies object-structures applicable to any domain whatsoever; material ontology studies the specific essences of the three great regions — Nature, Consciousness, and Culture.

## Summary

Husserl introduces a fundamental distinction between **formal** and **material** ontology that Smith describes as "arguably new in the history of metaphysics." Where [[Thinkers/Aristotle]] gave us object-categories (substance, quantity, quality…) that were simultaneously formal and material, and where [[Thinkers/Immanuel Kant]] gave us concept-categories (for synthesising sensory intuition), Husserl separates the two levels: the purely formal logical structures that apply to *any* domain, and the substantive essences that apply to specific *regions* of being.

## Formal Ontology

**Formal ontology** studies the most abstract structures of objects *qua* objects — forms that apply universally regardless of what kind of thing the object is. Husserl's table of formal categories (*Ideas I*, §§10–11):

| Category | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Individual / Substrate** ("This-There", Tode Ti) | The ultimate bearer of essences; the concrete individual |
| **Species / Property / Relation** | Universals; instantiated in individuals via moments |
| **State of Affairs (Sachverhalt)** | The objective correlate of a proposition |
| **Number, Set, Group** | Mathematical structures |
| **Whole, Part** | Mereological structures |
| **Piece** | Independent part; can exist apart from the whole |
| **Moment** | Dependent part; cannot exist apart from the whole (e.g., this-whiteness-in-this-vase) |
| **Dependence / Foundation (Fundierung)** | A depends on B iff, by laws of essence, A cannot exist without B |
| **Manifold (Mannigfaltigkeit)** | Form of a field of objects; objective correlate of a theory's form |
| **Meaning / Noema** | Ideal intentional content; represents rather than instantiates |
| **Necessity, Possibility** | Formal modal structures |

**Formal necessities** are analytic: "Every whole has parts," "If A depends on B and B on C, then A depends on C."

Formal ontology correlates with **formal logic**: meaning-categories (Concept, Proposition, Connective) correspond to object-categories (Individual, State of Affairs, etc.). Semantic rules articulate the correlation. This prefigures modern model theory (Tarski, Carnap).

## Material Ontology

**Material ontology** studies specific substantive regions and their essences. The three **regions** — highest-level material genera:

1. **Nature (Natur)**: Spatiotemporal, causal. Physical things extended in space-time, subject to causal laws. The region studied by natural science.
2. **Consciousness (Bewusstsein)**: Intentional, lived experience. Not spatially extended; essentially characterised by directedness toward objects. The region studied by phenomenology.
3. **Culture / Geist**: Persons, norms, institutions, meanings — social and cultural entities. Constituted through persons in their practical and normative relations.

**Material necessities** are synthetic a priori: "Every bird has wings," "Every colour is extended," "Every sound has a pitch." These hold of the essences of particular kinds of things, not by formal logic alone.

**Generalization vs. formalization**: Two distinct modes of abstraction ascending from an individual:
- *Generalization*: Ascends within material genera (this eucalyptus → Eucalyptus → Tree → Plant → Living thing…).
- *Formalization*: Grasps formal structure (this eucalyptus → Individual, or → Physical thing-with-properties → Object-in-general).

These are independent operations and must not be conflated (a common source of error in traditional metaphysics).

## Mereology: Pieces and Moments

One of Husserl's most original contributions is his **mereology** (theory of parts and wholes, from *Logical Investigations*, Third Investigation).

**Piece (Stück)**: An independent part — can exist severed from the whole (a table leg; a branch cut from a tree).

**Moment (unselbständiges Teil)**: A dependent part — cannot exist apart from its whole (this-whiteness in this vase; this-shape in this body). Moments are the ontological basis for Husserl's account of universals:

> The ideal species *White* is not directly "in" the vase. This-whiteness-in-the-vase is a moment (dependent part) of the vase; that moment is a concrete instance of the ideal species *White*.

This synthesises Platonic ideal species (universals are real) with Aristotelian inherence (universals are in individuals): the species is real and atemporal; its *instance* (the moment) is concrete and temporal.

**Dependence / Fundierung**: The formal ontological relation: A is founded on (depends on) B iff, by laws of essence, A cannot exist unless B exists. This may be:
- *One-way*: A depends on B but not vice versa.
- *Mutual*: A depends on B and B depends on A (e.g., colour and extension are mutually dependent moments of a coloured thing).

## Mind-Body and the Regions

"I" am one individual substrate (*todeti*) whose moments fall under distinct regions:
- A **Consciousness-moment**: The intentional life, accessible from the inside through phenomenology.
- A **Nature-moment**: The body/brain, accessible from the outside through natural science.
- A **Culture-moment**: The person, accessible through empathy and cultural understanding.

These moments are *mutually dependent* — the Consciousness-moment depends on the Nature-moment (no experience without a brain) and also on the Culture-moment (no intentional content without a linguistic community). Yet their regional essences are irreducibly distinct: consciousness is not *identical* with neural activity any more than a melody is identical with the sound-waves that carry it.

This is Smith's proposed response to the contemporary "hard problem of consciousness": formal ontology (Dependence, Part, Moment) articulates the structure of the correlation without requiring identity. See [[Contradictions/Husserl vs Naturalism — Phenomenal Consciousness and the Hard Problem]].

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/Aristotle]]**: Husserl's formal ontology begins with Aristotle's ten categories (substance, quantity, quality, relation…) as the historical starting point; Husserl's formal/material distinction and his mereological moments extend Aristotle's particularised qualities while separating formal and material levels Aristotle conflated.
- **[[Thinkers/Immanuel Kant]]**: Kant's categories are conceptual (functions of synthesis applied to a sensory manifold), not formal-ontological (structures of objects themselves). Husserl's formal ontology is not epistemological but ontological — it describes how objects are, not merely how we cognise them.
- **[[Thinkers/Jean-Paul Sartre]]**: Sartre's pour-soi/en-soi distinction maps roughly onto Husserl's Consciousness/Nature regions, but Sartre gives it an existential-ontological reading (consciousness is a néant, a nothing). See [[Concepts/Being-for-itself and Being-in-itself (Sartre)]].

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] The status of Meanings/Noemata as a formal category is contested. In the *Logical Investigations*, meanings are classified as species of acts. By *Ideas I*, they constitute their own category (noema). Husserl provides no explicit argument for this change, and the two positions may be inconsistent.
- > [!warning] The boundary between "formal" and "material" ontology is not perfectly sharp: does Dependence belong to formal ontology (as Husserl claims) or does it require material content to be specified (as critics argue)?
