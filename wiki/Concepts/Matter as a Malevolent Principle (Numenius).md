# Matter as a Malevolent Principle (Numenius)

> [[Thinkers/Numenius of Apamea]]'s dualist claim that matter is not neutral or a mere privation but a *positive, uncreated, evil* principle — an "Indefinite Dyad" coeval with God — the doctrine [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] would explicitly reject.

## The Doctrine

Numenius sides with (his) Pythagoras against the Stoics on the nature of matter:

- **Matter is evil, not "indifferent."** The Stoics called matter neutral, "neither good nor evil." Numenius insists it is "plainly noxious" — "the divinity is the principle and cause of the Good, while matter is that of evil; and Plato thinks likewise" (fr. 15). What is *indifferent* is only the mixed product (the world), which derives from both Idea and Matter.
- **Matter is the uncreated Indefinite Dyad.** Pythagoras "applied the name of Unity to the divinity, but to matter the name of Doubleness" (fr. 14) — an indeterminate, unlimited dyad. Because it is indeterminate it is *ungenerated*, "coeval with the divinity by which it was organized." Matter is "a swift-flowing stream of infinite depth, breadth and length," incapable of stability (fr. 11).
- **Two world-souls.** Numenius praises Plato for teaching "two world-souls — one very beneficent, the other malevolent, namely matter"; matter, being in motion, must be alive and animated, and it "opposes Providence, plotting to attack its decisions by the power of its maliciousness" (fr. 16–17). The world arises from the commingling of God and matter, Providence and Necessity/chance.
- **Evil is necessary.** Because Providence entails matter, and matter is inherently bad, "evils must exist necessarily" — Numenius praises Heraclitus for faulting Homer's wish to abolish all evil, since to exterminate matter would be to destroy the world (fr. 16). God orders matter "without entirely eliminating its faults, lest material Nature should wholly perish" (fr. 18).
- **Descent into matter is a fall.** Souls are drawn down toward "wetness"/generation by desire; "all incarnations are of evil" (fr. 35a, 50); embodiment is imprisonment.

## The Fork in Neoplatonism

> [!warning] Conflicts with [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] and [[Concepts/The One (To Hen)]]
> This is the sharpest doctrinal break between Numenius and the Plotinian mainstream. **Plotinus** makes matter the *last, formless residue* of the One's overflow — evil as **privation** (lack of form/unity), not a positive rival power — and explicitly attacks the Gnostic view of a malevolent material principle or evil creator. Numenius's matter-as-absolute-evil, coeval with God, is a **dualism** the monist emanation scheme was built partly to avoid. The two positions define the split between *dualist* and *monist* Neoplatonism.

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Numenius / Kronius:** evil is added to the soul "from outside, from Matter" (fr. 49b); matter is a genuine, animate, hostile second principle.
- **Stoics:** matter is corporeal and *neutral*; evil comes from "perversity of germs" — a view Numenius says cannot explain the origin of that perversity given only God + neutral matter.
- **[[Thinkers/Plotinus]]:** monist — one source (the Good/One); matter is privation, the vanishing lower limit of emanation; evil is non-being, not a power.
- **Gnostics:** a malevolent Demiurge and evil cosmos — a strain Numenius's dualism shares and Plotinus polemicized against.
- Contrast [[Thinkers/Plato]]'s *Timaeus*, where the Receptacle/matter is disorderly but not straightforwardly *evil*, and the Demiurge is good.
