# Pythagorean Society and Politics
> The political dimension of early Pythagoreanism: how a charismatic teacher came to dominate Croton and Magna Graecia, what kind of association the brotherhood actually was, and how it fell. The strand that most resists the "mystic sage" reading of [[Thinkers/Pythagoras]].

## Summary
On Catherine Rowett's chapter in [[Sources/A History of Pythagoreanism - Carl A. Huffman (ed.) (2014)]], Pythagoras' political role belongs entirely to his **Croton** period (after leaving Samos c. age 40 under Polycrates' tyranny). His influence was *charismatic and persuasive, not a coup*: the earliest tradition (Dicaearchus via Porphyry) shows him addressing the council of elders, then in turn the youths, the children, and the women — each in a different sanctuary. The picture is of "a creeping enthusiasm for certain ideas" spreading upward and across the Greek cities of southern Italy through networks of *philia*, not a seizure of power. This complements Zhmud's reframing (see [[Schools/Pythagoreanism]]) of the group as a **political *hetairia*** rather than a religious sect.

## What Kind of Association?
Rowett challenges the standard view (Minar) that Pythagoreanism was a reactionary *aristocratic* faction:
- Members reportedly **owned no private property** — "for friends everything is *koinon* (common)" — which precludes a property-based aristocracy and pushes the comparison instead toward Plato's *Republic* and the myth of the metals.
- The early tradition presents only **exoteric "polis religion"** teaching (virtue, education, purity) — "nothing about life after death, reincarnation, abstinence or magic words." The esoteric brotherhood (the *acusmata*, [[Concepts/Metempsychosis (Transmigration of Souls)|metempsychosis]]) is a *distinct* layer, kept separate from the public political role.
- Aristoxenus stressed that Pythagoras "revered the business relating to numbers most of all... by withdrawing it from the usage of merchants" — fusing intellectual and civic reform.

Women received a sacralized life-course (*korē* → *nymphē* → *mētēr* → *maia*); Theano, Damo, and Bitale are named. Theories tying Pythagoras to Croton's **incuse coinage** or a monetary union (Seltman, Seaford) are judged "unlikely."

## Sybaris and Moral Authority
Pythagoras' role in Croton's war with **Sybaris** (510 BC) is read by Rowett morally rather than militarily — defending suppliants at Hera's altar — casting him as "Croton's moral conscience rather than its political leader." An alternative chronology even has him arriving *after* Sybaris' fall to curb the victors' decadence.

## The Fall: Cylon and the Fire
The society's collapse runs through the **Cylonian conspiracy** and the burning of **Milo's house**, where the Pythagoreans met. The sources conflate sixth- and fifth-century events; Rowett floats Cylon as a power-seeking *infiltrator* rejected by the group. Crucially, **[[Thinkers/Archytas]]** later served as elected general under *democratic* Tarentum — which undercuts the thesis that Pythagoreanism was inherently anti-democratic. Survivors (Philolaus, Lysis) fled to mainland Greece (Thebes, Phlius); see [[Schools/Pythagoreanism]] for the full political arc on Aristoxenus' catalogue.
