# Strong AI and Computational Sufficiency (Chalmers)
> Implementing the right computation suffices — with natural necessity — for conscious experience; a dualist's defense of machine consciousness against Searle, Dreyfus, and Penrose.

## Summary

*The Conscious Mind* ch. 9. Chalmers, despite his dualism, defends what Searle calls strong AI: "there is a nonempty class of computations such that the implementation of any computation in that class is sufficient for a mind." Nonreduction doesn't undercut AI: which systems *give rise to* consciousness is separate from whether it reduces — "there is no clear reason why computers should be any worse off than brains."

**Implementation**, against Searle's "observer-relative" triviality charge (his wall implementing WordStar) and Putnam-style worries: a system implements a **combinatorial-state automaton (CSA)** when its causal state-transition structure reliably mirrors the CSA's formal transitions, substates mapped to separate regions — "a perfectly objective criterion." Implementing the right CSA fixes fine-grained functional organization, and by [[Concepts/Organizational Invariance and the Qualia Arguments (Chalmers)]], organization fixes experience.

**Chinese Room reply**: build a continuum from a Chinese speaker's brain to the room via neuron-replacing demons consolidated to one demon and billions of slips. Organization is preserved; fading/dancing qualia show experience persists. The slips "constitute a concrete dynamical system with a causal organization that corresponds directly to the organization of the original brain"; the demon is "a kind of causal facilitator." Searle's syntax/semantics syllogism ignores implementation's "causal heft": recipes are syntactic, yet implementing one yields a cake. **Simulation vs replication**: "A simulation of X is an X precisely when the property of being an X is an organizational invariant" — hurricanes no, minds yes.

**Gödelian objections** (Lucas, Penrose): "there is no reason to believe that humans can see the truth of the relevant Gödel sentences, either." Continuity/uncomputability worries fail because biological noise means "a close approximation is as good as the real thing." Conclusion: "the outlook for machine consciousness is good in principle, if not yet in practice."

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **[[Thinkers/John Searle]]** ([[Concepts/Chinese Room Argument (Searle)]]): the principal opponent; Chalmers's is the vault's most developed reply, distinct from Hofstadter's complexity rejoinder ([[Contradictions/Hofstadter vs Searle - Strange Loops vs Chinese Room]]) — it concedes the hard problem and still defends machine minds.
- **[[Thinkers/Alan Turing]]** ([[Concepts/Imitation Game (Turing)]]): Chalmers supplies what Turing's behavioral criterion lacked — an argument that the right *internal organization*, not just behavior, guarantees experience; Block's look-up table passes behaviorally but not organizationally.
- **Penrose** and the [[Concepts/Argument from Consciousness (Jefferson)]] lineage: both rejected; consciousness is a real problem but not a computational limit.
- Grounds [[Concepts/Machine Consciousness and Mind Uploading (Chalmers)]] and, downstream, the simulation-realism arguments of *Reality+*.
