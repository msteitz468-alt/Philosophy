# Potentiality and Actuality (Aristotle)

> *Dynamis* (potentiality/capacity) and *energeia*/*entelecheia* (actuality/being-at-work). The pair that lets Aristotle explain change, unify form and matter, and argue to a divine pure actuality. Its master-thesis: **actuality is prior to potentiality** — in formula, in time, and in substance. *Metaphysics* IX (Theta).

## Summary

A thing can *be* in two ways: **potentially** (the bronze that could become a statue, the person asleep who can see, the child who can become a builder) and **actually** (the finished statue, the one actively seeing, the builder building). Potentiality (*dynamis*) is "a source of change in another thing or in the thing itself qua other." Aristotle distinguishes **active** potency (in the agent — the art of building) from **passive** potency (in the patient — the buildable), and **rational** potencies (arts and sciences, which can produce *contraries* — medicine knows both health and disease) from **non-rational** ones (fire only heats).

Actuality resists strict definition and is given by **analogy**: "as that which is building is to that which can build, as the waking to the sleeping, as that which sees to that which has shut eyes but has sight, as the shaped to the unshaped." Within actuality Aristotle marks a crucial distinction between **complete actualities (*energeiai*)** — seeing, thinking, living well, which are complete at every moment ("we are seeing and have seen at the same time") — and **incomplete processes (*kinēseis*)** — learning, building, walking, which aim at an end outside themselves ("it is not the case that one has walked at the same time as one is walking"). The word *entelecheia* (holding-the-end-within) marks the former.

**The central thesis — actuality is prior to potentiality** — in three ways:

1. **In formula/account**: to define a potential ("can build") you must refer to the actuality ("building"). The *logos* of the potency presupposes the actuality.
2. **In time**: although *this particular* seed precedes *this* plant, the actual is prior because "from the potential the actual is always produced by something already actual" — man from man, the teacher actually knowing before the learner. "There is always a first mover, and it exists actually."
3. **In substance**: actuality is the *end* (*telos*) and *form* for whose sake the potency exists — "animals do not see in order that they may have sight, but they have sight that they may see." The form/end is intrinsically prior to the matter/means ([[Concepts/Form and Matter - Hylomorphism (Aristotle)]]).

Finally, **eternal things are pure actuality**: whatever is merely potential "is capable also of not being," hence perishable; so the imperishable and eternal contain no potentiality. And actuality is **better** than potency, since a potency for contraries can issue in the bad, while the good is one determinate actuality — "there is no evil in eternal things." This is the bridge to Book XII: the eternal cosmos requires an eternal, purely actual mover — the [[Concepts/The Unmoved Mover (Aristotle)]].

## Key Claims / Positions

- **Two modes of being**: potential and actual; change is the actualization of a potentiality *qua* potential (the Physics definition restated in XI).
- **Energeia vs. kinēsis**: complete activities (their end is internal) vs. incomplete processes (their end is external).
- **Actuality prior to potentiality** in formula, time, and substance.
- **Eternal = purely actual**; potentiality entails perishability and the possibility of evil.
- Resolves the [[Concepts/Form and Matter - Hylomorphism (Aristotle)]] unity problem (matter = potency, form = act) and answers the flux objection to [[Concepts/Principle of Non-Contradiction (Aristotle)]] (a thing is potentially two contraries, actually only one).

## How This Sits Among Thinkers

- **Versus [[Thinkers/Parmenides]] and the Eleatics**: the act/potency distinction is Aristotle's solution to the Parmenidean denial of change ([[Concepts/Parmenidean Being and the One (Parmenides)]], [[Concepts/Zeno_s Paradoxes of Motion (Zeno)]]). Becoming is not from sheer non-being but from *potential* being — so coming-to-be is intelligible without contradiction.
- **Versus [[Thinkers/Heraclitus]]**: stabilizes flux — there is a *something* that persists (matter/form) through change.
- **Downstream — [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]**: act/potency is *the* organizing distinction of Thomist metaphysics; the priority of actuality drives the argument from motion to a *purely actual* God (*actus purus*). See [[Concepts/The Unmoved Mover (Aristotle)]].
- **[[Thinkers/Leibniz]]**: the monad as an active, striving force ([[Concepts/Monad (Leibniz)]]) and *conatus* echo Aristotelian *energeia*; cf. [[Concepts/Conatus (Spinoza)]].
- **[[Concepts/Becoming (Kelly)]] / [[Concepts/Great Chain of Being (Lovejoy)]]**: the actualization-of-potential schema underlies later developmental and hierarchical pictures of nature.
