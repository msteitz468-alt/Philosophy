# Mitigated Scepticism (Hume)

> Hume's own epistemic stance, stated in the final section (*Enquiry* Section XII, "Of the Academical or Sceptical Philosophy"). Against *excessive* (Pyrrhonian) scepticism — unanswerable in the study but powerless in life — Hume advocates a **mitigated** or **Academical** scepticism: the durable residue of Pyrrhonian doubt once "corrected by common sense and reflection." It yields (1) a lasting **modesty and diffidence** that cures dogmatism, and (2) a **limitation of inquiry** to subjects within "the narrow capacity of human understanding" — namely *quantity and number* (demonstration) and *matters of fact* (experience). Everything else is consigned "to the flames."

## Sorting the Scepticisms

- **Antecedent scepticism** (the [[Concepts/Method of Hyperbolic Doubt (Descartes)|Cartesian]] universal doubt of all faculties, prior to inquiry): if taken absolutely it is "entirely incurable" (no privileged first principle, and any advance would have to use the very faculties doubted). In a *moderate* form, however, it is "a necessary preparative to the study of philosophy" — begin with self-evident principles, advance by sure steps, review conclusions. (Hume thus *uses* a tempered Cartesian caution while rejecting the hyperbolic version.)
- **Consequent scepticism** (arising *after* inquiry): the genuine sceptical arguments. Against the **senses** — the representative theory: "nothing can ever be present to the mind but an image or perception," so we can have no experience of the connexion between perceptions and external objects (the appeal to God's veracity to bridge the gap is "a very unexpected circuit"); and the collapse of the primary/secondary quality distinction (bereave matter of all sensible qualities and only "an unknown, inexplicable something" remains). Against **reason** — the paradoxes of infinite divisibility, where the clearest geometrical demonstrations yield conclusions that "shock the clearest and most natural principles of human reason."

## Why Pyrrhonism Fails — and What It Leaves Behind

Excessive (Pyrrhonian) scepticism may "flourish and triumph in the schools," but "the great subverter of Pyrrhonism… is action, and employment, and the occupations of common life." Its doubts "vanish like smoke" before "the first and most trivial event in life": **"Nature is always too strong for principle."** The decisive objection is practical: "no durable good can ever result from it" — if its principles "universally and steadily… prevail, all human life must perish." (Cf. the naturalism of [[Concepts/Custom and Belief (Hume)]].)

But Pyrrhonism, *mitigated*, has two lasting uses:

1. **Modesty / diffidence**: most people are "affirmative and dogmatical," seeing only one side. "A small tincture of Pyrrhonism" abates pride and prejudice; "there is a degree of doubt, and caution, and modesty, which… ought for ever to accompany a just reasoner."
2. **Limitation of inquiry**: a "correct judgment" confines itself "to common life, and to such subjects as fall under daily practice and experience; leaving the more sublime topics to the embellishment of poets and orators, or to the arts of priests and politicians." Philosophy is just "the reflections of common life, methodized and corrected" — it must not "go beyond common life."

## The Proper Objects of Inquiry — and "the Flames"

Section XII restates [[Concepts/Hume's Fork (Relations of Ideas vs Matters of Fact)]] as the positive payoff: the only objects of **demonstration** are *quantity and number* (abstract sciences); all else is **matter of fact**, "incapable of demonstration" and known only through cause and effect grounded in experience. "Divinity or theology" has a foundation "so far as it is supported by experience," but "its best and most solid foundation is faith and divine revelation"; morals and criticism belong to "taste and sentiment," not the understanding. Hence the incendiary close:

> "When we run over libraries, persuaded of these principles, what havoc must we make? If we take in our hand any volume — of divinity or school metaphysics, for instance — let us ask, *Does it contain any abstract reasoning concerning quantity or number?* No. *Does it contain any experimental reasoning concerning matter of fact and existence?* No. *Commit it then to the flames: for it can contain nothing but sophistry and illusion.*"

(A direct ancestor of the logical positivists' verification criterion of meaning.)

## Companion: the Critique of the Design Argument (Section XI)

The preceding section ("Of a Particular Providence and of a Future State") supplies the mitigated sceptic's verdict on natural religion, voiced by a friend speaking *as Epicurus* to the Athenians. The governing rule of **proportionality**: when we infer a cause from an effect, "we can never be allowed to ascribe to the cause any qualities but what are exactly sufficient to produce the effect," and we may not "return back from the cause, and infer other effects… beyond those by which alone it is known to us." Therefore, from a finite, imperfect, mixed world one may infer only a proportionately finite, imperfect cause — never an *infinitely* powerful, wise, and just deity, and never a *future state* of rewards and punishments not already displayed in the present. The "religious hypothesis" can yield "no new fact" — it is explanatorily idle. This is the same engine, in the domain of metaphysics, that [[Concepts/Of Miracles (Hume)]] is in the domain of testimony; together they form Hume's two-front limitation of rational religion, and a frontal challenge to the design argument (cf. Aquinas' Fifth Way).

## Relevance to Vault

- **The third path on scepticism** — completes the vault's set: Pyrrhonian suspension → ataraxia ([[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]], [[Thinkers/Sextus Empiricus]]); Cartesian hyperbolic doubt → certainty ([[Concepts/Method of Hyperbolic Doubt (Descartes)]]); Humean **mitigated scepticism → modesty + limitation + naturalism**. Hume explicitly names and positions against both. Where Augustine answered Porphyry with grace and Descartes answered Sextus with the cogito, Hume answers *both* dogmatism and Pyrrhonism with common life.
- **Maintainer temperament (direct vault meta)**: mitigated scepticism is almost a charter for the disciplined wiki-keeper — *a standing diffidence* (flag uncertainty, present opposing views, date and source claims) plus *a disciplined scope* (work on what the method can actually reach — the sources, the index, the verifiable; leave "the origin of worlds" alone). "Philosophy is the reflections of common life, methodized and corrected" reads almost as a description of the vault's purpose.
- **Lint heuristic**: the Section XII fork + "the flames" is a usable meaning-test (does a claim reduce to relations of ideas or matters of fact? if neither, flag it) — the mature form of the [[Concepts/Impressions and Ideas - Copy Principle (Hume)|Copy-Principle microscope]].
- **AI**: a calibrated stance for an agent — neither paralytic doubt nor overconfident dogmatism, but *graded confidence proportioned to evidence*, with explicit acknowledgment of the bounds of competence (and refusal to confabulate beyond the data — the anti-hallucination analogue of "go no beyond common life").
- **Religion/metaphysics thread**: the proportionality rule (Section XI) is a reusable check against inferring more in a cause than its effect warrants — a guard against over-reading evidence in any domain (including over-claiming mechanism from correlation; cf. [[Concepts/Necessary Connexion and the Two Definitions of Cause (Hume)]]).

**Open questions**:
- **Self-application / stability**: is "mitigated" scepticism a *stable* resting point, or does it slide back toward either dogmatism or the Pyrrhonism it claims to tame? (Hume's own oscillation in Section XII.)
- Does the "flames" criterion consume *itself* (is the principle a relation of ideas or a matter of fact?) — the classic positivist self-refutation worry.
- Does limiting inquiry to "common life" illegitimately foreclose metaphysics, or rightly discipline it?
