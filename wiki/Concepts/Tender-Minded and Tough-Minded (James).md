# Tender-Minded and Tough-Minded (James)

> One-sentence hook: James's diagnosis that "the history of philosophy is to a great extent that of a certain clash of human temperaments" — a "tender-minded" rationalist type and a "tough-minded" empiricist type — with pragmatism offered as the mediator that lets a fact-lover keep a religion.

## Overview

The framing device of Lecture I ("The Present Dilemma in Philosophy"). James's provocative claim: "the potentest of all our premises is never mentioned" — a philosopher's *temperament* "really gives him a stronger bias than any of his more strictly objective premises… he trusts his temperament." Avowing this openly, James sorts thinkers into two "mental makeups," written as two columns:

| **The tender-minded** | **The tough-minded** |
|---|---|
| Rationalistic (going by "principles") | Empiricist (going by "facts") |
| Intellectualistic | Sensationalistic |
| Idealistic | Materialistic |
| Optimistic | Pessimistic |
| Religious | Irreligious |
| Free-willist | Fatalistic |
| Monistic | Pluralistic |
| Dogmatical | Sceptical |

James immediately concedes the "barbaric disjunction" is "monstrously oversimplified," "a brutal caricature" — "few of us are tenderfoot Bostonians pure and simple, and few are typical Rocky Mountain toughs." Most people are an inconsistent "hodgepodge," "living vaguely in one plausible compartment" at a time. But the types "do both exist," "have a low opinion of each other," and the contrast is "literally true" as a force shaping which philosophy a person can stomach: "we measure the total character of the universe as we feel it, against the flavor of the philosophy proffered us, and one word is enough."

## The dilemma it sets up

The modern person James addresses is "almost born scientific" yet "has not neutralized in us all religiousness." He "wants facts… but he also wants a religion" — and finds on offer only "an empirical philosophy that is not religious enough, and a religious philosophy that is not empirical enough." The tough-minded program gives materialism (Haeckel, Spencer) and "the conflict between science and religion in full blast"; the tender-minded gives transcendental idealism (the Anglo-Hegelian Absolute) or "fighting-a-slow-retreat" theism — both "remote," "abstract," dwelling "on so high a level of abstraction that they never even try to come down." Spencer is the telling case: "all skin and bone," yet revered because "his books try to mould themselves upon the particular shape of this particular world's carcase" — "the right kind of thing for the empiricist mind."

**Pragmatism's pitch** (resolved across the whole book): it is "a mediator and reconciler" — "she can remain religious like the rationalisms, but at the same time, like the empiricisms, she can preserve the richest intimacy with facts." It "has no such materialistic bias as ordinary empiricism," yet "no *a priori* prejudices against theology"; "she will count mystical experiences if they have practical consequences." The temperamental axis recurs at the book's end: the absolutistic religion "appeals to the tender-minded," the pluralistic/meliorist scheme "to the tough" — and pragmatism brokers the "mixed" majority.

## Contrasts / Lineage

- James names "Plato, Locke, Hegel, Spencer" as "temperamental thinkers" who "set their stamp on philosophy." Within the vault the columns sort recognizably: tender-minded ≈ [[Thinkers/Plato]], [[Thinkers/Spinoza]] (monist), [[Thinkers/Plotinus]] (the One), the Absolutists; tough-minded ≈ [[Thinkers/Hume]], [[Thinkers/Epicurus]]/[[Thinkers/Lucretius]] (materialist/pluralist), [[Thinkers/Sextus Empiricus]] (sceptical).
- **Resonance with [[Concepts/Philosophy as Involuntary and Unconscious Autobiography (Nietzsche)]]**: James's "temperament is the potentest premise, never mentioned" is strikingly close to Nietzsche's "every great philosophy is the confession of its originator… involuntary and unconscious autobiography," with "the moral purpose as the vital germ." Both unmask the impersonal pose of philosophy. (James: democratic, conciliatory; Nietzsche: genealogical, suspicious.)
- **Vs. [[Concepts/Equipollence (Isosthenia) and the Modes]]**: where Sextus opposes equal arguments to suspend judgment, James opposes equal *temperaments* — and then refuses suspension, brokering a working synthesis.
