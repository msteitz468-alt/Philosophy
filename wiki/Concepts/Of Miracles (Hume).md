# Of Miracles (Hume)

> Hume's argument (*Enquiry* Section X) that testimony can never rationally establish a miracle as the foundation of a religion. Its governing rule — *"A wise man proportions his belief to the evidence"* — yields the maxim: **no testimony suffices to establish a miracle unless its falsehood would be more miraculous than the fact it endeavours to establish.** Since a miracle is "a violation of the laws of nature," against which stands "a firm and unalterable experience," the proof against it is "as entire as any argument from experience can possibly be" — and (Part II) no actual report has ever met the bar. The most famous argument in the philosophy of religion.

## Part I — The Maxim (an a priori weighing)

Testimony's authority is itself empirical: we trust witnesses only because experience shows a "usual conformity of facts to the reports of witnesses." So testimony is *one* experience-based proof, to be weighed against any contrary experience. When the fact reported is *marvellous*, "there is a contest of two opposite experiences," and we must "deduct the smaller number from the greater."

A **miracle** is "a violation of the laws of nature." But "a firm and unalterable experience has established these laws"; "there must… be a uniform experience against every miraculous event, otherwise the event would not merit that appellation." Thus the very thing that makes an event a miracle (maximal contrariety to experience) also makes the evidence against it maximal — "a direct and full proof, from the nature of the fact."

Hence the maxim: *"That no testimony is sufficient to establish a miracle, unless the testimony be of such a kind, that its falsehood would be more miraculous than the fact which it endeavours to establish."* Confronted with a report of a resurrection, "I weigh the one miracle against the other… and always reject the greater miracle." (This balancing of likelihoods is widely read as a proto-**Bayesian** comparison of priors and the likelihood of testimonial error.)

## Part II — No Actual Miracle Meets the Bar (four a posteriori reasons)

Even granting Part I's concession, no testimony has in fact amounted to a full proof:

1. **Witnesses**: never enough men "of such unquestioned good-sense, education, and learning… undoubted integrity… and credit and reputation," attesting facts in so public a manner as to secure against delusion or design.
2. **The passion for wonder**: surprise and wonder are "agreeable emotions" that bias us toward the marvellous; religion joined to the love of wonder ends "common sense." Enthusiasts may sincerely deceive themselves, or use "pious frauds" for a holy cause.
3. **Provenance among "ignorant and barbarous nations"**: miracle reports "abound" chiefly among, or descend from, the credulous; as nations grow enlightened, prodigies "grow thinner every page" (the false prophet Alexander chose ignorant Paphlagonia).
4. **Mutual destruction of rival religions**: every religion's miracles aim to establish *that* religion and thereby discredit all the others; since the religions of Rome, Turkey, Siam, and China cannot all be true, their miracles are "contrary facts" that cancel one another's testimony.

Hume tests the *best*-attested cases — Vespasian's cures (Tacitus), the doorkeeper's restored leg (Cardinal de Retz), and the well-documented miracles at the tomb of the Jansenist **Abbé Pâris** (numerous credible witnesses, a learned age, never refuted by the hostile Jesuits) — and rejects them all: even maximal human attestation is outweighed by the "absolute impossibility or miraculous nature of the events." The Queen Elizabeth thought-experiment: he would sooner believe an elaborate hoax than a genuine resurrection.

## Limits and the Ironic Coda

Hume carefully limits the thesis: a marvel of "the decay, corruption, and dissolution of nature" (e.g., eight days' universal darkness), if extensively and uniformly attested, *could* be rationally accepted and investigated as a natural event — what cannot be established is a miracle *as the foundation of a system of religion*. He closes with notorious irony: "Our most holy religion is founded on Faith, not on reason," and whoever "is moved by Faith to assent to it, is conscious of a continued miracle in his own person" — putting religion to a rational trial "it is, by no means, fitted to endure."

## Relevance to Vault

- **Evidence discipline (highest practical value)**: "proportion your belief to the evidence" + the testimony calculus is a near-**Bayesian** rubric for weighing *any* surprising report against base rates and the prior probability of error. Directly serviceable for the vault's contradiction-flagging and source-reliability practice, and for professional judgment (CPCU risk assessment, claims evaluation, due diligence): an extraordinary claim demands evidence whose falsehood would be more surprising than the claim.
- **Religion thread**: the epistemic complement to Section XI's metaphysical attack on the [[Concepts/Universal Way of Salvation (Augustine)|design/providence]] arguments; together they form Hume's two-front critique of rational religion. Engages the vault's [[Thinkers/Thomas Aquinas]]/[[Thinkers/Augustine]] material from the *outside* (probability of testimony) rather than the inside (faith).
- **Skeptical cluster**: a *targeted* scepticism — not Pyrrhonian suspension on everything ([[Concepts/Epoché (Suspension of Judgment)]]) but a precise rule for one class of claims, expressing the [[Concepts/Mitigated Scepticism (Hume)|mitigated-scepticism]] temperament (modesty + proportion).
- **AI / vault meta**: a model for *report aggregation under uncertainty* — weighting testimony by source reliability and prior plausibility, and recognizing that independent, mutually contradictory sources can cancel rather than corroborate. A check on credulity toward fluent but extraordinary claims (including a model's own confident outputs).

**Open questions**:
- **Circularity / question-begging**: does building "uniform experience against" the event into the *definition* of a law of nature prejudge whether a miracle could ever be established? (The standard objection.)
- Does the argument prove too much — would it bar belief in *any* genuinely unprecedented but real event?
- Is the "more miraculous falsehood" balance a sound probabilistic principle, or does it under-weight the cumulative force of independent reliable witnesses?
