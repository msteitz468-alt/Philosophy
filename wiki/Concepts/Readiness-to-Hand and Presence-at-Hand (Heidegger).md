# Readiness-to-Hand and Presence-at-Hand (Heidegger)
> The hammer in use disappears into its purpose; only when it breaks does it show up as a mere thing — and in that breakdown, the entire referential structure of the world flashes into view.

## Summary

The distinction between **ready-to-hand** (*zuhanden*) and **present-at-hand** (*vorhanden*) is one of Heidegger's most influential and concrete contributions to philosophy. It captures two fundamentally different ways entities can show up in experience, and it grounds his critique of the Western ontological tradition from [[Thinkers/Aristotle]] to [[Thinkers/Descartes]].

### Ready-to-Hand (Zuhanden)

Entities are *ready-to-hand* when they are encountered as **equipment** — things used transparently within a practical context. The hammer in the hand of a carpenter is not an object of contemplation; it withdraws into its function. One does not "perceive" the hammer and then decide to use it; one hammers. The hammer refers to the nails, the nails to the boards, the boards to the house being built, the house to the family that will dwell in it. Equipment exists within a **referential totality** — a web of "in-order-to" relations that terminates in a "for-the-sake-of" that is always [[Concepts/Dasein (Heidegger)|Dasein]]'s own being.

Crucially, ready-to-hand entities are **inconspicuous**: they do their work precisely by not drawing attention to themselves. A well-functioning tool is, in a phenomenological sense, invisible.

### Present-at-Hand (Vorhanden)

Entities are *present-at-hand* when they are regarded as **mere things** — detached from practical context, available for disinterested theoretical inspection. A rock on a laboratory table, examined for its chemical properties, is present-at-hand. The mode of access is *staring* (*Hinsehen*) rather than *using*.

Heidegger's key claim is that presence-at-hand is **derivative**: it is not the primary way entities show up. The tradition from Aristotle through Descartes made it primary by treating all entities as substances with predicable properties — bearers of "presence." Heidegger reverses this: the theoretical, detached stance is a *modification* of practical engagement, not its ground.

### Breakdown and Disclosure

The transition from ready-to-hand to present-at-hand occurs through **breakdown**. When the hammer head comes off, the leather is missing, or the light is too dim, the tool becomes *conspicuous* — it intrudes into awareness. Three modes of breakdown:

1. **Conspicuousness** (*Auffälligkeit*): the tool malfunctions.
2. **Obtrusiveness** (*Aufdringlichkeit*): required equipment is missing.
3. **Obstinacy** (*Aufsässigkeit*): something blocks the task.

In each case, the referential totality — normally invisible — **flashes into view**. The broken hammer reveals the entire workshop, the project, and Dasein's purposes in a single disclosure. Breakdown is thus philosophically productive: it makes the worldly structure of significance visible.

This insight has proven remarkably fertile beyond philosophy: Hubert Dreyfus applied it to AI (expert systems model only the present-at-hand, missing the ready-to-hand competence of skilled coping); the distinction shapes design theory (good design is invisible — it becomes ready-to-hand).

## From Being and Time (Primary Text)

Stambaugh renders the pair **handiness** (*Zuhandenheit*) / **objective presence** (*Vorhandenheit*), and *Zeug* as "useful thing." §15: "We shall call the useful thing's kind of being in which it reveals itself by itself handiness" — and "No matter how keenly we just look at the 'outward appearance' of things … we cannot discover handiness"; "the less we just stare at the thing called hammer, the more we take hold of it and use it." A totality of useful things always precedes any individual one ("there 'is' no such thing as a useful thing" alone). §16 gives the three breakdown modes (conspicuousness/obtrusiveness/obstinacy) through which the referential totality — world — lights up; §17 analyzes signs as useful things whose serviceability is *indicating*, making a referential totality explicit for circumspection (*Umsicht*).

The Descartes critique (§§19–21) in the primary text: Descartes defines world as *res extensa* (hardness reduced to resistance to change of location), evades the meaning of being common to God, *res cogitans*, and *res extensa*, and lets mathematical *intellectio* dictate access — "Descartes does not allow the kind of being of innerworldly beings to present itself, but rather prescribes to the world, so to speak, its 'true' being on the basis of an idea of being (being = constant presence) the source of which has not been revealed" (§21). Adding "value predicates" to extended things cannot recover handiness — values are just more objectively present determinations. §69b then explains science positively: theory arises by a change-over (*Umschlag*) in the understanding of being — the hammer's place becomes a mere position, the surrounding world is "released" — and mathematical physics is decisive because its projection of nature "discloses an a priori."

## Key Claims / Positions

### How Different Thinkers Approach This

- **[[Thinkers/Aristotle]]**: Treated all entities as *ousia* (substances) with essential and accidental properties — everything is, in Heidegger's terms, present-at-hand. The ontological difference between a tool-in-use and a rock-under-inspection is invisible in Aristotle's categorial framework.
- **[[Thinkers/Descartes]]**: Reduced the world to *res extensa* — matter characterised solely by measurable properties (shape, size, motion). This is the most extreme version of treating everything as present-at-hand. See [[Concepts/Being-in-the-World (Heidegger)]].
- **Husserl**: His account of perceiving a table (walking around it, synthesizing perspectival appearances) is better than Descartes's, but still treats the table as an object of contemplation rather than something used. Heidegger: "when direction on an object is taken as the basic structure of consciousness, being in the world is characterized far too explicitly and sharply."
- **[[Thinkers/Aristotle]]'s *phronesis***: Heidegger was deeply influenced by Aristotle's practical wisdom (*Nicomachean Ethics* VI), which recognises a form of knowledge embedded in action. The ready-to-hand could be seen as Heidegger's ontologisation of Aristotelian *phronesis*.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- > [!warning] If readiness-to-hand is primary and presence-at-hand derivative, how does natural science — which treats entities as present-at-hand — achieve such spectacular success? Heidegger's account of the "projection" underlying science (a deliberate modification of pre-scientific understanding) has been criticised as underdeveloped.
- The distinction maps cleanly onto workshop contexts but becomes strained for natural phenomena (mountains, storms) that are neither tools nor objects of disinterested theory.
