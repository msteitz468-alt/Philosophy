# Consciousness and the Everett Interpretation (Chalmers)
> "The Schrödinger equation holds, and that is all" — one big superposed world, with the appearance of discreteness supplied by minds; "all interpretations of quantum mechanics are to some extent crazy."

## Summary

*The Conscious Mind* ch. 10. "The problem of quantum mechanics is almost as hard as the problem of consciousness." QM cannot explain consciousness (same explanatory gap), but a theory of consciousness can illuminate QM: both are problems "about the relationship between the physical structure of the world and our experience of the world."

The measurement problem: "measurement" and "macroscopic" cannot figure in fundamental laws. The only literal collapse criterion "remotely tenable" is consciousness-collapse (London & Bauer, Wigner) — which presupposes dualism, yet Chalmers rejects it (his near-ubiquitous experience would collapse superpositions too early; collapse is nonlocal, "an arbitrary, tacked-on element"). Decoherence explains decomposition, not actuality. GRW and Bohm "postulate complexity behind simplicity" — of Bohm, quoting Albert & Loewer, "the God of the Bohm view does not play dice, but he has a malicious sense of humor."

Chalmers's preference is a "one-big-world" **Everett** reading: superposition everywhere; splitting "is only in the minds of observers." His own theory supplies what Everett left unanalyzed, via the **superposition principle**: if physical state P gives rise to maximal phenomenal state E, superpositions containing P do too — derived from computational implementation, linearity, and [[Concepts/Organizational Invariance and the Qualia Arguments (Chalmers)]]. So discreteness comes from the mind, no preferred basis needed; which successor is *me* is "just another indexical phenomenon," with Parfit dissolving the identity worry. The unresolved cost is probability: why squared amplitudes govern my experiences. Verdict: Everett is "easily the most intellectually appealing... but I confess that I cannot wholeheartedly believe it. Maybe it is simply too strange to believe."

## How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Wigner / London & Bauer**: consciousness-collapses-the-wave-function — the dualist route Chalmers conspicuously declines.
- **Penrose**: quantum gravity as consciousness's ground is doubly rejected (ch. 3 on explanation, ch. 9 on Gödel); Penrose's own demand — "a theory of consciousness would be needed before the many-worlds view can be squared with what one actually observes" — is precisely what Chalmers claims to supply.
- **[[Thinkers/Douglas Hofstadter]]**: quoted on the branching-self puzzle ("What attaches me-ness to the viewpoint of this body evolving down this branch...?").
- **[[Concepts/Conditional Rendering and Quantum Indeterminacy (Virk)]]**: Virk reads quantum indeterminacy as computational optimization; Chalmers reads it through psychophysical law — two radically different uses of the same physics.
