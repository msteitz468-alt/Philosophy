# Principle of Continuity (Lovejoy)

> The assumption that nature makes no leaps; that between any two natural species or forms there must (and therefore does) exist an intermediate type; that qualitative differences constitute continuous series with only infinitesimal gradations; that "the transition from plants to animals is continuous." Derived from Aristotle and directly deducible from the principle of plenitude (gaps would imply the Source was not fully "good" or the creation not "full"). One of the three unit-ideas fused into the Great Chain of Being. Lovejoy's term for this recurrent motif in Western thought.

## Summary

From Lovejoy, Ch. II:

Aristotle furnished the definition of the continuum ("Things are said to be continuous whenever there is one and the same limit of both wherein they overlap and which they possess in common") and applied it to quantities, motions, time, and space. He did not assert a single continuous series for all qualitative differences, but he supplied the materials and examples for later thinkers.

Crucially, from plenitude one can deduce continuity: "If there is between two given natural species a theoretically possible intermediate type, that type *must* be realized — and so on *ad indefinitum*; otherwise, there would be gaps in the universe, the creation would not be as 'full' as it might be, and this would imply the inadmissible consequence that its Source or Author was not 'good,' in the sense which that adjective has in the *Timaeus*."

Aristotle's biological examples (endlessly repeated for centuries):
- "The transition from plants to animals is continuous; for one might question whether some marine forms are animals or plants, since many of them are attached to the rock and perish if they are separated from it." (Zoophytes as the favorite, endlessly cited illustration.)
- Seals "in some sense land and water animals in one"; bats "intermediate between animals that live on the ground and animals that fly, and may therefore be said to belong to both or neither."
- "Participating in the nature of both man and quadrupeds is the ape."

This introduced into natural history (and philosophy) the habit of thinking in terms of continuity, "infinitely delicate shadings-off of everything into something else, of the overlapping of essences," as opposed to (or in tension with) Aristotle's other influence: fixed genera, indivisible species, and sharp classifications indispensable for language but not always true to nature.

The principle becomes central to the Chain: the series must be "composed of an infinite... number of links... every one of them differing from that immediately above and that immediately below it by the 'least possible' degree of difference."

## Key Claims / How Different Thinkers Use This

- **Aristotle**: Introduced the idea and examples into natural history; recognized classification limits and non-conformity of nature to sharp divisions; provided the psychological hierarchy of soul powers (each higher possessing all of lower + one more) that lent itself to continuous gradation.
- **Neoplatonists / medieval**: The Chain as continuous series (Macrobius's chain and mirrors metaphors; "every possible grade up to the ens perfectissimum").
- **Leibniz et al.**: "Nature never makes leaps" (a Leibnizian slogan with older roots); infinitesimal gradations support the "best" (fullest) world.
- **18c biology**: Search for missing links to realize the continuous full series; pre-Darwinian transformism often assumes continuous gradation over time.
- **Lovejoy**: The principle, though supposed to be implied by plenitude, is at variance with it when consequences are drawn: a universe "full" of maximal diversity of *kinds* must be full of qualitative leaps; "a qualitative continuum... is a contradiction in terms." Wherever a new *quale* appears, there is eo ipso a breach of continuity. The two principles (plenitude and continuity), though allied, conflict.

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- With plenitude: see [[Concepts/Great Chain of Being (Lovejoy)]] and Plenitude page. Maximal diversity of kinds requires abrupt passages to something different; "there is at every point an abrupt passage to something different, and there is no purely logical principle determining where or when such a passage must occur."
- With sharp classification (Aristotle's other legacy): the "non-conformity of nature to those sharp divisions which are so indispensable for language and so convenient for our ordinary mental operations." Tension between discrete concepts (necessary for thought/language) and continuous reality.
- With modern science: discontinuities, phase transitions, quantum leaps, punctuated equilibria, emergent properties at new levels — all breaches of strict continuity. Cross to Kuhn (revolutions as non-continuous gestalt shifts), Pearl (interventions as "graph surgery" that cut/re-wire, not smooth gradients), Hofstadter (tangled hierarchies and strange loops as non-unilinear self-reference).
- Mathematical vs. qualitative: the paradoxes of the mathematical continuum (resolved or not by modern math) vs. the qualitative case, where new kinds appear.
