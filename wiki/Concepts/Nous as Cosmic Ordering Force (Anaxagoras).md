# Nous as Cosmic Ordering Force (Anaxagoras)

> The cosmological doctrine that Mind (Nous) is the ultimate, pure agent that initiates the motion of the universe and imposes order upon primordial matter.

## Summary

The introduction of **Nous** (νοῦς) by Anaxagoras represents a turning point in Western philosophy. Prior to Anaxagoras, cosmological motion was explained either by assuming matter is alive and self-moving (Milesian hylozoism) or by proposing physical forces of attraction and repulsion (Empedocles' Love and Strife). Anaxagoras introduced an intellectual, immaterial principle (Mind) as the source of cosmic order and physical motion.

---

## Core Qualities of Nous

**1. Absolute Purity**:
- While all material objects are mixed and contain portions of everything else ("everything is in everything"), Nous is completely unmixed and exists by itself: *"Nous is mixed with nothing, but is alone, itself by itself."*
- If Nous were mixed with anything else, it would be hindered from ruling and knowing all things.

**2. Omniscience and Omnipotence**:
- Nous possesses complete knowledge of all things, past, present, and future, and has absolute power over all things that have life (souls).

**3. Initiator of Motion (The Vortex)**:
- primordial matter was originally a static, uniform mixture of all things.
- Nous initiated a rotation (vortex or *perichoresis*) at a single point.
- This rotation spread rapidly, separating out the opposite qualities (hot/cold, wet/dry, light/dark) and forming the stars, sun, moon, and earth.

---

## Historical Significance and Critiques

**The Socratic Disappointment**:
- In Plato's *Phaedo* (97b-99d), Socrates describes his excitement at hearing that Anaxagoras claimed "Mind is the disposer and cause of all." Socrates assumed this meant Nous arranged everything for **the best** (teleology).
- However, upon reading Anaxagoras, Socrates was deeply disappointed to find that Nous is only used as a *deus ex machina* to initiate the initial vortex, after which Anaxagoras explains all subsequent physical phenomena using mechanical, material causes (air, ether, water) rather than teleological purposes.

**Foundations of Idealism**:
- Despite this critique, the concept of Nous paved the way for Plato's Demiurge (in the *Timaeus*) and Aristotle's Unmoved Mover (in the *Metaphysics*)—metaphysical systems where intellect/form is superior to matter.

## Contradictions / Open Questions
- > [!warning] Material or Immaterial? Although Nous is described as "the thinnest of all things and the purest," and is contrasted with mixed matter, it is still described in physical terms (having spatial location and material properties), reflecting the early difficulty in conceiving a purely immaterial mind.
